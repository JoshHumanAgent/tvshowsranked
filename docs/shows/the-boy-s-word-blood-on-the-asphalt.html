What It Feels Like

1980s Kazan, USSR. Teenage street gangs fight for territory, respect, and survival. Marat is drawn into this world. The Soviet Union's collapse is coming; the boys live in its shadow. Russian crime drama based on real events: youth violence, generational trauma, the end of an empire.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

The ensemble cast creates the gang dynamics: loyalty, betrayal, coming of age in violence. The performances are raw—young actors representing Soviet youth with no future. The intensity is Russian drama at its most visceral.

World Building (9/10)

1980s Kazan: the Soviet Union before collapse, the courtyard gangs, the codes of honor, the generational poverty. The world is specific and historical—a society rotting before it falls.

Cinematography (8/10)

Russian production: 1980s USSR rendered with detail, the violence staged with realism, the courtyards photographed with period accuracy. The style is gritty—authentic to time and place.

Visual Spectacle (6/10)

The spectacle is violence: gang fights, street battles, the brutality of Soviet youth. The production doesn't glamorize—the violence is ugly and real. Not for everyone.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Boys without fathers, a society collapsing, violence as expression, the codes that replace law, what happens when empires fall. The show explores Soviet collapse through youth gangs. Profound historical context.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Marat's initiation, the gang wars, the Soviet collapse approaching. The structure is rise and fall—youth drawn into violence, the consequences, the historical shadow. The pacing is relentless.

Narrative Path & Resolution (8/10)

Complete miniseries. The story of the gangs, the collapse, the aftermath. Based on real events, the ending is history. Satisfying as narrative and document.
