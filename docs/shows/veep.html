What It Feels Like to Watch

Veep is the funniest show ever made about American politics, and also the meanest. Julia Louis-Dreyfus plays Selina Meyer, Vice President of the United States, a woman of boundless ambition and minimal competence whose team is a collection of sycophants, incompetents, and actively malicious actors. They scheme, they fail, they blame each other, they abuse their staff, they insult everyone around them. And through it all, they keep failing upward.

Created by Armando Iannucci (The Thick of It, In the Loop), Veep imports British political satire's signature viciousness and applies it to Washington D.C. The dialogue is obscenely creative—insults that would make a sailor blush, delivered at lightning speed by a cast that seems to be competing for who can be the most horrible. There's no moral center; everyone is awful, everyone is compromised, everyone deserves what they get. And yet you can't stop watching.

The show evolves over seven seasons. Selina becomes President, loses the presidency, runs again, wins, loses again. Her daughter Catherine becomes a person (after being ignored for seasons). Her chief of staff Amy (Anna Chlumsky) and communications director Dan (Reid Scott) circle each other like sharks. Jonah Ryan (Timothy Simons), the most annoying person on Earth, becomes a Congressman. It's a portrait of a system that rewards mediocrity and punishes integrity, and it's screamingly funny.

The finale is brutal and perfect. Selina becomes President again, at the cost of everything that made her human. She trades her daughter's happiness for political advantage. Her staff turns on each other. She "wins," but victory is empty. Then there's a time jump, and she dies alone. At her funeral, no one has anything kind to say. It's a stunningly dark ending for a comedy, and it's exactly right.
