What It Feels Like to Watch

Normal People is about two people who can't figure out how to be together or apart. Marianne and Connell grow up in the same small Irish town. He's popular, working-class, the soccer star. She's rich, isolated, the outcast. They start a secret relationship in secondary school. He won't acknowledge her publicly. They go to Trinity College Dublin together. Now she's popular and he's the outsider. They keep circling each other for years.

Sally Rooney's novel became a phenomenon—and the adaptation captures what made it special. This isn't a story about grand gestures or dramatic obstacles. It's about the small ways people hurt each other, the misunderstandings that compound, the patterns we repeat even when we know better. Marianne and Connell love each other, but love isn't the same as knowing how to be in a relationship.

Paul Mescal and Daisy Edgar-Jones give performances that feel less like acting than like existing. The show is famous for its intimate scenes, filmed with an intimacy coordinator and treated with unusual care—not as performance, but as communication. You understand why these people keep coming back to each other by watching how they touch.

The structure follows the novel: short chapters, time jumps, scenes that feel like fragments of a life you're piecing together. We see Marianne in Sweden, Connell in Dublin, and then them together again. We see their families, their friends, their therapists. The show is interested in how class and trauma shape what we think we deserve. Marianne comes from money and abuse. Connell comes from poverty and silence. They understand each other, and they also don't.

Twelve half-hour episodes. A complete story. An ending that's hopeful without being false. It's rare to see a romance take emotional complexity this seriously without becoming bleak.

Characters & Acting (10): Paul Mescal and Daisy Edgar-Jones are revelatory. Their chemistry is unmistakable, but so is their ability to play distance, confusion, and the specific ways this particular relationship fails.

Resolution (9): The ending is perfect—not a conclusion, but a continuation. They're not fixed. They're still figuring it out. But they've grown.

Cinematography (8): The intimate scenes are shot with unprecedented care. But so are the quiet moments—Marianne alone in Sweden, Connell at a party he doesn't fit into.
