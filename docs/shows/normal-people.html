Normal People

2020 • Hulu/BBC • 12 Episodes

Score: 7.85
Drama, Romance

What It Feels Like

Connell and Marianne go to the same school. He's popular; she's a loner. They have sex. They don't tell anyone. They love each other and can't say it. Sally Rooney's novel adapted: millennial romance as miscommunication, intimacy as vulnerability, two people learning to be together.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (10/10)

Paul Mescal as Connell: the working-class boy at university. Daisy Edgar-Jones as Marianne: the middle-class girl who feels different. Their chemistry is electric. The sex scenes are filmed with rare honesty.

World Building (8/10)

Small-town Ireland, then Trinity College Dublin. The class differences, the friend groups, the parties, the isolation. The world is specific to millennial Ireland.

Cinematography (8/10)

BBC/Hulu production: intimate close-ups, silence as communication, the landscape of longing. The style is observation—watching two people fail to connect.

Visual Spectacle (1/10)

No spectacle. This is young love: misunderstandings, silences, sex that matters. The "spectacle" is emotional—the intensity of not saying what you feel.

Conceptual Density (9/10)

Class and love, intimacy as risk, whether we can be seen, communication vs. miscommunication, mental health in relationships. The show explores these with precision.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Every few episodes: together, apart, together, apart. The structure is relationship—will they say what they mean? The drive is hope they will.

Narrative Path & Resolution (8/10)

Complete miniseries. The ending is right for the story—not definitive, but earned. Millennial romance at its most honest.
