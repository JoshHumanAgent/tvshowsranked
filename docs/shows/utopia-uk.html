What It Feels Like to Watch

Utopia is the most visually distinctive conspiracy thriller ever made for television. Its colors are oversaturated neon—yellows, greens, magentas that hurt to look at. Its violence is creative and disturbing. Its plot is dense, paranoid, and completely gripping. It's about a graphic novel that predicts future disasters, a conspiracy that spans decades, and the unlucky people who stumble into something much larger than they imagined.

A group of online fans of an underground comic called "The Utopia Experiments" meet in real life after one of them acquires the manuscript for the sequel. Within hours, they're being hunted by a shadowy organization called The Network. The comic predicts pandemics, economic collapses, wars. The Network wants the manuscript. The fans have it. And Jessica Hyde (Fiona O'Shaughnessy) has been hiding for thirty years, waiting for this moment.

The show is British conspiracy at its most paranoid and violent. The opening scene—a torture sequence involving a spoon and chili peppers—establishes that this won't be polite television. The Network operates through blackmail, assassination, and a network of sleeper agents embedded in government, science, and media. The conspiracy's goal isn't money or power in the usual sense—it's something much stranger.

Fiona O'Shaughnessy as Jessica Hyde is the show's heart. She's been running since she was a child, trained to survive, unable to trust anyone. Her relationship with the group—particularly with Ian (Nathan Stewart-Jarrett), the cynical skeptic—gives emotional weight to the paranoid thriller plot. The show needs you to care about these people as they're hunted, tortured, and killed.

Two seasons, complete story. The American remake on Amazon was cancelled after one season, and it couldn't match the original's visual boldness. The UK version is brutal, beautiful, and completely its own thing—television as graphic novel, with all the blood that implies.
