Itaewon Class

2020 • Netflix • 16 Episodes

Score: 7.30
Drama, Korean, Romance

What It Feels Like

Park Sae-ro-yi spends seven years in prison for assaulting the man who killed his father. He emerges with a single goal: open a bar in Itaewon and destroy the chaebol empire that destroyed his family. Itaewon Class is class warfare as David-and-Goliath revenge: a nobody with principle against a corporate dynasty with no limits. Park Seo-joon plays Sae-ro-yi as stubborn righteousness—the man won't bend, won't cheat, won't abandon his code. Kim Da-mi's Jo Yi-seo is genius-level antisocial, and she falls for him completely. The show spans years, building bar to franchise to empire while the villain grows more desperate. You'll root for principle over power.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Park Seo-joon's Sae-ro-yi is infuriatingly principled—he won't cut corners, won't use connections, won't compromise. This makes him noble and sometimes stupid. Kim Da-mi's Jo Yi-seo steals every scene as a sociopathic mastermind who discovers love. The villain Jang Dae-hee is chilling—a man who believes ruthlessness is virtue. Every character is ideology made flesh.

World Building (7.5/10)

Itaewon as microcosm—international, gentrifying, full of misfits finding community. The bar industry, the franchise business, the chaebol world all feel distinct and researched. The seven-year timespan lets characters age believably. Social class is visible in every interaction.

Cinematography (7/10)

Bright, modern K-drama aesthetic. The bar sequences glow with warmth. The chaebol offices gleam cold. The time-jumps are handled smoothly. Nothing revolutionary, but the visuals serve character and emotion.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

No spectacle. The drama is human-scale: business negotiations, restaurant openings, emotional confrontations. The "war" is corporate. The show trusts character over production design.

Conceptual Density (7.5/10)

Principle versus pragmatism. Class mobility in Korea. Whether revenge can justify any means. The show asks whether success requires corruption—and whether Sae-ro-yi's stubbornness is virtue or vanity. The themes are right on the surface.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

The multi-year structure creates genuine stakes. We watch Sae-ro-yi grow from ex-con dishwasher to franchise owner. The villain escalates in parallel. Each episode advances business and personal plots. The finale confrontation is earned.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Complete series with definitive ending. Romance resolves, business triumphs, revenge pays out. Some find the villain's fate too merciful; others find it earned. The show prioritizes principle over satisfaction. It ends as it begins: Sae-ro-yi staying true.
