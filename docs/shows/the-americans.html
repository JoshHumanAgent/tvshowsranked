What It Feels Like to Watch

The Americans is the best spy show ever made, and it's not about spying. Philip and Elizabeth Jennings (Matthew Rhys and Keri Russell) are Soviet KGB officers posing as a married couple running a travel agency in Washington D.C. It's 1981. They have two children who don't know their parents are Russian. Their next-door neighbor is an FBI counter-intelligence agent. The premise is Hitchcockian dread applied to domestic life.

The show's thesis is that marriage is the ultimate cover. Philip and Elizabeth were ordered to marry; they fell in love for real, or did they? The tension isn't will they get caught—it's can they stay together? Philip wants to defect, to be American. Elizabeth is a true believer. Their children, Paige and Henry, grow up in the lies. The show is about what happens to a family built on deception when the deception becomes the family.

The spy craft is meticulous—dead drops, honey pots, disguises that actually work. But the violence is intimate and disturbing. These aren't action heroes; they're middle-aged parents who kill people and then go home to help with homework. The toll is physical and psychological. "We're in this together," Elizabeth says. Are they? The show keeps asking.

Six seasons, seventy-five episodes, one of television's great finales. The Jennings escape to Russia, leaving their children behind. Paige chooses to stay in America. Henry doesn't know who his parents are. "I don't know what to say to that," the embassy employee tells them about their future. "You'll figure it out," Philip says. They won't. The Americans was always about the cost of lies—political, personal, marital. In the end, everyone pays.
