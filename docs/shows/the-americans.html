What It Feels Like to Watch

The Americans is a spy thriller that becomes a family drama, or maybe a family drama that happens to be about spies. Philip and Elizabeth Jennings (Matthew Rhys and Keri Russell) are KGB officers posing as a married couple in suburban Washington, D.C., running a travel agency and raising two children who have no idea their parents are Soviet agents. Their neighbor, Stan Beeman (Noah Emmerich), is an FBI counterintelligence agent hunting Soviet spies. They have barbecues together.

The show is set during the Reagan administration, and the Cold War history is meticulously researched. But the espionage plots are secondary to the marriage. Philip and Elizabeth were paired by the Centre, not by choice, and their relationship evolves over six seasons from professional partnership to genuine love to something more complicated. They lie to everyone—including each other—constantly. They kill for their country. They wonder what they're doing to their children.

Keri Russell is ferocious as Elizabeth, a true believer whose commitment to the cause never wavers, even as it costs her everything. Matthew Rhys plays Philip as a man who begins to question—his mission, his country, his marriage. The tension between them drives the series. Their daughter Paige (Holly Taylor) gradually discovers the truth and wants to join the family business; their son Henry remains blissfully unaware.

Six seasons build to a finale that is perfect—devastating, quiet, inevitable. The Jennings are exposed, they flee, they make choices that can't be undone. The final image is Elizabeth and Philip in Soviet exile, looking at their home from a distance, understanding what they've lost. It's one of television's great endings.
