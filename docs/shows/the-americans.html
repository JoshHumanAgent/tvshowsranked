What It Feels Like to Watch

Six seasons of spy tension as marriage metaphor. Joe Weisberg's The Americans uses Cold War espionage to explore something more dangerous than international conflict: a long-term relationship between two people who didn't choose each other.

Philip (Matthew Rhys) and Elizabeth Jennings (Keri Russell) are Soviet spies embedded in Reagan-era Washington DC, posing as a married couple running a travel agency. They have two children who don't know the truth. Their neighbor is an FBI counter-intelligence agent. The premise is Hitchcockian; the execution is Shakespearean.

The show's central question isn't whether they'll be caught—it's whether their arranged marriage will become real. Philip wants to, Elizabeth can't. Over six seasons, the spy game and the marriage intertwine until they're inseparable. The finale—"START"—delivers the most perfect ending in television history: Paige walking away on a train platform, Elizabeth and Philip back in Moscow, the mission completed, the marriage over, everything lost except survival.

The wigs! The show's use of disguises becomes a running commentary on identity, performance, and the selves we create for each other.
