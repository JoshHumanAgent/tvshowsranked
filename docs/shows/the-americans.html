What It Feels Like to Watch

The Americans is a spy thriller about a married couple who are Soviet spies living undercover in Washington, D.C. Matthew Rhys plays Philip Jennings, Keri Russell plays Elizabeth Jennings. Theyhave two children who don't know their parents' true identity. Their neighbor is an FBI counter-intelligence agent. The year is 1981. The Cold War is heating up. The Jennings are deep-cover illegals—Soviet citizens trained to pass as Americans, embedded for decades.

The show's genius is its focus on marriage. Philip and Elizabeth were paired by the KGB—they didn't choose each other, they were assigned. Over six seasons, the show asks: can an arranged marriage become real love? Philip wants to defect, to live as Americans. Elizabeth is a true believer, loyal to the cause. "In war, everybody loses," their handler tells them. The show proves it: the spy work destroys everyone, including the spies.

The tension isoperatic. The Jennings seduce, manipulate, and murder in service of the Soviet Union. Their assets become people, then become corpses. Their children—Paige (Holly Taylor) and Henry (Keidrich Sellati)—grow up suspicious, then ignorant, thencomplicit. Paige joins the family business; Henry just plays video games. The FBI neighbor, Stan Beeman (Noah Emmerich), becomes Philip's friend. The betrayal is intimate.

Six seasons, seventy-five episodes, complete. The finale is devastating: Philip and Elizabeth are exposed, flee to Russia, leave their children behind. "We'll get used to it," Elizabeth says of their Siberian apartment. They won't. They've lost their children, their identities, their lives. "I had to," Philip tells Stan. He did. Waris loss. The Americans is the best spy show ever made because it understands: spies don't win. They survive. Barely.
