What It Feels Like to Watch

Yellowstone is a modern Western with Shakespearean ambitions. Kevin Costner plays John Dutton, patriarch of the largest ranch in Montana. His enemies are developers, politicians, and a Native American tribe—all wanting his land. His weapons are his children: Lee (dies in the pilot), Jamie (the lawyer, despised), Beth (the daughter, feral), and Kayce (the former SEAL, torn). The Dutton family is a crime family with sunsets.

The show's appeal is its conservatism—not political (though it is that), but aesthetic. Taylor Sheridan films Montana like a cathedral. The mountains, the horses, the branding, the violence—it's a fantasy of pre-modern America, where men solve problems with guns and loyalty means everything. Costner is perfect as John Dutton, a man whose love for his land exceeds his love for his children. "I made two promises in my life," he says. "One was to this land."

Beth Dutton (Kelly Reilly) is the show's breakout character—a vicious, brilliant, damaged woman who destroys everyone who threatens her father. "I made two promises," she echoes. "One was to my father." Her relationship with Rip (Cole Hauser), the ranch foreman, gives the show its romantic heart. Their love is earned through violence and loyalty. The show's thesis: the modern world is corrupt; only the land (and the violence that protects it) is pure.

Five seasons (as of 2024), fifty-three episodes, with Costner departing and the show's future uncertain. The prequels (1883, 1923) are better written; the spin-offs expand the universe. Yellowstone itself is soap opera with cowboy hats—entertaining, beautifully filmed, shallow. "The world is purple," Beth says, meaning political power has limits. The show's power is its fantasy of a world where men on horses still matter. It's a conservative daydream, but it's beautifully rendered.
