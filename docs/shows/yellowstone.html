What It Feels Like to Watch

Yellowstone is a modern Western with Shakespearean ambitions. Kevin Costner plays John Dutton, patriarch of the largest ranch in Montana, a man who will do anything—legal or illegal—to protect his land from developers, Native American claims, and government interference. The show is a study in power: who has it, who wants it, what people will sacrifice to keep it.

The Dutton family is a collection of damaged warriors. There's Beth (Kelly Reilly), the daughter who's never recovered from her mother's death and channels her rage into corporate warfare. There's Kayce (Luke Grimes), the former Navy SEAL who married a Native woman and lives on the reservation. There's Jamie (Wes Bentley), the adopted son and Harvard-educated lawyer who's never good enough. And there's Rip (Cole Hauser), the foreman who's been raised as a son and serves as the ranch's enforcer. They love each other viciously, betray each other constantly, and close ranks against any outside threat.

Taylor Sheridan writes the West as it wishes it was: rugged, honorable, violent. The show's politics are conservative in the old sense—suspicious of government, protective of tradition, convinced that the world is changing for the worse. But it's also surprisingly critical of its own characters. The Duttons are monsters by any reasonable standard. They have people killed. They manipulate elections. They use the legal system as a weapon. And yet the show asks: what would you sacrifice for your home?

Five seasons, currently ongoing. The show became a cultural phenomenon, spawning prequels (1883, 1923) and making "train station" a euphemism for murder. It's soap opera for people who think they hate soap operas—melodrama in cowboy hats, with beautiful scenery and brutal violence. Costner's departure in season five created chaos both on and off screen, but the show's power remains: a vision of America that the country tells itself, even as it falls apart.
