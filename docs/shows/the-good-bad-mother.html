The Good Bad Mother

2023 • JTBC/Netflix • 14 Episodes

Score: 7.45
K-Drama, Family Drama

What It Feels Like

Young-soon raised her son Kang-ho alone on a pig farm in rural Korea. She was hard on him—beating, berating, demanding he become a prosecutor. He became one, but he grew cold and distant. Then an accident leaves him with the mind of a seven-year-old. He returns to the farm; she cares for him; they both learn what went wrong. Ra Mi-ran and Lee Do-hyun deliver devastating performances. A mother-son story about damage and repair.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Ra Mi-ran as Young-soon is monumental: the hard mother who can't express love, the woman whose cruelty came from trauma. Lee Do-hyun as Kang-ho transforms twice—before the accident (cold prosecutor) and after (childlike, learning). The performances are K-drama acting at its finest: emotional, detailed, devastating.

World Building (7/10)

Rural Korea: the pig farm, the village, the traditions, the poverty. Young-soon's world is specific—a single mother in a judgmental village, a farmer whose son is her ticket out. The world is rendered with care; the farming life is real.

Cinematography (7/10)

K-drama rural aesthetic: green fields, the farm, the village gatherings. The style is warm when Kang-ho is present, harsh in flashbacks. The photography supports the emotional beats without calling attention to itself.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

No spectacle. Farm life, family drama, emotional confrontation. The "spectacle" is acting—watching a mother and son find each other after decades of damage.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Motherhood, sacrifice, the damage done "for your own good," the possibility of repair when the mind resets. The show explores whether a bad mother can become good, whether a cold son can become warm, whether trauma can be undone. Heavy themes handled with K-drama emotion.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

The accident happens early; the rest is repair. Kang-ho regresses and slowly recovers; Young-soon learns to be the mother she couldn't be. The pacing is classic K-drama: emotional beats, revelations, tearful resolutions. The drive is toward understanding and forgiveness.

Narrative Path & Resolution (9/10)

Complete in 14 episodes. The mother-son relationship resolves; the past is forgiven; the ending is earned. K-drama structure at its best: a complete story with emotional payoff.
