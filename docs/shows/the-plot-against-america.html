What It Feels Like to Watch

The Plot Against America arrived as HBO's adaptation of Philip Roth's 2004 counterfactual novel—a vision of America where Charles Lindbergh defeats Franklin Roosevelt in the 1940 election and institutes policies friendly to Nazi Germany. Created by David Simon and Ed Burns (The Wire), the series depicted fascism's rise through one Jewish family's experience in Newark, New Jersey, as America slides toward authoritarianism.

The experience of watching The Plot Against America is one of dread recognition. The series captures how fascism arrives—not through dramatic revolution but through incremental normalization, through laws that don't seem so bad at first, through neighbors who look away. The Lindbergh administration begins with "voluntary" relocation programs and escalates gradually, each step plausible, each step building on the last. The series understood that totalitarianism succeeds through compartmentalization: people accept each step because it doesn't seem so different from the previous step.

The Levin family provides the narrative anchor—Herman (Morgan Spector) and Bess (Zoe Kazan), their sons Philip (Azhy Robertson) and Sandy (Caleb Malis), Bess's sister Evelyn (Winona Ryder). They are prosperous, assimilated American Jews who believe in American democracy. As the country changes around them, each family member responds differently: resistance, accommodation, denial, collaboration. The series captures how fascism divides families, how it forces impossible choices.

The series' great power is its ordinariness. This isn't Nazi Germany; it's America with its particular institutions, its particular rhetoric, its particular forms of discrimination. The fascism is American, conducted through American procedures, justified through American values. The series asked: how close did America actually come? How close could it come again?
