What It Feels Like to Watch

3 Body Problem adapts Liu Cixin's Hugo-winning novel for television. It begins during China's Cultural Revolution, where a young physicist watches her father beaten to death by Red Guards. Ye Wenjie (Rosalind Chao/Zine Tseng) will grow up to make a choice that dooms humanity—or saves it, depending on perspective. She decides humans are beyond redemption and invites an alien civilization to conquer Earth.

The San-Ti are coming. Their world orbits three suns in an unstable trinary system—a "three body problem" that makes their planet alternately habitable and uninhabitable. They need a new home. Earth is the target. They'll arrive in 400 years.

In present-day London, a group of scientists connected by their Oxford days investigate a wave of physicist suicides. Someone is killing scientific progress to prepare Earth for San-Ti domination. The "Oxford Five"—Jin Cheng (Jess Hong), Saul Durand (Jovan Adepo), Auggie Salazar (Eiza González), Jack Rooney (John Bradley), and Will Downing (Alex Sharp)—become humanity's unlikely defenders.

The show's genius is making physics feel urgent. The three-body problem isn't just orbital mechanics—it's existential. The San-Ti have technology that seems like magic: they can project numbers onto retinas, render scientists unable to lie, communicate instantaneously across light-years. They're not evil. They're desperate, like us.

DB Weiss and David Benioff (Game of Thrones) bring scale and humanity to hard science fiction. The VR sequences aboard the San-Ti world are breathtaking. The science, while simplified, remains smart.
