What It Feels Like to Watch

The Office adapted Ricky Gervais's cringe-comedy masterpiece for American network television and, against all odds, became something better. Steve Carell plays Michael Scott, regional manager of Dunder Mifflin Paper Company's Scranton branch—a man desperate to be loved, incapable of reading social cues, prone to saying exactly the wrong thing at every opportunity. The show should be unwatchable. Instead, it's a portrait of American work life that's equal parts painful and profound.

The mockumentary format allows characters to break the fourth wall, sharing their thoughts in confessionals while the camera captures every awkward silence and stolen glance. Jim (John Krasinski) and Pam (Jenna Fischer) became television's sweetest couple—not through dramatic gestures, but through years of small moments: desk pranks, stolen glances, voice messages. Their wedding episode is perfect, not despite, but because of its willingness to let reality intrude on romance.

What elevates The Office beyond pure comedy is its heart. Michael Scott is unbearable, but he's also lonely and trying. Dwight (Rainn Wilson) is absurd, but his loyalty runs deeper than his absurdity. Even Creed (Creed Bratton), the sketchy quality assurance manager, becomes beloved through sheer specificity. The writers knew every character, and the actors committed completely. Nine seasons, 201 episodes, and a parade of guest directors (J.J. Abrams, Joss Whedon, Jason Reitman) who wanted to be part of something special.

The show declined after Carell left—seasons eight and nine are rough—but the finale found the grace notes. Michael returns for Dwight's wedding, steals one last moment of inappropriate joy, and disappears. "I thought it was good," he says. It was. Sometimes cringe becomes something else: tenderness.
