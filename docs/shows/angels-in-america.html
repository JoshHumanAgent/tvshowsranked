What It Feels Like to Watch

Tony Kushner's Angels in America is the Great American Play, and Mike Nichols' 2003 HBO adaptation is the Great American Miniseries. Six hours of television that encompasses AIDS, Reaganism, Mormonism, McCarthyism, gay identity, Jewish identity, and angels crashing through ceilings—and somehow makes it all feel intimate.

Justin Kirk's Prior Walter is the prophet nobody asked for, abandoned by his lover (Ben Shenkman's Louis) as AIDS ravages his body. Al Pacino's Roy Cohn is the villain as tragedy—a closeted power broker dying of AIDS while denying he could have a "homosexual disease." Meryl Streep plays multiple roles (including an elderly rabbi and the Angel of America). Jeffrey Wright reprises his stage role as Belize, Prior's best friend and Roy's nurse.

The play's structure—Millennium Approaches and Perestroika—translates to three hours each. The fantastical elements (angels, hallucinations, ghosts) coexist with the political (the AIDS crisis, Reagan conservatism, the collapse of communism). Kushner's thesis: "The Great Work Begins"—the work of creating a world where people can live without hypocrisy.

Emma Thompson as the Angel (and a homeless woman), Mary-Louise Parker as Harper (the Mormon wife addicted to Valium), Patrick Wilson as Joe (the closeted Republican)—every performance is career-defining. This is television as literature.
