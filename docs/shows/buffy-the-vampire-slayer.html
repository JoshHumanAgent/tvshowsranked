What It Feels Like to Watch

Buffy the Vampire Slayer is high school as hell—literally. "Into every generation, a slayer is born," the prophecy begins. "One girl in all the world, a chosen one." Buffy Summers (Sarah Michelle Gellar) is that girl—blessed with strength, speed, and the burden of fighting vampires, demons, and the forces of darkness. But she's also a teenage girl, trying to pass math, going on dates, arguing with her mom. The show's thesis: growing up is monstrous. Every vampire is a metaphor: adolescence, sexuality, addiction, depression, death itself.

The seasons are structured around Big Bads—Master, Angelus, The Mayor, Adam, Glory, The First—each representing different threats. Season two's Angelus arc is the peak: Buffy's vampire boyfriend turns evil after they have sex, becoming a monster wearing her lover's face. It's the ultimate teenage nightmare—your first love becomes your worst enemy. Season five's Glory is a god, insane, hunting Buffy's sister Dawn (a sister who didn't exist until monks created her to hide a mystical key). Season six is the darkest: Buffy is pulled from heaven, returns depressed, abuses sex with Spike (James Marsters), a vampire who loves her without a soul.

The supporting cast defines the show. Willow (Alyson Hannigan) grows from shy hacker to world-threatening witch. Xander (Nicholas Brendon) is the heart—ordinary, loyal, brave without powers. Giles (Anthony Stewart Head) is the father figure— watcher, librarian, occasionally dark past. The show invented television's season-long arcs while delivering monster-of-the-week episodes. Its innovations: musical episode ("Once More, With Feeling"), silent episode ("Hush"), episodes without Buffy ("The Zeppo"). Joss Whedon's dialogue is distinctive—witty, self-aware, influential on everything that followed.

Seven seasons, 144 episodes, complete. Buffy the Vampire Slayer invented modern television horror—before it, genre shows weren't taken seriously. After it, The WB became a network, serialized storytelling became standard, and "grow up as monster" became a template. It's uneven—season four is weak, season seven is rushed—but the peaks are television history. "The hardest thing in this world is to live in it," Buffy says. She's right. She does it anyway.
