What It Feels Like to Watch

Buffy the Vampire Slayer shouldn't work. A high school cheerleader fights vampires? It sounds like a joke. But Joss Whedon took the ridiculous and made it mythic. Buffy Summers is the Chosen One, but she'd rather be at the mall—a tension that fuels seven seasons of brilliant television.

The show's genius is using monsters as metaphors. High school is literally hell. First love turns you into something you don't recognize. Depression is a demon you can't fight alone. Buffy wasn't the first genre show to do this, but it did it with such wit and emotional honesty that it rewrote the rules.

Sarah Michelle Gellar carries the show, but the ensemble—Alyson Hannigan as Willow, Nicholas Brendon as Xander, Anthony Head as Giles—creates a found family that earns every tear. The musical episode alone ("Once More, with Feeling") would secure its legacy.

Characters & Acting (8.5): Buffy, Willow, Xander, Giles, Spike, Angel—these are iconic characters that defined a generation of television. The chemistry is undeniable. The character arcs are Shakespearean.

Conceptual Density (8.5): The show's metaphor engine never stops. Every monster means something. The Big Bads represent different terrors: The Master (tradition), Angelus (betrayal), The Mayor (authority), Glory (insanity), The First (evil itself).

Resolution (7.5): The finale works—Buffy shares her power, the Hellmouth closes, she can finally breathe. But the final season feels rushed, and some character choices (Willow's magic addiction, Spike's redemption arc) were controversial.

Key Episodes

"Hush" (S4E10) — 28 minutes without dialogue. Emmy-nominated horror. The Gentlemen remain terrifying.

"The Body" (S5E16) — Joyce's death. No music, no monsters. Just grief, rendered with devastating honesty.

"Once More, with Feeling" (S6E7) — The musical. A demon makes everyone sing their secrets. Television's best musical episode.
