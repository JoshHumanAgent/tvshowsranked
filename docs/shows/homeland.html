What It Feels Like to Watch

Homeland is about a CIA officer who might be right or might be insane. Carrie Mathison (Claire Danes) is bipolar—and brilliant. She believes Nicholas Brody (Damian Lewis), a Marine rescued after eight years as an Al Qaeda prisoner, has been turned. He's returning toAmerica a hero. Carrie thinks he's a terrorist. Her bosses disagree. She might be crazy. She might be the only one who sees clearly.

The first season is masterful: is Brody a terrorist or isn't he? The tension builds through car chases, surveillances, political intrigue, and Carrie's deteriorating mental state. She sleeps with Brody—compromising everything—for information that might not exist. "The only thing I'm guilty of is being good at my job," she insists. But is she good at her job, or is she unstable? The show forces viewers to confront how we judge women, how we treat mental illness, how we define sanity in an insane world.

The show evolves across eight seasons: the Middle East, the White House, Russia, Afghanistan. Carrie and Saul Berenson (Mandy Patinkin), her mentor, have a relationship that defines the series—mentor and protege, father figure and difficult daughter, two people who understand each other when no one else does. The final season, set in Afghanistan, is unexpectedly powerful: Carrie goes native, works with the Taliban, betrays Saul for the greater good. Their final conversation, across political divides, is devastating.

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes, complete. Homeland's quality varied—seasons after Brody's death struggled—but it remained relevant. It captured the post-9/11 anxiety: who can we trust? What is security worth? When does protecting America betray America? Carrie Mathison is one of television's most complex characters: brilliant, broken, impossible, essential. "I was right about Brody," she says. She was. It cost her everything.
