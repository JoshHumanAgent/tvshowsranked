What It Feels Like to Watch

Homeland's first season is among the best television thrillers ever made. Carrie Mathison (Claire Danes) is a CIA officer with bipolar disorder who becomes convinced that Nicholas Brody (Damian Lewis), a Marine rescued after eight years in Al-Qaeda captivity, has been turned. He's returning to America as a hero. She thinks he's a terrorist. No one believes her.

The show's genius was making both Carrie and Brody compelling, unreliable protagonists. Carrie is brilliant but unstable, crossing ethical lines, sleeping with Brody to get close to him, convinced she's right without proof. Brody is traumatized, conflicted, possibly radicalized—and also a father, a husband, a man who just wants to go home. We don't know if Carrie is a hero or a danger.

Damian Lewis won an Emmy for walking that line—sympathetic one moment, terrifying the next. Claire Danes won for her portrayal of intelligence work as mania, her face constantly working through calculations, suspicions, breakdowns. The tension of season one builds to a climax that remains stunning.

Later seasons couldn't maintain that quality. The show kept reinventing itself—moving to Israel, to Pakistan, to Germany, to the US presidential campaign—but Carrie and Brody's cat-and-mouse game was the premise's heart. After Brody's death in season three, the show became competent spy drama without the same electricity.

The final seasons, with Carrie opposing a president who might be compromised, felt like the show trying to recapture its original magic. It couldn't, but the first season remains essential viewing—a post-9/11 thriller about the wars we fight and the people who fight them.
