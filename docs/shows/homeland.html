What It Feels Like to Watch

Homeland began as one of television's most electrifying thrillers, then became something more interesting: a study of how the war on terror changed everyone it touched. Claire Danes plays Carrie Mathison, a CIA operations officer with bipolar disorder who's convinced that a rescued prisoner of war has been turned by Al Qaeda. The first season is a cat-and-mouse game between Carrie and Sergeant Nicholas Brody (Damian Lewis), each trying to determine if the other knows the truth.

The show's early genius was its uncertainty. Is Brody a terrorist? Is Carrie paranoid? The answers shift from scene to scene, and the audience is never sure whom to believe. Danes gives one of television's great performances—a woman whose mental illness both enables and undermines her work, whose brilliance and instability are inseparable. Her face registers everything: suspicion, desire, mania, heartbreak. She's exhausting to watch, and impossible to look away from.

After three seasons focused on Brody, the show reinvented itself. Carrie moved from the CIA to private security to the White House to international operations. Each season tackled a different aspect of the post-9/11 world: drone warfare, intelligence manipulation, presidential politics, Russian interference. The quality varied, but the show's commitment to interrogating American foreign policy never wavered. It asked uncomfortable questions and refused easy answers.

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes, complete story. The finale finds Carrie choosing love over country, defecting to Russia with former enemy Saul Berenson's asset. It's a controversial ending that fits the show's thesis: the war on terror corrupted everyone, loyalty has no pure form, survival is the only victory. Carrie Mathison is one of television's most complicated protagonists, and Danes never lets you forget her cost.
