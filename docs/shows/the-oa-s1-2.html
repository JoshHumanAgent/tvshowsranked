What It Feels Like to Watch

The OA is impossible to describe. A blind woman returns after seven years calling herself "the Original Angel." She assembles a group of misfits and teaches them five movements—strange, ritualistic gestures—that can open a portal to another dimension. Also her captor was studying near-death experiences. Also the movements can resurrect the dead.

It's either transcendent or ridiculous, depending on your tolerance for ambiguity. Brit Marling and Zal Batmanglij created something genuinely singular: a sprawling mythology about interdimensional consciousness wrapped in a teen drama about trauma survivors. The first season is beautiful and confounding. The second goes full sci-fi.

Netflix cancelled it after season 2's cliffhanger, so we'll never know the ending. But what exists is unlike anything else—a meditation on grief, storytelling, and whether we can trust the narratives we create to survive.

World Building (9): The OA created a mythology around near-death experiences, dimensions, and human potential that's genuinely original. The rules are opaque but consistent.

Conceptual Density (8.5): This is the show's strength. It trusts viewers to piece together a puzzle without explaining anything. The movements, the dimensions, the captivity narrative—it's all ambiguous on purpose.

Resolution (4): Cancelled mid-story. The season 2 finale is the end, and it's devastating. Brit Marling has hinted at plans to finish it, but nothing concrete.
