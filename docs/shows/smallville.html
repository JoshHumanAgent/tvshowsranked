Smallville

2001 • Superhero Origin • 217 Episodes

Score: 7.02
Drama, Superhero

What It Feels Like to Watch

Smallville reimagined Superman as a teen drama, and in doing so, discovered something fundamental: Clark Kent's adolescence is the most interesting part of his story. Before he can fly, before he works at the Daily Planet, before he becomes the icon—there's just a kid from Kansas with abilities he doesn't understand trying to navigate high school, friendship, and the gradual discovery of who he's meant to be.

Tom Welling plays Clark during his formative years, and the show's genius is in treating the superhero origin as something to be discovered rather than shown. We know he'll become Superman; the pleasure is in watching him not become Superman, episode after episode, as he learns control and responsibility. The kryptonite-of-the-week formula allowed for monster-of-the-week storytelling while developing the central relationships: Clark and Lana, Clark and Chloe, and especially Clark and Lex Luthor.

Smallville is a show that defined the CW aesthetic for a decade—glossy, emotional, serialized in ways that network television hadn't fully embraced for genre shows. Its production values date it, and its ten-season run extended past its natural endpoint, but at its peak it was a genuinely innovative approach to superhero storytelling. It proved that you could strip away the costume and the iconography and still have compelling television. Every modern superhero origin story owes something to what Smallville attempted and often achieved.

Why It Ranks Here

Smallville earns its ranking for defining the superhero-origin-as-teen-drama template, for its central Clark-Lex relationship, and for a run that maintained quality past the point where most network shows decline. The score reflects its influence and its peaks.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Welling grows into the role over ten seasons, starting as a convincingly awkward teenager and ending as someone approaching heroic weight. Michael Rosenbaum's Lex Luthor is the show's secret weapon—their friendship and its deterioration is more compelling than any of the weekly villains. Kristin Kreuk and Allison Mack create memorable female characters in a genre that often forgets them.

World Building (7/10)

Smallville, Kansas becomes a character—a place of secrets, kryptonite, and meteor freaks. The show built its own mythology while connecting to Superman lore in ways that rewarded longtime fans. The origin elements—discovering powers, finding the fortress, learning about Krypton—were paced effectively across seasons.

Cinematography (6/10)

Early 2000s television aesthetics: bright lighting, attractive casts, composition that serves story without style. The show looks like its era, which hasn't aged particularly well. The visual effects improved over the run but never reached film quality. The direction is functional rather than distinctive.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

The spectacle is in powers discovered—super speed, heat vision, the gradual development of abilities. But television budgets limited what could be shown, and Smallville relied more on character drama than action spectacle. The occasional flying sequences in later seasons were notable for finally happening.

Conceptual Depth (6/10)

The show genuinely explored what it would mean to grow up with abilities you couldn't control, to be different in a small town, to have to hide who you are. The Clark-Lex relationship is the most interesting: watching a good man become a villain through choices that feel inevitable only in retrospect.

Narrative Drive (6.5/10)

The monster-of-the-week structure provided reliable episode arcs while the serialized elements—Clark's powers, Lex's descent, the mythological revelations—developed over seasons. Ten seasons is too long; the show repeated itself past natural conclusions. But at its peak, it balanced episodic and serialized storytelling well.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10)

The finale delivered what fans wanted—Clark puts on the suit, becomes Superman—but the journey to that endpoint meandered through seasons of filler. The show found its natural ending several times and kept going. The final episodes honor what came before without matching the peak.
