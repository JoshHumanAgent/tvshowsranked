What It Feels Like to Watch

The Last of Us premiered on HBO on January 15, 2023, created by Craig Mazin and Neil Druckmann from Naughty Dog's acclaimed 2013 video game. The nine-episode first season follows Joel (Pedro Pascal), a smuggler tasked with escorting Ellie (Bella Ramsey), a teenage girl immune to the fungal infection that has destroyed civilization, across a post-apocalyptic America. The series represents the most successful video game adaptation in television history, achieving both critical acclaim and massive viewership.

The series distinguishes itself from typical post-apocalyptic narratives through its focus on character relationships rather than action set pieces. While the infected (zombie-like creatures created by the Cordyceps fungus) provide horror and tension, the series is fundamentally about Joel and Ellie's evolving relationship—from reluctant protector and cargo, to surrogate father and daughter. The emotional beats land with devastating impact because the series invests in character development.

Pedro Pascal's performance as Joel is a masterclass in understated grief. Joel has lost his daughter Sarah (played in the devastating opening episode by Nico Parker) in the outbreak's early hours, and twenty years later he is emotionally shut down, surviving rather than living. Pascal conveys Joel's pain through minimal dialogue and physical performance—his guardedness, his violence when necessary, his gradual softening toward Ellie. The performance earned Pascal an Emmy and established him as a leading man.

Bella Ramsey's Ellie is equally remarkable—foul-mouthed, defiant, vulnerable, and brave. Ramsey captures Ellie's teenage bravado masking her fear, her grief over her own losses (her friend Riley, her guardian Marlene), and her growing attachment to Joel. The character's queerness (established in Episode 7 "Left Behind," which depicts Ellie's relationship with Riley) is handled with matter-of-fact authenticity. Ramsey's performance earned an Emmy and made Ellie an iconic character.

The supporting cast creates a rich post-apocalyptic world: Gabriel Luna as Tommy, Joel's brother who has found purpose in a community; Anna Torv as Tess, Joel's partner who sacrifices herself to ensure Joel and Ellie can continue; Nick Offerman as Bill, the survivalist who finds love with Frank in a beautifully self-contained Episode 3 "Long, Long Time"; Murray Bartlett as Frank; Melanie Lynskey as Kathleen, the revolutionary leader whose grief has made her ruthless; Lamar Johnson as Henry and Keivonn Montreal Woodard as Sam, the brothers whose tragedy in Episode 5 "Endure and Survive" provides the season's most devastating moment; and Storm Reid as Riley in the flashback episode.

Episode 3 "Long, Long Time" stands as one of television's finest hours—a self-contained love story between Bill and Frank that spans decades, from the outbreak's early days to their elderly years. Nick Offerman and Murray Bartlett deliver performances of extraordinary tenderness and grief. The episode's structure—depicting their life together in vignettes, culminating in their assisted suicide pact—provides emotional catharsis that resonates throughout the series. The episode demonstrates that The Last of Us is not merely a zombie show but a meditation on love, loss, and what we endure to survive.

Episode 5 "Endure and Survive" provides the season's most devastating sequence—Henry and Sam's story, culminating in Henry's suicide after Sam (infected) attacks him. The episode's structure—building hope for the brothers' survival, then crushing it—demonstrates the series' willingness to devastate its audience. The performances by Lamar Johnson and Keivonn Montreal Woodard make the tragedy unbearable.

Episode 7 "Left Behind" uses a flashback structure to depict Ellie's relationship with Riley (Storm Reid), her friend from the military school who is bitten alongside Ellie in an abandoned mall. The episode's structure—alternating between the past (Riley and Ellie's day together) and the present (Ellie trying to save Joel after he's injured)—creates emotional resonance between Ellie's past loss and her determination not to lose Joel. The depiction of teenage queer romance in the apocalypse is handled with authenticity and tenderness.

The season finale "Look for the Light" brings Joel and Ellie's journey to its devastating conclusion. Joel's massacre of the Fireflies to save Ellie from the surgery that would kill her (and potentially create a cure) represents the series' central moral question: what do we owe to humanity versus what do we owe to those we love? Joel's choice—saving Ellie at the cost of humanity's hope—and his lie to Ellie about what happened, sets up the moral complexity that will drive future seasons. The finale's ambiguity—whether Joel's choice was right or wrong—is the series' strength.

The series' engagement with video game adaptation is revolutionary. Previous game adaptations had largely failed critically, treating the source material as mere IP to be exploited. The Last of Us, with creator Neil Druckmann's involvement and Craig Mazin's writing (Chernobyl), treats the game as serious source material worthy of artistic adaptation. The series expands on the game's narrative, adding depth to supporting characters and creating episodes (like "Long, Long Time") that stand alone as artistic achievements.

Critical reception was exceptional—the series holds 96% on Rotten Tomatoes, with critics praising the performances, writing, and emotional depth. It became HBO's most-watched debut since Boardwalk Empire and won numerous awards including Emmys for Outstanding Drama Series, Lead Actor (Pascal), and Guest Actor (Offerman). Critics noted that the series transcended its genre to become a profound meditation on love, loss, and survival.

The series stands as the definitive video game adaptation and one of the finest television dramas of recent years. It proves that genre entertainment—zombies, post-apocalypse—can achieve the emotional depth of any prestige drama when treated with intelligence and respect. Pascal and Ramsey's performances, the exceptional writing, and the willingness to devastate its audience make The Last of Us essential viewing. It is television that respects its audience's intelligence and emotional capacity.

Surrogate Family: The series' central theme—the family we choose versus the family we lose. Trauma and Survival: The series explores how trauma shapes survivors—Joel's grief, Ellie's immunity as both gift and burden, the various characters' adaptations to the post-apocalyptic world. The infected represent one threat; the living represent another.

Why 8: Strong thematic exploration of family, sacrifice, and survival. The limitation is occasional familiarity—the themes are well-trodden in post-apocalyptic fiction.
