Murderville

2022 • Comedy Mystery • 6 Episodes

Score: 6.65
Comedy, Mystery

What It Feels Like to Watch

Murderville is an improvised comedy mystery series where Will Arnett plays a detective who has to solve murders with a different celebrity guest each episode—and the guests haven't been given a script. They've been told this is a murder mystery, but they have to improvise their way through interrogations and clue-gathering without preparation. The result is chaotic, uneven, and often genuinely hilarious.

The format is both the show's strength and its limitation. When the guests are game—Conan O'Brien commits fully to the absurdity, Sharon Stone plays the straight woman effectively—the improvisations generate comedy that feels spontaneous in the best way. When guests are less comfortable, the show drags as Arnett tries to carry scenes that aren't clicking. The murders themselves are functional but not memorable; they're scaffolding for the comedy, and the fun is watching familiar faces navigate chaos.

Will Arnett's commitment is complete. As Detective Terry Seattle—a divorced, barely competent cop whose ex-wife is his boss—he anchors the show with the same blend of arrogance and desperation that defined Gob Bluth in Arrested Development. He's playing a character who has to maintain a show while everything around him is falling apart, and his ability to roll with genuinely surprising guest responses creates moments that can't be scripted. Murderville is ultimately more interesting as a format experiment than as consistent entertainment, but when it works, it's delightful. The six-episode count is right—it ends before the premise exhausts itself.

Why It Ranks Here

Murderville earns its position through Will Arnett's performance, its format audacity, and genuinely funny moments that couldn't exist without improvisation. The score reflects a show that's more interesting than consistently excellent.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Will Arnett is the only constant, and he carries the show through sheer force of will. The rotating guest cast provides variety that ranges from inspired (Conan O'Brien) to awkward (guests who freeze under pressure). The scripted supporting cast serves the format, but this is Arnett's show.

World Building (5/10)

The police department and crime scenes are functional backdrops without texture. The show's interest is clearly in the improvisational comedy, and the mystery setting exists to provide that opportunity. Nothing about Murderville would be memorable without the format.

Cinematography (5/10)

The visual approach is sitcom standard—coverage that captures improvisation, lighting that serves comedy without atmosphere. The show looks like what it is: a studio-based comedy production. The direction prioritizes capturing the guests' reactions.

Visual Spectacle (2/10)

There is nothing spectacle-driven. Murderville is entirely about performance and interaction. The visual interest is in watching trained comedians try to keep straight faces while improvisation goes off the rails.

Conceptual Depth (4/10)

This is pure entertainment without thematic ambition. The themes are whatever emerges from improvised scenarios—inconsequential, spontaneous, forgotten by the next episode. Murderville doesn't pretend to be anything other than a comedy experiment.

Narrative Drive (6.5/10)

Each episode has to introduce the mystery, guide the guest through investigation, and reach a solution, all while improvising. When it works, the energy is infectious. When it doesn't, you feel the structure stretching. The format creates urgency even when individual jokes miss.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10) Each episode resolves its murder in ways that don't matter because the solution is arbitrary. The wrap-up is for form's sake; the pleasure is in the journey, not the destination. The show ends before repetition becomes fatigue.
