What It Feels Like to Watch

Mare of Easttown is a murder mystery that's actually about grief. Kate Winslet plays Mare Sheehan, a detective in a Pennsylvania town where everyone knows everyone, where the local hero high school basketball star is now a chain-smoking divorcée investigating a murder while her own life falls apart. The show uses the whodunit structure to explore what happens when you can't move on.

Easttown is Delaware County, Pennsylvania—working class, Catholic, insular. Everyone went to high school together. Everyone knows Mare's history: her basketball glory days, her son's suicide, her failed marriage, her grandson living with her because her daughter-in-law is a recovering addict. The town remembers everything. Mare carries it all.

When a young mother is murdered, the investigation opens old wounds. One year earlier, another girl disappeared and Mare couldn't solve it. The town has lost faith in her. Now she has a chance to redeem herself, but the case keeps connecting to people she knows, people she grew up with, people whose families are intertwined with hers.

Winslet is astonishing. Her accent—Delco, specific and strange to outsiders—never slips. Her posture, her walk, her exhaustion, all of it feels lived in. She's playing someone who has been slowly crushed by life but refuses to stop moving. When she finally breaks, it's devastating because she's been holding it together for so long.

The supporting cast is uniformly excellent. Jean Smart plays Mare's mother with perfect comic timing that never undermines the drama. Julianne Nicholson is Mare's best friend, carrying a secret that could end their friendship. Evan Peters is the county detective assigned to help, a fish-out-of-water whose fresh perspective is exactly what Mare doesn't want.

Characters & Acting (10): Kate Winslet delivers one of the great television performances. The ensemble—Jean Smart, Julianne Nicholson, Evan Peters, Guy Pearce—matches her level. Every character feels specific, real, fully lived.

World Building (9): Easttown exists. The Delco accent, the Wawa references, the Catholic guilt, the high school glory that defines adulthood—it's a fully realized place with its own rules and history.

Resolution (9): The mystery resolves in a way that honors everything that came before. The solution isn't a cheat; it emerges from character. And the final scene—Mare finally entering her son's room—is perfect.

Key Episodes

"Father's Day" (S1E1) — Mare's introduction. The town, the case, the weight she carries. Everything established in one hour.

"Illusions" (S1E4) — The investigation deepens. Old secrets surface. Mare's family life fractures further.

"Sacrament" (S1E7) — The finale. The killer revealed. The grief confronted. A woman finally allowed to mourn.
