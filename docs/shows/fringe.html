What It Feels Like to Watch

Fringe is The X-Files for the J.J. Abrams era—a procedural about a small FBI team investigating cases that defy scientific explanation, with a serialized mythology that spans parallel universes, time travel, and observers from the future. Anna Torv plays Olivia Dunham, an FBI agent drawn into Fringe Division, a secret unit investigating "the Pattern"—unexplained phenomena happening worldwide.

Her partners are Walter Bishop (John Noble), a brilliant but deeply damaged scientist released from a mental institution, and his estranged son Peter (Joshua Jackson), a hustler who becomes Walter's caretaker. Walter's genius and insanity coexist uneasily—he's done terrible things for science, he's forgotten most of them, and they're all coming back to light.

The show's great invention is the alternate universe—a parallel world where the World Trade Center still stands, where things are slightly different. Walter's greatest sin was stealing Peter from that universe after his own son died. This trauma drives the mythology: the cracks between worlds, the shapeshifters, the observers, the war between universes.

John Noble's Walter Bishop is one of television's great characters—funny, heartbreaking, amoral, desperate for redemption, unable to remember why. The episodes where he confronts his past self ("Walternate") are devastating. The fifth and final season jumps to the future, where observers have conquered humanity, giving the show a dystopian conclusion.

Fringe does what X-Files did but with a coherent mythology that actually pays off. It's a miracle of network television—a five-season arc that sticks the landing.
