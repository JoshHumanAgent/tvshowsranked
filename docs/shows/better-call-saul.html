What It Feels Like to Watch

Better Call Saul is a prequel that became its own masterwork. It traces Jimmy McGill's transformation into Saul Goodman, the criminal lawyer from Breaking Bad, but it's not just an origin story—it's a tragedy about a man who can't stop sabotaging himself. Bob Odenkirk's Jimmy is charming, funny, and deeply damaged, and watching his slow slide into criminality is as compelling as Walter White's descent into Heisenberg.

The show parallel-tracks two stories: Jimmy's legal career and Mike Ehrmantraut's (Jonathan Banks) work for the cartel. They intersect occasionally, but mostly they're separate studies in moral compromise. Jimmy wants to be good; circumstances (and his own nature) push him toward bad. Mike wants to provide for his family; his skills draw him deeper into the criminal underworld. Both characters are doomed—we know where they end up—but the journey is the point.

The visual language is pure Breaking Bad: deliberate pacing, symbolic imagery, montages set to music, sequences without dialogue. But Better Call Saul is slower, more patient, more interested in character than momentum. It rewards attention in ways its predecessor didn't always attempt. A character might spend an entire episode trying to return a mistaken delivery, and it's riveting.

The finale brings everything together—Jimmy and Kim (Rhea Seehorn), Jimmy and Walter White, Jimmy and the law. The final episodes are as good as anything in Breaking Bad. The ending is perfect: Jimmy finally accepts responsibility, finally stops running, and finally becomes—briefly, honestly—Jimmy McGill again. Then he goes to prison, where his reputation precedes him. Even behind bars, he's Saul Goodman. Even behind bars, he can't help performing.

Resolution (10): Six seasons, perfect ending. Jimmy's confession in court is one of TV's great final moments.

World Building (9): Albuquerque, expanded. The legal world, the cartel operations—seamless extensions of Breaking Bad.
