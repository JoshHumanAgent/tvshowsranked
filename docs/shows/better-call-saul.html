What It Feels Like to Watch

Better Call Saul is a prequel to Breaking Bad—and somehow, impossibly, it's just as good. Jimmy McGill (Bob Odenkirk) is a small-time con artist turned lawyer, struggling to be taken seriously by the legal establishment. His brother Chuck (Michael McKean) is a respected attorney who believes Jimmy hasn't changed—that he's still "Slippin' Jimmy," the scammer from Cicero. "You're not a real lawyer," Chuck tells him. The cruelty is devastating, deliberate, and formative. Jimmy becomes Saul Goodman because the straight world rejected him.

The show's parallel story: Mike Ehrmantraut (Jonathan Banks), the ex-cop turned fixer from Breaking Bad. His arc is quieter—grief over his son's death, moral compromise, descent into the criminal underworld. Mike is what Jimmy could have been: a man who did bad things for a good reason, then lost the reason. The intersection of Jimmy's comedy and Mike's tragedy creates the show's rhythm—light and dark, joke and violence, Saul and Mike. "I know what I was," Mike says. "I know what I became."

The addition that elevates Saul beyond mere prequel: Kim Wexler (Rhea Seehorn), Jimmy's partner in love and law. Kim is the show's moral center—and also its greatest tragedy. She loves Jimmy, believes in him, follows him into ethical gray areas. "We're bad for each other," she finally realizes. She leaves. It's the moment Jimmy's soul dies; Saul Goodman is what remains. Rhea Seehorn's performance is revelatory—patient, intelligent, slowly breaking. The show's thesis: people become what the world lets them become. Jimmy was good; Chuck refused to see it. Kim believed; Jimmy destroyed her trust. "It's all good, man"—Saul's catchphrase becomes tragic: it was never good.

Six seasons, sixty-three episodes, complete. Better Call Saul ends with Jimmy in prison, finally confessing, finally becoming Jimmy again—if only for Kim. "I said I was sorry," he tells her. "I'm not sorry anymore." The show is a tragedy disguised as a comedy: every joke from Breaking Bad revealed as a cry for help. Jimmy McGill was a good man. The world wouldn't let him stay that way.
