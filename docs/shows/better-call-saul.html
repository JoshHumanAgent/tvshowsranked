What It Feels Like to Watch

Better Call Saul is the prequel that shouldn't have worked. How do you build a show around Saul Goodman, the comic relief lawyer from Breaking Bad? How do you expand his story without diminishing him? Vince Gilligan and Peter Gould solved both problems by making him tragic. Jimmy McGill (Bob Odenkirk) isn't a punchline—he's a man who wants to be good, who tries to be good, and who fails because he can't stop himself from cutting corners.

The show operates on multiple timelines. There's Jimmy's journey from struggling public defender to the criminal lawyer Saul Goodman. There's Mike Ehrmantraut's (Jonathan Banks) descent from fixer to cartel enforcer. There's Nacho Varga's (Michael Mando) fatal involvement with the Salamanca family. And there's Kim Wexler (Rhea Seehorn), Jimmy's partner in love and crime, whose moral deterioration and eventual redemption give the show its emotional center.

The brilliance is structural. Where Breaking Bad was a thriller that accelerated constantly, Better Call Saul is a tragedy that moves with deliberate slowness. Episodes go by without obvious plot movement, then a single scene recontextualizes everything. The black-and-white Gene Takovic sequences show Jimmy's fate after Breaking Bad, adding weight to every choice he makes. The cartel storyline brings violence into a show that's mostly about legal corruption, creating tension between two worlds that will eventually collide.

Six seasons, sixty-three episodes, perfect ending. Jimmy confesses to everything and receives eighty-six years in prison. Kim visits him. They share a cigarette through the bars, a callback to their first meeting. "It's over," he says. The show found tragedy in comedy, humanity in caricature, and proved that some stories are worth telling backwards.
