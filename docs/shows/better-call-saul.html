What It Feels Like to Watch

Better Call Saul is the prequel to Breaking Bad—and somehow, impossibly, its equal. It tells the story of Jimmy McGill (Bob Odenkirk), a con man trying to be a lawyer, who eventually becomes Saul Goodman, the criminal attorney who helps Walter White. The show could have been a cynical cash-grab. Instead, it's a tragedy about good intentions, bad choices, and the slow corruption of a man who wanted to be better than his past.

Jimmy is a small-time lawyer in Albuquerque, struggling to escape the shadow of his brilliant, respected brother Chuck (Michael McKean). "You're not a real lawyer," Chuck tells him. "You're a two-bit hustler." The wound is mortal. Jimmy spends the series trying to prove his brother wrong—and slowly becomes exactly what Chuck feared. Meanwhile, in a parallel story, Mike Ehrmantraut (Jonathan Banks), the fixer from Breaking Bad, descends from ex-cop to criminal enforcer. The two storylines intertwine but remain separate: Jimmy the lawyer, Mike the muscle, both sliding toward the world of Walter White.

The show's brilliance is patience: scenes play out in real time, montapes unfold wordlessly, and the black-and-white flashforwards show Saul after Breaking Bad, hiding in Omaha, managing a Cinnabon. "It's all good, man" becomes "Saul Goodman"—the name itself a joke. He built a persona to hide the pain. It worked until it didn't.

Six seasons, sixty-three episodes, complete. The finale matches Breaking Bad's: Saul confesses, accepts prison, finally admits he's "Saul Goodman" and "Jimmy McGill"—he is both. He loved Kim Wexler (Rhea Seehorn), his partner and conscience, and lost her. The tragedy is: Jimmy was good. He could have stayed good. But he chose the con, chose the game, chose to become Saul. He pays the price. "I broke us," he tells Kim. Yes. He did.
