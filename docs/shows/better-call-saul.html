What It Feels Like to Watch

Better Call Saul shouldn't have worked. A prequel to Breaking Bad focused on the comic relief character? It sounded like a cash grab. Instead, it became a tragedy as profound as its predecessor, exploring how a decent man becomes a cartoon criminal lawyer. Jimmy McGill (Bob Odenkirk) starts as a struggling public defender—earnest, desperate, trying to live down his past as "Slippin' Jimmy," the con artist. He wants to be good. He wants his brother's respect. The system won't let him.

The show's central tragedy is Chuck McGill (Michael McKean), Jimmy's brilliant, mentally ill older brother. Chuck loves Jimmy but doesn't believe he can change. Every time Jimmy does something decent, Chuck sees the old con. Every time Jimmy bends a rule, Chuck's fears are confirmed. The dynamic is Shakespearean: a brother who loves and resents, a man who wants to be good but keeps being told he's bad. Eventually, Jimmy stops trying.

Kim Wexler (Rhea Seehorn) is the show's secret weapon—a lawyer who falls for Jimmy's charm and descends into his schemes. Her arc is devastating: she believes in Jimmy, enables his worst impulses, then realizes what she's become. The finale reveals her fate: she's alive, working as a public defender, but she'll never practice law with passion again. She's the price of Saul Goodman.

Six seasons, sixty-three episodes, a finale that broke hearts and stuck the landing. Better Call Saul is patient where Breaking Bad was urgent, sad where its predecessor was explosive. But the craft is equal—the cinematography, the editing, the acting. By the end, Jimmy McGill is gone, replaced by Saul Goodman, replaced by Gene Takavic, a Cinnabon manager in Omaha. "It's all good, man," he used to say. It never was.

Characters & Acting (10): Bob Odenkirk and Rhea Seehorn are extraordinary. Michael McKean's Chuck is heartbreaking. Every performance is perfect.

Resolution (10): The finale is perfect. Jimmy confesses, accepts his sentence, shares a cigarette with Kim. Closure for everyone.

Cinematography (10): Vince Gilligan's visual mastery continues. Every frame is composed artfully. The black-and-white Gene sequences are haunting.
