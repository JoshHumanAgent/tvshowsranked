Industry

2020 - Present • HBO/BBC • Ongoing

Score: 7.58
Drama, Workplace

What It Feels Like

Graduate trainees at a London investment bank. The competition is brutal, the hours are impossible, the drug use is constant, the sex is transactional. Harper is American with secrets; Yasmin is posh and insecure; Robert is partying toward burnout; Eric is the managing director they all want to impress. Finance as warfare, twenty-somethings as cannon fodder, the city as playground and prison. British prestige drama with the filth turned up.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Myha'la Herrold as Harper is the center: brilliant, damaged, impossible to stop watching. Marisa Abela as Yasmin, Harry Lawtey as Robert, Ken Leung as Eric—the ensemble creates a complete social ecosystem. The performances capture the grad-student desperation: the need to succeed, the willingness to sacrifice. No one is sympathetic; everyone is compelling.

World Building (8/10)

The trading floor, the after-work drinks, the late-night deals, the client dinners, the weekend escapes. The show captures finance culture: the jargon, the hierarchy, the money, the emptiness beneath. London as global capital rendered with precision. The world is specific, cutthroat, educational.

Cinematography (6/10)

HBO production values: the offices gleam, the suits fit, the nightlife is rendered in neon. The style is competent; the setting does the work. Trading floors are shot with appropriate chaos; intimate scenes have space to breathe. Functional rather than distinctive.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

The spectacle is money: expensive dinners, designer clothes, exclusive clubs. But the show avoids glorifying the lifestyle—the emptiness is always visible. The "spectacle" is behavior pushed to extremes: the drugs, the sex, the self-destruction. Uncomfortable rather than impressive.

Conceptual Density (7/10)

Meritocracy as myth, the cost of success, class anxiety in British finance. The show explores who survives: Harper's talent, Yasmin's background, Robert's charm—none are sufficient alone. The transactional nature of every relationship, the impossibility of authentic connection. Sophisticated without being academic.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

The competition format: five trainees, limited positions. Each season has a propulsion—will they survive? The show sustains momentum through professional and personal crises. The pacing is sharp; episodes end with stakes raised. Binge-worthy in the HBO tradition.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10)

Ongoing. The show continues to follow the survivors as they advance in their careers. Whether it will reach a conclusion or continue indefinitely is unclear. The trajectory is upward movement without apparent endpoint. Potential unfulfilled or continuing.
