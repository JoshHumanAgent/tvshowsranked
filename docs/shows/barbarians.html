What It Feels Like to Watch

Barbarians is German historical drama about the Battle of the Teutoburg Forest—9 CE, when Germanic tribes annihilated three Roman legions. It's Germany's origin myth: the moment the tribes united, defeated the empire, and preserved their identity. The show follows three characters caught between worlds: Thusnelda (Jeanne Goursaud), a Seer's daughter who sees visions of Roman destruction; Folkwin (David Kross), her childhood friend and warrior; and Arminius (Laurence Rupp), a Germanic prince raised in Rome as a soldier, returned to his people as a Roman officer.

The central tension: Arminius commands Roman auxiliaries, but his loyalty belongs to his people. "I am Germanic," he says. "I am also Roman." The Romans see the tribes as barbarians; the tribes see Romans as occupiers. Thusnelda sees both—raised learning Latin, married to a Roman ally, but dreaming of freedom. When Folkwin is captured and tortured, the choice becomes clear: Roman law or tribal justice. Arminius chooses the tribes, leading Varus's legions into the forest, into the ambush he planned.

The production values are strong: forest battles with practical effects, Roman military precision contrasted with tribal chaos, bodies on spikes. The show is spoken in German and Latin, with subtitles, adding authenticity. The performances are committed, though the script sometimes favors speech-making over character development. Laurence Rupp's Arminius is the center—a man torn between two identities, two loyalties, two names (Arminius to Romans, Hermann to Germans). "You became one of us," a German tells him. "But we never forgot who you were."

Two seasons, twelve episodes, complete (a season three has not been confirmed). Barbarians is national myth as television spectacle—entertaining, occasionally stirring, historically grounded. The Teutoburg Forest remains a symbol: the empire stopped, the tribes united, Germany born in blood. "Freedom or death," the tribes chanted. Both were delivered.
