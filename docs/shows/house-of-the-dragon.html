What It Feels Like to Watch

Game of Thrones' heir—and in some ways, its superior. House of the Dragon tells the story of the Targaryen civil war (the Dance of the Dragons), two centuries before the original series. The dragons are here, but so is political intrigue that rivals the best of Thrones.

Paddy Considine's Viserys I is the wounded king—literally, his body decaying across seasons—trying to hold his family together. Matt Smith's Daemon is the chaos agent, charismatic and terrifying. The women—Emma D'Arcy's Rhaenyra, Olivia Cooke's Alicent—drive the conflict, their childhood friendship curdling into rivalry over succession.

The production matches Game of Thrones at its peak: dragons that feel massive, battles that devastate, political machinations that surprise. The time jumps are bold—replacing actors mid-character, aging the world in ways Thrones never attempted. The Black Council vs. Green Council dynamic provides the house-divided tension.

Two seasons and continuing. Season 2's smaller scope (fewer episodes, tighter focus on characters) drew mixed response, but the production quality and political maneuvering remain exceptional.
