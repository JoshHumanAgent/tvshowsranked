What It Feels Like to Watch

Bloodline is a Florida Keys noir about a family with secrets that destroy them. The Rayburns run a beachside hotel, look respectable, and have buried something terrible in their past. When the eldest son Danny (Ben Mendelsohn) returns for a family celebration, decades of resentment, manipulation, and crime begin to surface.

Kyle Chandler plays John Rayburn, the responsible son, a detective who's spent his life cleaning up his family's messes. Linda Cardellini is Meg, the youngest, a lawyer who thinks she can control the damage. Norbert Leo Butz is Kevin, the volatile middle child who inherited nothing but anger. Sam Shepard and Sissy Spacek are the parents, whose decisions created the catastrophe.

But the show belongs to Ben Mendelsohn as Danny. He's a chaos agent, a black sheep who returns not to reconcile but to expose. He knows what the family did, and he wants his share. But Danny isn't a simple villain—he's a victim who became a monster, and Mendelsohn plays him with such wounded, manipulative complexity that you understand why the family fears him even as you sympathize with his grievances.

The structure is masterful. The first season begins with the aftermath—John standing over Danny's body on the beach—then flashes back to show how we got there. We know from the first frame that this ends badly. The tension isn't in what happens but in watching decent people decide, step by step, that they have no choice but to become criminals.

The Florida Keys setting is perfect—paradise that feels like a trap, all water and heat and isolation. The Rayburns' hotel is beautiful, but it's built on foundation that's rotting, just like the family. By the time you understand what they've hidden, you understand why the show is called Bloodline.

Characters & Acting (10): Ben Mendelsohn's Danny Rayburn is one of television's great performances. The entire ensemble is excellent, but he's the reason to watch.

Narrative Drive (9): Knowing the ending from the start makes every scene tense. The flashback structure is masterful, building dread as choices accumulate.

Resolution (6): Season one is perfect. Seasons two and three continue the story but can't match the first season's tight construction.
