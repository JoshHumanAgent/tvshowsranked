What It Feels Like to Watch

Dexter Morgan works as a blood spatter analyst for the Miami Metro Police Department. He's also a serial killer. Michael C. Hall plays him with a chilling calmness—he narrates his own life in voiceover, explaining that he has no emotions, no conscience, only a "dark passenger" that demands blood. His father, a police officer, recognized Dexter's nature early and taught him to channel it: only kill other killers, never get caught. It's a moral code wrapped around a void.

The first season adapts Jeff Lindsay's novel Darkly Dreaming Dexter, but the show quickly finds its own path. Dexter hunts other murderers—child killers, rapists, criminals who escaped justice—while pretending to be normal. He has a girlfriend, a sister, colleagues who like him. The tension between his performance and his true self drives the show. As seasons progress, the performance becomes more real, or at least more layered. He develops attachments. He cares about people, or thinks he does, or convinces himself he should.

Seasons one through four are excellent television. The fourth season, with John Lithgow as the Trinity Killer, is a masterpiece of tension and dread. Lithgow's Arthur Mitchell is terrifying—a family man, a pillar of the community, a serial killer who's been active for decades. His final confrontation with Dexter changed television forever. Then season five happened, and six, and seven, and eight. The show lost its way, forgotten what made it work, and delivered a finale so reviled it still echoes in TV discourse.

But for a while, Dexter was essential viewing—dark, daring, and anchored by one of television's most unforgettable performances. Michael C. Hall made you root for a monster. The tragedy is that the show didn't know when to stop.
