What It Feels Like to Watch

Dexter is serial killer as protagonist—and as hero. Dexter Morgan (Michael C. Hall) works as a blood spatter analyst for Miami Metro Homicide. He's also a serial killer, but only kills other killers—people who escaped justice. His foster father Harry, a cop, recognized Dexter's "dark passenger" early and channeled it: a code that restricts killing to those who deserve it. "I'm a very neat monster," Dexter narrates. He follows the code. Until he doesn't.

The show's tension is identity: is Dexter human? He claims he has no feelings, no capacity for love. But he forms attachments—to his sister Deb (Jennifer Carpenter), to his girlfriend Rita (Julie Benz), to his colleagues. "Am I a good person doing bad things?" he wonders. "Or a bad person doing good things?" The show never quite answers—it lets Dexter remain ambiguous, sympathetically monstrous, a predator who hunts predators. Michael C. Hall's performance is extraordinary: charming, awkward, dead-eyed when killing, searching when human.

Season four is the peak: John Lithgow as Trinity, a serial killer who's managed family and murder for decades—the model Dexter might become. "I have a family," Trinity says. "I'm normal." He isn't—his family is terrorized, his cycles are rigid, his destruction is comprehensive. The season finale is devastating: Rita dead in a bathtub, Dexter's son in blood, "born in blood" like his father. After that peak, the show declined—seasons became sillier, threats less plausible, Dexter's invincibility absurd. The original finale (2013) was hated: Dexter becomes a lumberjack, abandoning everything. The revival (2021, Dexter: New Blood) tried to fix it—with mixed results.

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes (plus revival). Dexter's premise is high and its execution often brilliant—moral ambiguity, tension of discovery, black comedy of Dexter's double life. The supporting cast is strong: Jennifer Carpenter's Deb is the emotional center, discovering her brother's truth and spiraling. But quality varies drastically: season four is masterpiece; seasons six through eight are barely watchable. "Tonight's the night," Dexter opens each episode. Eventually, it wasn't—but for a while, it was.
