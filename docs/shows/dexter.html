What It Feels Like to Watch

Dexter is about a serial killer who only kills other killers. Dexter Morgan (Michael C. Hall) works as a blood spatter analyst for the Miami Metro Police Department. By night, he hunts murderers whoescaped justice, kills them, and disposes of their bodies in the ocean. He learned this from his adoptive father, Harry (James Remar), a cop who recognized Dexter's violent urges and channeled them toward "productive" ends. The "Code of Harry": only kill those who deserve it; never get caught.

The show's hook is Dexter's narration: he explains his lack of emotion, his "dark passenger," his need to kill. He describes himself as a monster pretending to be human. Michael C. Hall's performance is extraordinary—charming, funny, terrifying, sympathetic. We root for him. He's a monster with a code. The question the show asks: is the code enough? Can a murderer be moral? Dexter thinks so. The show isn't so sure.

The first four seasons are genuinely great television. Season four is the peak: John Lithgow plays the Trinity Killer, a serial killer who's been active for decades, hiding in plain sight as a family man and church deacon. Dexter tries to learn from him—to balance killing and family—and discovers Trinity's family is a prison of abuse. The season finale is devastating: Dexter comes home to find his wife Rita (Julie Benz) murdered, their son in her blood. "Born in blood, like me." The tragedy is complete.

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes, complete—but the ending is infamous. Dexter fakes his death, becomes a lumberjack. The final season betrayed what the show built. The 2021 limited series Dexter: New Blood tried to fix it, but it's still controversial. Dexter is an excellent show undone by its ending. The journey is worth it. The destination isnot.
