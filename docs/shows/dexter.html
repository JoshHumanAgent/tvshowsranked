What It Feels Like to Watch

Dexter is a high concept executed brilliantly for four seasons, then executed badly for four more, then executed worse in a revival nobody asked for. The premise: Dexter Morgan (Michael C. Hall) is a forensic blood spatter analyst for the Miami Metro Police Department who also happens to be a serial killer. He only kills other killers—an ethical code taught to him by his adoptive father Harry. He has no conscience, no emotions, no capacity for love. Or so he thinks.

Michael C. Hall's performance is magnetic. He makes Dexter sympathetic without ever making him good—you understand his hunger, his loneliness, his desperate need to fit into a world he doesn't understand. The voiceover narration lets us into his head, where he's constantly calculating, judging, hungering. The supporting cast gives him worthy foils: Jennifer Carpenter's Debra, his foul-mouthed detective sister who loves him without knowing what he is; David Zayas' Batista, the good-hearted colleague; James Remar's Harry, appearing in flashbacks as the father who made Dexter what he is.

Season four is the peak. John Lithgow plays the Trinity Killer, a serial killer who's evaded detection for thirty years by living an apparently normal life. The season builds to one of television's most shocking finales, a gut-punch that redefines everything. Then the show fell apart. Seasons five through eight meandered through increasingly implausible plots, Dexter's code became a suggestion rather than a rule, and the series finale—Dexter becoming a lumberjack—was a joke without a punchline.

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes, plus a nine-episode revival that tried to fix the ending and made it worse. Dexter is a case study in what happens when a show lives past its natural endpoint. Watch the first four seasons. Stop there. Trinity's ending is the real ending.
