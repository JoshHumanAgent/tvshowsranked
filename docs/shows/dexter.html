What It Feels Like to Watch

Dexter is about a serial killer who only kills other serial killers. Dexter Morgan (Michael C. Hall) works as a blood spatter analyst for the Miami police. He's also a psychopath, adopted as a child by a cop who recognized his homicidal urges and taught him to channel them toward "deserving" targets. Harry's Code: only kill killers, never get caught. "Tonight's the night," Dexter narrates in the pilot. "And it's going to happen again and again. It has to happen."

The show's premise is brilliant: a monster who passes as human, working within the system, eliminating monsters the law can't touch. Michael C. Hall's performance is extraordinary—Dexter is empty, learning to fake emotions, gradually discovering he might feel something real. His sister Debra (Jennifer Carpenter) is the emotional heart—a foul-mouthed detective who loves her brother but doesn't know his secret. The show is cat-and-mouse: Dexter hunting killers while evading detection, maintaining relationships while being incapable of genuine connection.

Season four is legendary: John Lithgow as the Trinity Killer, a family man who's been murdering for decades. The season finale—"The Getaway"—shocked audiences: Dexter kills Trinity, comes home, finds his wife Rita dead in the bathtub, their son crying in blood. It's one of TV's most devastating endings. But the seasons that followed never matched that peak. Season eight's finale—with Dexter becoming a lumberjack—was widely mocked. The 2021 revival, Dexter: New Blood, tried to fix it. (Spoiler: it didn't.)

Eight seasons, ninety-six episodes, plus revival. Dexter is a brilliant premise with an iconic performance that couldn't sustain its early quality. The first four seasons are riveting; the later seasons are a slide into absurdity. The show's legacy: proving that serial killers can be protagonists, that moral ambiguity can work in television, and that ending a show well is harder than starting one.
