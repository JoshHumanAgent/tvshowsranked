What It Feels Like to Watch

Hannibal is the most beautiful show ever made about cannibalism. Bryan Fuller transformed Thomas Harris's serial killer franchise into a baroque art object—every frame composed, every death tableaux designed for maximum aesthetic impact. This is network television that feels like prestige cinema, horror that aspires to opera.

Hugh Dancy plays Will Graham, an FBI profiler whose empathy is so extreme it's psychological illness. He can inhabit the minds of killers, see through their eyes, think their thoughts. It's destroying him. Mads Mikkelsen plays Dr. Hannibal Lecter—therapist, gourmet chef, serial killer. The show's premise: Lecter is Graham's psychiatrist, secretly manipulating his unraveling while the FBI hunts a killer. Spoiler: it's Lecter.

The show's audacity is making Lecter both villain and co-lead. We watch him cook elaborate meals (human meat, beautifully prepared), attend therapy sessions with his own psychiatrist (a magnificent Gillian Anderson), and slowly, terrifyingly, become Will Graham's only stable relationship. The homoerotic subtext becomes text. "I've always regarded you as a friend, Will," Lecter says. He means it. That's the horror.

Three seasons, thirty-nine episodes, cancelled too soon but complete. Season three goes full mythological—a Florence-set saga where Lecter is a monster in plain sight, then the Red Dragon adaptation, then the cliffside finale where Will andHannibal kill together and fall. "See? This is all I ever wanted for you, Will," Hannibal says. "For both of us." "It's beautiful," Will answers, before they fall. The show's thesis: beauty can be monstrous. Hannibal proved it.
