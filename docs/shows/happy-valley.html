What It Feels Like to Watch

Happy Valley is a police drama that doesn't feel like a police drama. Sally Wainwright created a show about a Yorkshire police sergeant named Catherine Cawood, but the crimes she investigates are secondary to the grief she carries. Her daughter committed suicide after being raped. Her grandson is the child of that rape. The man responsible is out of prison. This is the context for everything.

Sarah Lancashire plays Catherine with a ferocity that's hard to describe. She's not a tortured detective—she's a grandmother raising a child who reminds her of the worst thing that ever happened to her family, and she's trying to do right by that child while working a job that exposes her to other people's tragedies. She's tired, she's angry, she's deeply competent, and she never stops.

The title is bitterly ironic. Happy Valley is what the area is called because of a drug problem that makes people numb. There's nothing happy about it. The show is set in Calderdale, West Yorkshire, and the landscape is a character—rolling hills, industrial towns, a beauty that doesn't erase the struggle.

James Norton plays Tommy Lee Royce, the man who destroyed Catherine's daughter and fathered her grandson. He's terrifying not because he's a criminal mastermind—he's not—but because he's chaotic, narcissistic, and genuinely believes he's the victim. The show understands that the scariest villains are petty men with grudges.

Three seasons, seven years apart. The first two deal with the same crime. The third, which arrived in 2023, feels like a different show—quieter, focused on Catherine's possible retirement, her relationship with her grandson, her decision about whether to keep fighting. It's a masterful conclusion to a story about people who can't stop, even when they want to.

Characters & Acting (10): Sarah Lancashire's Catherine Cawood is one of the great television performances. James Norton makes a villain who's both pathetic and terrifying. Every role is cast perfectly.

World Building (9): Calderdale exists. The show captures a specific place—its geography, its economy, its people—with authenticity that never feels like tourism.

Resolution (9): The third season provides a perfect ending. Catherine's choice. The cycle broken. Seven years of story completed in a way that feels inevitable and right.
