What It Feels Like to Watch

Bodyguard is political thriller as nerve-shredder. David Budd (Richard Madden), a PTSD-suffering war veteran, works for London's Metropolitan Police Protection Command. After thwarting a terrorist attack on a train, he's assigned to protect Home Secretary Julia Montague (Keeley Hawes)—a politician whose policies he despises. The assignment becomes complicated: political assassins target Julia, conspiracies multiply, and David's mental health deteriorates. "I'm fine," he keeps saying. He isn't.

The show's opening is legendary: twenty minutes on a train, a suicide bomber, David talking her down while his children are aboard. It's excruciating tension, brilliantly executed—and sets the tone for everything after. Julia's assassination (episode three) shocked audiences: a main character eliminated mid-season, the protagonist's assignment failed. The aftermath exposes conspiracy: police corruption, political maneuvering, Julia's own secrets. David, suspended and under investigation, hunts for truth.

Jed Mercurio (Line of Duty) specializes in institutional corruption and shocks. Bodyguard delivers both: Julia's death, the bombing, the reveal that David's own superior might be complicit. Richard Madden's performance is Emmy-worthy— his PTSD isn't heroically overcome; it's debilitating, affecting judgment, fracturing reality. "I did what I was told," he says. "I did my duty." The institution used him. Now it's hunting him.

One season, six episodes, complete (though a second has been discussed). Bodyguard was BBC's most-watched drama since 2008—44 million views in 30 days. The finale divided critics: some found the resolution satisfying; others found it convoluted. But the journey is exceptional: political thriller, military trauma, conspiracy, and one man's breakdown under pressure. "Trust no one," the tagline says. Including yourself. David Budd learns that too late.
