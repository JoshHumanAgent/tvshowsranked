What It Feels Like to Watch

The Leftovers is about grief—the kind that doesn't resolve, the kind that lives in you forever. Two percent of the world's population disappears instantly. No warning, no explanation, no pattern. The show begins three years later, when the initial shock has faded but the wounds haven't healed.

Kevin Garvey (Justin Theroux) is the police chief of Mapleton, a small town in New York. His family has shattered: his wife Laurie (Amy Brenneman) joined the Guilty Remnant, a silent cult that follows people around with cigarettes; his son Tom joined another cult led by a man named Holy Wayne; his daughter Jill is acting out. Everyone is broken. Everyone is searching for meaning in an event that had none.

What makes The Leftovers special is its willingness to sit in the pain. The show doesn't offer easy answers about what happened or where the disappeared went. It's interested in how people live with impossible loss. Some join cults. Some pretend nothing happened. Some try to prove it was all a hoax. Some just keep moving forward, one day at a time, because they don't know what else to do.

Season one is heavy, almost unrelentingly grim. Season two moves the story to Jarden, Texas—a town where no one disappeared—and becomes something else: a family drama tinged with magical realism. Season three goes to Australia for an ending that is either literal or metaphorical, depending on what you believe. The final season is one of television's great conclusions, an ending that gives its characters peace without giving the audience certainty.
