What It Feels Like to Watch

The Leftovers is about grief. Two percent of the world's population vanishes instantly—no explanation, no bodies, no warning. The show doesn't care why. It cares about the people left behind. Kevin Garvey (Justin Theroux) is the chief of police in a small New York town. His wife joined a cult. His son joined a different cult. His daughter is acting out. Everyone is broken, and no one knows how to heal.

The show's first season is brutal—dark, oppressive, nearly unwatchable. The Guilty Remnant is the show's most haunting invention: a cult whose members smoke cigarettes, dress in white, and take vows of silence to remind everyone that the world ended. "We are the living reminders," they say. The second season moves to a Texas town where no one departed, and the show finds unexpected humor, beauty, and hope. The third season goes to Australia and ends the story with a perfect finale.

Carrie Coon plays Nora Durst, a woman who lost her entire family in the Departure. Her journey—through grief, through a magical realism that may or may not be real, through acceptance—is the show's emotional spine. "I'm here," she says at the end. "I'm here." The finale is controversial: Nora claims she crossed over to a world where the departed went, found her family, realized it wasn't her life, and returned. The show never confirms or denies. It doesn't matter. What matters is that Nora chose to live.

Three seasons, twenty-eight episodes, complete. The Leftovers is the best drama about religious experience since The Wire was about work. It asks: What do we do with unanswerable loss? How do we go on? The answer is: we go on. "Let's be still," the theme song urges. We can't. But we try. That's enough.
