What It Feels Like to Watch

Borgen is Danish political drama at its finest—the story of Birgitte Nyborg, a minor party leader who becomes Denmark's first female Prime Minister through coalition politics, chance, and sheer will. The show's title refers to Christiansborg Palace, the seat of Danish government, and the series tracks Birgitte's navigation of the corridors of power and the personal costs of her ambition.

Sidse Babett Knudsen plays Birgitte as intelligent, principled, and increasingly isolated. She wins the election unexpectedly, builds a coalition, and learns that maintaining power requires compromises she never anticipated. Each season tracks her evolution: from idealistic outsider to calculating insider, from wife and mother to something else. The show never judges her—it shows the impossible demands of modern politics and accepts that survival requires compromise.

The ensemble around Birgitte shows the machinery of government. Her spin doctor Kasper Juul (Pilou Asbæk) is a brilliant, damaged political operative who manipulates media while his personal life collapses. Katrine Fønsmark (Birgitte Hjort Sørensen) is a journalist whose coverage of Birgitte's government shifts from adversarial to sympathetic to something more complex. The civil servants, party operatives, and family members create a complete picture of what it costs to govern.

The show's genius is making coalition politics dramatic. Denmark's parliamentary system requires parties to form alliances, and every vote needs negotiation. The show turns these negotiations into compelling television—ministers arguing over policy, party leaders trading support for concessions, Birgitte balancing competing demands while trying to maintain some version of her principles.

The fourth season, released eight years after the original run, catches up with Birgitte as Foreign Minister tackling Greenland's independence movement. It shows what the original three seasons built toward: a woman who lost her marriage and partly herself to politics, and who finds a different kind of power in acceptance.
