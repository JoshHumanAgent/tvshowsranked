Extraordinary Attorney Woo

2022 - Present • ENA/Netflix • Ongoing

Score: 7.48
Legal Drama, Comedy-Drama

What It Feels Like

Woo Young-woo is a lawyer with autism spectrum disorder and a genius for finding novel legal arguments. She graduated top of her class; she struggles with eye contact and social cues. Each case is a puzzle she solves differently than anyone else. The show is K-drama at its warmest: courtroom scenes mixed with duck-themed animation, a romance that respects difference, and a protagonist whose neurodivergence is her strength. A global phenomenon for a reason.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Park Eun-bin's performance is the show: precise, detailed, respectful, never condescending. The supporting cast—Kang Tae-oh as the love interest, Kang Ki-young as the supportive colleague—creates a firm that believes in her. The clients each episode provide emotional weight. The performances are K-drama earnest at its best.

World Building (6/10)

Seoul law firm, courtroom, home with her single father. The world is functional, serving the cases rather than developing independently. The legal world is rendered somewhat idealistically—this is feel-good television, not The Good Wife.

Cinematography (7/10)

K-drama polish: the law firm looks expensive, the courtrooms look official, the streets of Seoul provide background. The animated sequences (whales swimming when Young-woo has breakthroughs) are charming. The visual style supports the emotional tone without calling attention to itself.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

No spectacle. The "spectacle" is Young-woo's legal reasoning, the breakthrough moments when she connects disparate concepts. The whale animations provide visual poetry. Otherwise this is lawyers in rooms.

Conceptual Density (6/10)

Neurodivergence as strength, acceptance, the value of different perspectives. The show explores autism without defining the protagonist by it—Young-woo is a lawyer who happens to be autistic, not an autistic lawyer. The themes are handled with warmth; the education is gentle.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Episodic cases with serialized character arcs. Each episode presents a legal problem Young-woo solves uniquely; the season builds toward her relationship development and her father's story. The structure is K-doma classic: problem, solution, emotional reward. Highly bingeable.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10)

Ongoing. The first season provides satisfying closure for many arcs; the second season continues. The trajectory suggests more seasons. Currently delivering quality with open ending.
