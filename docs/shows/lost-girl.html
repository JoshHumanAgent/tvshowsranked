Lost Girl

2010 - 2015 • Showcase • 77 Episodes

Score: 7.15
Fantasy, Urban Fantasy

What It Feels Like

Bo is a succubus who grew up human. She discovers she's Fae—one of a hidden race that feeds on humans. She refuses to choose a side in the Fae war between Light and Dark. Anna Silk is Bo: sexual, powerful, trying to do good while feeding on life energy. Michelle Lovretta created urban fantasy with sex-positive bisexual protagonist.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Anna Silk as Bo is charismatic and grounded. Zoie Palmer as Lauren (the human doctor), Ksenia Solo as Kenzi (the human best friend), Kris Holden-Ried as Dyson (the shifter). The ensemble creates a found family. The performances are committed to the fantasy.

World Building (8/10)

The Fae: Light and Dark, countless species with different powers and needs. The Fae society hidden within human cities. The world expands across seasons—mythology, politics, ancient powers. The rules are consistent.

Cinematography (5/10)

Canadian TV production: urban settings, fantasy creatures rendered with budget, the Fae clubs and rituals. The style is functional—the world carries what budget can't show.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

Fae powers: succubus feeding, shifter transformation, elemental abilities. The spectacle is TV-budget fantasy—practical effects where possible, CG where necessary. The sexuality is more notable than the effects.

Conceptual Density (6/10)

Identity, choosing your own path vs. following tradition, consent and sexuality, found family. The show explores Fae politics through Bo's outsider perspective. The themes are present amid the fantasy adventures.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Episodic cases with serialized Fae politics. The Light/Dark conflict, Bo's parentage, ancient powers awakening. The drive is mystery and relationship—who is Bo, who will she choose, what's the truth of the Fae?

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Five seasons with conclusion. Bo's parentage revealed, the ancient threats resolved, relationships concluded. The ending provides closure for the main characters. Complete.
