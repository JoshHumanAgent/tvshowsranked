What It Feels Like to Watch

Godless premiered on Netflix on November 22, 2017, created by Scott Frank (The Queen's Gambit). The seven-episode Western miniseries is set in the 1880s in La Belle, New Mexico—a town where most of the men died in a catastrophic mining accident, leaving a community run almost entirely by women. When ruthless outlaw Frank Griffin (Jeff Daniels) and his gang terrorize the region, the women of La Belle must unite to defend their town, aided by a mysterious stranger Roy Goode (Jack O'Connell) who arrives seeking shelter.

The series is a stunning visual achievement, shot on location in New Mexico with cinematographer Steven Meizler capturing the vast, harsh beauty of the American West. The New Mexico landscapes—canyons, mesas, endless sky—provide breathtaking backdrop to the intimate human drama. Frank's direction and Meizler's photography create a Western that feels both epic in scope and deeply personal, reminiscent of classic American Westerns while offering fresh perspective through its focus on female characters.

Michelle Dockery delivers a commanding performance as Alice Fletcher, a widow running a ranch on the outskirts of La Belle with her son and Native American mother-in-law. Dockery sheds her Downton Abbey elegance for a harder, more weathered character—a woman who has survived loss and built a life through sheer determination. Jack O'Connell as Roy Goode brings wounded stoicism to the role of the outlaw with a conscience, his character's backstory gradually revealed through flashbacks.

Merritt Wever shines as Mary Agnes McNue, the town's defacto leader who wears pants, smokes cigars, and maintains a relationship with the local schoolteacher (Tantoo Cardinal). Wever's performance captures Mary's fierce independence and hidden vulnerability. Jeff Daniels is terrifying as Frank Griffin, a charismatic preacher-turned-outlaw who justifies his violence through twisted religious conviction. Daniels makes Frank dangerously seductive—you understand why his gang follows him, even as you fear his brutality.

The supporting cast creates a rich community: Scoot McNairy as Sheriff Bill McNue, struggling with his inadequacy; Thomas Brodie-Sangster as Whitey Winn, a young deputy finding his courage; Kim Coates as Ed Logan, a rival sheriff; and the women of La Belle—Tess Frazer as Callie Dunne, the schoolteacher; Audrey Moore as Sarah Doyle; and many others who create a believable frontier community.

The series' exploration of gender roles in the Old West feels organic rather than didactic—the women's competence and strength emerges naturally from circumstances. When the men are gone, the women step up, running businesses, enforcing law, building lives. The series suggests that frontier mythology has ignored women's contributions, that the West was tamed not just by gunslingers but by mothers, widows, and survivors.

Cinematographer Steven Meizler, who would later work with Frank on The Queen's Gambit, creates stunning visuals that honor Western tradition while feeling fresh. The wide shots of New Mexico landscapes recall John Ford, while the intimate close-ups capture weathered faces and determined eyes. The color palette shifts between golden-hour warmth and harsh midday sun, creating visual variety that serves the story's emotional beats.

The action sequences are brutal and realistic—gunfights are messy, mistakes are made, the violence has weight. The climactic battle at La Belle is both spectacular and grounded, showing both tactical competence and raw courage. The series doesn't shy away from the ugliness of Western violence while celebrating the heroism of those who stand against it.

Critical reception was positive, with particular praise for the performances, cinematography, and the series' fresh take on Western conventions. It holds 86% on Rotten Tomatoes, with critics noting the strong female characters and visual beauty. While some found the pacing deliberate, most praised Frank's patient storytelling and the ensemble's performances.

Godless stands as a worthy addition to the Western canon—a series that honors the genre's traditions while expanding its perspective, that delivers both visual spectacle and emotional depth, that proves the Western still has stories to tell. Scott Frank would go on to create The Queen's Gambit, but Godless established his ability to create period pieces with contemporary relevance, to combine genre entertainment with genuine artistry.
