What It Feels Like to Watch

Michaela Coel's I May Destroy You is the most honest show ever made about sexual assault. Coel plays Arabella, a writer who goes out for a drink with a friend and wakes up with fragmented memories and mounting evidence that something terrible happened. She reconstructs the night. Someone put something in her drink. Someone raped her.

The show chronicles what comes after—the police interviews, the self-doubt, the rage, the friendships that fracture. But it's not a trauma memoir. It's an exploration of consent, memory, and agency, centered on Arabella but also following her best friends: Terry (Weruche Opia), an aspiring actor navigating her own sexual minefields, and Kwame (Paapa Essiedu), a gay man whose experience with a stranger raises different consent questions.

Coel wrote, directed, and stars, and her authorship matters. The show refuses easy categories—the men are not monsters, the women are not saints, and Arabella is sometimes her own worst enemy. It's funny, because Arabella is funny. It's warm, because her friendships are warm. Then it's devastating, because the violation underneath everything can't be erased.

The finale offers different possible endings—fantasies, projections, the story Arabella might tell to make peace. It's experimental, meta, and emotionally overwhelming. The show is about how we survive things that shouldn't have happened, but did.

This is television as testimony. Coel turned her own experience into art that doesn't flinch. Emmy voters ignored her; history won't.
