What It Feels Like

Arabella is a writer whose drink is spiked. She can't remember what happened. She reconstructs the night, and the reconstruction changes everything. Michaela Coel created, wrote, directed, and stars: sexual assault explored with radical honesty, the aftermath as comedy-drama, trauma without easy resolution.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (10/10)

Michaela Coel as Arabella: the writer reconstructing her night. Weruche Opia as Terry: the best friend. Paapa Essiedu as Kwame: the friend with his own trauma. The performances are raw, specific, fearless.

World Building (8/10)

London's creative scene: the publishing industry, the bars, the friendships, the dating. The world is Arabella's community—supports and stresses, witnesses and participants.

Cinematography (8/10)

BBC/HBO production: London rendered with specificity, the night out reconstructed from fragments. The style serves the investigation—the camera helps Arabella remember.

Visual Spectacle (1/10)

No spectacle. This is trauma: the reconstruction, the aftermath, the friendships. The "spectacle" is emotional—watching someone piece together what happened.

Conceptual Density (10/10)

Consent, memory, trauma, whether we can heal, what we owe to our friends, how assault reshapes everything. The show explores these without simplification. Essential.

Narrative Drive (9/10)

What happened that night? Each episode is a reconstruction, a memory, a consequence. The structure is investigation—Arabella's mind, our understanding. The drive is truth.

Narrative Path & Resolution (10/10)

One season, complete. The ending offers multiple possibilities—Arabella reclaims her narrative. The conclusion is radical and appropriate. Essential viewing.
