What It Feels Like to Watch

Alice in Borderland is Japanese survival horror. Arisu (Kento Yamazaki) is a gamer, unemployed, wasting his life. He and two friends hide in a Tokyo bathroom—when they emerge, the city is empty. No people. No cars. Just silence. Then a screen flickers: a game has begun. Survive, or die. Each game is designated by a playing card—hearts for psychological, clubs for teamwork, diamonds for intelligence, spades for physical. The games are lethal: laser-tag where death is real; tag where the "it" is a machine gun; a game of hide-and-seek where the seekers are wolves.

The show's thesis: games reveal character. Arisu is a natural—he can analyze, strategize, win. But his friends die in the first game, and his guilt drives him. Usagi (Tao Tsuchiya) is a climber, a survivor, his eventual partner. Together they navigate the Borderland—a post-apocalyptic Tokyo where players compete for "visas" that extend their lives. Without visas, players die. The games are run by "citizens"—people who've chosen to stay, to orchestrate death. The mystery: what is the Borderland? How did it happen? The answer, when it comes, is devastating.

Season one is brutal, creative, surprising—each game a different kind of horror, each solution unexpected. The production design is impressive: empty Tokyo, game arenas built in stadiums and subways. The pacing is relentless, the body count high. Season two adapts the manga's ending: the king of hearts, the queen of hearts, the final game. "Life is a game," a character says. "So make your move." The show's philosophy: life has meaning because it ends. The Borderland is purgatory—characters who return to life must choose to live.

Two seasons, sixteen episodes, seemingly complete. Alice in Borderland is Japanese Squid Game: higher budget, better games, darker philosophy. It shares the same premise (death games as social commentary) but executes differently. The manga ending is preserved: ambiguous, philosophical, hopeful. "I will live," Arisu decides. "This is my move."
