What It Feels Like

Eun-gyeol is a CODA—child of deaf adults—who can hear but lives in both worlds. He finds a mysterious music shop, travels back in time to 1995, and meets his parents as teenagers. He must ensure they fall in love or he won't exist. Ryeoun and Choi Hyun-wook star in the K-drama time-travel romance: family, music, and changing the past.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Ryeoun as Eun-gyeol: the CODA son navigating past and present. Choi Hyun-wook as young father Yi-chan: the rebellious teen falling for the wrong girl. Seol In-ah and Shin Eun-soo as the mother in two timelines. The performances capture generational connection.

World Building (8/10)

2023 and 1995: the deaf community, the music that connects generations, 90s Korea rendered with nostalgia. The world is specific to CODA experience—sound as connection between worlds.

Cinematography (7/10)

K-drama production: 1995 rendered with period detail, the two timelines visually distinguished, music as emotional throughline. The style is warm and nostalgic.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

The spectacle is emotional: the time travel, the music, the connections across generations. This is romance and family drama—not action, but feeling. The music is the real spectacle.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Deaf culture and CODA experience, generational understanding, whether we can change the past, the music that connects us across time. The show explores family through time-travel metaphor.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Eun-gyeol must ensure his parents fall in love while hiding his identity. Each episode brings him closer to changed history. The structure is time-travel puzzle—will changing the past improve the future?

Narrative Path & Resolution (9/10)

Complete. The past is changed, the present is affected, the family dynamics shift. The ending is emotional and satisfying. Korean drama with proper conclusion.
