What It Feels Like to Watch

Fargo is a television miracle—an adaptation of the Coen Brothers' 1996 film that somehow matches its tone while expanding its universe. Noah Hawley took "Minnesota nice" and revealed the violence underneath. Each season is a new story, new characters, same snow-drenched landscape where ordinary people make extraordinary choices, and bodies pile up while everyone apologizes.

Season one is the template: Lester Nygaard (Martin Freeman), a milquetoast insurance salesman, encounters hitman Lorne Malvo (Billy Bob Thornton). A chance encounter spirals into murder, and Lester discovers he's capable of terrible things. The season is funny, shocking, and genuinely philosophical—Malvo is a force of nature, a devil tempting decent people into sin. Allison Tolman plays Molly Solverson, the cop who sees the truth nobody else believes. It's a perfect season of television.

Season two is a prequel set in 1979—a crime family versus a butcher family, with a mob war, UFOs, and Ronald Reagan. It's even more ambitious than season one, with a stunning ensemble including Kirsten Dunst, Jesse Plemons, and Ted Danson. Season three features Ewan McGregor playing twins and explores guilt in the digital age. Season four goes to 1950 Kansas City, pitting Black and Italian crime families against each other. Each season is distinct, yet they're all recognizably Fargo—snow, blood, Midwestern politeness, cosmic cruelty.

Five seasons, fifty-one episodes (season five in 2024). The anthology format means you can jump in anywhere—each season is complete. "This is a true story," every episode claims. It isn't. But it feels true—in the way that folk tales feel true, in the way the Coen Brothers feel true. The universe is indifferent, the snow keeps falling, and someone is always apologizing while they commit murder.
