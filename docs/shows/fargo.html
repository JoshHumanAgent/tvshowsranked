What It Feels Like to Watch

Fargo is a television miracle—an anthology series based on a Coen Brothers film that doesn't just honor the original but expands its universe. Each season is a self-contained story set in the upper Midwest, exploring the collision between ordinary people and extraordinary violence, all filtered through the Coens' sensibility: absurdist, moral, and deeply weird.

Season one follows Lester Nygaard (Martin Freeman), a pathetic insurance salesman who, through a series of accidents and bad decisions, becomes entangled with a hitman named Lorne Malvo (Billy Bob Thornton). The season captures the film's tone perfectly—snowbound Minnesota, cheerful people doing terrible things, violence erupting from mundane frustrations. Allison Tolman plays Molly Solverson, a police officer investigating crimes that keep getting stranger. Thornton's Malvo is a mystery—part trickster god, part force of nature, spreading chaos wherever he goes.

Season two goes back in time to 1979, exploring the Sioux Falls incident referenced in season one. It's even more ambitious—a crime epic involving a crime family, a hairdresser, a state trooper, and a UFO. Yes, a UFO. The show commits to its weirdness without ever losing its emotional core. Season three features Ewan McGregor playing twins and Carrie Coon as a police chief investigating a tangled web of murder and fraud. Season four goes back further, to 1950s Kansas City, exploring gang wars between Italian and Black crime families.

Each season is distinct, but they share DNA: ordinary people making bad choices, violence that's both brutal and darkly funny, landscapes that feel like purgatory. Noah Hawley proved that you can adapt a masterpiece without copying it—to understand what makes the original work and extend that logic into new territory.
