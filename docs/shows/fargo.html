What It Feels Like to Watch

Fargo is an anthology series inspired by the Coen Brothers' 1996 film, but it's not a remake or a sequel—it's an expansion of a universe. Each season tells a self-contained crime story set in the upper Midwest, complete with snow, woodchippers, and ordinary people pushed to extraordinary violence. The tone is unmistakable: deadpan black comedy, sudden brutality, and a moral universe where cruelty is punished but goodness isn't always rewarded.

Season one is a masterpiece. Lester Nygaard (Martin Freeman) is a small-town insurance salesman bullied by everyone until a chance encounter with hitman Lorne Malvo (Billy Bob Thornton) gives him permission to become someone else. Malvo is a force of chaos—part demon, part trickster—whose presence corrupts everyone he touches. Allison Tolman plays Molly Solverson, the deputy who sees through the lies when no one else will. The season builds to a confrontation on frozen ice, redemption for some, destruction for others.

Season two jumps to 1979, following Molly's father as a young cop investigating a triple homicide that connects to a crime family, a butcher shop, and a UFO. Yes, a UFO. The show earns its surrealism. Season three brings Carrie Coon as a small-town police chief facing Ewan McGregor playing twin brothers at war over a parking lot inheritance. Season four moves to 1950s Kansas City, where crime families battle for territory. Season five returns to Minnesota for a different kind of crime story.

Noah Hawley's genius is making each season distinct while maintaining the Fargo DNA: snow, woodchipper references, ordinary people making catastrophic choices, and the sense that God—or something—is watching.
