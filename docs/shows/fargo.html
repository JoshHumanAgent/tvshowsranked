What It Feels Like to Watch

Fargo is an anthology series inspired by the Coen Brothers' 1996 film, set in the same universe of Minnesota niceness hiding Midwestern malice. Each season is a self-contained story of ordinary people making catastrophic choices, murderers who don't look like murderers, and law enforcement officers trying to make sense of chaos. The show captures the Coens' voice—dark comedy, existential dread, violence that's both brutal and absurd—while expanding their world into something uniquely its own.

Season one is the closest to the film. Lorne Malvo (Billy Bob Thornton) arrives in Minnesota like a demon testing humanity's weakness, manipulating a put-upon insurance salesman (Martin Freeman) into murder. Allison Tolman's Molly Solverson is the audience surrogate, a smart cop who sees what others miss. Season two goes wider—1979, a crime family at war with a syndicate, a young Lou Solverson (Patrick Wilson) caught in the middle. Season three is about siblings and identity, starring Ewan McGregor in dual roles. Season four goes to Kansas City, 1950, where crime families compete for territory.

The show's visual language is distinctive: split screens, chapter titles, overhead shots of snowy landscapes, a color palette of whites and grays and occasional shocking red. The violence is sudden and extreme, but the tone is philosophical. Characters debate the nature of truth, the randomness of the universe, whether God exists and whether He cares. The Coen Brothers' themes—fate, randomness, the absurdity of human plans—are expanded into theological inquiry.

Five seasons, fifty-one episodes, each self-contained. Season one is near-perfect television; season two matches it; three and four are ambitious but uneven. The anthology format allows risk-taking—if a story doesn't work, the next season starts fresh. But the show's consistency is remarkable: "okay then" as a philosophy of acceptance in a universe that refuses to make sense.
