What It Feels Like to Watch

The Sopranos is about a New Jersey mob boss in therapy. Tony Soprano (James Gandolfini) has panic attacks, a difficult mother, a troubled marriage, a rebellious children, and a crime family that's both his support system and his source of stress. Dr. Melfi (Lorraine Bracco) is his psychiatrist—the scenes in her office are the show's engine, where Tony reveals his psychology while refusing to change. "I came in at the end," he says of the mafia. "The best is over."

The show invented prestige television. Before The Sopranos, TVwas episodic; after, it could be novelistic. David Chase created antiheroes before anyone called them that. Tony is charismatic, funny, violent, and monstrous. He kills his friend Big Pussy, his cousin Tony Blundetto, and his nephew Christopher—he rationalizes every murder. "He was a grown man," he says of Christopher. "He made his choice." Tony never accepts responsibility. He never changes.

The supporting cast is legendary: Carmela (Edie Falco), Tony's wife, who knows where the money comes from; Paulie Walnuts (Tony Sirico), the superstitious soldier; Silvio Dante (Steven Van Zandt), theconsigliere; Uncle Junior (Dominic Chianese), the aging boss; and Phil Leotardo (Frank Vincent), the New York rival whose vendetta destroys everything. The show's violence is brutal, but its psychology is more brutal. These are men who cannot change, trapped byhistory, expectations, and their own limitations.

Six seasons, eighty-six episodes, complete. The finale isinfamous: Tony in a diner, "Don't Stop Believin'" playing, his daughter arriving, a bell rings, cut to black. Did he die? David Chase refuses to say. The point is: this is how it ends. Or doesn't. "You probably don't even hear it when it happens, right?" a character says earlier. Right. The Sopranosis the show that changed television. It's never been surpassed.
