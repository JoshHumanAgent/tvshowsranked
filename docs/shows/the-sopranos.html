What It Feels Like to Watch

The Sopranos didn't just change television—it created the television we have now. Before Tony Soprano (James Gandolfini), TV protagonists were mostly good guys who solved problems. Tony was a villain who caused them—a New Jersey mob boss who solves problems with violence, manipulates everyone around him, and somehow remains sympathetic. We watch because we understand him, even when we shouldn't.

The show's innovation is simple: what if a gangster went to therapy? Tony's sessions with Dr. Melfi (Lorraine Bracco) provide the structure. He talks about his mother, his father, his panic attacks, his marriage. He's a patient like any other, except that his stress comes from organized crime. The therapy scenes are the show's engine, revealing Tony's psychology while advancing plotlines.

Eighty-six episodes over six seasons trace Tony's reign and decline. His crew is a collection of memorable characters: Paulie Walnuts, Silvio Dante, Christopher Moltisanti. His family is equally vivid: wife Carmela (Edie Falco), daughter Meadow, son AJ. The two families—mob and biological—constantly intersect. The show asks: can a man who does terrible things still be a good father? A good husband? A good person?

The answer is complicated, as is everything in David Chase's vision. The series finale, "Made in America," ends with a cut to black that is still debated. Did Tony live or die? The question is the point. Chase refused to give closure in a medium defined by it. The show ends as Tony's life might end—suddenly, unmarked, or maybe not at all. The ambiguity is the message.

This is television's Citizen Kane. Everything after is in its shadow.
