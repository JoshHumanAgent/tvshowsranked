What It Feels Like to Watch

The Sopranos invented prestige television. Before Tony Soprano (James Gandolfini) walked into Dr. Melfi's office, television protagonists were heroes or villains. Tony was both—a New Jersey mob boss who suffered panic attacks, loved his family, loved hisother family, and killed people without remorse when business required it. The show asked: What if the bad guy is the hero? What if we care about someone who does terrible things?

David Chase created the template thatBreaking Bad, Mad Men, and The Wire would refine. The therapy sessions let Tony articulate his inner life—his mother's cruelty, his father's shadow, his fear that he's becoming his father. Melfi (Lorraine Bracco) is our guide into his psychology, and she's troubled by her own attraction to him. The show doesn't judge Tony; it understands him. That's more unsettling.

The supporting cast is legendary: Carmela (Edie Falco), Tony's wife who knows what he does and loves the lifestyle; Christopher (Michael Imperioli), his nephew and protégé whose drug addiction becomes a tragedy; Paulie Walnuts (Tony Sirico), the old-school soldier who's hilarious and terrifying. The show balances comedy and violence in ways that shouldn't work but do. One minute, Paulie is hilarious; the next, he's executing someone. The tonal shifts are whiplash-inducing, and that's the point.

Six seasons, eighty-six episodes, the most controversial finale in television history. The screen goes black. "Don't Stop Believin'" cuts off mid-word. Did Tony die? David Chase refuses to say. The point is the uncertainty—the life Tony leads means death could come at any moment, from any direction. Journey keeps playing, the onion rings arrive, and everything goes black. "I think you're good at it," Melfi once told Tony. He was. And that was the tragedy.
