What It Feels Like to Watch

The Sopranos didn't just invent the antihero—it invented modern television. Before Tony Soprano (James Gandolfini), television dramas had clear heroes and villains. After him, everything changed. Tony is a mob boss who sees a therapist for panic attacks, loves his children, kills without remorse, and can't understand why he's unhappy. He's both monster and human, and the show never lets you forget either.

Dr. Melfi (Lorraine Bracco) is our window into Tony's psyche. Their therapy sessions reveal a man who can articulate his traumas—his mother's cruelty, his father's infidelity, the violence of his profession—but cannot change. The show's thesis is that understanding is not transformation. Tony knows exactly why he is the way he is. He just can't stop being that way.

The ensemble is extraordinary: Edie Falco as Carmela, the wife who knows and doesn't want to know; Michael Imperioli as Christopher, the nephew who wants to be a screenwriter but can't escape the life; Lorraine Bracco as Dr. Melfi, the therapist who's drawn to Tony despite herself. And Nancy Marchand as Livia, Tony's mother, whose psychological cruelty explains everything and excuses nothing.

Six seasons, eighty-six episodes, the most controversial ending in television history. The final scene: a diner, Journey on the jukebox, Tony looking up as the door opens—and then black. Nothing. Did he die? Did he live? David Chase refuses to say, though he's dropped hints that Tony's fate is exactly what it appears to be. The ending is perfect: a mob boss never knows when the bullet's coming. He just stops living the moment it arrives. We all go black with him.
