What It Feels Like to Watch

Cullen Bohannon is a former Confederate soldier hunting the Union soldiers who murdered his wife. His quest for revenge leads him to the Hell on Wheels camp—a moving tent city that follows the construction of the First Transcontinental Railroad. This is America being built, one rail at a time, by damaged people.

The setting is the star. The American West as Industrial Revolution—steam engines, TNT, survey crews, railroad barons, freed slaves, immigrants, prostitutes, and preachers. The scale of the undertaking bleeds into every frame. Anson Mount's Bohannon is the classic Western antihero: laconic, lethal, haunted.

What elevates Hell on Wheels above typical Western pulp is its supporting cast. Colm Meaney's Thomas Durant is a railroad speculator who'd sell his mother for government subsidies. Common's Elam Ferguson is a freed slave trying to carve out dignity in a world designed to deny it. The Swede is one of television's most unsettling villains—Norwegian, possibly supernatural, definitely insane.

World Building (8): The Hell on Wheels camp itself is a character—lawless, transient, essential. The show captures the chaos of American expansion with remarkable authenticity.

Cinematography (8): Alberta, Canada stands in for 1860s Wyoming and it's stunning. Wide shots of prairies, tight shots of soot-covered faces, steam engines cutting through wilderness.

Resolution (7): The show ran its course. Bohannon's journey completes, the railroad reaches its destination. Not every thread resolves perfectly, but it ends rather than stops.

Key Episodes

"Pilot" (S1E1) — Bohannon's introduction. A hanging, a camp, a quest. The Western restored.

"Durant, Nebraska" (S2E2) — Railroad politics and frontier justice. The Swede's machinations begin.

"Done" (S5E14) — The finale. The railroad is complete, Bohannon's journey ends. A proper Western conclusion.
