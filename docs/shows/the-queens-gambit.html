What It Feels Like to Watch

The Queen's Gambit is sports drama as character study—chess as addiction, genius as curse, orphan girl as world champion. Beth Harmon (Anya Taylor-Joy) discovers chess in an orphanage basement, taught by the janitor Mr. Shaibel. She's a prodigy, seeing moves on the ceiling, visualizing entire games before playing them. She's also damaged: her mother's death, the orphanage's tranquilizers, the foster system's indifference. "The strongest person is the one who's most alone," someone tells her. Beth learns the opposite: strength comes from connection she's denied herself.

The structure is tournament arc: Kentucky, Ohio, Las Vegas, Mexico City, Paris, Moscow. Each victory brings higher stakes; each loss brings spiral. Beth's addiction— to chess, to pills, to alcohol—mirrors her mother's decline. "You're not supposed to have those," a nun tells young Beth about the tranquilizers. She takes them anyway. The green pills are comfort, escape, then prison. By mid-series, Beth is winning internationally while losing personally— blackout drunk before matches, hallucinating on the ceiling, alone in hotel rooms with only chess for company.

The chess sequences are directorial triumph: Scott Frank films matches as duels, pieces as weapons, clocks as heartbeats. The period production—1960s America, then international tournaments—is meticulous: costumes, cars, hotels. Anya Taylor-Joy is magnetic: Beth's intensity, her vulnerability, her ferocious intelligence. The supporting cast: Harry (Harry Melling), her first rival turned coach; Benny (Thomas Brodie-Sangster), the American champion; Jolene (Moses Ingram), her orphanage friend, the person who saves her.

One season, six episodes, complete miniseries. The Queen's Gambit was Netflix's surprise phenomenon—62 million households in first month, chess sets selling out worldwide, a miniseries about board games becoming cultural event. The finale is triumphant: Beth sober, surrounded by friends, beating the Russian world champion, then walking through Moscow streets adored by children. "Let's play," she tells an old man in the park. She's not alone anymore. She's the strongest she's ever been.
