What It Feels Like to Watch

Chernobyl is a five-part miniseries about the 1986 nuclear disaster. It opens with the explosion: Reactor 4 at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, in Soviet Ukraine, has blown its roof off. The men in the control room don't believe it. "It's impossible," they say. The radiation can't be that high. The readings must be wrong. The denial is absolute—because admitting the truth means admitting the Soviet system failed. "What is the cost of lies?" Valery Legasov (Jared Harris) asks. "It's that the lies we tell each other, the lies we tell ourselves, become the truth."

Legasov is a nuclear physicist, the man who understands what happened and must explain it to a system that doesn't want to hear. Boris Shcherbina (Stellan Skarsgård) is the party official assigned to manage the crisis—a loyal Soviet man who gradually realizes hisgovernment is lying to him. Ulana Khomyuk (Emily Watson) is a fictional composite of the scientists who helped uncover the truth. Together, they fight a system built on denial to save millions of lives.

The show is horror: firefighters dying of radiation sickness, their skin sloughing off; miners digging under the reactor, naked, knowing they're being poisoned; "bio-robots" (soldiers) shoveling radioactive graphite off the roof, each allowed 90 seconds before they've absorbed a lethal dose. The cost is counted in lives, and the Soviet Union is willing to spend them to hide the truth. "We did everything right," a party man says. They didn't. They lied.

Five episodes, complete. Chernobyl is a procedural about truth—the cost of denying it, the heroism required to speak it. Legasov testifies at the trial, tells the world the design was flawed, and sacrifices his career. Later, he kills himself. "Every lie we tell incurs a debt to the truth," he says. "Sooner or later, that debt is paid." The Soviet Union collapsed. Chernobyl helped. The show is a warning—about lies, about systems, about what happens when ideology replaces truth.
