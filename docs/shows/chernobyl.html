What It Feels Like to Watch

Chernobyl is about the explosion of a nuclear reactor—and the explosion of lies that followed. On April 26, 1986, Reactor 4 at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in Soviet Ukraine exploded. The show tells the storythrough three perspectives: Valery Legasov (Jared Harris), the physicist who understood what happened; Boris Shcherbina (Stellan Skarsgård), the bureaucrat assigned to manage the crisis; and Ulana Khomyuk (Emily Watson), a composite character representing the scientists who uncovered the truth. "What is the cost of lies?" Legasov asks. "It's that we're doomed."

The show's brilliance is making physics terrifying. We can't see radiation; Chernobyl makes us feel it. The firemen who touched graphite and died. The workers who looked into the core and saw the same reactor that would kill them. The "liquidators" who cleaned the roof, ninety seconds each, before their radiation exposure became fatal. The miners who dug under the reactor to prevent a second explosion. The show honors sacrifice—ordinary people doing extraordinary things because someone had to.

But Chernobyl is also about the Soviet system: the lies, the denial, the face-saving. "The official position of the state is that global nuclear catastrophe is not possible in the Soviet Union," a bureaucrat says. The reactor had a flaw known for years, covered up. Operators were never told. People died because transparency was incompatible with ideology. Legasov testifies truthfully at the trial; the state erases him. The final episode is his suicide. "I cost us the truth," he says. "And truth will cost us everything."

Five episodes, complete. Chernobyl is a masterpiece—perfectly paced, devastatingly acted, historically accurate enough to honor the victims while dramatic enough to grip. It won every Emmy it was nominated for. It's about a disaster thirty years past and a warning eternal: when systems value ideology over reality, people die. "We did everything right," Shcherbina says. They did. The system itself was wrong.
