What It Feels Like to Watch

Twin Peaks changed everything. Before it, television was a medium of formulas: cop shows, medical dramas, sitcoms. David Lynch and Mark Frost took those formulas and torched them. The premise is simple—who killed Laura Palmer, homecoming queen of a small Washington town?—but the execution is unlike anything before or since. The show is a soap opera, a detective story, a horror movie, and a surrealist dream. Sometimes in the same scene.

Agent Dale Cooper (Kyle MacLachlan) arrives in Twin Peaks to investigate Laura's murder and finds a town where everyone has secrets, the supernatural bleeds into everyday life, and a place called the Black Lodge exists in another dimension. Cooper is the moral center—optimistic, eccentric, in love with coffee and cherry pie—but the town itself is the star. Every eccentric character, from the Log Lady to the dancing dwarf, feels like they wandered in from a different story. That's the point: Lynch creates a world where dream logic is the only logic.

Season one is near-perfect. Season two collapses after the killer is revealed, then recovers with one of television's greatest finales. ABC cancelled the show, leaving Cooper trapped in the Black Lodge. Twenty-five years later, Lynch returned with The Return—eighteen hours of pure vision, unfiltered by network notes. It's difficult, disturbing, often impenetrable. It's also television as high art, the closest any series has come to being the singular vision of a single artist.

The influence is incalculable. The Sopranos, Lost, Breaking Bad, Hannibal—television's prestige era owes its existence to Twin Peaks. It proved that TV could be weird, that audiences would follow ambiguity, that the medium could dream. "Fire walk with me." We did. We still are.
