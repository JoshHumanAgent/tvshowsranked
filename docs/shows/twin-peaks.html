What It Feels Like to Watch

Twin Peaks changed television. When Laura Palmer's body washed up on shore wrapped in plastic in 1990, network executives didn't know what they had. David Lynch and Mark Frost created a murder mystery wrapped in soap opera satire wrapped in supernatural horror. Its influence is everywhere—The X-Files, Lost, The Killing, every show that prioritizes atmosphere over answers.

FBI Agent Dale Cooper (Kyle MacLachlan) arrives in Twin Peaks to investigate Laura's murder. He's cheerful, eccentric, in love with coffee and cherry pie, and receives clues from dreams where a giant and a dancing backwards-talking dwarf speak in riddles. He's the moral center of a town where everyone has secrets: the high school quarterback who was seeing Laura secretly, the married businessman who was her john, the psychiatrist who was her counselor, the mother who knows more than she says.

The show's tone is impossible to describe because it contains everything—comedy so broad it's surreal (Lucy the receptionist, Andy the crying deputy), horror so visceral it scars (the reveal of who killed Laura), and moments of transcendence that feel like nothing else on television (Cooper's dream, the Black Lodge, the atomic bomb episode in season three).

Season two collapsed under network pressure to solve the mystery. Once Laura's killer was revealed, the show flailed. Lynch returned for The Return in 2017—25 years later, exactly as foretold in the original series—and created television's most audacious third season. It was horror, experiment, comedy, and tragedy in 18 hours that some critics called the best film of Lynch's career.

This is art-house cinema disguised as a TV show. It demands patience, rewards attention, and leaves images that haunt for decades.
