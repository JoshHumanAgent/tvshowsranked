What It Feels Like to Watch

Altered Carbon is cyberpunk noir. In the future, human consciousness can be digitized—stored in "stacks" and downloaded into new bodies ("sleeves"). Death is optional, if you can afford a new sleeve. The rich live forever, swapping bodies like clothes. The poor sell theirs. "Everyone is born in a body," a character says. "Not everyone gets to keep it."

Takeshi Kovacs (Joel Kinnaman in season 1, Anthony Mackie in season 2) is a former soldier, a "Envoy" trained to survive in any body. He's been "on ice"—stored in prison—for 250 years. A wealthy immortal, Laurens Bancroft (James Purefoy), wakes him to solve his own murder. Bancroft was killed, but his backup was restored—the death was temporary. Who killed him, and why? Kovacs investigates, navigating Bay City's underworld, the corrupt police, and the immortal elite who treat humans as disposable.

Season one is a murder mystery wrapped in philosophy. The premise—which bodies are owned, which memories are real, whether identity persists—demands exploration. The show delivers: AI hotels with personality, religious cults who believe sleeves are sacred, revolutionaries fighting immortal tyranny. The action is brutal, the production design stunning (Hong Kong meets Blade Runner), and the central question resonates: if you can live forever, what does it mean to be human?

Season two is weaker—Anthony Mackie replaces Kinnaman, the story feels smaller, the philosophy fades. But season one is exceptional cyberpunk television: cyberpunk as social critique, noir as philosophy. "You don't have to die," Kovacs tells someone. "You just have to stop living." The distinction, in this world, is crucial. Eighteen episodes, two seasons, cancelled. Season one is worth your time.
