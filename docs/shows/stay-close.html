What It Feels Like

Megan is a suburban mom with a past. Ray is a photographer who can't escape his. Broome is a detective obsessed with a missing persons case. Their stories intertwine when the past returns. Harlan Coben adaptation: secrets, lies, and the danger of buried truths.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Cush Jumbo as Megan, James Nesbitt as Ray, Richard Armitage as Broome. The ensemble creates the web of secrets. The performances are thriller-competent—everyone has something to hide.

World Building (7/10)

Suburban Atlantic City: the comfortable life, the strip clubs of the past, the missing persons who connect them. The world is noir hidden in suburbia—two Americas, one visible, one buried.

Cinematography (6/10)

Netflix Coben production: suburbia and strip clubs, past and present, the contrast between who they were and who they are now. The style is thriller standard.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

The spectacle is thriller-standard: chases, reveals, confrontations. The "spectacle" is the plot twists—Coben's trademark surprises. Thriller mechanics.

Conceptual Density (6/10)

Whether we can escape our pasts, suburban perfection as mask, the cost of reinvention, obsession vs. justice. The themes are present but secondary to the plot. Coben thriller depth.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

The past returns: Megan's secrets surface, Ray's photographs resurface, Broome's cold case heats up. The structure is thriller momentum—each episode reveals more connections.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Complete miniseries. The connections are revealed, the past is confronted, the endings are provided. Coben adaptations are designed for resolution. Satisfying.
