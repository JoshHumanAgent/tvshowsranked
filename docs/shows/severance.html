What It Feels Like to Watch

Severance is a workplace thriller that becomes increasingly unsettling with every episode. The premise is simple and horrifying: Lumon Industries has developed a procedure that surgically divides your memory between work and personal life. When you're at work, you have no memory of your outside life. When you leave, you have no memory of your workday. Your "innie" and "outie" are different people who share a body.

Mark (Adam Scott) is a severed employee working in Lumon's Macrodata Refinement department. His team sorts numbers into bins—meaningless work for an unknown purpose. His wife died in a car accident, and the severance lets him escape his grief for eight hours a day. But when a colleague leaves unexpectedly and a new hire arrives, cracks appear in Lumon's carefully controlled environment. Questions accumulate. What is the company actually doing? What happened to Mark's predecessor? Why does the building feel like a labyrinth?

The production design is Kubrickian: endless white corridors, fluorescent lights, offices that feel like hospitals. The tone shifts from corporate satire to existential horror to conspiracy thriller without ever losing its composure. Ben Stiller's direction is precise, patient, and deeply creepy. Every frame suggests something wrong beneath the surface.

Season one ends on a cliff-hanger so audacious it became an instant internet sensation. Season two continues the story, expanding the world while maintaining the mystery. The show is about work, memory, identity, and the corporations that own both. But it's also about grief—the lengths we'll go to escape pain, and what we lose when we succeed.
