What It Feels Like to Watch

Work-life balance taken to nightmare extreme. Dan Erickson's Severance imagines a procedure that separates your work memories from your personal life—"innies" at work, "outies" at home, never the twain shall meet. Adam Scott's Mark leads a team at Lumon Industries, discovering that what they do—and what the company hides—is profoundly wrong.

The aesthetic is corporate uncanny: fluorescent-lit hallways that stretch forever, offices that feel like liminal spaces, a 1970s-80s design sensibility that suggests time has stopped. The tone shifts between workplace comedy and existential horror.

The mystery structure is classic: what does Lumon do? What happened to Mark's wife? What is the board room? Season 1 builds to one of television's great cliffhangers—the innies briefly awake in the outside world.

Two seasons and ongoing. Ben Stiller's direction creates sustained uncanny tension. The show's thesis is implicit: we compartmentalize at what cost?
