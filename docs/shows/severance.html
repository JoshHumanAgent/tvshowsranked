What It Feels Like to Watch

Severance begins with a woman waking up on a conference room table, with no memory of who she is or how she got there. She's undergone a surgical procedure called "severance"—her memories have been surgically divided. When she's at work, she has no memory of her outside life. When she leaves, she has no memory of work. Two people in one body, neither knowing the other exists.

This is Lumon Industries, a mysterious company where employees process "macrodata refinement"—they stare at numbers on screens and feel something, though they don't know what. The severed employees live in a windowless maze of sterile corridors, pastel colors, and cultish corporate mythology. They're happy—they think. The work is mysterious and important—they're told. And the outside world? They don't know anything about it.

Mark Scout (Adam Scott) leads the Macrodata Refinement division. His colleagues—Dylan (Zach Cherry), Irving (John Turturro), and Helly (Britt Lower)—are all severed, all prisoners in paradise. Helly is new, and she wants out. She's tried everything—suicide notes, escape attempts, violence—and Lumon has an answer for everything. The horror is slow, bureaucratic, corporate. This is oppression by HR committee.

The show becomes a metaphysical thriller about identity, free will, and corporate dehumanization. Season one is nearly perfect television—atmospheric, strange, deeply unsettling. Season two expands the world while keeping the mystery. We still don't know what Lumon does, why the severance procedure exists, what the "numbers" mean. The show trusts us to sit in uncertainty. That's the point: we're all severed from something.

World Building (10): Lumon Industries is a complete, lived-in nightmare. The sterile corridors, the cultish mythology, the mystery of what they actually do.

Conceptual Density (10): Identity, free will, corporate dehumanization. What makes us who we are? Can we consent to being someone else?

Characters & Acting (9): Adam Scott is perfect as the broken everyman. John Turturro and Christopher Walken bring unexpected depth to their supporting roles.
