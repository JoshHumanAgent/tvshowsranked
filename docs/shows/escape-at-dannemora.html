What It Feels Like to Watch

Escape at Dannemora arrived as Ben Stiller's unexpected directorial turn—a limited series about the 2015 Clinton Correctional Facility escape in upstate New York, where two convicted murderers broke out with the assistance of a prison employee. Based on true events, the series combined prison-break thriller with character study, the mechanics of escape with the psychology of why people enable it.

The experience of watching Escape at Dannemora is one of uncomfortable intimacy. The series spends time with unsympathetic people: Richard Matt (Benicio Del Toro) and David Sweat (Paul Dano) are convicted murderers, not misunderstood antiheroes; Joyce "Tilly" Mitchell (Patricia Arquette) is a bored, manipulative prison worker who engaged in sexual relationships with both men. The series doesn't romanticize them; it explains them. The result is fascinating without being likable.

Patricia Arquette's Tilly is the series' revelation—a woman whose life has been a series of disappointments, whose work in the prison gives her access to men who pay attention to her, whose boredom and loneliness lead her into situations she doesn't fully control. Arquette played Tilly without vanity, without asking for sympathy, without making her a villain or a victim. She was both, somehow, simultaneously—the agent of her choices and the product of circumstances.

Benicio Del Toro's Richard Matt was charming and dangerous, manipulative and genuinely talented (he was a painter), a man whose appeal others couldn't see but the characters felt. Paul Dano's David Sweat was the planner, the technical mind, the one whose careful preparation made the escape possible. Both actors found humanity without forgiving crimes; both made understandable how Tilly might have been drawn in.
