What It Feels Like to Watch

WandaVision was Marvel's first TV series for Disney+, and it arrived as a complete surprise—a sitcom parody that revealed itself as a meditation on grief, trauma, and what we'll sacrifice to avoid pain. Wanda Maximoff (Elizabeth Olsen) and Vision (Paul Bettany) are living in a classic American sitcom, complete with laugh track, but something is wrong. The decades keep changing, the neighbors are hiding secrets, and outside the town, SWORD is trying to understand what's happening.

Each episode mimics a different era of sitcom television—the 50s, 60s, 70s, 80s, through to modern mockumentary style. The production design captures each period with perfect precision. But beneath the wholesome surface, cracks appear. Vision sees colleagues frozen mid-motion. Wanda can't remember details of her life before Westview. Radio signals break through asking who's doing this to her.

The show's central question is how we process grief. Wanda lost Vision in Avengers: Infinity War—watched him die twice, in fact. Her powers have created a reality where he's alive, where they're married, where everything is fine. The town of Westview is her prison and her paradise, populated by real people she's enslaved to play her neighbors.

Agatha Harkness (Kathryn Hahn) arrives as the nosy neighbor who turns out to be a witch, delighted to find a more powerful witch to study. Monica Rambeau (Teyonah Parris) is the SWORD agent who passes through the barrier and gains powers in the process. Both threads expand the Marvel universe while staying grounded in Wanda's emotional journey.

The finale split viewers—some wanted more Agatha, more action, more traditional Marvel climaxes. But the show's real achievement is making a superhero story about mental health, about the denial that protects us and the acceptance that frees us.
