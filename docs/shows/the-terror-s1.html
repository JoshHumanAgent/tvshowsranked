What It Feels Like to Watch

The Terror adapts Dan Simmons' novel about the Franklin expedition—two ships trapped in Arctic ice, starving men, and something else stalking them through the white. It begins as historical drama, becomes survival horror, and ends as something stranger. Jared Harris plays Captain Crozier, watching his crew die while trying to maintain order. Ciaran Hinds is Sir John Franklin, optimist until the end.

The real monster is the Arctic winter, but the show adds something supernatural—a creature of indigenous myth that hunts the men. The tension between natural horror (scurvy, cold, starvation) and the creature works. You're never sure if it's real or hallucination until it tears someone apart.

What elevates The Terror is its treatment of the Inuit. Rather than the usual noble savage stereotype, the Netsilik are fully realized characters. The show's shift to their perspective in the final episodes is remarkable—a corrective to the original expedition's imperial blindness.

Cinematography (8.5): The Arctic is shot to emphasize isolation. The endless white, the trapped ships, the body horror of scurvy—all rendered with horrifying beauty.

Characters & Acting (8): Jared Harris is magnificent. His Crozier is a man watching everything he loves die. Paul Ready's Dr. Goodsir provides the moral center.

Resolution (7.5): The finale shifts perspective to the Inuit. It's bold and necessary—a corrective to the original British imperial narrative.
