Marvel's Agent Carter

Score: 7.15

What It Feels Like

1946. Peggy Carter works for the SSR—the precursor to SHIELD—ignored by her male colleagues. She takes on secret missions on her own. Hayley Atwell reprises her Captain America role: feminist spy thriller, period piece, Marvel universe. The show that provedMarvel could do television.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Hayley Atwell as Peggy Carter: capable, frustrated, brilliant. James D'Arcy as Edwin Jarvis provides the support. The supporting cast creates post-war America. Atwell carries with authority.

World Building (8/10)

Post-war America: the SSR office, the Howard Stark technology, the emerging Hydra threat. The world connects to Captain America while standing alone. The 1940s are rendered with style.

Cinematography (7/10)

ABC Marvel production: 1940s rendered with style, the spy action staged with period flair. The aesthetic is retro-Modern—the look of post-war with comic book energy.

Visual Spectacle (6/10)

Spies, gadgets, fights, villainy. The spectacle is TV-budget Marvel—enough to sell the universe, constrained compared to films. The period style compensates.

Conceptual Density (7/10)

Sexism in post-war America, women's work erased while men take credit, competence vs. perception. The show explores 1940s gender dynamics without preaching. Feminist superhero.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Each season has a mission: clearing Howard Stark's name, stopping Zero Matter. The structure is spy thriller—mission, investigation, resolution. The drive is Peggy proving herself.

Narrative Path & Resolution (5/10)

Two seasons, cancelled. Peggy's story continues in films and other shows, but Agent Carter ended before its time. The cancellation was controversial. Incomplete.
