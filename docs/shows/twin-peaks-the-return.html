What It Feels Like to Watch

Twin Peaks: The Return came back twenty-five years after the original series ended, and it wasn't a reunion special. It was an eighteen-hour art film about trauma, evil, the nuclear age, and the impossibility of going home. David Lynch and Mark Frost created something that bears almost no resemblance to the quirky ABC soap opera of 1990—this is darker, stranger, and more ambitious than anything on television.

Agent Dale Cooper (Kyle MacLachlan) has been trapped in the Black Lodge for twenty-five years, while his evil doppelgänger has been walking the earth. The series follows multiple threads: Cooper's escape attempt, his doppelgänger's criminal empire, the original characters still in Twin Peaks, and new characters who may or may not be connected to the supernatural forces that have always haunted this town.

Part 8 is the show's most famous hour—a nuclear test in 1945, the birth of evil, a creature crawling across a desert. It's experimental filmmaking on television, a largely wordless sequence that recontextualizes everything the show is doing. Lynch isn't telling a story; he's conjuring images, sounds, feelings. The show has episodes that are pure horror, episodes that are absurdist comedy, and episodes that defy categorization.

The return to Twin Peaks is not comforting. The familiar characters have aged, suffered, and died. Big Ed is still waiting for Norma. Sheriff Truman is sick. The Log Lady dies. Audrey Horne is trapped in an unhappy marriage—maybe. Laura Palmer's face appears and disappears. The town that was quirky and charming now feels haunted, contaminated by the evil that has been here all along.

The ending is controversial, brilliant, and devastating. Cooper goes back in time to save Laura Palmer, and the show fractures. The final scene—Cooper outside Laura's house, asking "What year is this?"—leaves you with profound unease. This isn't a resolution. It's an accusation. The past cannot be changed. The horror continues.
