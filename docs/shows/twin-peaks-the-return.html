What It Feels Like to Watch

Twin Peaks: The Return premiered on Showtime on May 21, 2017, created by David Lynch and Mark Frost as a continuation of the original Twin Peaks (1990-1991). The 18-episode limited series returns to the strange Pacific Northwest town 25 years after the original series, following FBI Agent Dale Cooper (Kyle MacLachlan) as he attempts to escape the Black Lodge and return to the real world, while a doppelganger Cooper wreaks havoc and a new murder mystery unfolds in Twin Peaks.

The Return is not conventional television—it is an 18-hour film by David Lynch, a surrealist masterpiece that defies narrative expectations and challenges viewers to surrender to its dream logic. Lynch, given complete creative control by Showtime, creates a work of uncompromising artistic vision that is by turns terrifying, hilarious, heartbreaking, and incomprehensible. This is television as avant-garde art, a work that expands the medium's possibilities.

Kyle MacLachlan's performance is extraordinary in its range—he plays not only the original Dale Cooper (trapped in the Black Lodge), but also the doppelganger Cooper (a violent criminal), Dougie Jones (a simple man whose body Cooper possesses), and various other iterations across dimensions. MacLachlan's ability to distinguish these characters while maintaining their connections is remarkable. The Dougie Jones storyline—Cooper trapped in a simpleton's body, slowly awakening—spans much of the series and is played by MacLachlan with minimal dialogue and maximum physical comedy.

The supporting cast includes returning actors from the original series: Sherilyn Fenn as Audrey Horne, now a troubled woman in a loveless marriage; Mädchen Amick as Shelly Johnson, still working at the diner; Dana Ashbrook as Bobby Briggs, now a deputy; Kimmy Robertson as Lucy Brennan, still the receptionist; and Harry Goaz as Deputy Andy Brennan. These returning actors provide continuity while showing the passage of time.

New cast members expand the Twin Peaks universe: Laura Dern as Diane Evans, Cooper's secretary who appears in person for the first time; Naomi Watts as Janey-E Jones, Dougie's wife; Tom Sizemore as Anthony Sinclair; Jim Belushi as Bradley Mitchum; Robert Knepper as Rodney Mitchum; Balthazar Getty as Detective Fusco; Tim Roth and Jennifer Jason Leigh as assassins; and many others in the sprawling cast.

The series' structure defies conventional television. Episodes vary wildly in length (from roughly 50 minutes to over an hour), in narrative focus, and in tone. Episode 8 is a largely wordless experimental film depicting the birth of evil. Episodes spend significant time on seemingly tangential characters and storylines that may or may not connect to the main narrative. The series demands patience and surrender to Lynch's vision.

The cinematography by Peter Deming creates a distinctive visual language that shifts between the mundane and the surreal. The Twin Peaks locations—the diner, the sheriff's station, the Great Northern Hotel—are shot with nostalgic warmth, while the Black Lodge sequences are abstract and terrifying. The series uses light and shadow, color and desaturation, to create emotional atmosphere.

The sound design by Dean Hurley and the music by Angelo Badalamenti (returning from the original series) create an audio landscape of unease. The familiar Twin Peaks themes are present, but expanded with new compositions that range from jazz to ambient to experimental. The sound design uses silence, ambient noise, and sudden loud sounds to create tension and unease.

The series' engagement with the original Twin Peaks is complex—The Return is not simply a revival but a deconstruction and reconstruction of the original. It answers some questions (the fate of Cooper, the identity of Judy) while raising new ones. It brings closure to some characters while leaving others in mystery. It is both a continuation and a standalone work, accessible to newcomers while rewarding to fans.

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive, with many critics calling The Return a masterpiece and one of the greatest achievements in television history. It holds 96% on Rotten Tomatoes and received numerous awards including the Golden Globe for Best Limited Series. Critics praised Lynch's uncompromising vision, the performances (particularly MacLachlan), and the series' expansion of television's artistic possibilities.

The series stands as a landmark in television history—an 18-hour film by one of cinema's greatest artists, a work that expands the medium's possibilities and challenges viewers to engage with difficult, beautiful, mysterious art. Twin Peaks: The Return is not for everyone—it demands patience, surrender to dream logic, and tolerance for ambiguity—but for those willing to engage with it, it offers rewards unmatched in television. It is a masterpiece.
