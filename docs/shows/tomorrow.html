Tomorrow

2022 • MBC • 16 Episodes

Score: 7.05
Fantasy, Drama, Thriller

What It Feels Like

The afterlife is a bureaucracy. The Grim Reapers who escort the dead are civil servants with quotas and paperwork. Choi Jun-woong is a young job-seeker who accidentally gets recruited into the elite "Jumadeung" crisis management team—a squad that saves people from suicide. Tomorrow is Korean fantasy with a social conscience: each episode confronts a different kind of despair—student overwork, elderly poverty, bullying, parental pressure, transgender identity. The reapers are people who died before their time, stuck in eternal civil service until they earn redemption. Kim Hee-sun plays the team leader as exhausted, angry, and relentless. Rowoon plays Jun-woong's idealism meeting the reality of suicidal despair. It's bleak subject matter packaged in slick K-drama production—fantasy bureaucracy meets suicide prevention PSA. The message is sincere; the execution is sometimes didactic.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Kim Hee-sun's Goo-ryeon is the show's heart: bitter, capable, hiding trauma behind efficiency. Rowoon's Jun-woong is comic relief turned advocate—naive humanity in afterlife bureaucracy. Yoon Ji-on's administrator provides corporate absurdity. The guest casts in each case-of-week bring different despair experiences.

World Building (7/10)

Afterlife as Korean corporate structure: Jumadeung division, paperwork in triplicate, Grim Reapers as middle managers. The living world is modern Korea—pressures documented as social issues. The bureaucracy is absurdist but grounded in relatable pain.

Cinematography (6/10)

Standard K-drama production: bright, crisp, emotional close-ups. The afterlife is sterile corporate office; the living world is varied despair locations. Visual style reinforces the theme: bureaucracy versus human suffering.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

Minimal spectacle. The reapers can freeze time and enter memories, but effects are used sparingly. The show relies on emotional stakes, not production budget.

Conceptual Density (7/10)

Suicide prevention as afterlife civil service. The show asks: Who is responsible for despair? What structures create hopelessness? The cases-of-the-week are social commentary—overwork in Korea, elderly poverty, trans identity, student pressure. Didactic but sincere.

Narrative Drive (6.5/10)

Episodic structure: each case is a suicide to prevent. Overarching mystery: why is Goo-ryeon trapped? The team dynamics create serial investment. Jun-woong's journey from naive to advocate provides growth. 16 episodes, each case meaningful but formulaic.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Complete season. Goo-ryeon's story resolves, Jun-woong's arc completes, the team continues. Open for season 2 but not required. The emotional payoffs land.
