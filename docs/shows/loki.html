What It Feels Like to Watch

Loki is Marvel's most clever Disney+ series—Tom Hiddleston's trickster given space to breathe, scheme, and unexpectedly grow. The Loki we meet is the 2012 version who escaped with the Tesseract during the time heist in Avengers: Endgame. The Time Variance Authority (TVA) arrests him for crimes against the "Sacred Timeline." His choice: be pruned from existence or help hunt a.variant—another Loki.

The TVA is bureaucracy as metaphysics. An organization outside time, staffed by workers who believe they were created for this purpose, maintaining one "Sacred Timeline" by pruning any branches that create alternate realities. The aesthetic is retro-futuristic—file cabinets, bad computers, endless bureaucracy. It's Brazil meets Marvel via time travel.

Sylvie (Sophia Di Martino) is the variant Loki—a female version from another timeline, raised hiding from the TVA, determined to destroy the organization that erased her life. She and Loki become unlikely allies, then something more, their connection complicated by being different versions of the same person.

The show explores identity—what makes Loki, Loki? Is the God of Mischief destined to be a villain, or can he choose differently? Owen Wilson's Mobius, a TVA analyst, becomes fascinated by Loki's potential. Jonathan Majors appears as He Who Remains, a variant of Kang, setting up the MCU's next big threat.

Season one is stronger than season two, but both feature Hiddleston at his most charming, the character's wit and vulnerability finally given room. The show is Marvel at its most philosophically ambitious—what is free will if the timeline is sacred?
