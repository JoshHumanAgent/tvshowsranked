What It Feels Like to Watch

Pose is a celebration and a memorial. Set in New York's ballroom scene of the late 1980s and early 1990s, it tells the story of Black and Latino LGBTQ+ people who created chosen families—"houses"—and competed in balls where they could be the stars society refused to let them become. It's also the story of the AIDS crisis, which was destroying that community while the government looked away.

The show features the largest cast of transgender actors in television history. Mj Rodriguez plays Blanca, a trans woman who leaves her house to start her own, gathering a family of outcasts. Billy Porter plays Pray Tell, the ballroom emcee whose flamboyance hides his fear and grief. Indya Moore plays Angel, a trans sex worker who dreams of love and modeling. These aren't token characters—they're the center of the story.

Ballroom culture gave the world voguing, drag competitions, houses as family structures, and vocabulary that mainstream culture now uses without attribution ("throwing shade," "reading," "realness"). Pose documents this moment with reverence and joy. The ball scenes are spectacular—costumes, choreography, categories like "Executive Realness" where working-class kids dress like the corporate executives society would never let them become.

But Pose is equally about loss. The AIDS crisis haunts every celebration. Characters get sick, characters die, the community gathers for funerals as often as for balls. The show refuses to look away from what was lost, but it also refuses to define its characters solely by tragedy. They are artists, lovers, rivals, family. They fight for survival and they fight for joy.

Ryan Murphy's involvement ensures high production values and star power, but the show's authenticity comes from its trans creators and performers. This isn't representation as charity—it's representation as revolution. A community telling its own story on its own terms.
