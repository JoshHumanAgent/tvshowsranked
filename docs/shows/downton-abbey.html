What It Feels Like to Watch

Downton Abbey is the most successful British costume drama since Brideshead Revisited—a soap opera dressed in Edwardian splendor, following the Crawley family and their servants from the sinking of the Titanic through the Jazz Age. Robert Crawley, Earl of Grantham (Hugh Bonneville), and his American wife Cora (Elizabeth McGovern) have three daughters but no male heir. The estate and title must pass to a distant cousin. This is the engine that drives six seasons of marriage plots, inheritance crises, scandals, wars, and deaths.

Lady Mary (Michelle Dockery) is the eldest daughter—beautiful, sharp, andInitially cold, she becomes the show's central figure. Lady Edith (Laura Carmichael) is the overlooked middle daughter, forever in Mary's shadow, whose journey from disappointment to independence is the show's quiet triumph. Lady Sybil (Jessica Brown Findlay) is the youngest, who marries the Irish chauffeur and dies in childbirth, the first major death that reminds us this world is not immune to tragedy.

Below stairs, the servants have their own stories—romances, rivalries, ambitions. Mr. Carson (Jim Carter) the butler and Mrs. Hughes (Phyllis Logan) the housekeeper are the moral anchors of the service world. Thomas (Rob James-Collier) the footman is the villain you sometimes root for. Daisy (Sophie McShera) the kitchen maid rises to assistant cook through sheer determination. Anna (Joanne Froggatt) and Mr. Bates (Brendan Coyle) have one of television's most enduring love stories, tested by prison, violence, and endless obstacles.

Maggie Smith as the Dowager Countess delivers every line as a weapon—acerbic, traditionalist, secretly sentimental. "What is a weekend?" she asks, perfectly capturing her character's oblivious aristocracy.

The show's pleasure is costume drama as comfort food—beautiful clothes, beautiful houses, beautiful performances, with enough drama to keep watching and enough reassurance that order will be restored.
