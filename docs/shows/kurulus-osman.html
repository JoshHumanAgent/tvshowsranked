Kurulus: Osman

2019 • Turkish Historical Drama • 194 Episodes

Score: 6.63
Drama, History, Action

What It Feels Like to Watch

Kurulus: Osman is a Turkish historical epic following the founder of the Ottoman Empire, and its scale is staggering—nearly two hundred episodes of conquest, betrayal, and state-building. The show positions itself as both historical record and nationalist myth-making, dramatizing the life of Osman I with a level of production ambition that rivals any television epic in the world. For viewers interested in Islamic history, Turkish culture, or simply the mechanics of empire-building, the show offers a window into a world that Western television rarely explores.

The strength of Kurulus: Osman lies in its world-building—not the fantasy kind but the historical sort. The series reconstructs 13th-century Anatolia with meticulous attention to costume, architecture, military tactics, and political structures. The camps of nomadic Turkic tribes, the fortified Byzantine cities, the complex web of alliances and rivalries—all of it feels lived-in and carefully researched. The show was clearly made with resources and conviction, and for much of its run, it rewards the investment of attention.

But 194 episodes is a lot of television, and Kurulus: Osman doesn't escape the problems of extreme length. Plots are padded and recycled, characters die and are replaced by functionally identical successors, and the endless cycle of battle, council, and betrayal becomes predictable after the first several dozen hours. The show's patriotic fervor, which provides emotional propulsion for Turkish audiences, may feel one-dimensional to international viewers accustomed to more morally complex television. Osman himself is portrayed less as a complicated historical figure than as a mythic hero—a choice that simplifies the drama but limits its depth.

Why It Ranks Here

The ranking reflects a tension between ambition and excess. Kurulus: Osman does remarkable work in reconstructing a historical era largely absent from Western screens, and its production values legitimize the story it wants to tell. But the sheer length dilutes the impact, and the reverential treatment of its subject prevents the moral complexity that marks the best historical dramas. Its score represents respect for what it attempts, tempered by recognition of what it sacrifices in the attempt.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

The lead performance conveys the gravitas of a foundational historical figure—Osman is portrayed with the weight befitting someone whose decisions would echo through centuries. Supporting characters range from compelling to functional; the ensemble is large, and not everyone receives adequate development. The acting style is declamatory in a way that suits the epic tone, though viewers expecting subtlety may find it broad.

World Building (7.5/10)

This is where Kurulus: Osman shines. The series creates a fully realized vision of medieval Anatolia—the political rivalries between Turkish beyliks, the declining Byzantine Empire's desperate hold on its territories, the Mongol threat looming from the east. The material culture is rendered in detail: saddlery, weaponry, clothing, food, tents, fortifications. You leave the show with a sense of how these people lived and fought.

Cinematography (7/10)

The production values are impressive for television. Battle sequences involve hundreds of riders, sieges employ practical effects, and the landscape photography makes Turkey's terrain a character in itself. The camera work is conventional but handsome, favoring wide shots that emphasize scale over intimate close-ups. Some effects show their budget limitations, but the overall visual impression is of a production that takes its historical setting seriously.

Visual Spectacle (6/10)

By television standards, the spectacle is substantial. Massed cavalry charges, castle sieges, large-scale battles—the show stages military action with resources that few series can match. But compared to cinema or to the very highest-end productions, the spectacle has limits. The battles are impressive within television constraints but won't replace your memory of large-screen epics.

Conceptual Depth (7/10)

The show engages with the founding mythology of the Ottoman state, exploring themes of leadership, faith, sacrifice, and the responsibilities of power. Islam is treated with respect and assumed as a framework for understanding the world—refreshing for audiences accustomed to secular or Christian-centered historical dramas. But the treatment rarely questions its subject; Osman is a hero, and the show isn't interested in complicating that picture.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Within episodes, the momentum is strong—battles, councils, betrayals, and speeches keep things moving. Across the full run, the show suffers from the problem common to extremely long series: repetition. After a while, the pattern of conflict and resolution becomes predictable, and the stakes can feel artificial when they've been raised and resolved dozens of times before. The dedicated viewer will find reward; the casual viewer may find exhaustion.

Narrative Path & Resolution (5/10)

This dimension suffers from the show's ongoing nature. Kurulus: Osman is less a story with an ending than a historical chronicle with entries. Individual arcs resolve, but the larger narrative continues indefinitely. By the time you've watched nearly two hundred episodes, the question of "resolution" becomes almost irrelevant—this is a world you inhabit rather than a story you complete.
