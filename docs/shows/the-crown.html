What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is the most expensive television series ever made, and every penny is on screen. Over six seasons, it charts the reign of Queen Elizabeth II from her coronation in 1953 to the early 2000s, following the private lives of the British royal family behind palace walls. The production design is lavish, the costumes are museum-quality, and the cast rotates every two seasons—the queen ages from young woman to grandmother while the actress playing her changes.

Claire Foy plays Elizabeth in seasons one and two, a young woman thrust onto the throne by her father's early death. She's uncertain, dutiful, learning to sacrifice her personal desires for the crown. Matt Smith plays Prince Philip, her husband, who resents walking two steps behind his wife. Vanessa Kirby plays Princess Margaret, Elizabeth's younger sister, whose love life becomes a constitutional crisis. The drama is quiet, internal—a family trying to function while an institution demands they suppress every feeling.

Olivia Colman takes over for seasons three and four, as middle age brings new challenges: a crumbling marriage, difficult children, a prime minister (Margaret Thatcher, played by Gillian Anderson) who clashes with the crown. Princess Diana arrives in season four, played by Emma Corrin, and the show becomes a tragedy of two women who cannot coexist: the queen and the princess who captured the world's heart.

Imelda Staunton plays Elizabeth in the final seasons, facing the disasters of the 1990s—Diana's death, the fire at Windsor Castle, public scrutiny that threatens the monarchy's survival. The show's thesis emerges: the crown demands everything. Elizabeth chose duty over self, and the cost was her family. The final image shows her planning her own funeral—a woman thinking about the institution's survival even in death. The show respects Elizabeth while questioning whether the sacrifice was worth it.
