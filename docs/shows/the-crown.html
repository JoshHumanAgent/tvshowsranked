What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is royal biography as prestige drama—Queen Elizabeth II's reign from 1947 through the early 2000s, six decades of history filtered through one woman's obligation. "The monarchy is too important to be left to personal preference," someone tells her early. She accepts the crown at 25, surrendering self to institution. The show's thesis: duty destroys the individual. Elizabeth (Claire Foy, then Olivia Colman, then Imelda Staunton) becomes less person and more symbol with each season—her humanity slowly consumed by the crown.

The seasons progress chronologically, recasting actors every two seasons. Seasons one and two: Elizabeth's ascension, marriage to Philip (Matt Smith), the Suez Crisis, the Kennedy visit. Seasons three and four: middle reign, Labour government, the Charles-Diana marriage and collapse. Seasons five and six: the Windsor fire, the deaths of Diana and Princess Margaret, the modern monarchy's precarious relevance. The Diana seasons (four through six) are the show's peak: Elizabeth Debicki's Diana is sympathetic, trapped, destroyed by the institution she entered.

The supporting cast is extraordinary: Vanessa Kirby and Helena Bonham Carter as Princess Margaret— the queen's wild sister, denied divorce, denied love, drinking herself into decline. John Lithgow as Churchill, aging and stubborn. Gillian Anderson as Margaret Thatcher, clashing with the queen over sanctions and principle. Dominic West as Charles, damaged by cold parenting, destroying Diana with Camilla in the wings. The production values are unmatched: palaces, costumes, recreations of historical events. Netflix's most expensive drama.

Six seasons, sixty episodes, complete. The Crown is biography as tragedy—individuals crushed by institution, love sacrificed to duty. "I am not a person," Elizabeth realizes. "I am a symbol." The show humanizes the royals while indicting the system. Diana's death is the culmination: the monarchy nearly destroyed by its own indifference, saved only by Elizabeth's belated humanity. "Are you a queen or are you a grandmother?" someone asks. She must be both. The Crown shows the impossibility.
