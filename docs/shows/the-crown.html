What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is a decades-spanning drama about Queen Elizabeth II and the British royal family. It begins with her father's death in 1952 and ends (in its Netflix iteration) with Prince Charles's wedding to Camilla in 2005. Across six seasons and three casts, it charts the transformation of a young woman into a symbol—and the cost of that transformation on everyone around her.

Claire Foy plays Elizabeth in seasons one and two, capturing a woman thrust into duty before she's ready. Matt Smith is Prince Philip, frustrated by his supporting role. Season three brings Olivia Colman as a middle-aged Elizabeth, settled into her position but struggling with changing times and a difficult prime minister (Margaret Thatcher, played by Gillian Anderson). Season four introduces Diana Spencer (Emma Corrin), whose marriage to Charles becomes a national tragedy. Seasons five and six feature Imelda Staunton as the elderly Elizabeth, navigating family scandals and a changing world.

The show's genius is making the personal political. Elizabeth's decisions—about her sister's marriage, her uncle's Nazi sympathies, her children's divorces—reverberate through history. The series dramatizes famous events: the Suez Crisis, the Profumo affair, the miners' strike, the death of Diana. But it also dramatizes private moments: Elizabeth talking to her horses, Philip's midlife crisis, Margaret's loneliness, Charles's impossible position.

The production values are unmatched: palaces, castles, costumes, ceremonies. Every frame looks expensive. The show doesn't judge the monarchy—it observes it. The question it raises is whether an institution built on duty can survive in a world that values self-actualization. The answer, so far, is yes—but at tremendous personal cost.
