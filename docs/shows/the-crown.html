What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is the most expensive television show ever made, and every dollar is on screen. Peter Morgan's saga of Queen Elizabeth II's reign spans six decades, three casts, and the entire transformation of Britain from empire to island. Claire Foy plays young Elizabeth, inheriting a throne she never wanted; Olivia Colman plays middle Elizabeth, watching her family self-destruct; Imelda Staunton plays late Elizabeth, a grandmother become an institution. Each cast brings something different—and the through-line is a woman who sacrificed everything for duty.

The show's strength is its ambiguity. Is the monarchy noble or absurd? Should Elizabeth have been warmer, more present, more human? Or is her coldness the price of stability? The show doesn't judge—it dramatizes. Margaret (Vanessa Kirby, Helena Bonham Carter) is the tragic sister who wanted love and was denied it. Charles (Josh O'Connor, Dominic West) is the sensitive son crushed by the weight of waiting. Diana (Emma Corrin, Elizabeth Debicki) is the victim who became more popular than the institution. And Elizabeth watches, steady, doing her duty, while everyon e around her suffers.

The production values are cinema-quality—palaces, state visits, private jets, royal weddings. But the show's best moments are intimate: Elizabeth and Philip in their bedroom, Elizabeth and Margaret arguing about love, Elizabeth and Charles misunderstanding each other completely. The dynasty is a business, and family is collateral damage. "The Crown must win," says LordPorchester. "Always."

Six seasons, sixty episodes, a conclusion that coincided with the real Elizabeth's death. The final episode imagines Elizabeth planning her own funeral, a fitting end to a show about a woman whose entire life was preparation for death. "I have no distance from my life," she admits to a vision of her younger self. The Crown gave her distance—and gave us the most lavish, intelligent portrait of power ever filmed.
