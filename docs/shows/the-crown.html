What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is about what duty costs. Elizabeth II (Claire Foy, Olivia Colman, Imelda Staunton across three eras) becomes queen at twenty-five, thrust into a role she didn't seek and cannot escape. The show spans her reign—from Winston Churchill to Margaret Thatcher to Tony Blair to Princess Diana. It asks: What does it mean to be a symbol? What does it cost to be monarch? "The crown must win," her grandmother tells her. "Always the crown."

The show's structure is generational: every two seasons, the cast changes. Claire Foy plays young Elizabeth—uncertain, then steeling herself against husband, sister, and self. Olivia Colman plays middle Elizabeth—watching her children suffer, her empire shrink, her role become ceremonial. Imelda Staunton plays late Elizabeth—Diana's death, Charles's marriage, the monarchy's diminishing relevance. Each actress captures a different Elizabeth: learning, enduring, surviving.

But the show's heart is often its supporting characters: Princess Margaret (Vanessa Kirby, Helena Bonham Carter, Lesley Manville), the sister who wanted more and got less; Prince Philip (Matt Smith, Tobias Menzies, Jonathan Pryce), the husband who gave up everything and resented it; and Diana (Emma Corrin, Elizabeth Debicki), the most famous woman in the world, destroyed by the institution she married into. The Diana seasons are devastating—the beginning of the end for the monarchy's image, and for a young woman who deserved better.

Six seasons, sixty episodes, complete. The Crown is lavish, expensive, and thoughtful. It's not accurate—it's dramatized history—but it captures something true: institutions outlast people, duty crushes individuals, and the monarchy is an anachronism sustained by spectacle. "I have been queen for so long," Elizabeth says. "I don't know who I am without it." She never found out.
