What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is a royal drama that becomes something more interesting than a royal drama. Following Queen Elizabeth II from her coronation in 1953 through six decades of reign, the show uses the monarchy as a lens to examine power, duty, and the human cost of putting institution above individual. Peter Morgan, who wrote the 2006 film The Queen and the play The Audience, expands his vision into television's most ambitious biographical drama.

The show reinvents itself every two seasons. Claire Foy plays Elizabeth in seasons one and two, a young woman thrust into a role she never wanted, learning to suppress her personality for the crown. Olivia Colman takes over for seasons three and four, an older Elizabeth navigating political upheaval and family crisis during the turbulent 1960s-80s. Imelda Staunton concludes the journey in seasons five and six, an aging queen watching her family and relevance crumble. Each era brings its own energy, its own concerns, its own aesthetic.

What makes The Crown special is its ambiguity about monarchy itself. Is Elizabeth a hero for subordinating her desires to duty? Or is she a tragic figure who sacrificed her humanity for an institution? The show refuses to answer definitively. Some episodes are sympathetic; others are scathing. Margaret (Vanessa Kirby, then Helena Bonham Carter) serves as the anti-Elizabeth—the sister who wanted love and freedom, and was punished for it. Diana (Emma Corrin, then Elizabeth Debicki) exposes the cost of royal protocol when it destroys a human life.

Six seasons, sixty episodes, decades of British history rendered in lavish detail. Not every season is equal—seasons one, three, and four are masterpieces; seasons five and six feel crowded and occasionally soapy—but the ambition is staggering. The finale brings Elizabeth face-to-face with her own funeral, a meditation on mortality and legacy. She makes peace with her choices. Whether we should is left to us.
