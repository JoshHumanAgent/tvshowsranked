What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown is a decades-spanning saga about a job you can't quit. Queen Elizabeth II inherited the throne at 25 and reigned for 70 years. Peter Morgan's series follows her from wedding to funeral, showing how the crown shaped everyone it touched—her, her husband, her children, her prime ministers, her country.

The show is structured around recasting. Every two seasons, the actors change. Claire Foy plays young Elizabeth, learning the job while her husband Philip (Matt Smith) resents his reduced role. Olivia Colman plays middle-aged Elizabeth, watching her children's marriages collapse and her government change. Imelda Staunton plays elderly Elizabeth, the only monarch most Britons have ever known, confronting the twilight of her reign.

This could be gimmicky, but it works. The new actors bring new interpretations while the character remains recognizably herself. You see how a 25-year-old became a 90-year-old—how duty calcified into habit, how restraint became armor. Elizabeth is never cruel, never petty, never herself. She is the crown, and the crown has no personality.

The supporting characters provide the drama Elizabeth cannot. Margaret (Vanessa Kirby, then Helena Bonham Carter, then Lesley Manville) is the sister who had to give up love. Charles (Josh O'Connor, then Dominic West) is the heir who never wanted to wait. Diana (Emma Corrin, then Elizabeth Debicki) is the outsider destroyed by the institution. The show is at its best when showing how the royal family's rigid structures damage the people inside them.

Six seasons cover 70 years: the early reign, the Wilson years, the Diana era, the Blair years, and finally the William-Kate period and Elizabeth's death. The production is lavish—palaces, costumes, state occasions rendered in detail. But the show's real power is in quiet moments: Elizabeth watching news footage, Philip wondering what his life could have been, Charles trapped between his mother and his wife.

Characters & Acting (9): Six seasons, three casts, all excellent. Claire Foy, Olivia Colman, Imelda Staunton create a coherent Elizabeth. The supporting casts match them.

Visual Spectacle (10): Palaces, state occasions, period detail—all rendered with Netflix money. It looks as expensive as royalty should.

Narrative Drive (7): By nature episodic. Decades of reign don't build to a climax—they just accumulate. The Diana seasons are the most propulsive.

Key Episodes

"Wolferton Splash" (S1E1) — Elizabeth's wedding. The beginning. The crown awaits.

"Fairytale" (S4E3) — Charles and Diana's wedding. The fairy tale that wasn't.

"Ritz" (S6E10) — Princess Margaret's end. One of television's most beautiful death scenes.
