Maniac

2018 • Netflix • 10 Episodes

Score: 7.33
Sci-Fi, Drama, Comedy

What It Feels Like

Annie and Owen participate in a pharmaceutical trial that connects their minds across multiple therapy simulations. Maniac is Netflix's most beautiful experiment: Jonah Hill and Emma Stone fall through genres—film noir, fantasy, heist, spy thriller—while processing depression, trauma, and isolation. It's visually gorgeous, emotionally raw, and deeply strange. Each simulation explores character through metaphor; their real lives bleed into fiction. By the end, you've watched two broken people learn to see each other. The show is therapy as adventure, and it works.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Emma Stone's Annie channels manic energy into desperation and hope. Jonah Hill plays Owen as depression made performance—every version of himself a mask, including the real one. Their chemistry is subtle but earned. Supporting cast (Sally Field, Justin Theroux, Sonoya Mizuno) grounds the weirdness. You believe these people belong together.

World Building (8/10)

The near-future is retro-future: CRT monitors, clunky technology, a world that feels abandoned. The trial's simulations are richly detailed dream logic. Each world has rules, style, purpose. The connecting device—a machine that makes minds visible—creates impossible intimacy. The world-building is dreamlike, which is the point.

Cinematography (9/10)

Cary Fukunaga directed every episode, and it shows. Each simulation has distinct visual language: noir shadows, fantasy color, spy-movie framing. The real-world sequences feel drab and sad against the dream brilliance. This is cinema applied to television. Netflix money well spent.

Visual Spectacle (8/10)

The spectacle is the genre shifts—watching these same actors inhabit entirely different realities. There's action, fantasy imagery, and emotional close-ups. The computer that connects minds becomes a character. The effects serve the dream logic.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Therapy as narrative. Depression as identity. Whether connection is possible between broken people. The show uses genre as metaphor—every simulation reveals something real about Annie and Owen. It asks whether we can truly know anyone, including ourselves. The therapy is literal and figurative.

Narrative Drive (7.5/10)

The trial structure provides clear stakes—will it work? will they connect? Each simulation deepens character and relationship. The momentum is emotional: watching two people learn to trust across impossible circumstances. The plot is secondary to the feeling.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Complete limited series. The ending is open: connection made, futures uncertain. The show doesn't promise happy endings, just possible ones. The resolution satisfies without tidying. Depression is managed, not cured. Love is possible, not easy. That's the point.
