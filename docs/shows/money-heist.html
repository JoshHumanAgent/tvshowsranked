What It Feels Like to Watch

Money Heist (La Casa de Papel) is a Spanish heist thriller about a criminal mastermind called The Professor who recruits eight people to rob the Royal Mint of Spain. The plan: print 2.4 billion euros over eleven days. They take 67 hostages. They wear Salvador Dalí masks. They sing "Bella Ciao," an Italian anti-fascist resistance song. The show was a flop on Spanish television. Netflix bought international rights. It became a global phenomenon.

The Professor (Álvaro Morte) is the show's engine—a chess player who anticipates every move. Histeam is named after cities: Tokyo (the narrator, volatile), Berlin (the field commander, psychopathic), Nairobi (theforger, maternal), Rio (the hacker, young), Denver (the muscle, impulsive), Helsinki and Oslo (Serbian thugs), and Moscow (Denver's father). They're criminals, not heroes, but the show makes us root for them. The police are the antagonists, and Inspector Raquel Murillo (Itziar Ituño) is the Professor's worthy opponent—and eventual love interest.

The show's structure is cat-and-mouse: every police move has a countermove, every crisis has a contingency. "I'm not a thief," the Professor says. "I'm a man with a plan." The first two parts (the Mint heist) are tight, tense, and brilliant. Parts three through five (the Bank of Spain heist) are messier—more soap opera, more melodrama, more dead characters miraculously returning. The logic frays. The spectacle expands.

Five parts, forty-eight episodes, complete. Money Heist is pop entertainment at its most addictive: ridiculous, emotional, thrilling, and weirdly political. The Professor becomes a symbol of resistance. "Bella Ciao" becomes a global anthem. The Dalí mask becomes Halloween costume. The show's message: the system is rigged, the banks are thieves, and sometimes you have to break the law to find justice. It's not profound. But it's fun.
