What It Feels Like to Watch

Money Heist (La Casa de Papel) is Spanish heist television at its most addictive. A mysterious man called the Professor recruits eight criminals to break into the Royal Mint of Spain and print billions of euros. They have a plan for everything—the police, the hostages, the media, the government. What they don't have a plan for is each other.

The Professor (Álvaro Morte) is the mastermind, a man who's spent years preparing for this moment. The robbers take city names as aliases—Tokyo (Úrsula Corberó), Berlin (Pedro Alonso), Nairobi (Alba Flores), Rio (Miguel Herrán)—and wear red jumpsuits and Salvador Dalí masks that become global symbols of resistance. Inside the Mint, they must manage hostages, maintain discipline, and execute the plan. Outside, the Professor plays cat-and-mouse with police negotiator Raquel Murillo (Itziar Ituño).

The first two parts are tight—nine episodes each, the heist expanding in unexpected directions, relationships forming between captors and captives. The robbers aren't simple villains; they have code, personality, and tragic backstories. Berlin is charismatic but dangerous. Nairobi brings warmth and leadership. Tokyo is chaos, narrating the story with hindsight that warns you nothing goes as planned.

Parts three through five return for a second heist—this time the Bank of Spain—with higher stakes, global attention, and mounting casualties. The formula stretches, but the emotional investment in the characters keeps you watching. The show knows how to make you care, how to make you feel every loss, every betrayal.

"Bella Ciao," the Italian resistance song, becomes the show's anthem. It plays when characters are about to die, about to risk everything. The show understands what heist stories are really about—not the money, but the people and what they're willing to sacrifice.
