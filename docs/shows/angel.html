What It Feels Like to Watch

Angel isBuffy's darker sibling. Angel (David Boreanaz) leaves Sunnydale for Los Angeles, a city of demons disguised as humans and humans disguised as monsters. "The vampire with a soul" is unique—a creature of evil cursed with conscience, seeking redemption for centuries of atrocities. His mission: "Help the helpless." With Cordelia Chase (Charisma Carpenter), who receives visions of people in danger, and Doyle (Glenn Quinn), a half-demon guide, he opens Angel Investigations: supernatural detective agency.

The show evolves across five seasons. Season one is procedural: case-of-the-week, noir atmosphere. Season two introduces Wolfram & Hart, a law firm serving evil itself—Angel's recurring antagonist. Season three brings Connor (Vincent Kartheiser), Angel's son, born of vampire parents, stolen into a hell dimension, returned as a vengeful teenager. Season four is serialized chaos: the Beast, Jasmine (a goddess who offers peace at the cost of free will), and the gang's takeover of Wolfram & Hart itself. Season five is reinvention: running the evil law firm while trying to do good.

The show's thesis: redemption is possible but hard. Angel saves people constantly, but his debt is never paid. "If nothing we do matters," he asks, "then all that matters is what we do." It's existentialist vampire fiction: the fight itself is the meaning. The supporting cast deepens: Wesley Wyndam-Pryce (Alexis Denisof) transforms from comic relief to tragic figure; Lorne (Andy Hallett) is a demon who reads souls through singing; Fred (Amy Acker) is a physicist rescued from a hell dimension. Then there's Spike (James Marsters), Buffy's other vampire with a soul, arriving in season five as rival and comic relief.

Five seasons, 110 episodes, cancelled on a cliffhanger. Angel is Joss Whedon's most ambitious work—dark, serialized, unwilling to provide easy answers. The finale: "Let's go to work," as Angel and his team face an army they cannot defeat. The fight continues offscreen. That's the point.
