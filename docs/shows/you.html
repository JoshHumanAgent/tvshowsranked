What It Feels Like to Watch

You puts you inside the mind of a killer. Joe Goldberg is a stalker, a murderer, a sociopath—and also the narrator. You see everything through his eyes. Hear his justifications. Understand his logic. Penn Badgley's performance is so charming that you catch yourself rooting for Joe, before remembering what he's done.

The show is uncomfortable by design. Joe stalks women, murders obstacles, and frames it as love. The first season stays grounded. The second loses some tension. The third becomes self-aware. But Badgley never stops being magnetic, and the show keeps asking: why do we find monsters appealing?

It's a commentary on toxic masculinity wrapped in a thriller. The romanticization of Joe forces viewers to confront their own biases. He's Dexter with better PR.

Characters & Acting (8.5): Penn Badgley is the show. His fourth-wall breaks, his justifications, his charm—Joe is unforgettable. The supporting cast changes per season, keeping him the constant.

Narrative Drive (8): Each season is binge-worthy. The format—Joe meets woman, Joe obsesses, bodies pile up—wouldn't work if Badgley weren't hypnotic.

Resolution (5): Ongoing. Hasn't ended yet. Joe keeps escaping. The show needs an ending, but Netflix keeps renewing it.
