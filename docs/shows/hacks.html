Hacks

2021 • HBO Max • 28 Episodes

Score: 7.12
Comedy, Drama

What It Feels Like

Deborah Vance, legendary Las Vegas comedian, needs new material. Ava Daniels, cancelled LA comedy writer, needs a job. Their forced partnership becomes an exploration of generational conflict, comedy craft, and what happens when women refuse to disappear. Jean Smart and Hannah Einbinder have the chemistry of oil and water gradually becoming salad dressing. This is comedy about comedy—how jokes get made, why timing matters, which wounds become material. Smart gives the performance of her career; Einbinder matches her beat for beat. The show believes the work matters.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Jean Smart is incandescent—Deborah is vain, brilliant, cutthroat, and deeply lonely. Hannah Einbinder's Ava brings millennial entitlement crashing against boomer work ethic. The supporting cast (Carl, Marcus, various comedian guest spots) create a lived-in Vegas ecosystem. Every acting choice serves comedy that serves character.

World Building (7/10)

Las Vegas entertainment ecosystem: casinos, comedy clubs, the economics of fading fame. The show understands how comedians live—the road, the showcases, the hierarchy. LA comedy scene contrasts Vegas stability. The writing room dynamics feel real because the writers clearly know stand-up.

Cinematography (6/10)

Functional comedy cinematography—shot to serve jokes, not to impress. The Vegas locations provide natural glamour; the comedy club backstages feel cramped and real. The show looks professional without being showy.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

No spectacle. This is people in rooms talking, occasionally performing stand-up. The spectacle is the craft—watching a joke get built, refined, and delivered. If you need explosions, wrong show.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Generational warfare as comedy philosophy. Cancel culture examined from both sides. The intersection of craft and ego. Women's erasure in entertainment and the refusal to go quiet. The show takes comedy seriously—a set is built, not improvised, and the construction process is fascinating. It's about work, not just jokes.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Episodic comedy with serialized growth. Each season has goals (new act, tour, special). The conflict is Deborah's resistance to change versus Ava's insistence on relevance. You watch to see what material survives and what relationships evolve.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Season 3 aired 2024, renewal pending. Each season completes an arc—learning to work together, touring, a career-defining special. The relationship evolves without easy resolution. The comedy industry provides endless renewal potential.
