What It Feels Like to Watch

Before Boston Legal was even a glimmer, there was The Practice. Same creator (David E. Kelley), same obsession with moral ambiguity in the courtroom, but played straight. This isn't comedy-drama—this is grueling, ethical knife-fighting where the good guys sometimes lose and the bad guys sometimes walk free.

Dylan McDermott's Bobby Donnell leads a firm of defense attorneys who take cases no one else will touch. They defend murderers, rapists, monsters—because everyone deserves a defense. The show's thesis is that the legal system isn't about truth; it's about process, and the process is deeply, irredeemably messy.

The cast is stacked: Lara Flynn Boyle, Kelli Williams, Camryn Manheim, Steve Harris. But the real standout is the writing. Kelley's scripts are dense with ethical dilemmas that don't have easy answers. This is courtroom drama for people who think Law & Order is too neat.

Characters & Acting (8): McDermott burns with intensity. The ensemble is one of the strongest of any legal drama. Kelley wrote for actors, giving them speeches that become showpieces.

Conceptual Density (8): The ethical complexity here is ahead of its time. Guilty clients, compromised lawyers, systemic failures—The Practice grapples with issues television still avoids.

Resolution (7): The show lost its way in later seasons (budget cuts forced cast changes), but the early seasons are as good as television legal drama gets. The ending is bittersweet but not a betrayal.

Key Episodes

"Pilot" (S1E1) — The stakes are established immediately. Ellenor defends a clearly guilty man, and the team argues about whether any client deserves their best.

"Civil Wars" (S2E3) — Bobby faces his most challenging case. The moral center of the show fractures.

"Check Mates" (S4E8) — A chess game becomes a metaphor for everything. Kelley at his most literary.
