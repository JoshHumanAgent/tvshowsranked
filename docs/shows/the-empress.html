The Empress

2022 • German Historical Drama • 12 Episodes

Score: 6.65
Drama, Historical

What It Feels Like to Watch

The Empress reimagines the story of Elisabeth of Austria—known as Sisi—as a sleek, modern period drama with contemporary sensibilities wrapped in nineteenth-century dress. The series follows a rebellious young woman who accidentally captures the heart of Emperor Franz Joseph I, upending court politics and her sister's engagement in the process. It's a familiar fairy tale structure given fresh energy by committed performances and production values that make imperial Austria look both beautiful and stifling.

The show walks a line between historical drama and romance, between political intrigue and personal awakening. Elisabeth is portrayed as a proto-modern woman—rebellious, free-spirited, resistant to the strictures of court life—in a way that resonates with contemporary audiences even if it simplifies the historical figure. Devan Höll plays Sisi with a physicality that sells both her character's wildness and her growing sophistication, and the chemistry with Philip Froissant's Franz Joseph grounds the romance in believable attraction.

Where The Empress struggles is in balancing its various tones. The court intrigue can feel perfunctory, with scheming relatives and military tensions serving as obstacles for the central romance rather than fully developed storylines in their own right. The history is simplified to the point of distortion, which won't bother viewers seeking entertainment but may frustrate those hoping for genuine insight into the Habsburg Empire. The Empress is best approached as a lush costume drama with political window dressing—a story about a woman finding her power in a world designed to deny her autonomy.

Why It Ranks Here

The Empress scores well on the dimensions that costume dramas prioritize: world building, cinematography, and character work. Its weaknesses lie in narrative ambition and resolution—the show is clearly designed for continuation, and its opening season feels like extended setup rather than a complete story. The ranking reflects a polished production that knows its genre and executes competently within it, without reaching for the political or thematic depth that would elevate it to the top tier.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7/10)

Devan Höll makes for a compelling protagonist—her Elisabeth is willful and physical in ways that feel genuinely unconventional for the setting, not just performative rebellion. The supporting cast creates distinct personalities within the court, though most remain types: the jealous sister, the manipulative mother-in-law, the well-meaning but naive emperor. The acting serves the genre effectively without transcending it.

World Building (8/10)

The Habsburg court comes to life with impressive detail. The series films in real European palaces, and the locations provide a texture that soundstages can't replicate. The costumes are lavish without being merely pretty—they signify status and period in ways that deepen the world. Court protocols, military realities, and political tensions create a sense of a functioning empire rather than just a backdrop for romance.

Cinematography (8/10)

The Empress looks expensive. Natural light pours through palace windows, candles illuminate ballroom scenes with period-appropriate warmth, and the camera lingers on both the beauty of the settings and the faces of the actors. The visual style is contemporary—handheld cameras during moments of emotional intensity, unconventional framing—but the period elements never feel like they're being mocked or ironized. It's a confident aesthetic.

Visual Spectacle (6.5/10)

The spectacle here is one of richness rather than action. Grand halls, elaborate gowns, ceremonial events—The Empress delivers the visual pleasures that period drama audiences crave. There's no battle spectacle or large-scale action; the appeal is in the accumulation of beautiful details and the sense of a world built for display.

Conceptual Depth (6/10)

The show engages with themes of female autonomy, institutional constraint, and the cost of defying expectations. Elisabeth's struggle against court protocol is clearly framed as a fight for self-determination. But these themes remain personal rather than political—we see how the system constrains one exceptional woman without examining why the system exists or how it might change.

Narrative Drive (6.5/10)

The romance provides momentum, and the court intrigue adds sporadic tension. But the season suffers from being clearly designed as the first chapter of a longer story—many plot threads are established rather than resolved, and the finale feels like a pause rather than a conclusion. Viewers seeking a complete narrative will need to commit to the ongoing series.

Narrative Path & Resolution (5.5/10)

The first season ends with more questions than answers. Elisabeth has won the emperor but not the court; her position remains precarious, and her enemies are still circling. The show clearly expects continuation, and the lack of resolution limits the satisfaction of the season as a standalone viewing experience.
