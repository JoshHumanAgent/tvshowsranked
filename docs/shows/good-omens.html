Good Omens

2019 • Amazon Prime • 12 Episodes

Score: 7.32
Fantasy, Comedy, Drama

What It Feels Like

An angel and demon who've grown fond of Earth team up to prevent the Apocalypse. Neil Gaiman and Terry Pratchett's beloved novel becomes a joyous, chaotic, surprisingly heartfelt series about friendship, free will, and the audacity of choosing humanity over divine plans. Michael Sheen and David Tennant have the chemistry of a married couple who've been bickering for six millennia. Good Omens believes the world is worth saving—not because it's good, but because it's interesting, and because your friends are here.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Michael Sheen plays Aziraphale with delighted restraint—a being of light who's discovered earthly pleasures without losing his celestial awkwardness. David Tennant's Crowley slinks through history with rockstar swagger and hidden softness. Their chemistry carries the entire production. Jon Hamm's Gabriel is perfect bureaucratic absurdity.

World Building (8/10)

Heaven is corporate beige, Hell is bureaucratic nightmare. Earth is rendered with loving detail across centuries—biblical times to modern London. The accumulation of Aziraphale and Crowley's earthly experiences creates real history. The magic system is Pratchett's: rules exist but personality matters more.

Cinematography (7/10)

BBC quality with Amazon budget. The historical sequences have period-appropriate film stock. Modern London is cozy and lived-in. The heavenly and hellish spaces have distinct, memorable color temperatures. Nothing revolutionary, but everything serves the story.

Visual Spectacle (6/10)

Modest spectacle. Angels and demons have wings, possessions have effects, the apocalypse has consequences. But Good Omens is character-driven—the spectacle is watching Sheen and Tennant react to things, not explosions in the sky. The budget is visible but not ostentatious.

Conceptual Density (7.5/10)

Free will versus divine plan. The nature of good and evil. Whether institutions deserve loyalty. The value of human creation. Pratchett's humanism shines through Gaiman's romanticism. Questions about authority and propaganda feel unexpectedly modern. Plus: dogs. Everyone loves dogs.

Narrative Drive (7.5/10)

The Apocalypse clock provides propulsion, but the real engine is relationship—will these two idiot immortals finally admit they care about each other? The plot meanders through historical flashbacks and present-day chaos. Those who need constant momentum may find it indulgent. Those who love these characters want to linger.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Season 1 completes Pratchett and Gaiman's novel with earned emotional resolution. Season 2 exists because success demanded it, expanding on relationship dynamics without the source material's propulsive plot. Both seasons work, but Season 1 is the complete experience they intended.
