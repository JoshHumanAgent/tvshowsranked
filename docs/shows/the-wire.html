What It Feels Like to Watch

The Wire is a novel about Baltimore. Each season examines a different institution: the drug trade, the port, city politics, the school system, the newspaper. David Simon was a police reporter; he saw how the systems fail. The show dramatizes that failure. It's not about good vs. evil—it's about institutions vs. individuals, and the institutions always win. "All the pieces matter," a minor character says. They do. Everything connects.

The centerpiece is the drug war. Detective Jimmy McNulty (Dominic West) isan alcoholic detective who creates a case against Avon Barksdale's organization out ofspite. The show follows both sides equally: the cops building wiretaps, the dealers running corners. Stringer Bell (Idris Elba) is Avon's second-in-command, a drug lord taking business classes, trying to turn the gang into a legitimate enterprise. He's intelligent, disciplined, and eventually murdered. The game is rigged. The institution wins.

What makes The Wire great is its scope: no character is too minor, no plot is irrelevant. Omar Little (Michael K. Williams) robs drug dealers, whistles "The Farmer in the Dell," and is the most moral character on the show. "A man got to have a code," he says. He does. He dies from a random shot by a child. The randomness is the point. The institution is indifferent.

Five seasons, sixty episodes, complete. The Wire was ignored when it aired; it's now considered one of the greatest shows ever made. It requires patience: slow pacing, dozens of characters, no easy resolution. "The king stay the king," D'Angelo Barksdale explains. Yes. The institutions remain. The individuals change, suffer, die. The Wire is sociology as tragedy—and it's perfect.
