What It Feels Like to Watch

The Wire is often called the best television show ever made, which is both accurate and misleading. It doesn't feel like other prestige dramas—it's slower, denser, more patient. Creator David Simon was a police reporter for the Baltimore Sun, and he brings a journalist's eye to the series. Each of five seasons examines a different institution: the drug trade, the port, city government, the school system, the press. The thesis is that institutions devour the people who serve them, and Baltimore is the corpse.

Detective Jimmy McNulty (Dominic West) is the closest thing to a protagonist, but the show resists centering anyone. The drug crew—Stringer Bell (Idris Elba), Avon Barksdale, Omar Little (Michael K. Williams)—gets as much screen time as the cops. Omar is a stick-up man who robs drug dealers, lives by a code, and became television's most compelling antihero. "A man got to have a code," he says. He does, and the show respects it.

The dialogue is dense Baltimore vernacular, the plotting requires absolute attention, and the emotional payoffs are delayed for seasons. But the rewards are extraordinary. The show builds a complete city—corners, projects, city hall, newsroom—and shows how all the pieces connect. "All the pieces matter," says Freamon, one of the detectives. The show believes this: every bit character has a history, every plot thread ties back to institutional failure.

Five seasons, sixty episodes, a finale that's less an ending than a statement: nothing changes. The corners stay the same, the institutions grind on, the players rotate. But The Wire makes us see the system, understand the forces that shape lives, feel compassion for people we'd otherwise dismiss. It's sociology as art, journalism as storytelling, television as literature. Nothing else is like it.
