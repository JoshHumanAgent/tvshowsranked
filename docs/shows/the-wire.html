What It Feels Like to Watch

The Wire is often called the greatest television drama ever made. It's not an unreasonable claim. Over five seasons and sixty episodes, David Simon and his collaborators built a portrait of an American city—Baltimore—that doubles as a portrait of American institutions failing everyone they're supposed to serve.

Each season focuses on a different institution: the drug trade (season one), the port and unions (season two), city politics (season three), the school system (season four), and the media (season five). Characters recur, storylines cross-pollinate, and by the end you understand that everything is connected—the corner, the police department, city hall, the newspaper, the schools. No one escapes complicity.

The show's genius is its refusal to simplify. The drug dealers are not monsters; the police are not heroes. Both are doing jobs within systems that corrupt everyone who touches them. Detective Jimmy McNulty (Dominic West) is a brilliant investigator who destroys everyone around him. Omar Little (Michael K. Williams) robs drug dealers at gunpoint but lives by a strict moral code. Stringer Bell (Idris Elba) runs the drug operation like a corporation, taking business classes and plotting legit exits that never come.

Nothing ends cleanly. Victories are temporary, defeats are structural. At the end of season three, a police major legalizes drugs in his district—it works, reducing violence and crime—and is destroyed for it. At the end of season four, a brilliant teacher reaches one student while others fall through the cracks. The system is too big to change. But individuals resist, adapt, survive. Sometimes.

Sixty episodes. No filler. A novel for television, written by a man who covered crime for the Baltimore Sun and knew exactly what he was documenting.
