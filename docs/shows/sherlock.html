What It Feels Like to Watch

Sherlock is what happens when you take Arthur Conan Doyle's detective and drop him into modern London with a smartphone. Benedict Cumberbatch plays Sherlock Holmes as a consulting detective who texts, blogs, and uses nicotine patches instead of a pipe. Martin Freeman is Dr. John Watson, a wounded veteran of Afghanistan who becomes Sherlock's flatmate and chronicler. The game is the same—only the technology has changed.

Steven Moffat and Mark Gatiss created something that felt instantly definitive. The modernization is clever without being gimmicky: Sherlock's deductions appear as on-screen text messages; Watson's blog replaces his published stories; Moriarty operates through criminal networks and digital heists. But at its heart, this is still Holmes and Watson, the brilliant sociopath and the warm-hearted doctor.

The first two seasons are nearly perfect. Each 90-minute episode is a feature film in miniature. "A Scandal in Belgravia" gives us Irene Adler as a dominatrix who outsmarts Sherlock until she doesn't. "The Reichenbach Fall" ends with Sherlock apparently dead and Watson grieving at his grave. The cliffhanger between seasons two and three dominated internet speculation for two years.

Cumberbatch's Sherlock became a cultural phenomenon—the cheekbones, the coat, the rapid-fire deductions delivered with barely contained contempt for lesser minds. But Martin Freeman's Watson is equally important: he humanizes Sherlock, provides the emotional core, and serves as our entry point into a world of genius detectives and impossible crimes.

The ranking is for seasons 1-3 only. Season 4 is widely considered a disappointment—overplotted, self-indulgent, and more interested in teasing future twists than delivering satisfying conclusions. The show that delighted audiences with its cleverness ended by outsmarting itself.

Characters & Acting (10): Cumberbatch and Freeman are perfect. Their chemistry, their timing, their emotional range—the show lives on their partnership. Andrew Scott's Moriarty is terrifying and theatrical.

Narrative Drive (9): Each episode races. The mysteries are clever, the solutions satisfying. The show never drags across its first three seasons.

Resolution (6): Season 4 disappointed fans. The show became too clever for its own good, more interested in setups than payoffs.

Key Episodes

"A Study in Pink" (S1E1) — The introduction. Sherlock and Watson meet. A new Holmes for a new century.

"A Scandal in Belgravia" (S2E1) — Irene Adler. The Woman. The game at its most elegant.

"The Reichenbach Fall" (S2E3) — The cliffhanger. Sherlock versus Moriarty. An ending that broke the internet.
