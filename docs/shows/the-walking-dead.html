What It Feels Like to Watch

The Walking Dead was television's biggest show for years, and its decline is a case study in diminishing returns. Andrew Lincoln plays Rick Grimes, a sheriff's deputy who wakes from a coma to find the world overrun by zombies. He finds his family, joins a group of survivors, and learns that the living are more dangerous than the dead. The premise is classic. The execution was, for years, exceptional.

The show's strength was its characters: Rick's moral decay, Daryl Dixon (Norman Reedus) as the unexpected heart, Michonne (Danai Gurira) as the katana-wielding warrior, Carol (Melissa McBride) transforming from abused wife to ruthless survivor. The early seasons—especially seasons four and five—are genuinely great television. The Governor, Terminus, the hospital—each introduced new horrors and moral dilemmas.

The show's weakness was its refusal to end. Season seven introduced Negan (Jeffrey Dean Morgan), a charismatic villain whose introduction—beating two beloved characters to death with a baseball bat—was shocking. But the show kept going. Characters left. Actors wanted out. The narrative circled. By season eleven, the show was a shambling corpse, still moving but without purpose.

Eleven seasons, 177 episodes, with spin-offs continuing. The show's legacy is mixed: It proved horror could work on television, it launched the zombie renaissance, and it introduced characters who deserved better endings. "We're the ones who live," Rick says. The show lived too long. But for a few seasons, it was essential viewing—survival horror with genuine stakes, before the dead became the only ones still watching.
