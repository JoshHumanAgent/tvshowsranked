Pachinko

2022 • Apple TV+ • 16 Episodes

Score: 7.68
Drama, Korean, Historical

What It Feels Like

A multi-generational Korean saga told in two timelines: 1930s Busan where young Sunja makes a devastating choice, and 1989 Tokyo where her grandson explores his identity. Apple TV+'s finest drama weaves displacement, identity, sacrifice, and resilience through stunning cinematography and profound performances. You feel the weight of history in every frame—the brutal march of Japanese occupation, the quiet dignity of Korean immigrants, the impossible choices that echo through bloodlines. This is TV as literature: patient, devastating, beautiful.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Youn Yuh-jung won the Oscar for Minari—here she dominates as elderly Sunja, carrying decades of pain in her posture. The rotating cast of Sunjas across ages captures growth without losing essence. Kim Min-ha as young Sunja is revelation-level. The Japanese cast brings nuance to characters who could've been villains. Every face carries history.

World Building (8/10)

1930s Busan feels lived-in, desperate, pulsing with markets and debts and survival. 1989 Tokyo glitters with alienation. The pachinko parlors themselves become characters—chaotic, smoky spaces where Korean immigrants find community and exploitation in equal measure. The period detail is meticulous.

Cinematography (8/10)

Apple TV+ money shows. Every frame is composed with gallery precision—the symmetry, the light, the devastating close-ups. The two timelines have distinct palettes: warm oranges for Busan memories, cold blues for Tokyo present. Director Kogonada brings his Columbus eye to television.

Visual Spectacle (7/10)

The spectacle is human-scale: faces, hands, domestic spaces. But occasional sequences—a train journey through Korea, a bombing raid, a pachinko hall explosion—demonstrate the budget without becoming gratuitous. The spectacle serves story.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Colonialism, identity, belonging, sacrifice, family obligation versus self-determination, the immigrant experience, the weight of history on subsequent generations. Pachinko never stops asking what we owe our ancestors and what we owe ourselves. Multiple languages (Korean, Japanese, English) layer meaning naturally.

Narrative Drive (7.5/10)

The dual-timeline structure requires patience. Some find the 1989 thread less compelling than the brutal 1930s survival story. But the mystery of Solomon's arc pulls you forward. Those who surrender to the pace are rewarded.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Ongoing. The first season ends with questions, not answers. But unlike shows that use mystery as a crutch, Pachinko's uncertainty feels earned. These lives continue. The story is far from over.
