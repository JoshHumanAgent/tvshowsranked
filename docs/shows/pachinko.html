What It Feels Like to Watch

Pachinko is an epic across generations—Korea under Japanese occupation, the migration to Japan, and the children and grandchildren who inherit both countries and neither. Based on Min Jin Lee's novel, the show follows a Korean family from 1915 to 1989, tracing how history crushes individuals and how individuals survive anyway.

Sunja (played by Yu-na as a child, Minha Kim as young adult, and Yuh-jung Youn as elderly) is the center of the story. As a young woman, she falls in love with a Korean fisherman, becomes pregnant, and discovers he's already married to a Japanese woman. She accepts a marriage of convenience to a kind Christian minister and moves to Japan. The pachinko business—the gambling parlors that were one of the few industries Koreans could enter—becomes the family's livelihood and their cage.

The show interweaves timelines: 1915-1945 Korea and Japan under Sunja's perspective, and 1989 Tokyo following her grandson Solomon (Jin Ha). Solomon is navigating corporate Japan, trying to close a deal that requires displacing a Korean grandmother. The timelines don't just alternate—they speak to each other. What Sunja endured, Solomon inherits. What Solomon faces, Sunja's choices made possible.

The production is lavish without being gratuitous. Locations span Korea, Japan, and the United States. Japanese and Korean dialogue subtitles throughout—this is a story told in the languages of its characters. The colonial architecture, the postwar poverty, the 1980s corporate spaces—all rendered with period authenticity.

What makes Pachinko remarkable is its compassion. Japanese colonizers aren't monsters; they're products of empire. Korean immigrants aren't saints; they're people trying to survive. The show understands that history isn't about good and bad—that's for individuals—but the systems that grind everyone down and the ways people find to live anyway.
