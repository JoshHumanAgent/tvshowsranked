What It Feels Like to Watch

Damon Lindelof's Watchmen is not an adaptation of Alan Moore's graphic novel—it's a sequel, a response, and a reckoning. The show exists in a world 30 years after the comic, where Robert Redford has been president for decades, smartphones are banned, and the police wear masks to protect their identities from white supremacist terrorists.

Sister Knight (Regina King) is a Tulsa police detective investigating the murder of her mentor. The investigation leads her through a world where Adrian Veidt's squid attack on Manhattan is taught in schools, where Doctor Manhattan walks among mortals in disguise, and where the legacy of the original Watchmen is contested territory.

The show's central historical fact: The Tulsa Race Massacre of 1921, when white mobs destroyed a prosperous Black neighborhood, killing hundreds. The show's opening scene depicts this massacre before cutting to 2019, where Tulsa's police force is majority Black and the Seventh Kavalry—descendants of the KKK—targets them.

Jeremy Irons plays Adrian Veidt, the smartest man in the world, exiled to a manor where he stages elaborate plays with cloned servants. Jean Smart plays Laurie Blake, formerly Silk Spectre, now an FBI agent hunting vigilantes. Yahya Abdul-Mateen II plays Cal, Sister Knight's husband, whose identity becomes the season's most devastating reveal.

The show is about legacy—forgetting, remembering, and who gets to write history. It uses superhero mythology to discuss race in America in ways mainstream entertainment rarely attempts. It's also visually stunning, with Each episode revealing more of its mystery. The finale brings everything together without betraying what came before.
