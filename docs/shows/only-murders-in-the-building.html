Only Murders in the Building

2021 • Mystery Comedy • Ongoing

Score: 7.35
Mystery, Comedy

What It Feels Like to Watch

Only Murders in the Building is a murder mystery that loves murder mysteries, made by people who understand both why the genre works and why we love it. Three residents of an Upper West Side apartment building—who happen to share an obsession with a true crime podcast—find themselves investigating a death in their own building, and the result is a show that's simultaneously a loving tribute to cozy mysteries and a genuinely addictive whodunit.

Steve Martin, Martin Short, and Selena Gomez play the amateur detectives, and their chemistry is the show's engine. Martin and Short bring decades of comedic timing to characters who are precisely the kind of difficult eccentrics who would end up living alone in luxury apartments; Gomez plays a young woman whose cynicism provides the necessary contrast to their bumbling enthusiasm. The show's pleasure is watching them try to apply podcast logic to actual crime, with all the ways it doesn't translate and all the ways it surprisingly does.

Each season introduces a new murder and new complications, but the building itself remains central—a grand prewar construction full of richpeople with secrets, where every neighbor might be a suspect and every apartment could contain evidence. The mystery plotting is fair (clues are visible if you're paying attention), the comedy is character-based (the jokes come from who these people are, not from arbitrary gags), and the show maintains a tone that's warm without being cloying. It's comfort television that respects its audience's intelligence.

Why It Ranks Here

Only Murders earns its ranking through charm, clever plotting, and a perfect central trio whose interplay makes each episode feel like time with friends. The score reflects a mystery that takes its genre seriously while having fun with it.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

The three leads are perfectly cast: Martin's obsessive former actor, Short's financially precarious Broadway director, and Gomez's secretly lonely podcast superfan. Each has depth beyond their comedic function, and the relationships between them develop across seasons. Guest stars are used well without overwhelming the core.

World Building (7.5/10)

The Arconia is rendered as a fully realized environment—the lobby, the apartments, the hidden spaces, the social hierarchy of a Manhattan co-op. The building becomes a character, and the show uses its geography to create genuine mystery architecture.

Cinematography (7/10)

The visual approach is warm and bright, matching the cozy mystery tone. The apartment building is shot with attention to its elegant weirdness, and the mystery sequences use split screens and visual callbacks that feel like classic mystery filmmaking. The direction serves the puzzle without calling attention to itself.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

The spectacle is in the mystery structure rather than effects. There are stunts and sequences, but the visual interest comes from clues revealed, suspects observed, and the architecture of the building used for investigation. This is mystery comfort food, not action cinema.

Conceptual Depth (6/10)

The show engages with our obsession with true crime—the ethics of consumption, the way podcasts shape perception, the relationship between entertainment and actual tragedy. These themes are present but don't overwhelm the cozy mystery structure. The show raises questions without becoming a lecture.

Narrative Drive (7.5/10)

Each season is tightly plotted as a mystery, with red herrings that are actually relevant and clues that pay off. The season-long structure maintains momentum while individual episodes advance individual relationships. The show knows how to end an episode to make you start the next one.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7.5/10)

Mysteries are only as good as their solutions, and Only Murders delivers. Each season's resolution plays fair—the clues were there, the solution makes sense, and the reveal feels emotionally satisfying. The show understands that in mystery, how you get there matters as much as who did it.
