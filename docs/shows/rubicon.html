Rubicon

2010 • AMC • 13 Episodes

Score: 7.38
Thriller, Conspiracy

What It Feels Like

Will Travers is an intelligence analyst at a think tank that finds patterns. His wife and daughter died on 9/11. He discovers a pattern in crossword puzzles that leads to a conspiracy with global stakes. James Badge Dale is Will—brilliant, damaged, increasingly paranoid. AMC's slow-burn conspiracy thriller, cancelled too soon, remains one of television's most intelligent explorations of the intelligence world.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

James Badge Dale anchors the show: the analyst who can't stop seeing patterns, the widower who can't stop grieving. The supporting cast (Miranda Richardson, Arliss Howard, Dallas Roberts) creates a world of bureaucrats, spies, and analysts. The performances are understated—this is the intelligence world, not action.

World Building (9/10)

Intelligence analysis: the think tanks, the government contracts, the people who read reports for a living. The world is accurate—this is how intelligence analysis actually works, not the spy fantasy. The bureaucracy is the point.

Cinematography (7/10)

Intentionally drab: offices, conference rooms, brownstones. The style is bureaucratic realism—the conspiracy hides in plain sight, in memos and reports. The look serves the paranoia without calling attention to itself.

Visual Spectacle (2/10)

No spectacle. This is people reading documents, talking in offices, noticing patterns. The "spectacle" is intellectual—the thrill of deduction, the terror of what the deduction reveals. The driest thriller ever made.

Conceptual Density (9/10)

Intelligence analysis vs. intelligence action, whether seeing patterns means they exist, the banality of conspiracy, the intelligence-industrial complex. The show explores how organizations make decisions based on analysis that might be wrong. Profound and patient.

Narrative Drive (6/10)

Slow. Deliberate. The conspiracy unfolds through documents, meetings, small discoveries. The pace is the point—intelligence work is slow, and then suddenly it isn't. Patient viewers rewarded; others found it boring. The cancellation reflected the audience divide.

Narrative Path & Resolution (4/10)

Cancelled after one season. The conspiracy is revealed but not resolved. The show was building toward something larger; we got the first act. A cult classic because of what it promised, not what it delivered.
