What It Feels Like to Watch

Rome is HBO's glorious failure—a historical epic so expensive that it helped define the network's prestige aspirations, cancelled after two seasons because it nearly broke the budget. Set during the transformation of the Roman Republic into the Empire, it follows Julius Caesar's conquest, assassination, and the civil war that followed, seen through the eyes of both the great men of history and two ordinary soldiers caught in the turbulence.

Lucius Vorenus (Kevin McKidd) and Titus Pullo (Ray Stevenson) are our guides through the chaos. Vorenus is a principled soldier, devoted to tradition, struggling to adapt to a changing world. Pullo is his opposite: violent, impulsive, surprisingly moral in his own way. They're fictionalized versions of real legionaries mentioned briefly in Caesar's commentaries, and their friendship drives the show's emotional core. These are ordinary men whose lives keep intersecting with extraordinary history.

But the show's true stars are its vision of Rome itself—not the marble city of legend, but a crowded, dirty, vibrant metropolis where wealth and poverty exist side by side, where politics is violent and personal, where gods are believed in and manipulated. The production design is staggering: five acres of sets built at Cinecittà studios in Rome, costumes detailed enough for museum display, a budget of over $100 million for twenty-two episodes.

Two seasons, twenty-two episodes, complete story. The show ends with Octavian's victory over Antony and Cleopatra, the birth of the Empire. It's a perfect conclusion to an imperfect production: ambitious, flawed, unforgettable. Rome proved that television could do history as spectacle, that the small screen could compete with epics. It was cancelled too soon, but what remains is glorious.
