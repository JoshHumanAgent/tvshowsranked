What It Feels Like to Watch

Rome premiered on HBO in August 2005, created by John Milius, William J. MacDonald, and Bruno Heller. The series depicts the fall of the Roman Republic through the eyes of two soldiers: Lucius Vorenus (Kevin McKidd) and Titus Pullo (Ray Stevenson), who witness and participate in the transition from republic to empire under Julius Caesar (Ciarán Hinds), Mark Antony (James Purefoy), and eventually Augustus (Simon Woods/Max Pirkis). The series ran for two seasons (22 episodes), concluding with the deaths of Antony and Cleopatra and Octavian's consolidation of power.

The series operates simultaneously on two registers: historical epic (the great men of Rome, the battles, the political machinations) and intimate drama (Vorenus and Pullo's friendship, their families, their survival in a changing world). The contrast provides the series' unique quality—public history and private life intertwined, the personal and the political inseparable.

Vorenus and Pullo serve as audience surrogates and historical catalysts—men of no great importance who nonetheless appear at crucial moments. Pullo accidentally discovers Caesar's stolen eagle standard; Vorenus saves Caesar's life; both become entangled in the great events of their age while primarily concerned with their own survival and families. The series dramatizes how ordinary people experience history.

The depiction of Rome itself is the series' greatest achievement—the city as living organism, its populace, its corruption, its violence, its grandeur. The production filmed in Italy, achieving authentic locations and atmosphere. The contrast between the Forum's political theater and the Aventine's criminal underworld, between patrician villas and plebeian apartments, creates complete urban world-building.

The historical figures are rendered with complexity: Caesar as brilliant but ruthless, Antony as charismatic but self-destructive, Octavian as coldly calculating, Cleopatra as politically pragmatic. The second season's compression (history spanning decades in few episodes) forces narrative shortcuts but maintains character depth.

The series was expensive—reportedly $100 million for the first season—and HBO cancelled it after the second season due to cost, despite critical acclaim and solid ratings. It remains a cult favorite, appreciated for its ambition and its unique blend of historical accuracy and pulpy entertainment.

Vorenus and Pullo: Kevin McKidd and Ray Stevenson create television's most compelling odd-couple friendship—Vorenus the stoic, moral, inflexible Roman; Pullo the hedonistic, violent, loyal soldier.
