My Demon

2023 • Korean Rom-Com Fantasy • 16 Episodes

Score: 6.92
Romance, Comedy, Fantasy

What It Feels Like to Watch

My Demon is a Korean romantic comedy about a demon who loses his powers and a chaebol heiress who accidentally absorbs them through a contractual mix-up. What follows is a familiar rom-com structure elevated by charismatic leads and a willingness to play with genre conventions—the demon's immortality becomes a vehicle for exploring what it means to live forever without connection, while the heiress's coldness masks loneliness that the demon accidentally begins to address.

The show knows exactly what it is and doesn't pretend to be more. The fantasy elements are light—the demon loses his powers and has to live as a human, allowing for the fish-out-of-water comedy that the genre favors. There are other demons and supernatural threats, but they serve the romance rather than creating danger that feels meaningful. The stakes are always emotional; even when the demon's eternity is threatened, the real concern is whether the heiress will survive losing him.

What makes My Demon work better than many similar shows is the chemistry between the leads and the specificity the actors bring to stock types. Song Kang plays the demon with an appealing arrogance that gradually warms into genuine care; Kim Yoo-jung makes the heiress sympathetic despite the cold-female-lead trope that Korean drama has overused. The secondary characters provide comedy and obstacle without overwhelming the central relationship, and the sixteen-episode count is just right for the story being told—not so long that the will-they-won't-they becomes torturous, not so short that the emotional payoffs feel unearned.

Why It Ranks Here

My Demon ranks where it does because it executes its genre well without transcending it. The romance works, the leads have chemistry, and the fantasy elements provide novelty without disrupting the comfort-viewing experience. It's a competent addition to the rom-com canon that won't change your life but will pass sixteen hours pleasantly.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7.5/10)

The leads carry the show, and they're well-cast for their roles. The demon's transition from indifferent immortal to emotionally invested is conveyed through small shifts in performance, and the heiress's defenses come down at a believable pace. Secondary characters fill their roles—the scheming relatives, the loyal assistant, the romantic rivals—without transcending type, but the show doesn't require them to.

World Building (6/10)

The supernatural elements are sketched rather than developed—there are demons, they have powers, there are rules about contracts and souls. The show isn't interested in creating a rich mythology; the demon's world exists to facilitate the plot and provides danger when needed. The human world is standard Korean drama wealth—mansions, boardrooms, family politics.

Cinematography (6.5/10)

Korean drama production values apply: attractive leads in attractive locations, with lighting that flatters and compositions that emphasize emotional moments. The supernatural effects are competent without being impressive. The show looks like what it is—a well-funded television romance, not a cinematic experience.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

The spectacle here is romantic rather than action-oriented. There are some supernatural effects—the demon's powers manifest visually when he has them—but the visual interest is in faces and chemistry, not in anything the production builds or destroys.

Conceptual Depth (6/10)

The show uses the demon's immortality to gesture at themes about love and loneliness, about whether someone who can live forever would want to without the person they love. These ideas are present but not deeply explored; the format favors romantic resolution over philosophical inquiry. The themes serve the romance rather than vice versa.

Narrative Drive (7.5/10)

Rom-com plotting is formulaic but effective when done well, and My Demon does it well. Each episode advances the relationship through obstacles that feel imposed rather than contrived—family politics, supernatural threats, the demon's own past. The sixteen-episode arc builds naturally toward resolution.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

The conclusion delivers what it promises: the couple overcomes their obstacles, the demon makes the choice the audience expects, and the ending provides the emotional satisfaction that the genre requires. The fantasy elements are resolved in a way that allows for the expected happily-ever-after without feeling like a cheat.
