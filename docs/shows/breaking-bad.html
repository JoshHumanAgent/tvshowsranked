What It Feels Like to Watch

Breaking Bad is about a high school chemistry teacher who starts cooking methafter being diagnosed with terminal lung cancer. Walter White (Bryan Cranston) has a pregnant wife, a disabled son, and $737,000 in medical debt. "I have spent my whole life terrified," he tells his family. "I am awake." The transformation from Mr. Chips to Scarface is the show's engine. "I am the one who knocks," Walt says. He becomes Heisenberg.

Jesse Pinkman (Aaron Paul) is Walt's former student and partner. He's a small-time dealer, a screw-up, a junkie. But he's also the show's moral center—the only character who consistently feels guilt for what they've done. The relationship between Walt and Jesse is tragic: a father-son dynamic that destroys them both. Walt saves Jesse, manipulates Jesse, poisons Jesse's girlfriend's son, and finally rescues Jesse in the end. "You're the smartest guy I ever met," Jesse says. "And you're too stupid to see he made up his mind ten minutes ago."

The show's construction is perfect. Every season escalates: from an RV in the desert to a superlab under a laundry, to a drug empire with international distribution. The villains are escalating threats: Krazy-8, Tuco Salamanca, Gus Fring, the cousins, Uncle Jack. Gus (Giancarlo Esposito) is the standout—a fast-food franchise owner who runs the Southwest's major meth operation. His death, half-face blown off, is the show's most iconic image.

Five seasons, sixty-two episodes, complete. Breaking Bad is the most tightly plotted show ever made. Every detail pays off. The finale resolves everything: Walt admits, "I did it for me," and dies in a meth lab. Jesse escapes. The money goes to his children. "I won." He did. The tragedy is complete. The show is perfect.
