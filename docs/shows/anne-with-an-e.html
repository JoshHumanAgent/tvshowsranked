Anne with an E

2017 • Netflix/CBC • 27 Episodes

Score: 7.02
Drama, Period, Coming-of-Age

What It Feels Like

Anne Shirley-Cuthbert arrives at Green Gables an unwanted orphan and proceeds to make Avonlea unable to imagine life without her imagination. This adaptation of Lucy Maud Montgomery's classic is not cozy period drama—it's trauma recovery, found family, and the revolutionary act of a young woman refusing to shrink. Amybeth McNulty plays Anne as hurricane: passionate, articulate, exhausting, magnificent. The show uses its 1900s setting to explore modern themes: identity, belonging, the stories we tell about ourselves. You'll cry. You'll cheer. You'll wish you could see the world as Anne sees it.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Amybeth McNulty is revelatory—every emotion genuine, every monologue earned. Geraldine James and R.H. Thomson as Marilla and Matthew create the exact right warmth and reserve. The supporting cast (Cole, Diana, Gilbert) grow across three seasons into people you care about. This is ensemble work that never loses Anne as the sun.

World Building (8/10)

Prince Edward Island in 1907: rural, stratified, beautiful, suffocating. The show builds period authenticity without museum distance—this is a living world where class and gender shape daily life. Green Gables becomes home because we see every stone and flower. Avonlea's social dynamics are as complex as any urban setting.

Cinematography (8/10)

Canadian landscape cinematography at its finest. The show knows PEI is a character—every wide shot is a painting. The intimate interiors (Green Gables, schoolhouses, barns) feel lived-in. Light changes across seasons; you feel time passing. This is period drama that looks like the BBC wishes it still could.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

No spectacle in the effects sense. The spectacle is emotional: Anne's imagination sequences, the first snow at Green Gables, the wedding that shouldn't be spoiled here. The show trusts period aesthetics and human faces. If you need explosions, wrong show.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Orphan trauma and the creation of found family. Gender constraints and how imagination subverts them. The immigrant experience in small-town Canada. Indigenous presence and Canadian colonial reality (the show integrates First Nations characters more than the books). Anne with an E uses its setting not for nostalgia but for relevance.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Anne's drive is emotional—acceptance, belonging, love. The plots (school rivalries, town secrets, romantic triangles) are familiar, but execution is sincere. Each season escalates stakes: local, then regional, then existential. You watch for character growth, not plot twists.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7.5/10)

Cancelled after three seasons, the finale nonetheless provides emotional closure for major arcs. Anne's journey from unwanted orphan to confident young woman completes. The cancellation hurts—there was more story—but what exists is full-hearted and satisfying. Fans have campaigned for continuation; the ending still works.
