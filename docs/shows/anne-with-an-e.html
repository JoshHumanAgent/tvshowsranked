What It Feels Like to Watch

Anne with an E adapts Lucy Maud Montgomery's beloved novel into something darker, more modern, and deeply resonant. Anne Shirley (Amybeth McNulty) is an orphan mistakenly sent to aging siblings Matthew and Marilla Cuthbert, who wanted a boy to help on their Prince Edward Island farm. They get Anne instead—a redhead with a vivid imagination, an endless vocabulary, and a heart determined to find family.

The show doesn't sugarcoat the source material. Anne's past in orphanages and abusive foster homes is rendered explicitly. The trauma of being unwanted, of being passed around, shapes her character. Her imagination is survival—a way to transform bleak circumstances into beauty. When she arrives at Green Gables, she's desperate to belong, terrified of being sent back, and fiercely loyal to anyone who shows her kindness.

Amybeth McNulty is perfectly cast—gangly, fierce, emotional, articulate. Her Anne is not just cute; she's complicated, sometimes exhausting, always endearing. The supporting cast expands the world: Gilbert Blythe (Lucas Jade Zumann) as Anne's academic rival and eventual love interest, Diana Barry (Dalila Bela) as her "bosom friend," and the town of Avonlea as a community that slowly accepts the strange redhead in their midst.

The show tackles themes the novel only hinted at: Indigenous displacement, racism, sexism, and the rigidity of small-town society. Some purists objected, but the expansion makes the world feel real rather than nostalgic. This is 19th-century Canada with all its prejudices and possibilities.

Cancelled after three seasons despite passionate fan campaigns, the story is incomplete—but the 27 episodes we have are beautiful, heartfelt, and emotionally true.
