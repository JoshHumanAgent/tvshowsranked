What It Feels Like to Watch

Ozark is Breaking Bad with better money laundering. Jason Bateman plays Marty Byrde, a financial advisor who moves his family to the Missouri Ozarks after a cartel money-laundering scheme goes wrong. His partner was killed. Marty talked his way into a second chance. Now he has to wash $500 million in five years, or his family dies. The premise is simple. The execution is ruthless.

Laura Linney is Wendy Byrde, Marty's wife, who transforms from reluctant participant to ruthless player. She's the show's most compelling character—a political operative who discovers she's better at criminality than her husband. "You're not a good person, Marty," she tells him. Neither is she. Their children, Charlotte and Jonah, get pulled into the family business. The show asks: What happens to a family when crime becomes their bond?

The Ozarks setting is perfect—isolated, beautiful, dangerous. Marty embeds himself in local enterprises: a strip club, a casino, a rafting company. The criminals multiply: the Langmores, a local crime family; Ruth ( Julia Garner), a foul-mouthed 19-year-old who becomes Marty's protégée; Darlene Snell, a heroin-dealing matriarch with a hair-trigger temper; the Kansas City mafia; the Navarro cartel. The body count escalates. "I don't have the luxury of quitting," Marty says. He doesn't.

Four seasons, forty-four episodes, a complete story. The show's weakness is Marty's plot armor—somehow, he always survives. But the finale is satisfying: the Byrdes escape, Ruth dies, the cartel falls. "We're the lucky ones," Wendy says. The cost is everyone around them. Ozark is about success in crime—the family that kills together stays together, and everyone else is collateral. It's Breaking Bad's moral universe inverted: Walter White destroyed his family; the Byrdes destroyed everyone else's.
