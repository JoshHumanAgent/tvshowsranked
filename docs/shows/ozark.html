What It Feels Like to Watch

Ozark is Breaking Bad with less moralizing and more tension. Jason Bateman plays Marty Byrde, a financial advisor who launders money for a Mexican cartel. When a scheme goes wrong, he moves his family to the Missouri Ozarks to launder five hundred million dollars in five years—apologizing to drug dealers, making impossible promises, and gradually revealing himself to be more capable than anyone suspected.

The show's aesthetic is cold blues and grays, the lake reflecting an endless gray sky. Everything is muted, controlled, suppressed—until it isn't. Laura Linney plays Wendy Byrde, Marty's wife, who transforms from a reluctant participant to a ruthless operator who might be more dangerous than her husband. Their marriage is a series of negotiations, betrayals, reconciliations, and desperate compromises. The children, Charlotte and Jonah, are drafted into the family business, losing their innocence in real time.

What separatesOzark from its predecessors is its supporting cast. Julia Garner's Ruth Langmore is a marvel—a young woman from a criminal family who becomes the Byrdes' most valuable asset and most volatile relationship. She's foul-mouthed, fearless, and desperately trying to escape a destiny that everyone in her life seems determined to force upon her. Janet McTeer's Helen Pierce, the cartel's lawyer, is elegant menace personified. Tom Pelphrey's Ben Davis, Wendy's brother, brings mental illness into the equation and shows how the criminal world treats vulnerability.

Four seasons, forty-four episodes, complete story. The finale is controversial—Marty and Wendy survive, even thrive, but at the cost of everyone around them. It's a cynical conclusion that fits the show's worldview: the powerful protect themselves, the innocent die, and money flows on. "We're all right," Wendy says. It's not comfort—it's threat.
