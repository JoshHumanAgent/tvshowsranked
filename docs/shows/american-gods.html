What It Feels Like to Watch

American Gods is fantasy as road trip, mythology as Americana. Based on Neil Gaiman's novel, it tells the story of Shadow Moon (Ricky Whittle), released from prison to find his wife dead, who is recruited by Mr. Wednesday (Ian McShane)—actually Odin, the Norse god. Wednesday is gather ing old gods for a war against new gods: Media (Gillian Anderson), Technology (Bruce Langley), Mr. World (Crispin Glover). The premise: gods exist because people believe in them. When belief fades, gods die. The war is for survival.

The show's structure is episodic mythology: each episode, Shadow and Wednesday travel across America, meeting gods who've adapted—or failed to. Bilquis (Yetide Badaki), a love goddess, works as a prostitute. Anubis (Chris Obi) runs a funeral home. The Jinn (Mousa Kraish) drives a cab. These are immigrants, like their worshippers, displaced and diminished. "I am a god," Wednesday declares. "And gods don't die. Not really." The show's thesis: America is a land without old belief, where gods become what people need them to be—even if that's television or technology.

The production design is stunning: sequences set in immigrant histories, the afterlife visualized, gods revealed in moments of terrifying power. Ian McShane is magnetic: charming, manipulative, a con man who happens to be a god. "Believe," Wednesday tells Shadow. "It doesn't matter what. Just believe." The show struggles with its central character—Shadow is passive, a blank—but compensates with world-building, visual imagination, and a supporting cast of fallen deities.

Three seasons, twenty-six episodes, cancelled. Season one (Bryan Fuller and Michael Green's vision) is extraordinary; seasons two and three are inconsistent, as showrunners changed and budgets shrank. The show never finished Gaiman's story. What remains is a flawed masterpiece: American mythology rendered as immigrant literature, gods as forgotten refugees, belief as survival. "What are you?" Shadow asks. "I'm the one they pray to," Wednesday answers. Not anymore.
