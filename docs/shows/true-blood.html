What It Feels Like to Watch

True Blood is Southern Gothic vampire mayhem—a primetime soap opera with fangs. The premise: synthetic blood has been invented, allowing vampires to "come out of the coffin" and live openly among humans. They're fighting for civil rights, or at least the right to exist. Some humans accept them. Others, often religious, want them dead.

Sookie Stackhouse (Anna Paquin) is a telepathic waitress in Bon Temps, Louisiana, whose ability makes her an outsider among humans. When she meets Bill Compton (Stephen Moyer), a 173-year-old vampire whose thoughts she can't hear, she's drawn to him—not just because he's vampire, but because he's the first person whose inner world isn't constantly assaulting her.

Alan Ball (Six Feet Under) created the show from Charlaine Harris's novels, and he understood the material's appeal: supernatural creatures as metaphors for marginalized groups, the South as a character rather than a joke, and the eroticism of danger. The show is sexual, violent, sometimes absurd, always entertaining.

The cast is extensive: Jason Stackhouse (Ryan Kwanten), Sookie's dim but good-hearted brother; Eric Northman (Alexander Skarsgård), the Viking vampire sheriff who becomes a rival for Sookie's affections; Lafayette (Nelsan Ellis), the gay short-order cook and drug dealer who became the show's breakout character; Tara (Rutina Wesley), Sookie's angry, damaged best friend.

Seasons 1-3 are peak True Blood—the small-town murder mystery, the maenad, the werewolves. Later seasons got crazier (fairies, vampire politics, Hep-V), but the show never lost its sense of fun. It's HBO doing pulp, and it's glorious.
