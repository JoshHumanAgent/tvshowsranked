What It Feels Like to Watch

Shakespeare via corporate raid. That's Succession—a King Lear for the age of private equity, where the kingdom is a media empire and the daughters are sons raised in money's long shadow. Jesse Armstrong's creation is both savage satire and genuine tragedy.

Brian Cox's Logan Roy is the patriarch—vulgar, cunning, cruel, and somehow magnetic. His children are disasters: Kendall (Jeremy Strong), the addict son who wants the throne but can't win it; Roman (Kieran Culkin), the chaos agent hiding damage behind humor; Shiv (Sarah Snook), the political operative who can't escape her father's gravity; Connor (Alan Ruck), the forgotten eldest, a fool who wants to be president.

The dialogue is among the best on television—insult poetry, deflection art, power language deployed as weapons. The camera work (shaky, intrusive, documentary-style) makes every scene feel like a secret captured. The score (Nicholas Britell's theme is hip-hop strings over classical tension) announces that this is Shakespeare via Wall Street.

Four seasons of succession battles, betrayals, reconciliations, and the slow revelation that the throne everyone wants is actually a cage. The finale delivers what the title promises—and what tragedy requires. Nobody wins. Everyone loses. The company continues.
