What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is King Lear with private jets. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) is the founder and CEO of Waystar Royco, a media conglomerate worth billions. He's eighty, having health problems, and his four children are circling. Kendall (Jeremy Strong) wants to succeed his father—and has been publicly denied. Shiv (Sarah Snook) is a political consultant who thinks she deserves the throne. Roman (Kieran Culkin) is the vulgarian, a chaos agent who might be smarter than he pretends. Connor (Alan Ruck) is the eldest, the ignored, the fool running for president.

The show's genius is making billionaires sympathetic—or at least understandable. These are horrible people: Logan destroys anyone who crosses him; Kendall enables a waiter's death and covers it up; Shiv betrays her husband for corporate power; Roman makes jokes about children. But they're also family: Logan, dying, asks his children to come. Kendall, destroyed by his father, still wants his approval. "He's a monster," Shiv says about her father. "He's our monster." They can't escape. They don't want to. They just want to win.

The dialogue is Shakespearean profanity—insults as art, cruelty as love language. "You're not serious people," Logan tells his children. He's right. They're not. They're grown adults with billion-dollar trust funds, fighting for a father who will never love them enough. The show's thesis: wealth doesn't make people evil; it reveals what they already were. These people were broken before the money. The money just bought them jets to crash in.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, complete. The finale is devastating: Kendall doesn't get the company. Shiv votes against him. Tom (Matthew Macfadyen), the husband she never loved, becomes CEO. Roman walks away broken. Logan dies mid-season, and his children scatter. "Who is the number one boy?" Logan once asked Kendall. In the end, no one is. Logan's chair is empty. His children stare at the water.
