What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is Shakespeare by way of corporate America. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) is the founder and CEO of Waystar Royco, a global media and entertainment conglomerate. He's 80 years old, in declining health, and refuses to name a successor. His four children—Connor (Alan Ruck), Kendall (Jeremy Strong), Shiv (Sarah Snook), and Roman (Kieran Culkin)—circle like sharks, waiting for blood in the water.

The show is about power, family, and the corruption of both. The Roys are billionaires, but they're also damaged children of a damaged father. Logan built an empire through ruthlessness, and he raised his children to compete for his approval, never giving it, always keeping them hungry. The result is four adults who are brilliant, damaged, and constantly at war with each other and themselves.

Jeremy Strong's Kendall is the would-be heir who can never quite take the throne—addicted, ambitious, and haunted by his own failures. Sarah Snook's Shiv is the political operator who wants the job but can't quite do what's required. Kieran Culkin's Roman is the chaos agent, the joker, the one who might be the most capable or the least stable. And Brian Cox's Logan is King Lear as media mogul—tyrannical, manipulative, and strangely magnetic, a monster whose children still want his love.

The dialogue is Shakespearean in its complexity, profane in its modernity. "Fuck off" becomes poetry. Corporate boardroom battles have the stakes of medieval succession wars. Every episode is a masterclass in tension, revelation, and emotional devastation.

Four seasons. A complete ending. The question of who succeeds Logan is answered in the finale, but the answer isn't what anyone expected. Succession is about what power costs, what love means, and whether anyone can ever really win.
