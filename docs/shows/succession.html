What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is Shakespeare for the Elon Musk era. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) is the founder of Waystar Royco, a global media conglomerate. He's old, possibly dying, and hasn't named a successor. His four children—Connor (Alan Ruck), Kendall (Jeremy Strong), Shiv (Sarah Snook), and Roman (Kieran Culkin)—circle the throne. They want love. They want money. They want power. They can't have all three.

The show's genius is making billionaires into tragic figures. These are some of the richest people on Earth, and they're miserable. Kendall wants his father's approval more than anything, and Logan withholds it. Shiv thinks she's the smart one, but she's emotionally stunted. Roman performs cruelty as protection. Connor is deluded and ignored. The dialogue is rapid, profane, and devastating—one-liners that should be printed on T-shirts: "You can't make a Tomlette without breaking some Greggs." "Am I a cunt? Will I be a cunt?" "I wonder if the golden goose is just a goose."

The supporting cast is perfect: Matthew Macfadyen as Tom Wambsgans, Shiv's husband, a man who knows he's despised and weaponizes it; Nicholas Braun as Cousin Greg, tall and pathetic and unexpectedly dangerous; J. Smith-Cameron as Gerri, the general counsel who might be the only competent executive. Every scene is tension—will this conversation destroy someone? Probably.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, complete. The series finale is perfect: Kendall loses, Shiv betrays him, Tom wins, and Roman walks away broken. "I'm the eldest boy!" Kendall screams. He is. It doesn't matter. Logan died without choosing, and the children destroy each other trying to claim his crown. Succession is about family as corporate structure, love as transaction, and the impossibility of escaping your upbringing. "We're all bullshit," Roman says. He's right.
