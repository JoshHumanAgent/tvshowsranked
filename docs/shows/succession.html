What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is Shakespeare for the Uber era. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) is a media mogul in failing health, and his four children are circling. Kendall (Jeremy Strong) is the would-be heir who keeps imploding. Shiv (Sarah Snook) is the political operator who can't stop undermining herself. Roman (Kieran Culkin) is the vicious jokester hiding a lifetime of trauma. Connor (Alan Ruck) is the ignored eldest, running for president because someone has to pay attention to him.

The show is about power—who has it, who wants it, what it costs. Every conversation is a negotiation. Every alliance is temporary. Every betrayal is inevitable. The Roy siblings love each other, in their damaged way, but they'd sacrifice each other for the company without hesitation. That's the tragedy: they're trapped in a cage of their father's making, and they can't escape because they don't want to. They want him to love them. He doesn't. He can't. He's a monster who destroyed his children to build an empire.

The dialogue is brilliant—Jesse Armstrong's scripts are dense with corporate jargon, vicious insults, and moments of unexpected poetry. The "boar on the floor" scene. Kendall's rap. Roman's eulogy that becomes a condemnation. Shiv's realization, too late, that she's been played. The show's finale is perfect: Kendall loses everything because he can't imagine a world where he's not the heir. Roman retreats into nihilism. Shiv becomes what she hated. And the company is sold to a tech billionaire who doesn't care about any of them.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, one of television's great endings. "What is a CEO?" Logan asks early in the series. It's the question every character spends the whole show trying to answer. The answer is: someone who wins. But winning, in Succession, destroys everyone around you. The finale shows the Roy siblings standing together, alone, abandoned by the father who wouldn't choose any of them. They got what they wanted. It cost everything.
