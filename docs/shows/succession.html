What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is Shakespeare for the Instagram age—a family drama about an aging media tycoon and his children fighting for control of his empire. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) built Waystar Royco from nothing; now he's dying and won't admit it. His four children—Kendall (Jeremy Strong), Shiv (Sarah Snook), Roman (Kieran Culkin), and Connor (Alan Ruck)—circle like sharks, each desperate for validation, terrified of irrelevance, unable to function outside the ecosystem of wealth and power that created them.

The show's genius is its dialogue—dense, profane, endlessly quotable, often hostile. Characters speak in half-sentences and callbacks, corporate jargon mixed with brutal insults. "Fuck off" becomes a term of endearment. Every conversation is a negotiation, every relationship a transaction. The Roys can't express love directly; it comes out as cruelty or manipulation or desperate neediness. They destroy everyone around them, and they destroy each other, and they can't stop.

Four seasons build to an ending that's both inevitable and devastating. Kendall, who spent the entire series trying to earn his father's love, loses everything. Shiv, who thought she could play the game, is outmaneuvered by her husband. Roman, the family jester, is broken by grief and rage. Connor, the forgotten eldest, buys his way into a meaningless position. Logan dies without choosing a successor, because he couldn't imagine a world where he wasn't in control. The finale is a masterwork of ambiguity—who won? No one. Everyone. It depends on what you think winning means.

Succession is funny, cruel, and profound. It's about wealth, power, and the families that pass both down like genetic diseases. It's about the emptiness at the heart of capitalism and the people who profit from it. It's about wanting love and settling for money. Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, perfect from beginning to end. The king is dead. Long live—nobody.
