What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is King Lear meets corporate satire. Logan Roy (Brian Cox), the aging CEO of a global media empire, has a stroke. His four children begin circling: Kendall (Jeremy Strong), the addict son who wants the throne; Shiv (Sarah Snook), the liberal daughter who wants power more than principles; Roman (Kieran Culkin), the damaged jokester; and Connor (Alan Ruck), the oblivious eldest. The show asks: What happens when Daddy won't die and won't let go?

Jesse Armstrong's dialogue is Shakespearean profanity—insults so creative they're collected like poetry. The family weaponizes love and abandonment in equal measure. Logan pits his children against each other, then is wounded when they betray him. Kendall wants his father's approval more than the company; Roman wants to be seen; Shiv wants to prove she's not the little girl anymore. The tragedy is that they're all right about each other, and it doesn't matter.

The show's visual language is distinctive: zooms that close in on uncomfortable moments, classical music scoring corporate backstabbing, private jets as punishment chambers. The wealth is obscene—one episode features a birthday party with a literal helicopter and a rapper—but the emotions are raw. "I love you, but you're not serious people," Logan tells his children. He's right. None of them can run the company because all of them are broken by him.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, a finale that actually lands. Logan dies in episode three of season four, and the rest is aftermath—children who never learned to be adults fighting over an empire they're not qualified to run. The ending breaks hearts: Kendall loses Tom wins, Roman disappears to drink, Shiv realizes too late that her husband is her enemy. "I'm the eldest boy," Kendall insists. Nobody cares. Daddy's gone, and the kingdom falls to the most ruthless.
