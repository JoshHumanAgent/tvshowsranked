Succession

2018 • Corporate Drama | 39 Episodes

Score: 8.75
Drama, Satire

What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is a corporate drama about a media empire and the family fighting to inherit it, but that description misses the show's essence. What makes Succession the defining television drama of its era is its understanding of power as both aphrodisiac and poison, of family as both refuge and battlefield, of wealth as something that corrupts not through evil but through the absence of consequence. The Roy family is rich enough that nothing they do matters to anyone but each other—and that becomes the cage they're trapped in.

Brian Cox plays Logan Roy, the patriarch whose children circle him like sharks and supplicants in equal measure. Jeremy Strong plays Kendall, the son whose addiction to both substances and his father's approval makes him the most tragic figure in a show with no heroes. Sarah Snook's Shiv is the daughter whose competence is weaponized against her; Kieran Culkin's Roman is the son whose humor is armor for damage we only gradually understand. Matthew Macfadyen's Tom becomes the show's most surprising portrait: a man who understands power well enough to gain it and hates himself for what he becomes.

The dialogue is Shakespearean in its density—insults layered with business language layered with family code, all delivered at speeds that reward rewinding. The camera style is documentary-adjacent: handheld, close, capturing moments that feel stolen rather than staged. What Succession understands about wealth is that it doesn't make life easier; it concentrates life's essential difficulty into a smaller number of higher-stakes decisions. The Roys' tragedy isn't that they're evil—it's that they're human in a system that can't accommodate their humanity.

Why It Ranks Here

Succession earns top ranking through writing that combines Shakespearean family drama with corporate satire, performances that define a decade, and a conclusion that honors everything that came before. This is television at its peak.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9.5/10)

Every performance is definitive. Brian Cox's Logan is terrifying without raising his voice; Jeremy Strong's Kendall makes desperation sympathetic; Sarah Snook's Shiv makes competence heartbreaking; Kieran Culkin's Roman makes damage hilarious. The ensemble creates a family that's specific and universal simultaneously.

World Building (8.5/10)

The corporate setting is rendered with precision—boardrooms, private jets, estate grounds that become arenas for conflict. The financial details are accurate enough to reward attention without overwhelming general viewers. The world of the ultra-rich is shown as uncomfortable rather than aspirational.

Cinematography (8/10)

Documentary-style camera work creates constant intimacy—close-ups in confined spaces, movement that suggests we're capturing rather than staging. The direction serves performances without overwhelming them. The look is expensive without being glamorous.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

The spectacle is entirely in boardrooms and arguments—no effects, no action sequences. The visual interest comes from watching people who control billions argue in spaces designed for comfort. This is a show about what money can't buy.

Conceptual Depth (9/10)

This is a show about what capitalism does to people, about inheritance as both gift and curse, about whether family can survive business. The themes are explored through character rather than stated, but they're present in every negotiation. The show understands power in ways that television rarely attempts.

Narrative Drive (8.5/10)

Each episode builds on the previous without requiring recap. The succession question creates constant tension—when will Logan step aside, who will take over, what happens to the others. The pacing is deliberate but never slow; every scene adds to cumulative understanding.

Narrative Path & Resolution (9/10)

The finale is one of television's best—it resolves the central question while remaining true to everything the show established. No sudden conversions, no easy redemption, just consequence. The ending feels inevitable and surprising simultaneously, which is the achievement that only great drama accomplishes.
