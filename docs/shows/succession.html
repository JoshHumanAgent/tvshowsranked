What It Feels Like to Watch

Succession is King Lear meets corporate Shakespeare, a story about a media empire and the children fighting to inherit it. Logan Roy (Brian Cox) built Waystar Royco from nothing; now he's dying, and his four children—Kendall (Jeremy Strong), Shiv (Sarah Snook), Roman (Kieran Culkin), and Connor (Alan Ruck)—circle each other like sharks, waiting for blood. The show is simultaneously a family drama, a corporate thriller, and a satire of obscene wealth.

The dialogue is vicious poetry. Creator Jesse Armstrong and his writers have created a language of aggression, where every statement is a test, every question is an attack, every compliment is a trap. The Roy children speak in half-sentences and callbacks, insults they've polished over decades of hurt. "Fuck off" becomes a term of endearment. "You're not a serious person" is the deepest wound. The show finds tragedy in boardrooms and comedy in funerals.

Jeremy Strong's Kendall is the show's broken heart, a man desperate for his father's approval who keeps failing upward. Sarah Snook's Shiv is political ambition without principle. Kieran Culkin's Roman performs indifference while craving love. Alan Ruck's Connor, the forgotten eldest, runs for president to get his father's attention. And through it all, Brian Cox's Logan looms, a titan whose capacity for cruelty is matched only by his children's capacity for disappointing him.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, perfect ending. Kendall loses. Shiv betrays him. Roman breaks. The company is sold, and none of them were ever going to win—because Logan never intended to let any of them succeed. The final image: Kendall staring at the Hudson, a security guard watching him, everything he wanted destroyed. The tragedy was inevitable. The comedy was that any of them ever thought otherwise.
