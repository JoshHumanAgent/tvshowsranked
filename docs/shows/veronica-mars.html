What It Feels Like to Watch

Veronica Mars is noir filtered through a high school setting—and it works beautifully. Veronica (Kristen Bell) used to be popular, the girlfriend of congenial rich boy Duncan Kane, best friend of his sister Lily. Then Lily was murdered. Veronica's father, the sheriff, accused Duncan's father of the crime. The Kanes were Neptune's most powerful family. Sheriff Mars was recalled. Veronica's mother left. And Veronica became an outcast.

Now she works in her father's detective agency, taking cases from classmates who can pay. She's smarter than everyone, harder than she should be, and still trying to solve Lily's murder. The first season's arc is finding your best friend's killer while navigating a high school ecosystem of rich kids, motorcycle gangs, and family secrets.

The writing is sharp and funny: Raymond Chandler meets Buffy the Vampire Slayer, with Kristen Bell delivering voiceover narration that makes Nancy Drew look amateur. High school politics become genuinely dangerous when your classmates' parents are criminals and your accountant father is invested in the wrong people.

Logan Echolls (Jason Dohring), the obligatory psychotic jackass, transforms over seasons from Veronica's tormentor to her greatest love. "Epic love spanning years and continents" could have been ironic. Instead, it became one of television's most passionate, complicated romances.

Later seasons were less focused—the college years, the FBI-pilot-that-never-was. The 2014 crowd-funded movie and the 2019 Hulu revival returned to Neptune, older and darker. But seasons 1-2 remain unmatched—a perfect blend of teen drama and hard-boiled mystery.
