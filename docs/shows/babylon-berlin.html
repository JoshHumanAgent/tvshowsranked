What It Feels Like to Watch

Babylon Berlin premiered on German television (ARD and Sky Deutschland) on October 13, 2017, created by Tom Tykwer (Run Lola Run), Achim von Borries, and Henk Handloegten. The series is set in Berlin in 1929, during the Weimar Republic—the brief democratic period between World War I and the Nazi rise to power. Across four seasons and 42 episodes (with a fifth season filming), the series depicts a city of extremes: artistic experimentation and political violence, sexual liberation and social repression, glamour and poverty, democracy and the forces that would destroy it.

The series follows Gereon Rath (Volker Bruch), a police detective transferred from Cologne to Berlin, where he investigates a pornography ring, political conspiracies, and eventually the forces threatening the Republic. Charlotte Ritter (Liv Lisa Fries) is a flapper and part-time police clerk from a poor family, whose ambition and intelligence make her essential to Rath's investigations. The series combines police procedural, political thriller, and historical drama—there are crimes to solve, but the crimes are connected to the larger political forces (Communists, National Socialists, monarchists, industrialists) threatening German democracy.

The series' achievement is visual and historical. With a budget of approximately €40 million for the first two seasons, it became the most expensive German television production ever made. The production rebuilt 1920s Berlin on a massive scale—the streets, the clubs, the police headquarters, the apartments. The detail is exhaustive: costume, music, architecture, social mores, political factions. The series depicts Weimar Berlin as it was: a city of cabarets and cocaine, jazz and jazz, artistic experimentation and political murder.

The ensemble includes Bruno Wolter (Peter Kurth), Rath's mentor who has his own secrets; Wolter's sister Alex (Fries), whose family is caught between Communist and Nazi factions; and a large cast of historical figures (police chiefs, politicians, gangsters, artists) who populate the period. The series uses the historical setting not as backdrop but as subject—the Weimar Republic's politics, its culture, its eventual destruction are not just context but content.

The series achieved international distribution through Netflix (Seasons 1-4) and has been praised for its production values, its historical accuracy, and its visual ambition. It is the most successful German television series in international distribution, establishing a precedent for German-language prestige television.

Conceptual Density

Weimar Politics: The series depicts the Weimar Republic's political complexity—Communists, National Socialists, monarchists, industrialists, each threatening democracy in different ways. The series doesn't simplify to Nazis versus everyone else; it shows a Republic threatened from multiple directions by forces that hated each other as much as they hated democracy.

Modernism: The series depicts Weimar modernism—sexual liberation, artistic experimentation, political radicalism—as both liberation and destabilization. The modernism that makes Weimar culturally significant also made it unstable; the forces that created the culture also threatened the Republic.

Democracy's Fragility: The series examines how democracies fail—not through external conquest but through internal forces that exploit democratic institutions to destroy democracy. The Weimar Republic had elections, courts, and newspapers; it fell anyway. The series shows the process without simplifying the causes.

Trauma: World War I trauma is explicit—Rath shakes, has nightmares, uses drugs. The series depicts what we now call PTSD (then called shell shock) as a defining feature of a generation. The trauma is not decoration; it affects politics, work, and relationships.

Comparative Assessment: The series examines Weimar with historical accuracy and political complexity. The approach is educational without being didactic.

Why 8: Strong conceptual examination of Weimar politics, modernism, and democracy's fragility. The trauma of World War I is explicit and consequential. The limitation is that the political complexity can be difficult for viewers unfamiliar with the period.
