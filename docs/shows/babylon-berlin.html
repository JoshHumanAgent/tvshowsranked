What It Feels Like to Watch

Babylon Berlin is German noir set in the Weimar Republic. 1929: democracy is fragile, the Nazis are rising, and Berlin is the most decadent city on Earth. Inspector Gereon Rath (Volker Bruch) arrives from Cologne, haunted by trauma from WWI, investigating a pornography ring with political connections. Charlotte Ritter (Liv Lisa Fries) is a secretary by day, flapper by night, clawing her way out of poverty. Together they navigate a city where everyone has secrets, everyone is compromised, and the past—specifically the war—refuses to stay buried.

The show's scope is astonishing: Russian exiles plotting revolution, industrialists funding fascists, police corruption, cabaret culture, addiction, trauma. Berlin is a character: neon-lit streets, smoky jazz clubs, modernist architecture, and underneath, the scars of defeat. "We lost the war," someone says. "We just haven't admitted it yet." The show's thesis: Germany is already broken; the Nazis are just the ones who finish it. Babylon Berlin captures the moment before everything collapses—when democracy still exists, when hedonism is a response to horror, when the future could still have gone differently. It didn't.

Gereon Rath is the noir archetype: damaged, brilliant, compromised. He carries guilt from the war—specifically, what he did to his brother. Charlotte is his equal: ambitious, smart, struggling with a family that needs her income. Their relationship is professional, romantic, complicated—the show resists easy cliches. The mysteries are intricate: pornography, political assassination, gold from Russia. The production design is period-perfect: cars, clothes, music, the Moka Efti club.

Four seasons, fifty-two episodes, ongoing. Babylon Berlin is the most expensive German TV production ever, and it shows: scale, detail, ambition. It's also the best portrait of Weimar Germany on screen—capturing both the glamour and the rot underneath. "Berlin is a whore," someone says. "Everyone gets their turn." The show knows what's coming. We know too. That's the tragedy.
