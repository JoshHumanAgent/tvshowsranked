What It Feels Like to Watch

Weimar Berlin in its decadent twilight—cabarets, cocaine, communism, and the rise of fascism. Tom Tykwer's Babylon Berlin is the most expensive German television production ever made, and it shows: the period recreation is immersive, the scale is cinematic, the story sprawls across police procedure, political intrigue, and family drama.

Gereon Rath (Volker Bruch) is a Cologne detective transferred to Berlin, hiding trauma and secrets. Charlotte Ritter (Liv Lisa Fries) is a stenographer from the slums, working police by day and cabaret by night. The supporting cast includes Soviet exiles, Nazi Brownshirts, communist cells, and the fading aristocracy trying to survive the new Germany.

The show moves between genres—noir, political thriller, historical drama, cabaret spectacle. There are action sequences on trains, shootouts in nightclubs, and quiet moments in police stations. The period detail is exhaustive: the cars, the apartments, the clothing, the politics.

Four seasons and continuing. The show documents the slow slide from Weimar democracy to Nazi nightmare, with characters caught in currents they can't control. It's history as thriller, the kind where you know the ending but can't look away.
