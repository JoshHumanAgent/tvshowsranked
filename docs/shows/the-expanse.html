What It Feels Like to Watch

The Expanse is the science fiction series that Trekkies, Browncoats, and BSG fans had been waiting for—a show with rigorous physics, adult storytelling, and a universe that feels genuinely lived-in. Based on James S.A. Corey's novels, it spans centuries and solar systems while keeping its focus on the people caught between Earth, Mars, and the Belt.

The story begins with a missing girl and a mystery, then expands into interplanetary war, alien protomolecules, and humanity's first contact with something beyond comprehension. The crew of the Rocinante—James Holden (Steven Strait), Naomi Nagata (Dominique Tipper), Amos Burton (Wes Chatham), and Alex Kamal (Cas Anvar)—become a family forged in crisis, the only people who keep seeing the bigger picture when everyone else is fighting over resources and old grudges.

What makes The Expanse special is its attention to detail. Gravity is handled realistically—thrust creates "gravity," and when ships stop accelerating, people float. Belters are taller and thinner from growing up in low gravity. Martian marines are stronger from training in Earth-normal gravity. The politics are nuanced: Earth is overcrowded and declining, Mars is militaristic and ambitious, the Belt is exploited and revolutionary. No one is purely good or evil; everyone has legitimate grievances.

Six seasons, with the final arc rushed but satisfying. The protomolecule mystery evolves into something cosmic. The Ring gates open doors to a thousand worlds. Chrisjen Avasarala (Shohreh Aghdashloo) swears magnificently while managing geopolitics. Amos Burton becomes one of television's most fascinating characters—a mechanic with no moral compass except those he adopts from people he respects.

This is science fiction that respects both science and fiction. The hardware is functional. The stakes are human. The universe is bigger than we imagined.

Characters & Acting (9): Shohreh Aghdashloo's Avasarala is legendary. The Roci crew chemistry is perfect. Amos Burton is unforgettable.
