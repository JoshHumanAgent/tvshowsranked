What It Feels Like to Watch

Battlestar Galactica isn't really science fiction—it's9/11 fiction. The Cylons, robots created by humans, attack without warning. Twelve colonies are destroyed in minutes. Fifty thousand survivors flee in a ragtag fleet. The show asks: What happens to democracy under existential threat? How do you maintain humanity when you're being hunted to extinction?

Admiral Adama (Edward James Olmos) commands the Galactica, an aging warship. President Roslin (Mary McDonnell) leads the civilian government. They clash constantly—military versus democratic authority, securityversus liberty. The show is political philosophy in space: torture, occupation, rigged elections, religious fundamentalism. "So say we all," the crew chants—a ritual of unity that's constantly tested.

The Cylons look human now. "They have a plan," the opening promised. The plan turned out to be confusion, religious destiny, and finding Earth. But the early seasons are masterful paranoia—who can you trust when the enemy looks exactly like you? Boomer (Grace Park) discovers she's a Cylon and breaks. Baltar (James Callis), a brilliant scientist, collaborates and rationalizes. The show refuses easy heroes.

Four seasons, seventy-five episodes, a finale that divided viewers. "All Along the Watchtower" is literalized as coordinates. Starbuck dies and returns. Earth is a radioactive ruin, then found again. The show's ending leans into spirituality—"God's plan"—which frustrates viewers wanting harder sci-fi. But the journey is extraordinary: television's grimmest, most politically sophisticated space opera. "It's not enough to survive. One has to be worthy of surviving." Battlestar Galactica asked what that means.
