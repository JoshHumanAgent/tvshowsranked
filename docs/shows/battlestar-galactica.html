What It Feels Like to Watch

Battlestar Galactica is not a science fiction show. It's a war drama, a political thriller, a religious meditation, and a character study that happens to be set in space. The Cylons—robots created by humanity—have evolved. They look human now. They destroyed the Twelve Colonies in a nuclear attack, and the survivors—fifty thousand people on a ragtag fleet of spaceships—flee across the galaxy, searching for a mythical planet called Earth.

EdwardJames Olmos plays Admiral Adama, the military commander of the last battlestar. Mary McDonnell plays President Roslin, the former education secretary who becomes president when everyone above her in the line of succession dies. Their relationship—military and civilian, pragmatist and idealist—is the show's spine. "You're the president," Adama tells her. "I'm the hammer." They clash constantly. They trust each other absolutely.

The show asks impossible questions: Are the Cylons people? (Some of them don't know they're Cylons.) Is democracy possible when you're at war? (The president fixes an election.) What happens to morality when survival is at stake? (Everything happens.) The show's second season—especially the arc on New Caprica, where the humans are occupied by Cylons—is as good as television gets. The third season stumbles. The fourth season recovers. The finale is controversial but earned: they find Earth, but it's our Earth, 150,000 years ago. The cycle begins again.

Four seasons, seventy-five episodes (plus miniseries and movies), complete. BSG was revolutionary: it made science fiction adult, serialized, and morally complex. "All of this has happened before, and all of this will happen again." The show proved that television could be as ambitious as literature. So say we all.

Conceptual Density (10): Democracy under siege. AI personhood. Religious destiny. Moral philosophy without easy answers.

Characters & Acting (9): Olmos and McDonnell are titans. The ensemble is perfect. Gaius Baltar is unforgettable.
