What It Feels Like to Watch

The Boys is what happens when you ask: What if superheroes were real, and they were terrible people? In a world where "Supes" are celebrities owned by a corporation (Vought International), the Seven are the premiere team—led by Homelander (Antony Starr), a Superman analogue who's also a narcissistic sociopath. The show's opening scene says everything: a Supe races through a girl, liquefying her. Her boyfriend, Hughie (Jack Quaid), watches. Revenge begins.

The show's genius is making corporate satire visceral. Homelander isn't just evil—he's the logical endpoint of unchecked power and celebrity worship. He wants love more than anything, and he'll kill anyone who withholds it. Antony Starr's performance is terrifying: a man-child with laser eyes who smiles while threatening murder. "I can do whatever I want," he tells Starlight, a true believer learning her heroes are monsters.

The "Boys" are vigilantes fighting back, led by Billy Butcher (Karl Urban), a British operative who hates Supes with religious intensity. His team includes Frenchie and Kimiko, whose relationship humanizes the violence. Mother's Milk (Laz Alonso) is the conscience. But Butcher is the engine—a man whose hatred might destroy him before the Supes do. "There's no such thing as a good Supe," he insists. The show keeps asking: Is he right?

Four seasons (as of 2024), thirty-two episodes, with a final season planned. The show is brutally violent—the first episode features a boat exploding a whale, and it escalates from there—but it's also sharp about media, corporate power, and the fascism lurking in superhero worship. "Never meet your heroes," the tagline says. The Boys shows why. It's the anti-Marvel, the superhero critique made flesh, and it's one of the most entertaining shows on television.
