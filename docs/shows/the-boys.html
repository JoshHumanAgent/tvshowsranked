What It Feels Like to Watch

The Boys takes the superhero genre and turns it inside out. What if superheroes were real, corporate, and corrupt? What if the most powerful beings on Earth were also the most entitled, predatory, and dangerous? That's Vought International's business model: manage superheroes as celebrities, monetize their image, cover up their crimes.

The Seven is Vought's premier team, led by Homelander (Antony Starr)—a Superman analogue who is, in fact, a narcissistic sociopath with a god complex. He can't be killed. He can do whatever he wants. And he wants to be loved, worshipped, and feared in equal measure. Antony Starr plays him with a terrifying emptiness; Homelander is a god-sized toddler with laser eyes.

The Boys are the resistance: Billy Butcher (Karl Urban), a British ex-CIA operative whose hatred for supes is personal; Hughie Campbell (Jack Quaid), a regular guy whose girlfriend was killed by a speedster; Mother's Milk (Laz Alonso), Frenchie (Tomer Capone), and Kimiko (Karen Fukuhara)—each with their own reasons to fight. They have no powers. They have cigarettes, explosives, and rage.

The show is violently, graphically anti-corporate. Vought treats superheroes as products, scandals as PR problems, and collateral damage as a cost of doing business. The satire cuts deep: the military-industrial complex, media consolidation, celebrity culture, political manipulation—all filtered through superhero action.

Four seasons in, the scope keeps expanding. We learn where superheroes come from. We meet new supes with new pathologies. We watch Homelander become more openly fascist. And the body count keeps rising. It's brutal, funny, angry, and somehow still hopeful—because someone is always fighting back.
