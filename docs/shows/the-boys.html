What It Feels Like to Watch

The Boys is what happens when superheroes are corporate products and the corporation doesn't care about collateral damage. Based on Garth Ennis's comic, the show imagines a world where "Supes" are owned by Vought International, a media conglomerate that monetizes heroism through movies, merchandise, and theme parks. The Seven—Vought's premier superhero team—are celebrities with brand deals and PR teams. They're also, mostly, terrible people.

Billy Butcher leads a small group of vigilantes trying to expose Vought and take down the Supes. His wife was violated by Homelander, the Superman-analogue who leads the Seven—narcissistic, sociopathic, and powerful enough to destroy the world. Butcher is played by Karl Urban with a Cockney accent and a bottomless capacity for violence. Hughie (Jack Quaid) is the audience surrogate whose girlfriend is killed in the pilot, launching him into Butcher's war. The Boys fight dirty because the Supes fight lethal.

The show is spectacularly violent. Heads explode. People are dismembered. Blood sprays across rooms. The gore is played for shock and comedy—this isn't prestige television gravity, it's gonzo satire. But the violence has a point: power without accountability corrupts absolutely, and corporate power is just as dangerous as superpower.

The satire cuts deep. Vought's media manipulation, the superhero "cinematic universe," the way outrage is manufactured and deployed—it's all recognizable from our world. Homelander is what happens when you combine Superman's power with a fragile ego and corporate backing. He's not a villain with a plan; he's a tantrum with nukes.

Antony Starr's Homelander is the show's defining performance. He's terrifying not because he's evil in a complicated way, but because he's evil in a comprehensible way: a man-child with unlimited power who just wants to be loved. The supporting cast—especially Aya Cash as Stormfront, a Nazi Supe with influencer vibes, and Giancarlo Esposito as Vought CEO Stan Edgar—builds out a world where capitalism and superpowers are equally dangerous.
