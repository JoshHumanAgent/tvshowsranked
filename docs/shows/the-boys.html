What It Feels Like to Watch

The Boys is superhero satire as horror. What if superheroes were real—and they were awful? Not heroic, not noble, not even morally complex: just awful. Arrogant, cruel, protected by corporate PR, accountable to no one. The Seven, this world's Justice League, are owned by Vought International, a media conglomerate that monetizes heroism. Homelander (Antony Starr) is Superman with a god complex—a narcissist who murders when convenient, who presents a smiling face for cameras, who might destroy the world if properly annoyed.

The show follows two groups. The Seven: Homelander, Queen Maeve, A-Train, The Deep, Starlight, Black Noir—each with their own damage, their own secrets. The Boys: Billy Butcher (Karl Urban), a CIA operative whose wife was violated by a superhero; Hughie (Jack Quaid), a regular guy whose girlfriend was killed by A-Train; Mother's Milk, Frenchie, and Kimiko—a team of vigilantes trying to expose Vought's crimes. The conflict: corporate power versus vigilante justice, both corrupt, both violent, both claiming moral high ground they don't possess.

The show's thesis: power without accountability is tyranny, and corporate media is happy to sell you tyranny with a cape. Homelander is the logical endpoint of celebrity culture—someone who can do anything because his fans will forgive anything. "I can do whatever I want," he says. He's right. The satire is broad, the violence is extreme (bodies explode, heads pop, blood flows freely), but the criticism is sharp. The boys try to fight back—the boys, and women like Starlight and Maeve who realize they're complicit— but the system is designed to protect itself.

Four seasons, thirty-two episodes, ongoing. The Boys is the anti-Marvel: not hopeful, not inspiring, not fun. It's angry, cynical, and often hilarious. Antony Starr's Homelander is television's most terrifying villain—not because he's evil, but because he's a man-child with nuclear capabilities. "I'm the real hero," he insists. He believes it. That's the horror.
