What It Feels Like

An anthology series exploring interconnected futures—each episode a different society navigating technology's consequences, from AI governance to biotech ethics. Pluribus takes its name from the Latin "from many, one": disparate stories revealing shared humanity across diverging technological paths. The series is philosophical science fiction in the tradition of Black Mirror without the nihilism. Each episode has different cast, setting, and tone, but the episodes connect—a character in one story becomes technology in another. The ambition is impressive: eight futures, one thematic throughline about what we choose to become.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (6.5/10)

Anthology format means different actors each episode. Quality varies but overall competent. Some episodes feature standout performances; others rely more on concept than character.

World Building (7.5/10)

Eight futures: AI-managed democracy, biotech inequality, digital afterlife, post-scarcity economics. Each world is explored in 45 minutes with surprising depth. The interconnection reveals gradually.

Cinematography (7/10)

Each episode has distinct visual style matching its future. AI-governed society is clean; digital afterlife is saturated. The production values support each concept.

Visual Spectacle (6/10)

Futures technology realized: AI interfaces, biotech labs, virtual spaces. Budget constraints show but ambition compensates. The spectacle is conceptual as much as visual.

Conceptual Density (7.5/10)

Technology and choice. What we gain and lose with each innovation. The episodes don't offer easy answers—I Pluribus asks what "many" means when technology makes us one.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Each episode is complete story. The throughline—how futures connect—builds across the season. Anthology pacing means some episodes drag, others accelerate.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6.5/10)

Complete season with anthology structure. The final episode reveals connections, but each story stands alone. Philosophical science fiction without forced closure.
