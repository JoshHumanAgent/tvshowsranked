What It Feels Like to Watch

Tales from the Loop is based on the art of Simon Stålenhag—paintings of rural Sweden where scientists built an underground particle accelerator called the Loop, and strange machines bleed into ordinary life. The show is a quiet miracle: eight standalone stories about the people who live above the machine, connected by place and occasional character, each one about memory, loss, time, and what we'd give to hold onto something.

The first episode follows Loretta (Rebecca Hall), a woman who discovers a robot that looks exactly like her. The robot doesn't know it's a robot. It has Loretta's memories, her husband, her son. The real Loretta is forced to confront what it means to be herself—to be human—if a machine can have all the same feelings. Is the robot less than her? Or just another version?

Later episodes follow a boy who finds a way to freeze time, a woman who trades places with her younger self, a man who discovers his grandfather is living the same day over and over. The science fiction is never explained—the Loop does things, people live with the consequences. The show's interest is emotional, not logical. What would you do with more time? What would you give to see someone again? What happens when you can't let go?

The pace is slow, contemplative, European. There are long stretches without dialogue. The music by Philip Glass and Paul Leonard-Morgan creates an atmosphere of melancholic wonder. Each episode has the weight of a short film—complete, contained, devastating.

This is science fiction as meditation, not action. It's The Twilight Zone without the twist endings, Black Mirror without the cynicism. It's about people experiencing impossible things and finding their way back to something like normal, changed.

Cinematography (10): This is one of the most beautifully shot shows ever made. Every frame could be a Stålenhag painting.

World Building (9): The Loop exists beneath the town. Strange machines appear in fields. Life goes on. The world feels complete and mysterious.

Characters & Acting (9): Rebecca Hall, Jonathan Pryce, and the ensemble deliver quiet, devastating performances.
