What It Feels Like to Watch

Mindhunter is about the birth of criminal profiling—and the men who created it. 1977: FBI agent Holden Ford (Jonathan Groff) interviews imprisoned serial killers to understand how they think. Bill Tench (Holt McCallany) is his partner, a veteran agent skeptical of Ford's academic approach. Wendy Carr (Anna Torv) is the psychology professor who helps systematize their findings. "How do we get ahead of crazy if we don't know how crazy thinks?" Ford asks. It's a good question. The answer is: interview the monsters.

The show's genius is making conversation terrifying. The interviews with Ed Kemper (Cameron Britton), the Co-Ed Killer, are mesmerizing—he's articulate, intelligent, and utterly without remorse. He describes murder the way a chef describes cooking. The show's other killers—David Berkowitz, Charles Manson, Wayne Williams—are studies in pathology, each revealing something about the nature of evil. But the show's thesis is that these interviews create tools: criminal profiling allows the FBI to anticipate behavior, to catch killers before they kill again.

But Mindhunter is also about the cost of understanding monsters. Ford becomes increasingly unstable, seduced by the darkness he studies. Tench's personal life falls apart; his son shows disturbing behavior. Carr compromises her ethics for the work. The show asks: What happens to people who spend their days talking to serial killers? The answer: they become different. They understand evil too well. They see it everywhere.

Two seasons, nineteen episodes, cancelled. David Fincher (executive producer, director of four episodes) moved on to other projects. The show ends mid-story—real characters whose fates are known, fictional characters left hanging. But those nineteen episodes are some of television's most intelligent crime drama. "You want to understand the minds of serial killers," Kemper says. "Be careful what you wish for."
