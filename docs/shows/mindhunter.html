What It Feels Like to Watch

Mindhunter is about the invention of criminal psychology. Set in the late 1970s, it follows FBI agents Holden Ford (Jonathan Groff) and Bill Tench (Holt McCallany) as they interview imprisoned serial killers, trying to understand how they think, what motivates them, whether patterns can be identified that might prevent future murders. It's based on true events—the FBI's Behavioral Science Unit really did interview Ed Kemper, Jerry Brudos, Richard Speck, and others to develop criminal profiling.

The show is two things at once: a police procedural about catching killers, and a psychological study of the people who study killers. Holden is young, ambitious, increasingly obsessed with understanding evil. Bill is older, more pragmatic, trying to balance the horrors he encounters with his family life. They're joined by Wendy Carr (Anna Torv), a psychology professor who helps them systematize their findings—and who begins to worry about the cost of their work.

David Fincher executive produced and directed several episodes, and his aesthetic is everywhere: the muted colors, the meticulous period detail, the sense of dread that builds through stillness rather than violence. The interviews are often static—two people in a room, talking—but they're electrifying. Cameron Britton's Ed Kemper is terrifying not because he's threatening, but because he's intelligent, articulate, and obviously dangerous beneath a veneer of cooperation. "How do you get crazy?" he asks. The question haunts the whole series.

Two seasons, nineteen episodes, cancelled by Netflix in 2020. The show was expensive, the third season never got past the planning stage. But what exists is remarkable: a portrait of evil being catalogued, measured, understood—and the researchers being changed by what they learn. "You don't think about them coming home with you," one character says. But they do.
