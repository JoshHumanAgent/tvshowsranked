What It Feels Like to Watch

The Knick aired on Cinemax from August 8, 2014 to December 17, 2015, created by Jack Amiel and Michael Begler and directed entirely by Steven Soderbergh. The series is set in New York City in 1900-1901, at the fictional Knickerbocker Hospital ("The Knick"), following the surgeons, nurses, and administrators as they navigate the transition from Victorian medicine to modern practice. Across two seasons and 20 episodes, the series depicts blood-soaked operations, professional rivalries, racial and class tensions, and the birth of modern surgery in an era when mortality rates were high and innovation was dangerous.

Clive Owen plays Dr. John Thackery, the hospital's chief surgeon—a brilliant cocaine and heroin addict whose addictions fuel his innovation and threaten his life. Thackery is based on historical surgeon William Halsted, who pioneered modern surgical techniques while maintaining a secret addiction. Owen plays Thackery as volatile, brilliant, and self-destructive; the performance is charismatic even when the character is repellent.

The series' signature is its surgical sequences—long, graphic, unnerving. Soderbergh films operations in single takes with minimal editing, the camera observing as surgeons cut, suture, and improvise. The procedures are historically accurate: C-sections with high mortality, hernia surgeries before antibiotics, emergency operations under chloroform. The blood flows freely; the patients frequently die. The effect is educational and horrifying—this is what medicine was before modern practice.

The ensemble includes André Holland as Dr. Algernon Edwards, a Black surgeon trained in Europe who faces racism from the hospital's white staff; Eve Hewson as Nurse Lucy Elkins, who becomes involved with Thackery; Juliet Rylance as Cornelia Robertson, the hospital administrator's daughter who works as a nurse; and Jeremy Bobb as Herman Barrow, the hospital's financially desperate administrator. The series tracks multiple characters through the hospital's ecosystem—the wealthy benefactors, the poor patients, the immigrants, the Black community, the corrupt politicians.

The series was cancelled after two seasons despite critical acclaim. Soderbergh's direction (every episode, serving as his own cinematographer and editor) established a visual standard for television that matched feature film quality. The series was later released on HBO Max, introducing it to new audiences. The cancellation remains one of television's premature endings—the story was incomplete, the characters were developing, and the historical setting had more to reveal.

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.60

Dr. John Thackery: Clive Owen created a television-defining performance—a brilliant surgeon whose addictions drive his innovation and threaten his life. Thackery is charismatic, volatile, and frequently repellent; he performs surgery while high, he fires employees who cross him, he pursues dangerous experiments. Owen plays the contradictions: the addiction makes Thackery both better (he operates with enhanced confidence) and worse (his judgment is impaired). The performance required Owen to depict both surgical competence and addiction's deterioration.

Dr. Algernon Edwards: André Holland plays the Black surgeon trained in Europe who faces racism from the Knick's white staff. Edwards is more qualified than his colleagues but is assigned to the Negro ward and denied operating privileges. Holland plays Edwards as dignified without being passive; his anger fuels his ambition, and his ambition creates conflict. The character's arc (from rejected outsider to essential surgeon) is among the series' most satisfying.

Nurse Lucy Elkins: Eve Hewson plays the nurse who becomes involved with Thackery personally and professionally. Lucy is the series' moral center—not because she's virtuous (she becomes involved with men in power) but because she's clear-eyed about the hospital's dynamics. Hewson plays Lucy as ambitious and intelligent, a woman navigating a profession that hasn't yet defined itself.

Herman Barrow: Jeremy Bobb plays the hospital administrator who is financially desperate and morally flexible. Barrow takes bribes, makes deals with corrupt politicians, and compromises the hospital's integrity while trying to keep it open. Bobb plays Barrow as sympathetic despite his corruption; he's trying to manage an institution that shouldn't survive, in a system that doesn't support it.

The Supporting Cast: Juliet Rylance as Cornelia Robertson (the administrator's daughter who works as a nurse), Mayko Nguyen as Nurse Lincoln, Cara Seymour as Sister Harriet (the nun who runs the orphanage and performs abortions), Michael Angarano as Dr. Bertie Chickering Jr. (the young surgeon learning the trade)—each receives development. The ensemble is deep; even minor characters receive arcs.

Medical Practice: The series depicts 1900 medicine with accuracy—surgery before antibiotics (infection is the leading cause of death), anesthesia (chloroform and ether), blood transfusion (direct from donor to patient, with blood type compatibility not yet understood), obstetrics (C-sections with high mortality). The medical detail is both educational and horrifying.

Social Dynamics: The series depicts 1900 social dynamics—racism (Edwards faces discrimination from colleagues and patients), class (the poor patients in the Knick vs. the wealthy patients at other hospitals), gender (female nurses under male doctors, women's limited professional opportunities), immigration (the immigrant communities that the Knick serves). The social context is not background; it shapes the medical practice.

Medical Innovation: The series depicts medical innovation in real time—new surgical techniques, new equipment, new understanding. Thackery and Edwards pioneer procedures that are experimental in 1900 and routine now. The innovation is shown as dangerous; patients die from experimental treatments. The risk is the point.

Cinematography
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.43

Steven Soderbergh: The director shot every episode, serving as his own cinematographer (under the pseudonym Peter Andrews) and editor (under the pseudonym Mary Ann Bernard). The approach is unusual for television—typically, directors rotate—but Soderbergh's complete control created visual consistency across both seasons. The direction is feature-quality; every frame is composed.

The Surgical Sequences: The series' signature is its surgical sequences—long takes with minimal editing, the camera observing as surgeons cut, suture, and improvise. The sequences are graphic (blood flows freely) and educational (we learn what surgery was in 1900). Soderbergh's camera doesn't flinch; he shows what would have been shown to contemporary observers. The effect is clinical without being exploitative.

Period Style: Soderbergh chose a contemporary visual approach despite the 1900 setting. The series uses natural light where possible, handheld camera for immediacy, and modern editing rhythms. The approach is anachronistic but effective; the period feels present rather than distant. The choice to forgo period-style filmmaking (no soft focus, no classical composition) makes the 1900 setting feel immediate.

Color and Light: Soderbergh's palette is controlled—greys, browns, the red of blood, the white of surgical gowns. The color is not nostalgic; it's clinical. The lighting is natural where possible; the hospital scenes feel lit by gas lamps even when electronically lit. The approach creates period atmosphere without period technique.

Comparative Assessment: True Detective Season 1 (10) established feature-quality television cinematography. The Knick matches its quality with a director (Soderbergh) who has greater feature-film experience than most television directors.

Why 9.5: Feature-quality cinematography from director Steven Soderbergh across every episode. The surgical sequences are television's most distinctive visual achievement. The contemporary approach to period setting creates immediacy.

Conceptual Density

Medical Progress: The series' central concept is medical progress—the transition from Victorian medicine (infection, bleeding, limited techniques) to modern practice. The progress is depicted as dangerous: patients die from experimental treatments; innovation is not safe. The series asks what we owe to medical pioneers (and their patients) who risked everything for progress.

Addiction: Thackery's addiction is the series' central character concept. He operates while high; his addiction fuels his innovation and threatens his life. The series depicts addiction as both disease and performance-enhancement; it's complicated rather than condemned. The addiction is based on historical surgeon William Halsted, who maintained a secret addiction while pioneering modern surgery.

Race and Medicine: Dr. Algernon Edwards is a Black surgeon in 1900 New York—a professional in a field that excludes him. The series depicts the racism he faces from colleagues, patients, and the medical establishment. The racism is not incidental; it shapes every aspect of his career. The series asks what talent means when the system excludes you.

Class and Medicine: The Knick serves poor immigrants who have no other options. The wealthy patients go to other hospitals; the Knick gets those who can't pay. The series depicts the class dimension of medical care—poor patients as teaching subjects, experimental treatments tried on those who can't refuse, the hospital's financial dependence on wealthy benefactors.

Comparative Assessment: Deadwood (9) achieved comparable conceptual depth. The Knick matches its examination of institutional dynamics with medical focus.

Why 8: Strong conceptual examination of medical progress, addiction, race, and class. The series depicts the costs of innovation. The limitation is that the cancellation prevented full development.
