What It Feels Like to Watch

Steven Soderbergh's The Knick is turn-of-the-century medicine as horror show. New York's Knickerbocker Hospital, 1900: surgery without antibiotics, addiction without treatment, progress without ethics. Clive Owen's Dr. John Thackery is a cocaine- and heroin-addicted surgeon, brilliant and self-destructive, pushing medical innovation through experimentation on unwilling subjects.

The show operates at the intersection of period drama and medical horror. The surgical sequences are graphic—blood spurts, patients awake during procedures, experimental techniques that we now recognize as barbaric. The period detail is exhaustive: the technology, the racism, the class hierarchy, the nascent ambulance services.

André Holland's Dr. Algernon Edwards is the Black surgeon excluded from white practice, improvising his own clinic in the basement. Eve Hewson's Lucy Elkins is the nurse with her own agenda. The supporting cast captures the hospital as ecosystem—nuns, administrators, ambulance drivers, patients.

Two seasons of Soderbergh's complete authorship (he directed and shot every episode), followed by an unfortunate cancellation. The third season that never happened haunts the show's legacy.
