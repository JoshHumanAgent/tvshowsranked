The Knick

2014 - 2015 • Cinemax • 20 Episodes

Score: 7.25
Medical Drama, Period

What It Feels Like

1900 New York. The Knickerbocker Hospital. Dr. John Thackery is a brilliant surgeon addicted to cocaine and heroin. He clashes with Dr. Algernon Edwards, a Black surgeon hired against the staff's wishes. Steven Soderbergh directed every episode. The birth of modern medicine, rendered in blood and addiction.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (10/10)

Clive Owen as Thackery: the addicted genius, charismatic and self-destructive. Andre Holland as Edwards: the Black surgeon fighting every prejudice. The ensemble (Eve Hewson, Juliet Rylance, Jeremy Bobb) creates the hospital. Soderbergh's direction extracts performances of intensity.

World Building (10/10)

1900 New York: the hospital, the tenements, the wealthy, the immigrants. The world is specific—this is the birth of surgery, when medicine became modern. Every detail is researched.

Cinematography (10/10)

Steven Soderbergh as cinematographer: the operating rooms, the streets, the lighting. The style is period with modern energy—electronic score, handheld camera. The visual language is distinctive.

Visual Spectacle (8/10)

The spectacle is surgery: visceral, bloody, primitive by modern standards. The operations are staged with unflinching realism. This is medicine as horror and wonder.

Conceptual Density (9/10)

Medical innovation, addiction, race in America, class at the turn of the century, what we sacrifice for progress, the cost of genius. The show explores these through Thackery and Edwards.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Thackery's addiction, Edwards's struggle, the hospital's challenges, the medical innovations. The structure is hospital drama—each episode brings new cases and crises. The drive is constant.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Two seasons. The second season ends with Thackery's fate ambiguous. A third season was planned but never made. The show is excellent but incomplete. The medical drama at its finest.
