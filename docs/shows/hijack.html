Hijack

2023 • Apple TV+ • 7 Episodes

Score: 7.20
Thriller, Drama

What It Feels Like

24 on an airplane. Idris Elba plays a corporate negotiator who applies his business skills to a hijacking, talking down terrorists and managing passenger panic across a seven-hour flight to London. Real-time thriller mechanics at their most distilled—one location, one continuous timeline, escalating stakes. The premise is simple. The execution is propulsive. You watch for Elba's contained charisma and the adrenaline of a situation where talking is the only weapon.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Idris Elba carries this. His Sam Nelson is competent without being superhuman, empathetic without being soft. The terrorists have enough dimension to feel like people with agendas, not cardboard villains. The passengers represent human chaos—some panic, some scheme, some break. Elba keeps everyone focused.

World Building (7/10)

The airplane is claustrophobic but fully realized—even the cargo hold becomes a location. The ground-side investigation (a suspicious suicide, bureaucratic friction) parallels the in-air tension. The show creates its tight world and stays within it.

Cinematography (7/10)

Airplane cinematography is hard—every frame looks like metal and plastic. Director Jim Field Smith finds tension in tight spaces, using movement and reaction shots to create energy. The ground-side sequences expand the frame briefly, reminding you what's at stake.

Visual Spectacle (5/10)

Minimal. This is people in seats talking, then moving to different seats. The spectacle is psychological—what's in the cargo hold? who's working with whom? The show doesn't need explosions because the premise provides all the danger.

Conceptual Density (6/10)

Negotiation as survival. Corporate skills applied to life-or-death. The psychology of hijackers versus passengers. It's thin but focused—the show knows its lane and doesn't pretend to be about more than survival.

Narrative Drive (9/10)

Real-time structure means every minute counts. Episodes end with genuine cliffhangers. The escalating hijacker demands and Sam's counter-moves create constant momentum. This is a show you watch in one sitting.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Complete miniseries with clear endpoint. The landing (literally and narratively) pays off the tension. Some elements feel convenient, but the emotional core resolves. Apple TV+ got exactly what they paid for—a tight, propulsive thriller that doesn't overstay.
