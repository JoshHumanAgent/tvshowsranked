What It Feels Like to Watch

Watchmen arrived as television's most ambitious superhero adaptation—not an adaptation at all, but a sequel to Alan Moore and Dave Gibbons' 1986 graphic novel that took the original's political concerns and updated them for 2019 America. Created by Damon Lindelof, the series opened with the 1921 Tulsa race massacre—a real historical event that most Americans didn't know—and proceeded to explore race, power, trauma, and masked vigilantism in ways that both honored and expanded its source material.

The experience of watching Watchmen is one of constant surprise. The series refused to be what viewers expected: it wasn't about the original characters (though they appeared), it wasn't a conventional superhero story (though people flew and punched through walls), it wasn't a simple allegory (though it was clearly about America). Regina King's Angela Abar, a Tulsa police detective who dons a mask as Sister Night, anchored a story that moved between 1921, 1985, and 2019, between personal trauma and generational memory, between genre entertainment and genuine historical reckoning.

The series' great achievement was making political content inseparable from narrative pleasure. The Tulsa massacre wasn't backdrop—it was foundational. Angela's grandfather, Will Reeves, witnessed the massacre as a child and became the first Black masked vigilante; his trauma shaped Angela's life without her knowing. The white supremacist group Seventh Kavalry (descendants of KKK) used Rorschach masks as insignia; the original graphic novel's fascist-adjacent vigilante had become a real-world hate symbol. The show took superhero symbols seriously as political symbols.

Jean Smart's Laurie Blake (formerly the Silk Spectre) provided cynical commentary; Jeremy Irons' Adrian Veidt (Ozymandias) offered surreal interludes on Europa; Yahya Abdul-Mateen II's Dr. Manhattan revealed himself in ways the show had been hiding. The narrative complexity served the political content—the audience's disorientation paralleled the characters' reckonings with history they hadn't known.

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.60

Angela Abar: Regina King's Angela is the series' center—a Tulsa police detective who wears a nun's habit as her vigilante costume, whose toughness masks inherited trauma, whose investigation into her grandfather's past becomes an investigation into her own identity. King plays Angela with physical precision (her walk, her stillness, her violence) and emotional complexity (her marriage, her anger, her developing understanding).

Will Reeves: Louis Gossett Jr. and Jovan Adepo (young Will) created the character whose century-spanning life connected the 1921 massacre to present-day events. The character's journey from traumatized child to masked vigilante to elderly man withholding secrets gave the series its generational scope.

The Extended Cast: Jean Smart's Laurie Blake provided wry commentary as the former Silk Spectre now working for the FBI; Jeremy Irons' Adrian Veidt offered surreal comic relief as the world's smartest man imprisoned on Europa; Yahya Abdul-Mateen II revealed layers as Cal/Dr. Manhattan. Each performance contributed to the series' density.

World Building
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.20

The Altered Present: Watchmen built a world where the 1985 squid attack on New York (from the graphic novel) changed history—Robert Redford became president for decades, squids rain occasionally from the sky, Dr. Manhattan is publicly believed to be on Mars. The show's 2019 is both recognizable and altered: smartphones exist but people pay with coins, the internet exists but is monitored differently, Tulsa has reparations for massacre descendants.

The Historical Foundation: The 1921 Tulsa race massacre is the series' historical anchor—the real event where white mobs destroyed the prosperous Black neighborhood of Greenwood, killing hundreds. The series doesn't fictionalize this; it dramatizes it, making television viewers aware of history they'd been denied. The world built from this foundation is American history as it happened, plus superhero elements layered on top.

Comparative Assessment: The graphic novel (10 for world-building) established this universe. The series extends it with historical specificity.

Why 8: Exceptional world-building that combines alternate history with real history. The Tulsa foundation gives the politics authenticity.

Visual Language: Watchmen's visual style combined superhero imagery with historical realism—the masks and costumes existed alongside period-accurate 1921 Tulsa and present-day Oklahoma. The cinematography served the serious content; it didn't stylize away from the massacre's horror or toward superhero gloss.

The Yellow Masks: The Seventh Kavalry's Rorschach masks became the series' most disturbing visual element—white supremacists wearing the face of the original comic's fascist-adjacent vigilante. The imagery was politically pointed: symbols aren't neutral, and the show understood how appropriation works.

Comparative Assessment: The Boys (7) uses superhero imagery for satire. Watchmen achieves more sophisticated visual-political integration.

Why 7.5: Strong visual craft that serves both superhero elements and historical content. The imagery carries political weight.

Conceptual Density
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

Race in America: Watchmen's central subject was American racial history—slavery's legacy, the Tulsa massacre as representative trauma, reparations as political idea, the way symbols get appropriated by hate movements. The series made these concepts narrative rather than lecture; Angela's investigation was a mechanism for exploring history.

Vigilantism and Power: The original Watchmen examined who gets to be a vigilante and what that says about power. The series extended this: Will Reeves became the first Black vigilante because Black people in 1930s America couldn't rely on police protection. The Seventh Kavalry wore Rorschach masks because Rorschach's journal had become white supremacist scripture. Masks and power remained the subject.

Trauma Across Generations: The series explored how trauma transfers across generations—Will's witness of the massacre shaped his life, his absence shaped Angela's life, their reunion shaped her understanding. The superhero elements served generational trauma rather than replacing it.

Comparative Assessment: The original graphic novel (10 for conceptual density) remains the gold standard. The series matches that ambition with different subjects.

Why 9: Exceptional conceptual depth about race, power, and generational trauma. The series integrates political content with superhero narrative seamlessly.

Narrative Drive
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.05

The Mystery Structure: Watchmen employed mystery-narrative—who is Senator Keene, what is the Seventh Kavalry planning, what does Will know about Angela's past? The questions provided momentum across nine episodes. The structure worked; viewers wanted answers.

The Complexity: The series' narrative complexity—multiple time periods, hidden identities, superhero revelation—sometimes confused viewers less invested in close watching. The show required attention. For some, this was feature; for others, bug.

Comparative Assessment: Lost (7.5) achieved similar complexity with more episodes. Watchmen packs complexity into limited time.

Why 7: Moderate narrative drive through mystery structure. The complexity serves the ambitions but may challenge casual viewers.

Narrative Resolution
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.80

The Finale: The series finale provided resolution—Angela's confrontation with the Seventh Kavalry, her discovery of Dr. Manhattan's identity, her inheritance of his powers, her walk across water in the final image. The ending was both complete and open to continuation (though the creators have indicated it stands as finished).

The Political Ending: The resolution wasn't just narrative; it was political. Angela's inheritance of power meant a Black woman now had Dr. Manhattan's abilities. Will's survival and Angela's understanding meant generational trauma had been acknowledged. The Tulsa massacre had been witnessed by viewers who hadn't known.

Comparative Assessment: The Leftovers (8.5) achieved similar existential resolution. Watchmen matches that completion with more political content.

Why 8: Strong resolution that provides narrative closure and political completion. The ending honors the series' ambitions.
