What It Feels Like to Watch

Watching Damon Lindelof's Watchmen is like stepping into a fever dream where American history bleeds into superhero mythology, where the trauma of Tulsa 1921 echoes through the streets of 2019 Oklahoma, and where masks become both armor and prison. This is not comfortable television—it's confrontational, ambitious, and demands your full attention from the opening frame.

The series operates on multiple frequencies simultaneously. On one level, it's a police procedural wrapped in superhero trappings. On another, it's a pointed examination of white supremacy's institutional persistence. On yet another, it's a heartbreaking love story spanning decades and dimensions. Lindelof doesn't ease you in; he throws you into a world where vigilantes are illegal, where Robert Redford has been president for decades, and where a vast right-wing conspiracy wearing Rorschach masks threatens to unravel what little social fabric remains.

"This Extraordinary Being"

The episode that breaks the mold and breaks your heart. Episode 6's black-and-white descent into the origin of Hooded Justice is not just the best episode of this series—it's some of the finest television ever produced. It recontextualizes everything you thought you knew about heroism, identity, and the masks we wear to survive.

There's a quality to the viewing experience that mirrors the show's own preoccupation with time. The narrative fractures and reconstitutes itself. Characters you meet in the present carry histories that stretch back to the original graphic novel's events, and the show trusts you to piece together the chronology while simultaneously processing its weighty thematic concerns. Episode 6, "This Extraordinary Being," stands as the crown jewel—a nearly standalone hour that uses black-and-white cinematography to devastating effect as it reveals the true origin of the first masked vigilante, connecting the superhero myth directly to the brutal reality of American racial violence.

Sister Night—Angela Abar as played by Regina King—serves as our anchor through this labyrinth. Her performance carries the weight of the series' existential interrogations. When she asks "Who are you?" it's never just a question of identity; it's a challenge to the entire superhero enterprise. The show makes you complicit in the surveillance state it depicts; the cold open of Episode 1 alone—with its re-enactment of the Tulsa Race Massacre—is designed to shock viewers into recognizing how thoroughly American history has sanitized its own violence.

The lingering sensation after finishing Watchmen is not satisfaction but something more complex—a mixture of awe at the technical accomplishment and unease at the mirror it holds up to America's unresolved sins. This is television as cultural excavation, as speculative warning, as pure cinematic artistry. You don't just watch it; you absorb it, wrestle with it, and find yourself thinking about it weeks later when you see a mask on the news or hear a coded phrase about "greatness."

CHARACTERS

Characters

The character work in Watchmen operates on multiple temporal planes simultaneously. Regina King's Angela Abar/Sister Night anchors the series with a performance of remarkable physical and emotional precision. She embodies a character whose entire identity is performative—cop by day, masked vigilante by night, descendent of trauma survivors carrying generational weight. King makes Angela's contradictions feel organic rather than contrived; her confusion, rage, and eventual acceptance flow with devastating authenticity.

Jeremy Irons as Adrian Veidt spends much of the series isolated in what appears to be a bizarre pastoral prison, delivering theatrical monologues to clone servants. It's a risky gambit that shouldn't work but absolutely does—his grandiose delivery masks a character reckoning with the consequences of his godlike hubris. The slow revelation of his circumstances provides some of the season's most darkly comic and ultimately tragic moments.

Jean Smart's Laurie Blake—formerly Silk Spectre II—carries the entire weight of the original graphic novel's legacy. Her cynical, world-weary demeanor conceals deep wounds from her relationship with Doctor Manhattan, and Smart threads this needle with exacting precision. Her monologue via phone booth to Mars ranks among the show's most affecting scenes.

The supporting cast shines with similar specificity: Tim Blake Nelson's Looking Glass exists as a walking commentary on trauma and conspiracy; his mirror mask isn't just costume but psychological necessity. Yahya Abdul-Mateen II's Cal Abar undergoes one of television's most audacious character reveals, and his performance as the series progresses adds layers of melancholy cosmic awareness. Even minor characters like Hong Chau's Lady Trieu and Louis Gossett Jr.'s Will Reeves carry the weight of entire histories within brief screen time.

The series' character work falters slightly in its handling of certain antagonists—the Seventh Kavalry sometimes feel more like thematic devices than fully realized threats, though this may be intentional commentary on the banality of white supremacist ideology. Nevertheless, the central ensemble ranks among television's most complex explorations of identity, inheritance, and the performance of self.

WORLD BUILDING

World Building

Lindelof constructs an alternate America that feels simultaneously fantastical and uncomfortably plausible. The premise—that Robert Redford has served seven terms as president following Nixon, that reparations were paid to victims of racial violence ("Redfordations"), that Vietnam became the 51st state—creates a backdrop that invites both wish fulfillment and critical examination. This is world building as political argument, not mere escapism.

Tulsa, Oklahoma emerges as more than setting; it's a character unto itself, burdened with historical trauma that the series refuses to let remain buried. The show's commitment to filming on location grounds its supernatural elements in brick-and-mortar reality. The police wear masks to protect their identities from reprisals; this simple inversion of superhero tropes creates an entire taxonomy of visible and invisible power.

The series' integration of the original graphic novel's lore rewards familiar viewers without alienating newcomers. The squid rain falling from Kansas skies, the continued existence of Doctor Manhattan on Mars, the cultural status of "Oklahoma!" as metatextual commentary—all these elements weave together a coherent alternate history that interrogates our own timeline's failures.

However, the world building occasionally prioritizes concept over clarity. Certain rules about how this universe operates—particularly regarding technology, international relations, and the precise status of "costumed adventurers"—remain fuzzy around the edges. The series trusts its audience to fill gaps, which can occasionally leave viewers feeling untethered. The pacing of revelation sometimes withholds information past the point of frustration, though this mirrors Angela's own epistemological journey.

What elevates the world building is its thematic coherence. Every element connects back to questions of power, identity, and historical accountability. The Millennium Clock isn't just a plot device; it's a visual metaphor for time and trauma. The egg motif carries through from the opening credits to the finale's most crucial moment. This is construction with purpose, every brick laid to support the edifice of meaning.

CINEMATOGRAPHY

Cinematography

The visual language of Watchmen announces itself immediately. The series employs a palette of deep shadows and saturated neon that evokes noir tradition while updating it for the 21st century. Director Nicole Kassell and cinematographer Xavier Grobet establish a visual grammar that shifts between the harsh realities of Tulsa streets and the hyper-stylized world of costumed confrontation.

Episode 6, "This Extraordinary Being," stands as the cinematographic centerpiece. Shot predominantly in black and white with moments of selective color, it transforms the trauma of the Tulsa Race Massacre and its aftermath into visual poetry. The decision to desaturate Hooded Justice's origin story removes the distraction of period detail while amplifying the emotional horror. The use of archival footage integrated with stylized re-enactment creates a documentary-adjacent authenticity that makes the fantastical elements feel earned.

The series demonstrates remarkable visual consistency across multiple directors while allowing each episode distinct formal approaches. Episode 5's sitcom-inflected sequences depicting Doctor Manhattan's temporal experience employ aspect ratio shifts and color grading changes that externalize his nonlinear perception. Episode 8's extended conversation on Mars uses the desolate landscape as backdrop for intimate emotional revelation.

The integration of practical effects and digital enhancement serves the story rather than overwhelming it. Doctor Manhattan's blue radiance feels tangible rather than artificial; the squid attacks carry visceral weight. The production design of the various masked characters' costumes—from Sister Night's practical tactical gear to Looking Glass' uncanny mirror mask—merges functionality with symbolic weight.

Some visual choices court opacity over clarity; the rapid cutting and disorienting angles of certain action sequences occasionally obscure spatial relationships. And the Veidt sequences' deliberate artificiality, while thematically appropriate, sometimes drift toward visual tedium. Nevertheless, Watchmen represents some of the most technically assured small-screen cinematography of its era, treating television with the visual ambition typically reserved for cinema.

VISUAL SPECTACLE

Visual Spectacle

For a property rooted in superhero tradition, Watchmen demonstrates admirable restraint in its deployment of spectacle. When the show goes big, it earns every moment of visual wonder through careful narrative buildup. The Millennium Clock's activation, the Mars sequences' cosmic scope, and the finale's temporal fireworks all arrive with appropriate weight rather than numbing frequency.

The series understands that true spectacle resides in performance. Regina King's physical transformation into Sister Night—her combat prowess, her silhouette against the Tulsa skyline—creates iconic imagery without excessive CGI. The show's most visually arresting moments often involve simple compositions: Angela in her nun's habit, mask pulled down; Looking Glass' reflection staring back at himself; Doctor Manhattan's blue form standing motionless against the red Martian landscape.

Episode 8, "A God Walks into Abar," delivers the season's purest spectacle in its depiction of Doctor Manhattan's temporal existence. The visual representation of his simultaneous awareness—multiple versions of himself interacting across time—could have devolved into effects-heavy confusion, but instead achieves genuine awe through elegant simplicity. The bar sequence where he meets Angela for the first time (and simultaneously the last time) uses visual storytelling to achieve emotional effects that dialogue could not.

The squid rain, inherited from the graphic novel's apocalyptic conclusion, manifests as periodic environmental catastrophe with appropriately Lovecraftian horror. These moments ground the series' alternate history in visceral unpleasantness; this is spectacle as trauma, not entertainment. Similarly, the re-enactment of the Tulsa Race Massacre in the pilot refuses to aestheticize violence, instead using the full resources of HBO production to convey historical truth.

The visual effects occasionally show their budget constraints in wider shots or complex sequences. Some of the more ambitious set pieces—the climax of Veidt's storyline, certain mass crowd scenes—lack the polish of the series' intimate moments. The show wisely prioritizes character drama over empty scale, but viewers seeking the building-smashing destruction of traditional superhero fare may find the spectacle too cerebral for their expectations.

CONCEPTUAL DENSITY

Conceptual Density

Watchmen operates at the intersection of multiple intellectual traditions: superhero mythology, African American history, quantum physics, and institutional critique. The series wears its influences openly—James Baldwin quotes sit alongside references to Ozymandias, the Tulsa Race Massacre shares narrative space with Doctor Manhattan's Martian exile. This density rewards attentive viewing while potentially overwhelming casual consumption.

The central conceit—what if the first masked vigilante was a Black man surviving the Tulsa Race Massacre?—recontextualizes the entire superhero genre through the lens of racialized violence. Hooded Justice's noose becomes symbol of both execution and resistance, the mask simultaneously protective and traumatized. This is superhero scholarship at its most incisive, using alternate history to interrogate the genre's implicit assumptions about justice and power.

The show's temporal mechanics extend beyond Doctor Manhattan's nonlinear perception into the series' own structure. Episode 6's flashback structure, Episode 8's simultaneous past/present/future, and the finale's causality loops create a narrative that must be assembled rather than simply consumed. The audience becomes detective, piecing together chronology alongside characters.

However, this density occasionally crosses into didacticism. Certain thematic connections—between masks and racial passing, between superhero vigilantism and police power—are stated explicitly rather than allowed to emerge organically. The series' engagement with white supremacy, while necessary and welcome, sometimes reduces complex systemic analysis to more manageable (if less nuanced) conspiracy plotting.

The metatextual layering adds further density: the fictional "American Hero Story" show-within-a-show, the references to Moore and Gibbons' original, the integration of historical documentation. Some viewers will find this richness exhilarating; others may experience it as homework. Watchmen makes few concessions to accessibility, trusting its audience to keep pace with its intellectual ambitions. For those willing to engage, it offers rewards rare in superhero storytelling.

NARRATIVE DRIVE

Narrative Drive

The propulsive energy of Watchmen operates in distinct phases, each with varying degrees of urgency. The pilot establishes immediate stakes with Chief Crawford's murder and the Seventh Kavalry threat, creating conventional procedural momentum. However, Lindelof quickly complicates this forward motion with nonlinear storytelling, withholding crucial information to create mystery rather than merely advancing plot.

The middle episodes—particularly 4 through 6—represent the narrative high point. As Angela ingests her grandfather's Nostalgia pills, the series adopts her fragmented consciousness, forcing viewers to assemble chronology from memory shards. This formal experimentation sacrifices some narrative clarity for thematic resonance, but the urgency of Will Reeves' backstory provides sufficient momentum to carry through.

The pacing decisions reflect Lindelof's established preferences from Lost and The Leftovers. Mysteries accumulate faster than they resolve; questions beget deeper questions. While this creates sustained engagement, it occasionally frustrates viewers seeking concrete advancement. The series trusts that its thematic weight and character work will maintain interest even when plot machinery slows.

The Veidt storyline, while thematically essential to the series' conclusion, operates on a different temporal register than the Tulsa narrative. Its deliberate weirdness and apparent irrelevance to the main plot creates structural drag until the final episodes' convergence. Similarly, certain character decisions—particularly Angela's choice to take her grandfather's memories—serve thematic rather than plot purposes, potentially testing audience patience.

Episode 8's extended conversation between Angela and Doctor Manhattan essentially pauses the main narrative for an hour of philosophical and romantic exposition. It's audacious television that depends entirely on viewer investment in the characters; those seeking action or plot advancement will find themselves frustrated. The finale must then compress significant exposition while delivering emotional payoff, occasionally feeling rushed after the deliberate pacing of preceding hours.

RESOLUTION

Resolution

The finale, "See How They Fly," must accomplish multiple objectives: resolve the Seventh Kavalry threat, conclude Veidt's imprisonment, and provide emotional closure for Angela's arc. It succeeds more thoroughly on some fronts than others, delivering a conclusion that balances cosmic spectacle with intimate character moments while leaving certain questions deliberately unanswered.

The defeat of the Seventh Kavalry and Senator Keene's transformation plan resolves the season's immediate threat while commenting on the appropriation of power. Lady Trieu's intervention—her own bid for godhood—adds complexity to what could have been simple hero/villain confrontation. That Trieu and Angela represent different paths through intergenerational trauma provides thematic coherence to the action.

Veidt's resolution provides some of the finale's most satisfying moments. His return to Earth, facilitated by Laurie and Wade, brings his narrative full circle while denying him the satisfaction of his grand design. The capture of him by the authorities lacks some of the punch viewers might expect, but his final line—"I never said I was a hero"—perfectly encapsulates the series' interrogation of vigilantism.

The true resolution concerns Angela and the egg. The finale's final scene, with Angela stepping onto the pool to determine if she has inherited Doctor Manhattan's powers, provides a perfect ambiguous conclusion. We don't see whether she sinks or walks on water; we don't need to. The point is not the acquisition of power but the choice to reach for it, to accept the burden of possibility. It's an ending that honors the series' commitment to questions over answers.

Certain plot threads—Looking Glass's survival and transformation, the fate of the police department without its leadership, the broader international implications of Doctor Manhattan's death—receive less attention than they warrant. The series appears more interested in Angela's personal journey than in fully resolving its institutional portraits. This narrowing of focus feels appropriate to the story being told but may leave viewers wanting more exploration of the world Lindelof created.

The Verdict

Watchmen (2019) stands as one of the most formally adventurous and thematically rigorous works in superhero television history. Damon Lindelof and his team have created something genuinely sui generis—a series that uses the vocabulary of costumed adventure to interrogate American mythology, racial violence, and the very concept of heroism. It is never easy viewing; it demands attention, engagement, and emotional availability. But for viewers willing to meet it on its terms, it offers rewards rarely found in genre television: genuine surprise, profound thematic weight, and performances that will resonate long after the credits roll.

The series' legacy may ultimately rest on its boldness. In an era of superhero saturation, Watchmen dared to ask whether the genre could bear the weight of real history, real trauma, and real consequence. The answer, delivered over nine extraordinary hours, is a resounding yes—followed immediately by the more complicated question of what responsibilities come with such power.
