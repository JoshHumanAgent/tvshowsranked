What It Feels Like to Watch

The Pacific arrived as the companion to Band of Brothers—a 10-part HBO miniseries following the Marines who fought the Pacific theater of World War II, from Guadalcanal to Iwo Jima to Okinawa. Produced by Tom Hanks and Steven Spielberg, it adopted the same approach as its predecessor: adapting historical accounts, using real veterans' stories, and depicting combat with unprecedented intensity. But where Band of Brothers followed a single company through Europe, The Pacific followed three individuals through a campaign that was longer, more brutal, and more psychologically devastating.

The experience of watching The Pacific is one of sustained horror. The Pacific theater produced combat that was different from Europe: amphibious landings on beaches swept by machine guns, jungle warfare where enemies were invisible until they were fatal, islands where surrender was impossible and prisoners were nonexistent. The series captures the particular brutality of fighting an enemy who fought to the death, in conditions that rotted uniforms and spirits alike.

The three central figures—Robert Leckie (James Badge Dale), Eugene Sledge (Joseph Mazzello), and John Basilone (Jon Seda)—represent different experiences. Leckie was a writer whose memoir became source material; his character is the thinking Marine, the one who processes through words. Sledge was a student whose memoir "With the Old Breed" is considered one of the finest infantry accounts ever written; his character is the innocent who loses his innocence utterly. Basilone was the Medal of Honor winner who returned to combat and died; his character is the hero who cannot stop being heroic even when he knows it will kill him.

What distinguishes The Pacific from Band of Brothers is its attention to aftermath. The final hours devoted significant screen time to homecoming—the difficulty of returning to normal life, the permanent psychological damage, the inability to explain what had happened to those who hadn't been there. The series understood that war's violence continues after the shooting stops.
