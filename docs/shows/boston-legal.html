What It Feels Like to Watch

Boston Legal is what happens when courtroom drama gets an infusion of theatrical insanity. William Shatner's Denny Crane is a legal legend losing his mind while still winning cases—a character that shouldn't work but becomes the show's beating heart. James Spader's Alan Shore is his perfect foil: ethically flexible, verbally devastating, incapable of backing down from a fight.

The show operates on a spectrum from absurdist comedy to genuine legal thriller, sometimes within the same scene. One minute Denny is shooting a client's ex-husband's car; the next, Alan is delivering a closing argument that makes you want to stand up and cheer. The tonal whiplash is the point—this is television aware of its own theatricality.

At its core, this is a show about two men who find each other late in life and become best friends. Their balcony scenes, sharing a drink and discussing politics, philosophy, and their own mortality, are some of the most oddly touching moments in TV history. Denny Crane. Mad cow. But still here.

Characters & Acting (8.5): Shatner and Spader are an all-time great duo. The supporting cast—Candice Bergen, Rene Auberjonois, Julie Bowen—elevate every scene. This is ensemble comedy-drama at its finest.

Narrative Drive (8): Cases of the week merge with character arcs seamlessly. The show never drags because there's always a new argument to win, a new absurdity to navigate.

Resolution (7): The finale is genuinely moving—Denny and Alan getting married for tax/pension purposes, two friends facing the end together. It's perfect for the show's sensibility.

Key Episodes

"Let Sales Ring" (S1E1) — The pilot that establishes the Denny/Alan dynamic immediately. Shatner's entrance alone is worth the price of admission.

"Schadenfreude" (S2E11) — Alan Shore versus the Supreme Court. One of television's great legal speeches.

"Last Call" (S5E13) — The finale. Denny and Alan on the balcony, then married. A perfect sendoff.
