What It Feels Like to Watch

Interview with the Vampire arrived as television's most sumptuous vampire drama—AMC's adaptation of Anne Rice's 1976 novel that transformed the source material's gothic romance into something queerer, more explicitly political, and more visually daring than the 1994 film. Set across Revolutionary-era New Orleans, 1910s Storyville, and present-day Dubai, the series followed Louis de Pointe du Lac (Jacob Anderson) recounting his centuries-long relationship with the vampire Lestat de Lioncourt (Sam Reid) to journalist Daniel Molloy (Eric Bogosian).

The experience of watching Interview with the Vampire is one of intoxicating excess. The series embraces what vampire fiction always implied: that immortality is both gift and curse, that eroticism and violence are inseparable, that beauty and horror coexist. Jacob Anderson's Louis is a Creole brothel owner who becomes a Black man with immortal power in a society structured against him; Sam Reid's Lestat is charm and cruelty intertwined; Bailey Bass (S1) and Delainey Hayles (S2) as Claudia capture the rage of eternal childhood in a world that dismisses children.

What distinguishes this adaptation is its explicitness about what the novel and film coded. Louis and Lestat's relationship is unapologetically romantic and sexual; the racial dynamics of a Black vampire in white society are explored directly; Claudia's stunted development produces genuine rage rather than merely gothic pathos. The series updates Rice's vision for contemporary sensibilities without abandoning its essential gothic romanticism.

The present-day framing device—Louis recounting his story to Daniel Molloy in Dubai—adds layers the novel didn't have. Daniel's questions challenge Louis's reliability; the storytelling itself becomes a subject. Louis is beautiful, wealthy, and obviously troubled; the interview is therapy as much as journalism. The series understands that stories are told from perspectives, and perspectives have reasons.

The Gothic Philosophy: Anne Rice's vampire fiction has always been philosophical—immortality as curse, the burden of eternal life, the relationship between beauty and monstrosity. The series embraces these themes: Louis's struggle with his nature, Lestat's embrace of amorality, Claudia's rebellion against her makers. His transformation changes some dynamics but not others; he remains subject to white society's structures even with supernatural ability. The racial politics aren't lecture; they're narrative.

Queer Romance: Louis and Lestat's relationship is explicitly romantic and sexual in a way the novel coded and the film closeted. The series treats their love seriously—the attraction, the conflict, the codependency—as a marriage that spans centuries and survives everything including attempted murder.

Comparative Assessment: True Blood (7.5) explored similar themes with camp emphasis. Interview achieves more philosophical depth with explicit politics.
