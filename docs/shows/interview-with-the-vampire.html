What It Feels Like to Watch

AMC's Interview with the Vampire reimagines Anne Rice's novel for television, and somehow it's better than the book. Louis de Pointe du Lac (Jacob Anderson) tells his story to a journalist—again—but this time Louis is Black, his story set in Storyville, New Orleans' red-light district. The change isn't cosmetic. It deepens every frame.

Lestat de Lioncourt (Sam Reid) is still the charismatic monster who turns Louis, offering eternal life and demanding eternal companionship. Their relationship is passionate, toxic, codependent. They love each other. They destroy each other. They can't leave each other. It's a gothic romance about what happens when two predators try to be partners.

Claudia (Bailey Bass/Delainey Hayles) is their daughter, turned as a teenager and trapped forever in a child's body—though the show ages her physically more than the book did. Her rage at what Lestat did, at what Louis allowed, fuels seasons of tension. She wants the life she never got to have. She hates her makers for making her.

The show is visually sumptuous—New Orleans at its most decadent, European travels, elaborate costumes, blood as art. But the heart is the performances. Jacob Anderson's Louis is refined, wounded, articulate about his guilt. Sam Reid's Lestat is magnetic, terrifying, pathetic. Their chemistry is the show's engine.

This is vampire fiction that takes immortality seriously—the loneliness, the boredom, the accumulated loss. Everyone you love dies. Everyone you hate dies. You remain. The show is about what centuries do to love.
