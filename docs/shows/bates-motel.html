What It Feels Like to Watch

Bates Motel is the Psycho prequel that shouldn't work but does. It's a tragedy—we know Norman becomes Anthony Perkins' iconic killer—but the journey there is riveting. Freddie Highmore plays teenage Norman, and Vera Farmiga is Norma, his obsessive, damaged mother. Together they build something horrifying and weirdly heartbreaking.

The show reinvents White Pine Bay, Oregon as a town of secrets. Drug trafficking, sex trafficking, corrupt sheriffs—it's Twin Peaks by way of Hitchcock. The mystery-of-the-season format keeps things moving while the central tragedy unfolds: Norman's dissociative episodes, Norma's denial, and the inevitable collision with Psycho's events.

Farmiga deserved every award for her performance. She makes Norma sympathetic despite every terrible choice. Her love for Norman is genuine and poisonous. The final season merges with Psycho, and the result is haunting.

Characters & Acting (9): Farmiga and Highmore are perfect. She plays Norma as desperately loving; he plays Norman as fragile and increasingly fractured. The Oedipal tension is thick and disturbing.

Narrative Drive (7.5): The show kept inventing subplots to delay the inevitable. The drug trade in White Pine Bay kept things busy but sometimes distracted from the central tragedy.

Resolution (7): The finale merges with Psycho seamlessly. We see Norman become the man from the film. It's horrifying and earned.
