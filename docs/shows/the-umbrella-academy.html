What It Feels Like to Watch

The Umbrella Academy begins with forty-three women giving birth simultaneously despite not being pregnant when the day began. Seven of these miraculous children are adopted by Sir Reginald Hargreeves, an eccentric billionaire who trains them to be superheroes. The show picks up decades later, when the estranged siblings reunite for their father's funeral and discover that the apocalypse is coming in eight days—and one of them might cause it.

The siblings are dysfunctional, damaged adults. Number One/Luther (Tom Hopper) is the earnest leader who spent years on the moon for no reason. Number Two/Diego (David Castañeda) is a vigilante with a hero complex. Number Three/Allison (Emmy Raver-Lampman) can rearrange reality with rumors but has abused her power. Number Four/Klaus (Robert Sheehan) is a drug addict who can see the dead. Number Five (Aidan Gallagher) is a time-traveling assassin stuck in his teenage body. Number Six/Ben is dead, a ghost only Klaus can see. Number Seven/Vanya (Ellen Page, later Elliot Page) was told she had no powers—a lie with catastrophic consequences.

The show is superhero deconstruction through family drama. These aren't heroes—they're traumatized adults who were raised as weapons by a cold, manipulative father. Their powers are as much curse as gift. Klaus's ability to see the dead drove him to addiction. Five's time travel trapped him alone in a post-apocalyptic wasteland. Vanya's suppressed powers explode when she finally learns the truth.

The tone is wildly eclectic—dark comedy, family drama, superhero action, time travel puzzle. The soundtrack is excellent, featuring covers of pop songs in unexpected arrangements. Each season has a different apocalypse to prevent, and the siblings keep failing and resetting, trying to find a timeline where they can just be a family.

World Building (8): Time travel, multiple timelines, the Commission, the Sparrow Academy—the show builds a coherent multiverse mythology.
