What It Feels Like to Watch

House of Cards is political thriller as Shakespearean tragedy—Frank Underwood (Kevin Spacey), a South Carolina congressman, denied Secretary of State, determines to destroy everyone in his path to power. "Democracy is so overrated," he declares, breaking the fourth wall. He's right: the show's thesis is that power corrupts absolutely, and those who seek it are already corrupted. Claire Underwood (Robin Wright), his wife, is partner and eventually rival—Lady Macbeth as presidential contender.

Seasons one and two are the peak: Frank's manipulation, betrayal, murder of Zoe Barnes (Kate Mara), ascent to Vice President, then President. The political maneuvering is elegant—leveraging scandals, manufacturing crises, always three moves ahead. "I love that woman," Frank says of Claire. "I love her more than sharks love blood." They're predators in suits. Washington is their ocean. The supporting cast: Doug Stamper (Michael Kelly), Frank's fixer, alcoholic and ruthless; Remy Danton (Mahershala Ali), the lobbyist; Freddy (Reggie E. Cathey), the ribs joint owner—Frank's only genuine friend.

Post-season two, the show struggled: plot contrivances, character inconsistencies, declining relevance as real American politics outpaced fiction. Season five's election subplot paralleled 2016; reality made the show seem tame. Then Spacey's sexual misconduct allegations ended his career; the show ended with season six, Claire as President, Frank dead offscreen. The finale was hurried, unsatisfying—but seasons one and two remain essential.

Six seasons, seventy-three episodes, Netflix's first original series (with Lilyhammer, released same day). House of Cards proved streaming could produce prestige; its success launched Netflix's original content empire. Kevin Spacey's fall mars the legacy, but Robin Wright's Claire is the show's secret weapon— icier, smarter, ultimately more ruthless than Frank. "My turn," she says in the final shot. She earned it. He didn't.
