What It Feels Like to Watch

House of Cards was Netflix's first original series, and it changed television. Kevin Spacey plays Frank Underwood, a South Carolina congressman who, after being denied appointment as Secretary of State, decides to destroy everyone who slighted him. He breaks the fourth wall constantly, speaking directly to viewers, explaining his machinations. "Power is a lot like real estate," he says. "It's all about location, location, location."

Robin Wright plays Claire Underwood, Frank's wife, and she's the show's secret weapon. The first two seasons suggest she's Frank's partner; the later seasons reveal she's his superior. Frank is a man who will do anything for power. Claire is a woman who will do anything and make it look like virtue. Wright won aGolden Globe for the role, and she earned it—cold, precise, lethal.

The show's strength is its theatricality. Frank murders a dog in the first scene—he leans down to help it, then strangles it. "There are two kinds of pain," he says. "The sort of pain that makes you strong, or useless pain." The show is full of such moments: Frank pushing a reporter in front of a train, Claire smiling during betrayals, the Machiavellian plotting laid bare. It's melodrama for political junkies.

Six seasons, seventy-three episodes. The show collapsed when Kevin Spacey was credibly accused of sexual misconduct and fired. Season six attempted to continue with Claire as president, but the magic was gone. The early seasons remain—a fascinating document of the streaming revolution and a showcase for Robin Wright. "I'm not going to let you destroy everything we've built," Claire tells Frank. She doesn't. She destroys him instead.
