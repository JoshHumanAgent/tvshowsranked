What It Feels Like to Watch

House of Cards launched the streaming era. Netflix's first major original series, based on a British show, starring Kevin Spacey as Frank Underwood—a Southern congressman passed over for Secretary of State who decides to burn Washington to the ground and rebuild it with himself on top. He breaks the fourth wall constantly, talking directly to the audience, explaining his machinations with a charming sociopathy that makes us complicit. We're his co-conspirators.

The first two seasons are thrilling. Frank and his wife Claire (Robin Wright) are a political power couple with no principles beyond ambition. They destroy enemies, manipulate allies, and climb the ladder. The show is Shakespearean—Frank quotes Macbeth, Richard III, Julius Caesar. He's a tragic hero with no tragic flaw, because his flaw (ruthless ambition) is his strength. The cinematography is cold Washington blue, all marble corridors and fluorescent lights. The direction (David Fincher for the first two episodes) sets a tone of clinical dread.

Seasons three through five are diminishing returns. The plotting becomes convoluted, the stakes apocalyptic, the tone self-serious. Frank becomes president through murder and manipulation, but the show can't sustain the tension. Claire emerges as a rival, then a partner, then something stranger—the show tries to pivot toward female power, but the writing isn't sharp enough to pull it off.

Then Kevin Spacey was accused of sexual assault, and everything collapsed. Season six was filmed without him—Frank dies off-screen, Claire becomes president, and the show tries to reinvent itself in its final hours. It doesn't work. But the first two seasons remain influential: they proved that streaming services could create prestige television, that releasing entire seasons at once wouldn't destroy cultural conversation, and that antiheroes could thrive in the post-Sopranos era. House of Cards is flawed, sometimes terribly so, but it changed television forever.
