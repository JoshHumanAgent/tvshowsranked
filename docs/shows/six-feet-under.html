What It Feels Like to Watch

Six Feet Under is about a family that runs a funeral home—and about death, which they seeevery day. Nathaniel Fisher Sr. (Richard Jenkins) dies in the pilot; his widow Ruth (Frances Conroy) and children Nate (Peter Krause), David (Michael C. Hall), and Claire (Lauren Ambrose) inherit the business and the grief. Each episode opens with a death: some expected, some absurd, all inevitable. "Everything ends," Nate says. The show's thesis: accepting mortality is the only way to live.

The Fisher family is dysfunctional. Nate, who fled home, returns and falls for Brenda (Rachel Griffiths), a massage therapist with a troubled family of her own. David is closeted, datingKeith (Mathew St. Patrick), a Black police officer, navigating sexuality and faith. Claire is the rebellious youngest, artist, lost, seeking. Ruth grieves by trying to control everything, then learning she can't. The show's structure: death every week, but the real story is how the living continue. The dead appear as ghosts, projections, conscience—Nate sees his father everywhere. They're notreal. They're not gone either.

The show's genius is making mortality intimate. Death isn't abstract; it's a customer, a body, a family's grief. The Fishers see every kind of death: old age, suicide, accident, murder. And the show asks: what do we do with this knowledge? Nate faces a brain condition that might kill him. Brenda's brother Billy (Jeremy Sisto) is mentally ill, obsessed with her. David is carjacked and traumatized. Claire falls in love and is abandoned. "The only thing I know for sure is that I'm going to die," Nate says. The show's comfort: so is everyone else. That's the connection.

Five seasons, sixty-three episodes, complete. The finale is legendary: a montage showing every character's death, from Nate (already gone) to Ruth to David to Claire, dying old in 2085. "And I will try to fix you," Coldplay plays. It's the most cathartic ending in television history—a show about death that lets you see everyone's ending, and find peace. "Why do we have to die?" a child asks. "To make life important," Nate answers. It was.
