What It Feels Like to Watch

Six Feet Under arrived as television's most sustained meditation on mortality—a family drama set in a funeral home, where every episode opened with a death and the Fisher family's business was to manage the grief of others while avoiding their own. Created by Alan Ball (American Beauty), the series ran from 2001 to 2005, establishing HBO's reputation for television that took seriously what broadcast television ignored: death, grief, the mess of family life, and the impossibility of escaping either your genetics or your choices.

The experience of watching Six Feet Under is one of accumulating intimacy with death. Each episode's opening death—some random (car accidents, falling objects), some inevitable (old age, illness), some absurd (a woman crushed by an air conditioner, a man killed by his own security system)—established mortality as the show's constant backdrop. The Fishers lived with death professionally; they also lived with it personally, the series suggested, because everyone does.

The Fisher family—Nate (Peter Krause), David (Michael C. Hall), Claire (Lauren Ambrose), and their mother Ruth (Frances Conroy)—represented different relationships to mortality and family obligation. Nate was the prodigal son who returned reluctantly; David was the closeted gay son who embraced the family business while hiding his sexuality; Claire was the artistic youngest searching for identity. Their interactions were marked by the particular toxicity of families who know each other too well: the wounds that don't heal because they're constantly reopened.

The series' final episode remains television's most celebrated ending—a flash-forward that showed each major character's death, from natural causes to accident to old age, accumulated into a single sequence that made explicit the show's running thesis: we all die, and what matters is how we live. It was the perfect conclusion to a series about the impossibility of escaping death and the necessity of accepting it.

The Fisher Family: The core ensemble created one of television's most fully realized families—people who loved each other and damaged each other in equal measure. The Extended Ensemble: The supporting characters—Rachel Griffiths's Brenda (Nate's brilliant but damaged girlfriend), Freddy Rodríguez's Federico (the embalmer who rises to partner), Mathew St. Patrick's Keith (David's boyfriend and eventual husband)—each received substantial development. The show loved its characters; even minor characters had interiority.

The Evolution: Across five seasons, the characters changed in ways that accumulated rather than reset. David's coming-out journey wasn't resolved in an episode but extended across years. Nate's relationship with death evolved as his own mortality became imminent. Ruth discovered sexuality in her fifties. The characters aged authentically.

The Funeral Home: Fisher & Sons Funeral Home became a fully realized workplace—the preparation room, the viewing rooms, the business office, the family residence above. The funeral industry details (embalming, viewing arrangements, the business of death) provided unusual texture. The show found drama in the mundane: grief management as profession.

Los Angeles: The series captured a particular Los Angeles—not the industry version but the everyday city: strip malls, mid-century architecture, the particular light, the sprawl. The setting was present without being prominent; the Fishers' LA was residential and commercial rather than glamorous.

Comparative Assessment: The Wire (9) built Baltimore as comprehensive ecosystem. Six Feet Under creates its world within narrower scope—the funeral industry and one family's extended relationships.

Why 6.5: Effective world-building within its funeral-home scope. The industry details are specific; the broader world is less developed.

The Absence: Six Feet Under is family drama with professional death as backdrop; spectacle is not its aim. There are no action sequences, no visual set pieces, no cinematic showpieces. The drama is entirely domestic and emotional—conversations, arguments, grief, love.

The Deaths: Each episode's opening death provided occasional visual interest—a car crash, a falling object, a accidental shooting—but these were brief and served narrative function rather than spectacle. The show's interest was in aftermath, not event.

Conceptual Density
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

Mortality as Subject: Six Feet Under's central concept—death as constant companion rather than distant event—was explored with unusual seriousness. Each episode's opening death established mortality's randomness and inevitability; the family's professional relationship with death created ongoing meditation on grief, preparation, acceptance, and denial. The show asked viewers to live with death as the Fishers did.

Family Dynamics: The series examined family with unusual depth—the ways families damage each other while loving each other, the impossibility of escaping family patterns, the particular toxicity of those who know you best. The Fishers were neither idealized nor condemned; they were shown as families actually are: people who hurt each other and need each other simultaneously.

Sexuality: David's journey from closeted repression to out gay man was television's first sustained exploration of gay male sexuality in a family-drama context. The show treated David's sexuality as aspect of character rather than Very Special Episode; his relationship with Keith evolved across seasons with the same attention as the heterosexual relationships.

Comparative Assessment: The Sopranos (8.5) achieved comparable conceptual density with different subjects. Six Feet Under matches that depth with more explicit mortality focus.

Why 9: Exceptional conceptual depth about death, family, and sexuality. The show sustained its meditation across five seasons without exhaustion.

Narrative Drive
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 0.90

The Episodic Structure: Six Feet Under worked episodically—each episode had its death-of-the-week providing structure, but the real story was the ongoing family drama. The momentum was moderate rather than intense; this was character accumulation rather than plot escalation. Viewers seeking strong drive may find the pacing slow.

The Character Arcs: Investment came from character evolution rather than plot momentum: Will David come out? Will Nate settle down? Will Ruth find independence? Will Claire find direction? These questions provided drive across seasons, but the drive was emotional rather than narrative.

Comparative Assessment: Breaking Bad (10) represents maximum narrative escalation. Six Feet Under is deliberately moderate in momentum—appropriate to its subject but limiting for some viewers.

Why 6: Moderate narrative drive from character accumulation rather than plot acceleration. The pacing serves the subject but tests viewer patience.

10
Narrative Resolution
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 1.00

The Final Episode: "Everyone's Waiting" is television's most celebrated finale—a 75-minute conclusion that provided narrative resolution and then, in its final minutes, showed each major character's death across decades. The sequence made explicit the show's constant thesis: we all die, and what matters is how we live. The deaths were unexpected (accident, illness, old age) and inevitable; they were staged with the same matter-of-factness as the opening deaths had been for five seasons.

Thematic Completion: The finale didn't just end the story; it completed the meditation. Nate (who died earlier in the season) appeared to guide Claire through her future; the deaths accumulated into a single statement about mortality. The ending was devastating and beautiful, sad and affirming, exactly what the series had always been.

Comparative Assessment: The Sopranos (7) ended ambiguously; Breaking Bad (9) ended cleanly. Six Feet Under achieved both closure and transcendence.

Why 10: Television's finest series finale—a conclusion that honored the series' themes, provided character resolution, and achieved genuine artistic transcendence. The ending justifies everything that came before.
