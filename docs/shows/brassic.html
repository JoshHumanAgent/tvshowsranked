Brassic

2019 - Present • Sky • Ongoing

Score: 7.15
Comedy, Drama

What It Feels Like

Vinnie and his friends live in a small Lancashire town. They steal, they scheme, they survive. It's not glamorous, but it's theirs. Joe Gilgun created and stars in the show based on his own experiences. Northern working-class life as comedy: the schemes are absurd, the friendship is real.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Joe Gilgun as Vinnie: the chaotic leader, the mental health struggles, the loyalty. Michelle Keegan as Erin: the girlfriend trying to escape. The ensemble creates the friend group with specificity. The performances are genuine Northern working-class.

World Building (8/10)

Hawley, Lancashire: the council estate, the pubs, the schemes, the limited options. The world is specific to Northern working-class Britain. The details are authentic—the show is created by someone who lived it.

Cinematography (6/10)

Sky comedy production: the Lancashire town rendered with rough beauty, the schemes filmed for comedy. The style is functional—the setting and characters carry it.

Visual Spectacle (2/10)

No spectacle. This is working-class life: the schemes are small, the stakes are personal. The "spectacle" is the absurdity of the schemes and the loyalty of friendship. Comedy from lived experience.

Conceptual Density (7/10)

Working-class survival, mental health, friendship as family, escaping vs. belonging, what options exist in places with no opportunities. The show explores these through comedy, not tragedy.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Each episode has a scheme. The serialized element is character: Vinnie's mental health, Erin's escape attempts, the friend group's dynamics. The structure is episodic comedy with heart.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10)

Ongoing. Six seasons and continuing. The friend group evolves; some characters leave; life continues. The show maintains quality. No endpoint yet.
