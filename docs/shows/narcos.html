What It Feels Like to Watch

Narcos is the story of Pablo Escobar—and the Americans who hunted him. "Plata o plomo," Escobar says. Silver or lead. Take the bribe or take the bullet. The show opens with DEA agent Steve Murphy (Boyd Holbrook) narrating: "There's no reason this story should start with me. I'm nobody." But it does start with him, and with his partner Javier Peña (Pedro Pascal), and with their impossible task: bring down the man who controlled eighty percent of the world's cocaine.

Wagner Moura's Pablo Escobar is magnificent—charming, terrifying, oddly sympathetic, and utterly ruthless. He's a family man who orders assassinations. He's a populist who builds housing for the poor while bombing planes. He's a monster who believes his own mythology. "I'm not a criminal," he says. "I'm a businessman." He's both. The show's genius is showing how Escobar's rise was enabled by everyone: corrupt politicians, greedy bankers, American consumers, and an economic system that rewards those ruthless enough to exploit it.

The first two seasons follow Escobar's rise and fall: his election to congress, his war against the state, his imprisonment in La Catedral (a prison he built himself), his escape, and his death on a rooftop in Medellín. Season three follows the Cali Cartel—smoother, more corporate, more dangerous. "The Cali godfathers liked to say they were different from Pablo," Murphy narrates. "They were. They were worse." The show's thesis: the drug war is unwinnable because there's always another cartel, another kingpin, another supply meeting demand.

Three seasons, thirty episodes. Narcos spawned a Mexico sequel. It's essential viewing for understanding the drug war—not as a morality tale, but as a system. Escobar was a monster. He was also a symptom. "The cocaine business is a perfect market," a character says. "There will always be supply as long as there is demand." The war continues.

Characters & Acting (9): Wagner Moura IS Escobar. Pedro Pascal's Peña. Boyd Holbrook's Murphy. Brilliant ensemble.

World Building (9): 1980s Colombia, Medellín bureaucracy, cartel politics. Immersive, educational, thrilling.

Narrative Drive (9): Two seasons of Escobar cat-and-mouse, plus Cali. Never boring. Pacing is relentless.
