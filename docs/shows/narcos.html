What It Feels Like to Watch

Narcos is the definitive story of Pablo Escobar and the rise of the Colombian drug trade. Wagner Moura plays Escobar not as a criminal mastermind, but as a man—charismatic, ruthless, family-oriented, paranoid, and ultimately mortal. The show spans his rise from small-time smuggler to the world's richest criminal, his war with the Colombian government, and his eventual death on a rooftop in Medellín.

The series is anchored by DEA agents Steve Murphy (Boyd Holbrook) and Javier Peña (Pedro Pascal), who provide the American perspective on the drug war. But Escobar is the gravitational center. The show doesn't glorify him—it shows his brutality, his bombing campaigns, his ordering of assassinations—but it does humanize him. We see him with his family, playing soccer, worrying about his children. The cognitive dissonance is the point.

Seasons one and two chronicle Escobar's reign and fall. Season three shifts focus to the Cali Cartel—the sophisticated, corporate cocaine operation that took over after Escobar's death. Without Escobar, the show transforms into something different: less personal, more geopolitical, following the DEA's pursuit of the Rodriguez Orejuela brothers.

The production is cinematic, shot on location in Colombia with Spanish dialogue throughout. The pacing is propulsive. The violence is visceral. The politics are complicated—the DEA isn't heroic, the Colombian government is compromised, and the drug war has no clean winners.

Narcos: Mexico (a companion series) continues the story across the border, but the original Narcos stands as a complete chronicle of Colombia's cocaine wars and the man who started it all.
