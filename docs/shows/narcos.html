What It Feels Like to Watch

Narcos chronicles the rise and fall of Pablo Escobar (Wagner Moura), the Colombian drug lord who became one of the richest criminals in history. The show mixes archival footage with dramatization, narrated by DEA agent Steve Murphy (Boyd Holbrook) who was there when the United States decided to make drug enforcement its business in Latin America.

Escobar starts small—smuggling cigarettes, running rackets—but cocaine changes everything. By the 1980s, his Medellín Cartel is supplying 80% of America's cocaine, earning billions, and Escobar is building an empire. He runs for Congress, builds a prison shaped like a resort (La Catedral, where he "served" his sentence on his own terms), and declares war on the Colombian state when they try to extradite him.

The show captures Escobar's charisma and brutality—Moura's portrayal is terrifying because Escobar was terrifying. He ordered assassinations of judges, ministers, presidential candidates. He blew up an airplane. He held Colombia hostage. But he also built housing for the poor, which made him loved by the desperate. The show doesn't glorify him, but it doesn't caricature him either.

Javier Peña (Pedro Pascal) is Murphy's partner, the Colombian expert who understands the landscape better. Together they coordinate the hunt, but the show makes clear that the Americans were often obstacles—arrogant, slow, politically compromised. The real work was done by Colombian police, by the Search Bloc, by vigilantes who'd lost family to Escobar.

The first two seasons cover Escobar's story. Season three shifts to the Cali Cartel—the sophisticated operators who made Escobar look like a thug. The show remained compelling because the drug war remained compelling—endless, evolving, impossible to win.
