What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men is about ad men in 1960s New York—and about America in the decade that changed everything. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is a creative director at Sterling Cooper, a genius with a secret past, a ruined marriage, and an empty life he fills with alcohol and affairs. "People want to be told what to do so badly that they'll listen to anyone," he says. He's selling products, but he's also selling himself—a man who stole another man's identity and built a life on lies.

The show's genius is its attention to detail: the clothes, the offices, the cigarettes, the casual sexism and racism. It captures an America in transition—from the buttoned-up 1950s to the turbulent 1960s. Don's secretary, Peggy Olson (Elisabeth Moss), becomes a copywriter, then a creative director, then a partner—she's the show's feminist journey. Joan Harris (Christina Hendricks) is the office manager who uses what she's given to get what she wants. Roger Sterling (John Slattery) is the aging playboy who never grew up. The show is about what advertising does to people, and what people do to each other.

The storytelling is patient. Episodes are self-contained; seasons are novels. A character might disappear for episodes, then return. The show trusts the audience to follow. "The carousel" pitch in season one is the show's thesis: nostalgia is a potent pain. "It takes you to a place where you ache to go again." Don is selling the past. He can't return to it—he doesn't even know who he really is.

Seven seasons, ninety-two episodes, complete. Mad Men is the most beautifully crafted show ever made. Every frame is composed, every line has meaning. The finale: Don at a retreat, finding peace, inventing the Coca-Cola "Hilltop" ad. He didn't change—he just found a new way to sell. "I'd like to teach the world to sing." He taught it to buy. The perfection is the tragedy.
