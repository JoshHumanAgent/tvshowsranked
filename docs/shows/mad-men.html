What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men is a character study disguised as a workplace drama. Set in a 1960s Manhattan advertising agency, it follows Don Draper (Jon Hamm), a creative director with a secret past, as he navigates the golden age of advertising—the cigarettes, the whiskey, the women, and the slow cultural shifts that will eventually dismantle the world he's mastered.

Don Draper is one of television's great protagonists: a man who built an identity on lies and now can't find the truth underneath. He's brilliant at selling things to people—happiness, security, meaning—because he understands that advertising is about desire, not products. But he can't sell himself on his own life. He drinks, he cheats, he disappears, he returns. The women in his life—wife Betty (January Jones), secretary-turned-copywriter Peggy (Elisabeth Moss), and many others—are mirrors reflecting what he can't bear to see in himself.

The show is famous for its period detail, but the 1960s setting is more than aesthetics. It's the decade when America changed—civil rights, feminism, Vietnam, the death of the postwar consensus. The characters at Sterling Cooper watch these changes from inside their glass-walled offices, some embracing them, some resisting, all affected. The show uses advertising as a lens: every episode begins with Don crafting a pitch, and the pitch reveals what the culture wants to believe about itself.

Seven seasons trace Don's disintegration and possible redemption. The supporting cast—Peggy, Pete, Joan, Roger—get their own arcs, their own triumphs and disasters. The series finale is perfect: Don at a retreat, finding a kind of peace, then returning to the world with perhaps the most famous ad ever made. "I'd like to buy the world a Coke." The ad is real. The peace is ambiguous. That's Mad Men.
