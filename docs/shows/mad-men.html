What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men is television as literature. Matthew Weiner's portrait of 1960s advertising executives and the women who orbit them is a novel in moving pictures—patient, layered, committed to the messiness of human behavior. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is a creative director with a secret past, a perfect suit, and an emptiness he can't fill. He's also the engine that drives the show, a man who reinvented himself and now can't figure out who he is.

The series begins in 1960 and ends in 1970, tracing a decade of American transformation through the lens of a single advertising agency. The historical detail is obsessive—every brand, every outfit, every ashtray is period-accurate—but the real history is internal. Peggy Olson (Elisabeth Moss) rises from secretary to copy chief, learning to survive in a world designed to exclude her. Joan Holloway (Christina Hendricks) builds power through charm and calculation, then discovers that power has limits. Roger Sterling (John Slattery) drinks and quips and slowly erodes. Pete Campbell (Vincent Kartheiser) schemes and fails and grows, against all expectations, into a person.

The show's emotional intelligence is staggering. It depicts racism, sexism, homophobia, anti-Semitism not as problems to be solved but as facts of the era, absorbed into characters' bones. It allows characters to be contradictory, unlikable, self-destructive, and still compelling. It builds to moments of devastating simplicity: Don pitching the Kodak carousel, Betty shooting pigeons, Lane's tragedy, Peggy's walk through the office in darkness. "The Wheel," season one's finale, contains one of television's most perfect monologues about nostalgia, memory, and pain.

Seven seasons, ninety-two episodes, perfect from start to finish. The finale brings Don to a retreat where he learns to meditate, then cuts to the 1971 Coke commercial. "I'd like to teach the world to sing." It's ironic, sincere, beautiful, and possibly the only ending that could contain everything the show was about. The wheel turns.
