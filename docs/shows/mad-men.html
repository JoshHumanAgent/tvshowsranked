What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men takes place in the advertising world of 1960s New York, but it's really about the invention of modern identity. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is a creative director with a secret—he's not Don Draper at all. He stole a dead man's identity during the Korean War, deserted the army, and built an entirely new life. He's selling himself, selling products, selling an American dream that's as constructed as his name.

The show uses its period setting to examine themes that feel modern: gender, race, sexuality, class, the performance of self. Betty Draper (January Jones) is a perfect housewife who's quietly suffocating. Peggy Olson (Elisabeth Moss) is a secretary who rises to copywriter while navigating a world that doesn't want her. Joan Harris (Christina Hendricks) uses her sexuality as power and is punished for it. The office is a pressure cooker of ambition, resentment, and desire.

Don Draper is one of television's great enigmas—a womanizer who can't connect, a genius who doubts himself, a self-made man who doesn't exist. His therapy sessions, his flashbacks to childhood, his desperate marriages—they all circle the same question: can you invent yourself into happiness? The show's answer is no, but you can keep trying. The carousel pitch, the most famous scene in the series, is Don selling nostalgia as a way home, but he has no home to return to.

Seven seasons, ninety-two episodes, one of television's great endings. Don ends up at a retreat, chanting, finding peace—and then cutting to the famous Coke ad. Did he create it? The show refuses to say. The ending is perfect: the product is happiness, the ad is life, and Don Draper might have finally learned to sell something he feels.
