What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men is a novel disguised as a television show. Matthew Weiner, a veteran of The Sopranos, created something slower, more interior, more attentive to detail. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is a creative director at a Madison Avenue advertising agency in 1960. He's brilliant, handsome, and completely hollow—a man who stole another man's identity and built a life on lies. The show is about what happens when that life begins to crack.

The period detail is obsessive. Every cigarette, every cocktail, every dress, every advertisement is historically precise. But the show isn't nostalgic—it's forensic. It looks at the 1960s and sees the racism, sexism, homophobia, anti-Semitism, and environmental destruction that nostalgia erases. The characters smoke constantly because they didn't know it killed them. The men drink at work because they could. The women are secretaries, wives, or both because those were the options. The show doesn't judge them for being of their time; it shows us what that time cost.

The ensemble is extraordinary. Peggy Olson (Elisabeth Moss) starts as Don's secretary and becomes a copywriter, fighting every step. Joan Holloway (Christina Hendricks) runs the office and is punished for her sexuality. Pete Campbell (Vincent Kartheiser) is ambitious, petty, and somehow sympathetic. Betty Draper (January Jones) is Don's trapped first wife, a woman who married the fantasy and got the reality. And Don himself—the show keeps pulling back layers, revealing the trauma that made him, the emptiness that drives him, the possibility that he might, finally, change.

Seven seasons, ninety-two episodes, one of the great finales in television history. Don ends up at a retreat, meditating, and inventing the "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" ad. He discovered enlightenment and commodified it. That's Mad Men's thesis: we sell ourselves every day, and the question is whether we know what we're buying.

Characters & Acting (10): Jon Hamm is a movie star. Elisabeth Moss, Vincent Kartheiser, Christina Hendricks, John Slattery—an embarrassment of acting riches.

World Building (10): 1960s Madison Avenue rendered in obsessive detail. Every prop, every line, every gesture is period-perfect.

Resolution (10): "I'd like to buy the world a Coke." Don's breakdown, breakthrough, and commodification of enlightenment. Perfect.

Key Episodes

(The Suitcase" (S4E7) — Don and Peggy, all night. The best episode of the series. Character study as drama.
