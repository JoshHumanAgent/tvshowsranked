What It Feels Like to Watch

Mad Men is about advertising—and the lies we tell ourselves. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is a creative director at Sterling Cooper, a Manhattan ad agency in 1960. He's handsome, brilliant, and completely hollow. "You're born alone and you die alone," he says. He would know. Don Draper doesn't exist—he stole a dead man's identity in Korea. Dick Whitman is his real name. He's spent his whole life running from who he is, building someone new. Advertising is his métier: selling people what they want to believe.

The show spans a decade: 1960 to 1970. The culture shifts—Kennedy's assassination, Vietnam, civil rights, the pill, the sexual revolution. The agency shifts—mergers, acquisitions, new partners. The characters shift, or don't. Peggy Olson (Elisabeth Moss) starts as Don's secretary and becomes a copywriter, fighting every step. Joan Holloway (Christina Hendricks) runs the office and learns the cost of being a woman in a man's world. Pete Campbell (Vincent Kartheiser) wants everything and earns nothing. Roger Sterling (JohnSlattery) drinks, womanizes, and accidentally becomes the moral center.

Mad Men is the most literary television ever made. Every frame is composed, every line meaningful, every cigarette smoke an aria to mortality. The show's thesis: we're all advertising something. We tell stories about who we are, who we want to be, what we deserve. Don Draper is the master of reinvention. He's also the most lonely character on television. "People tell you who they are," he tells Peggy. "Why don't you believe them?" He can't take his own advice.

Seven seasons, ninety-two episodes, complete. The finale is perfect: Don meditates at a retreat, finds peace, and imagines the Coke commercial that became iconic. He didn't find enlightenment; he found an ad. That's the show. Healing and capitalism, spirituality and sales, the same impulse. "The most important idea in advertising is new," Don says. "It creates an itch." Mad Men spent seven seasons scratching.
