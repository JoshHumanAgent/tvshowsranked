What It Feels Like to Watch

Matthew Weiner's Mad Men is television as time capsule and character study—an excavation of 1960s America through the advertising industry that sold the American Dream back to Americans. Don Draper (Jon Hamm) is the enigma at the center: a self-invented man selling illusions to a country inventing itself.

The show moves slowly, deliberately, accumulating detail and character across seven seasons. There are no cliffhangers, no plot twists—just the gradual revelation of who these people are beneath their carefully constructed surfaces. Don's secret identity, Peggy's rise from secretary to copy chief, Pete's entitled malice, Joan's navigation of a man's world, Roger's privileged dissolve—the show tracks them with anthropological patience.

The period detail is obsessive and meaningful—costumes, sets, props, and especially the smoking, drinking, and sexism that define the era. But the period isn't decorative; it's the point. These people exist in the moment before everything changed, and we watch the 1960s transform them whether they like it or not.

The finale—"Person to Person"—provides one of television's great endings: Don at a retreat, finding himself (or not), cut to the 1971 "Hilltop" Coke commercial. Is Don's epiphany genuine, or did he just sell another illusion? The ambiguity is perfect.
