What It Feels Like to Watch

The Night Of is eight hours of anxiety distilled into television. Naz Khan (Riz Ahmed) is a Pakistani-American college student who borrows his father's cab to go to a party in Manhattan. He picks up a girl instead. They spend the night together. He wakes up to find her stabbed to death. He can't remember what happened. He didn't do it—he thinks. The evidence says otherwise.

What follows is the criminal justice system as it actually operates: slow, unjust, indifferent to truth. Naz is arrested, processed, and trapped in Rikers Island before he can understand what's happening. His lawyer is Jack Stone (John Turturro), a low-rent attorney with eczema and a lack of ambition who takes the case because no one else would. Stone becomes consumed by it, digging into details the police ignored, pursuing leads that might exonerate a client who's becoming a different person in prison.

Riz Ahmed's transformation is devastating. Naz enters Rikers as a soft, naive student. Prison hardens him. The show doesn't romanticize incarceration—it shows how the system manufactures criminals. By the time Naz is released (no spoiler on the verdict), he's shaved his head, gotten tattoos, and become capable of violence. Whatever happened that night, the system has destroyed him.

John Turturro's Jack Stone is one of television's great characters—a man who's never cared about truth suddenly caring too much. His investigation is meticulous. The show gives us theories, suspects, procedural details. It's also about race, class, and who gets presumed innocent.

The finale doesn't give easy answers. The question of who killed the girl is answered, but the question of who Naz is now—different for everyone.
