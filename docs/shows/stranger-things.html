What It Feels Like to Watch

Stranger Things is Spielberg by way of King—an 1980s nostalgia bomb that somehow transcends pastiche. The Duffer Brothers created a world where Dungeons & Dragons is mythology, walkie-talkies are lifelines, and the monster under the bed is real. Eleven (Millie Bobby Brown) escapes from a government lab, meets a group of boys searching for their missing friend, and a pop culture phenomenon is born.

The show's power is its ensemble. Mike (Finn Wolfhard), Dustin (Gaten Matarazzo), Lucas (Caleb McLaughlin), and Will (Noah Schnapp) areactual children playing children—their friendship feels earned, their dialogue improvised. Joyce Byers (Winona Ryder) is a mother whose belief in her son is absolute, even when everyone thinks she's crazy. Chief Hopper (David Harbour) is the broken protector who finds purpose. Steve Harrington (Joe Keery) starts as a jerk and becomes the babysitter we all need. The show loves its characters, and we love them back.

The Upside Down is genuinely ominous—a mirror dimension of rot and cold and monsters. The Demogorgon, the Mind Flayer, Vecna—each season introduces a new threat, and the stakes escalate. The production design is meticulous: every poster, every toy, every song is period-appropriate. The synth score by Kyle Dixon and Michael Stein is iconic. By season four, the show had become Netflix's crown jewel—a global event that broke streaming records.

Four seasons, forty-two episodes (season five forthcoming). The show has grown with its audience—the child actors are adults, the tone is darker, the runtime is feature-length. Not every season lands perfectly (season two drags, season three is pure summer blockbuster), but the best episodes are among television's most thrilling. "Friends don't lie," Eleven says. Stranger Things doesn't either—it's a love letter to a decade that never existed, rendered with absolute sincerity.
