What It Feels Like to Watch

Stranger Things is nostalgia distilled into television. Set in 1980s Indiana, the show opens with a boy vanishing and a girl with telekinetic powers appearing. Eleven (Millie Bobby Brown) escaped from a government lab where she was experimented on. Will Byers (Noah Schnapp) is trapped in another dimension—the Upside Down, a dark mirror of our world filled with monsters. His mother Joyce (Winona Ryder) will tear reality apart to save him. His friends will ride their bikes into danger because that's what friends do.

The show is built from references: ET, The Goonies, Stephen King, John Carpenter, Dungeons and Dragons. It wears its influences on its sleeve, and that's its strength and weakness. The first season feels fresh despite being entirely familiar—the Duffer Brothers understand that nostalgia isn't just repetition, it's emotional memory. We remember being twelve, believing our friends would face monsters for us, riding bikes into endless summer nights.

Thecast is extraordinary. Millie Bobby Brown became a star without speaking most of season one—her eyes convey everything. Winona Ryder is a hurricane of maternal desperation. David Harbour's Chief Hopper is the wounded father figure every 80s movie needed. The kids—Finn Wolfhard, Gaten Matarazzo, Caleb McLaughlin—grew up on screen, their friendship becoming the show's heart. Season four added Sadie Sink as Max, and her battle with Vecna in "Dear Billy" produced one of the show's best sequences.

Four seasons, thirty-four episodes, with a fifth and final season promised. The show's quality dipped in seasons two and three, recovered in four, but never matched the perfection of season one. The Upside Down remains mysterious, the mythology increasingly convoluted, the stakes apocalyptic. But the heart endures: a group of friends facing the darkness together. "Friends don't lie," Eleven learns. Stranger Things believes that friendship can save the world. It's corny, it's sincere, it's exactly what television should be sometimes.
