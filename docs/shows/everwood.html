Everwood

2002 - 2006 • The WB • 89 Episodes

Score: 7.15
Drama, Family

What It Feels Like

Dr. Andy Brown's wife dies. He moves his family from New York to Everwood, Colorado—a small town where he can practice medicine and connect with his son. Treat Williams is Andy; Gregory Smith is Ephram, the gifted pianist son. Greg Berlanti created the WB's most earnest drama: family, loss, and small-town healing.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Treat Williams as Andy Brown: the neurosurgeon starting over. Gregory Smith as Ephram: the son who lost his mother and resents his father. Emily VanCamp as Amy: the girl Ephram loves. The ensemble creates Everwood's community. The performances are WB sincere.

World Building (8/10)

Everwood, Colorado: the small town where everyone knows everyone, the mountains, the Main Street. The world is specific and lived-in—a community that grows across four seasons. The WB perfected the small-town aesthetic.

Cinematography (5/10)

WB network aesthetics: the town, the mountains, the houses photographed for warmth. The style is functional—this is family drama, not visual art. The Colorado settings provide natural beauty.

Visual Spectacle (2/10)

No spectacle. This is family drama: doctors, teenagers, relationships. The "spectacle" is emotional—watching characters confront loss and heal. Earnest television.

Conceptual Density (7/10)

Grief, father-son relationships, starting over, small-town vs. city values, the meaning of community. The show explores these through character—each episode has a lesson without being preachy.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Ephram and Amy's relationship, Andy's adjustment, the Abbott family next door. The drive is emotional—watching characters grow and struggle. The structure is episodic with serialized relationships.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Four seasons with appropriate ending. The relationships resolve; the characters move forward. The WB drama tradition of emotional closure. Complete on its own terms.
