What It Feels Like

Shim Deok-chul is 70 years old. He's never learned ballet, but it's his secret dream. Chae-rok is a young ballet dancer whose dreams are dying. They meet, they learn from each other, they give each other hope. Park In-hwan and Song Kang star in the Korean drama about dreams that don't fade with age.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Park In-hwan as Deok-chul: the 70-year-old whose life was duty, finally chasing his dream. Song Kang as Chae-rok: the damaged young dancer who's lost his way. The intergenerational friendship is the heart. The performances are gentle and genuine.

World Building (7/10)

Seoul ballet: the studio, the rehearsals, the performances, the financial struggles. The world is intimate—two people finding each other through their dreams. K-drama family dynamics provide texture.

Cinematography (7/10)

K-drama production: ballet photographed with attention, the generational differences visualized, Seoul as backdrop. The style is warm—this is humanist drama.

Visual Spectacle (4/10)

The ballet: the training, the rehearsals, the eventual performances. The spectacle is the art form—watching a 70-year-old attempt ballet. The emotion is the real show.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Dreams and age, whether it's ever too late, intergenerational connection, the cost of sacrifice, what we owe to ourselves vs. others. The show explores these through the ballet metaphor.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Deok-chul wants to perform before he dies. Chae-rok needs to rediscover his love. Each episode brings them closer to performance and further from despair. The structure is inspirational.

Narrative Path & Resolution (9/10)

Complete. The performance happens. The dreams are realized. The ending is emotional and appropriate. Korean drama with proper conclusion and emotional payoff.
