This Country

2017 • British Mockumentary • 19 Episodes

Score: 7.05
Comedy, Mockumentary

What It Feels Like to Watch

This Country is a British mockumentary about two cousins in a small Cotswolds village who have nothing to do and all the time in the world to do it. Kerry and Kurtan Mucklowe are the kind of people who would be featured in a documentary about rural England's forgotten youth—they have no jobs, no prospects, and elaborate opinions on matters of no consequence. The show's genius is in making them hilarious without making them pathetic, or rather, letting their small-town antics speak for themselves.

The mockumentary format works perfectly for this material. The camera follows Kerry and Kurtan as they investigate local crimes (someone put a brick through a window; someone is keeping chickens illegally), feud with neighbors, and pursue ambitions that remain resolutely local. The deadpan delivery of mundane observations—"I once saw a ghost, but it might have been a crisp packet"—captures something essential about boredom and the stories people tell to make their lives feel meaningful.

What elevates This Country above similar comedies is its genuine affection for its subjects. The show is created by and stars siblings Daisy May and Charlie Cooper, who grew up in exactly this environment, and the specificity feels lived-in rather than observed. The supporting characters—the local vicar who presides over events with weary patience, the various neighbors who become entangled in Kerry and Kurtan's schemes—are rendered with the same respect that The Office gave its working-class subjects. It's comedy about people who deserve to be taken seriously, even when they're being ridiculous.

Why It Ranks Here

This Country earns its position through authenticity, generosity toward its subjects, and comedy that emerges from character rather than caricature. The score reflects a mockumentary that knows its world inside out.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (7.5/10)

Daisy May and Charlie Cooper play Kerry and Kurtan with a naturalism that makes every interaction feel unscripted. The supporting cast, many playing versions of people the creators actually knew, maintains the documentary illusion. The characters are specific without being grotesque, funny without being punchlines.

World Building (7/10)

The Cotswolds village is rendered with detail that could only come from lived experience—the specific geography, the social hierarchies, the events that constitute local importance. The show captures rural England not as pastoral fantasy but as a place where people have too little to do and too much time to do it.

Cinematography (6/10)

The mockumentary style requires cameras that observe rather than compose, and This Country maintains that illusion effectively. The visual approach serves the comedy without calling attention to itself. The rural locations are captured without romanticism.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

There is no spectacle here. The show's pleasures are entirely character-based—specific people in specific situations. The Cotswolds landscape is present but not emphasized. This is comedy about the quotidian.

Conceptual Depth (6/10)

Beneath the comedy is an observation about rural neglect, about people left behind by economic change, about the dignity that people maintain even when they have nothing to do. The show doesn't argue these points; it demonstrates them through its characters' lives.

Narrative Drive (6/10)

Each episode has a structure—a scheme, a complication, a resolution—but the drive comes from character rather than plot. The show is comfortable with small stakes, and viewers expecting dramatic movement may find the pace too gentle. But the episodes build cumulative effect.

Narrative Path & Resolution (6/10)

The episodes are self-contained, with running jokes and character development across seasons but no overarching plot to resolve. The comedy doesn't require narrative resolution; the characters remain exactly who they are. Some viewers will find this comforting, others limiting.
