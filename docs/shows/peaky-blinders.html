What It Feels Like to Watch

Peaky Blinders is gangster glamour—the rise of Thomas Shelby from small-time bookmaker to international crime lord, told with style that borders on fetish. Cillian Murphy plays Tommy with ice-blue eyes and a cheekbone cut that became iconic. He walks through Birmingham in tailored three-piece suits, smokes cigarettes like he's punishing them, and builds an empire through violence, intelligence, and the trauma of World War I.

The Shelbys are a family, not a corporation. Arthur (Paul Anderson) is the older brother, violent and unstable, suffering from PTSD that expresses itself in rage. John (Joe Cole) is the youngest, hot-headed and loyal. Ada (Sophie Rundle) is the sister who wants out but can't escape. Aunt Polly (Helen McCrory) is the matriarch, the real brain behind the operation, the one person Tommy respects.

The show's first two seasons are street-level crime: fixing horse races, fighting the Italians, outmaneuvering the police. Then comes Inspector Campbell (Sam Neill), then the IRA, then the Communist Party, then the mafia, then the fascists. Each season escalates Tommy's power and his enemies. By season five, he's an MP, still gangster but playing politics, still haunted by the war that made him.

The style is the substance. The haircuts, the suits, the slow-motion walks set to rock music—it's all part of the package. Peaky Blinders created a look that influenced fashion for a decade. But the style has substance beneath it: these are men destroyed by war, rebuilding themselves in the only way they know how. Tommy's ambition is both his strength and his curse.

Helen McCrory's death in 2021 changed the show's final season. Polly is written out, the family mourns, and the ending feels incomplete. The movie that's planned may give closure, but the show ended with Tommy riding off, the past behind him, the future uncertain.
