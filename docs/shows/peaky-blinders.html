What It Feels Like to Watch

Peaky Blinders is gangster epic as style object. Cillian Murphy plays Thomas Shelby, leader of a Birmingham crime family in 1919. The title refers to the razor blades sewn into their caps—used to blind enemies. Thomas is the strategist, the ambition, the void at the center. "I'm not a good man," he says regularly. He's not. But he's compelling.

The show's aesthetic is its signature: charcoal suits, undercut hairstyles, slow-motion walks, cigarettes, horses, industrial Birmingham rendered in sepia tones. The soundtrack is anachronistic—Nick Cave, Arctic Monkeys, PJ Harvey—rock music for1920s gangsters. It shouldn't work. It does. The style creates mood, and the mood carries scenes that might otherwise falter.

Thomas Shelby wages war against everyone: rival gangs, communists, fascists, the IRA, the British government. Each season escalates. Season one is local—the Peaky Blinders versus a Belfast cop (Sam Neill). Season two expands to London. Season three introduces Russians and Italians. Season four features a mafia hitman. Season five goes political—Thomas becomes an MP and faces Oswald Mosley (Sam Claflin), Britain's fascist leader. Season six concludes the saga with illness, legacy, and a final reckoning.

Six seasons, thirty-six episodes, complete. The show's flaw is Thomas's invincibility—he always wins, somehow. But Murphy'sperformance is a masterclass in stillness. Thomas Shelby rarely raises his voice. He doesn't need to. "By order of the Peaky Blinders" became a meme and a catchphrase. The show earned both. It's gangster mythology for the Instagram age—beautiful, violent, shallow, hypnotic.
