## What It Feels Like to Watch

The Boys takes everything you think you know about superheroes and turns it inside out. This isn't the Marvel Cinematic Universe—it's a world where heroes are corporate products, where capes come with publicists, and where the most powerful beings on Earth are also the most corrupt.

Karl Urban is magnetic as Billy Butcher, a man on a crusade to take down the super-powered elite after a personal tragedy. He leads a ragtag team of vigilantes—each with their own grievances against the so-called "supes"—in a war against Vought International, the corporation that owns and markets heroes like products. Antony Starr is terrifying as Homelander, a Superman-analogue whose all-American smile hides a sociopathic monster. The gap between his public persona and private cruelty is the show's central horror.

The series is gleefully profane, brutally violent, and smarter than it needs to be. It skewers celebrity culture, corporate greed, and the military-industrial complex while delivering genuinely shocking moments of gore and dark comedy. The supporting cast shines—Jack Quaid's Hughie is an everyman caught in an impossible world, and Erin Moriarty's Starlight provides a glimpse of what heroism could actually look like.

What elevates The Boys is its commitment to consequences. When supers fight, civilians die. When secrets are exposed, lives are destroyed. And when you try to take down gods, you pay a price. It's superhero storytelling for a cynical age, but it never loses sight of why people might still want to believe.
