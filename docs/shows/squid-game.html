What It Feels Like to Watch

Squid Game is about desperation disguised as childhood. 456 people, deeply in debt, are invited to play children's games for money. "Red light, green light" is the first: move on green, freeze on red, die if you move. The winner takes ₩45.6 billion. Everyone else dies. The show's premise is Battle Royale meets Parasite—a critique of capitalism so sharp it drew global attention. It became Netflix's most-watched series ever.

Seong Gi-hun (Lee Jung-jae) is a gambling addict who lives with his mother, barely sees his daughter, and is broke beyond hope. He joins the game for money. Cho Sang-woo (Park Hae-soo) is his childhood friend, a former investment banker disgraced by embezzlement. Kang Sae-byeok (Jung Ho-yeon) is a North Korean defector trying to get her mother out and her brother to safety. Oh Il-nam (O Yeong-su) is an old man with a brain tumor who wants to play before he dies. They become allies, then rivals, then victims.

The games are simple: tug of war, marbles, the titular squid game. The horror is watching people kill each other for money—watching friendships form and break, watching humanity stripped away game by game. The twist: the old man (Il-nam) created the games. He's a billionaire who was bored. He wanted to play. He faked his death in the first round. The show's thesis: the rich treat the poor as entertainment. Capitalism is a game rigged for those who already won.

One season, nine episodes, season two coming. Squid Game is a phenomenon—not just for its violence, but for its clarity. It says what many feel: the system is broken, the poor are desperate, the rich are cruel. "You're all the same," Gi-hun tells the organizers. They are. He wins. He loses everyone he cared about. He doesn't spend the money. He can't. He's broken. Season two is promised. But the first season said everything it needed to say.
