What It Feels Like to Watch

Squid Game is South Korean survival drama that became a global phenomenon. 456 people in desperate financial circumstances are invited to play children's games. The prize is 45.6 billion won (about $38 million). The catch: if you lose, you die. The games are simple—Red Light Green Light, tug of war, marbles—but the stakes transform them into horrors.

Seong Gi-hun (Lee Jung-jae) is our protagonist—a gambling addict who owes money to loan sharks, can't pay his daughter's birthday party, and lives with his mother. He's not a hero; he's a failure who keeps failing. He joins the game because he has nothing left, and his journey through the games reveals both the worst and best of human nature.

The players wear green tracksuits, numbered 001-456. The guards wear pink jumpsuits with black masks showing a circle, triangle, or square indicating rank. The front man oversees it all from a modernist viewing room while VIPs watch from behind screens. The design is striking—bright colors, geometric shapes, playground aesthetics contrasted with industrial violence.

Each game eliminates players in batches—239 dead in Red Light Green Light alone. The survivors must decide whether to continue or take their chances in the outside world where their debts are waiting. Most return because the game offers something the real world doesn't: a chance, however small, at a different life.

The show's success came from its timing—a pandemic world watching a story about desperate people making desperate choices—and from its clarity. The games are simple to understand. The social commentary—capitalism's cruelty, inequality's consequences—is direct. The performances are committed. It's Korean cinema's tendency toward extreme emotion applied to a survival game format.
