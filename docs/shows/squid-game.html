What It Feels Like to Watch

Squid Game is a Korean survival drama about 456 desperate people playing children's games for life-changing money. The twist: losing means death. The first game is "Red Light, Green Light"—players must freeze when a giant doll turns around; movement means being shot. Within minutes, half the contestants are dead. The survivors are trapped in a secret facility, wearing numbered tracksuits, guarded by masked men in pink jumpsuits. The winner takes 45.6 billion won. Everyone else dies.

Lee Jung-jae plays Seong Gi-hun, a chauffeur with a gambling addiction who owes money to loan sharks. He's the show's moral center—not because he's good, but because he's human. He's selfish, cowardly, and desperate. So is everyone else. Oh Il-nam (O Yeong-su) is the elderly man who plays for fun. Cho Sang-woo (Park Hae-soo) is the childhood friend who sacrificed his morality. Kang Sae-byeok (Jung Ho-yeon) is the North Korean defector who needs money to rescue her mother. These aren't heroes. They're people capitalism failed.

The show's genius is its staging. The games are children's activities—marbles, tug-of-war, hopscotch—rendered in candy-colored sets that evoke playgrounds. The contrast between innocent fun and brutal murder is the show's visual thesis: capitalism is a game where losing means death, and the players never consented to play. The Front Man (Lee Byung-hun) runs the games for wealthy VIPs who watch for entertainment. This is class war as sport.

One season (nine episodes) released, with a second announced. The show was Netflix's most-watched series ever, and it deserves the attention. It's not subtle, but subtlety isn't the point. The point is: the system is rigged. "You can't trust people," Gi-hun learns. "But you have to try." He wins. He's destroyed. The game continues. Squid Game is horror as political commentary, and it's devastating.
