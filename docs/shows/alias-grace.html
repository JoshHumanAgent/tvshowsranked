Alias Grace

2017 • Netflix/CBC • 6 Episodes

Score: 7.55
Period, Drama

What It Feels Like

Grace Marks was convicted of murder in 1840s Canada. Did she do it? A doctor interviews her to find out. Margaret Atwood's novel adapted by Sarah Polley: memory as fiction, truth as mystery, the servant girl who may be innocent or may be playing everyone.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Sarah Gadon as Grace: the unreliable narrator, the possible murderer. Edward Holcroft as Dr. Jordan: the man trying to understand. The performances capture the ambiguity—Grace may be innocent, may be cunning, may be both.

World Building (9/10)

1840s Canada: the prison, the household, the class system. The world is specific—the servant's position, the master's power, the woman's lack of options.

Cinematography (8/10)

Netflix/CBC production: period detail, the domestic horror, the interviews. The style serves the ambiguity—nothing is certain.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

No spectacle. This is memory and interview: Grace tells her story, we decide if we believe her. The "spectacle" is psychological.

Conceptual Density (9/10)

Memory, truth, class and gender, what we tell ourselves vs. what happened, the stories women tell to survive. Atwood's themes adapted faithfully.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Did Grace kill her employers? Each interview reveals more, each memory is questioned. The structure is mystery—the truth is elusive.

Narrative Path & Resolution (9/10)

Complete miniseries. The truth remains ambiguous—the ending is appropriate to the material. Did she or didn't she? The show doesn't answer. Masterful.
