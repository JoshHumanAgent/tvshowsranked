What It Feels Like to Watch

Alias is J.J. Abrams' breakthrough: spy thriller as serial, with a female lead who can fight. Sydney Bristow (Jennifer Garner) is a graduate student recruited by SD-6, which she believes is a covert CIA branch. It's not—it's a criminal organization. When she learns the truth, she becomes a double agent, working for the real CIA to bring down SD-6. "Truth takes time," her handler Vaughn (Michael Vartan) tells her. Everything is a lie. Everyone has secrets. Trust no one.

The show's hook is propulsive: each episode, Sydney goes undercover, wears disguises, fights her way out. Jennifer Garner is perfect: athletic, vulnerable, determined. She carries the show. The supporting cast includes her father Jack (Victor Garber), also a double agent, and Arvin Sloane (Ron Rifkin), SD-6's leader, who may believe he's doing good. The mythology deepens: Rambaldi, aNostradamus-like prophet, left artifacts that predict the future. Everyone wants them. The conspiracy spans centuries.

Seasons one through three are peak Alias. Season two's post-Super Bowl episode reinvents the show: Sydney wakes up in Hong Kong, two years missing, everyone believes her dead. What happened? The mystery drives season three. The show's thesis: identity is constructed. Sydney learns her mother was a KGB spy, her father lied, her friends betrayed her. Who is she when everything she believed was false? The answer: she's still Sydney. Still fighting. Still believing in something.

Five seasons, 105 episodes, complete. But seasons four and five decline—mythology collapses, reinventions fail. The first three seasons are one of television's great spy thrillers: twisty propulsive, emotionally grounded. "I'm not who you think I am," someone says every episode. "Neither am I," Sydney could reply. She learns, adapts, survives. That's the spy's art. And hers.

Narrative Drive (9): Twisty, propulsive, addictive. Each episode ends on a hook. The two-year mystery is brilliant.

Characters & Acting (8): Jennifer Garner is iconic as Sydney. Victor Garber's Jack Bristow is equally compelling. Great ensemble.

Resolution (6): Seasons 1-3 are complete arc. Seasons 4-5 decline. The full series ending is uneven.
