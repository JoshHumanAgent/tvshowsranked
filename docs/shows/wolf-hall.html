What It Feels Like to Watch

Wolf Hall is the best period drama ever made about politics. Hillary Mantel's novels, adapted for television by Peter Straughan, tell the story of Thomas Cromwell not as a villain but as a survivor—the blacksmith's son who became the most powerful man in England, only to be consumed by the system he mastered.

Mark Rylance plays Cromwell as a man of extraordinary restraint. His face rarely moves. His voice stays soft. He watches everything, says little, and manipulates everything. It's a performance of stillness—power expressed through patience. When Cromwell finally moves, the effect is shocking because we've seen how much effort it takes to provoke him.

The series covers Henry VIII's divorce from Catherine of Aragon, his marriage to Anne Boleyn, and the beginning of the English Reformation. But these events are backdrop. The foreground is always Cromwell navigating a court where one wrong word means death. Damian Lewis plays Henry as a man whose whims are law, whose anger is lethal, whose favor must be constantly maintained. Claire Foy plays Anne Boleyn as exactly what she was: a woman who understood the game and played it with everything she had.

The production design is remarkable for what it doesn't do. There are no sweeping shots of palaces, no elaborate costumes paraded for the viewer. Everything is candlelit and close. The camera stays in rooms, often in corners, watching faces. The effect is intimate and claustrophobic—you feel the pressure of the court, the danger of being seen, the isolation of power.

The second season, The Mirror and the Light, arrived in 2024 and completed Cromwell's story. His fall is as methodical as his rise, and the series captures the terrible inevitability of a man who knows the system will turn on him but can't stop running it.

Characters & Acting (10): Mark Rylance's Cromwell is among television's great performances. The supporting cast—Damian Lewis, Claire Foy, Jonathan Pryce, Anton Lesser—is uniformly excellent.

Conceptual Density (9): The series explores power, religion, class, and survival. How does a nobody become indispensable? How long can you ride a tiger? The questions are dramatized, not discussed.

Resolution (9): The second season provides closure. Cromwell's fall is as masterfully rendered as his rise. The end is both inevitable and heartbreaking.

Key Episodes

"Three Card Trick" (S1E1) — The introduction. Cromwell's tragedy begins with loss.

"Anna Regina" (S1E4) — Anne becomes queen. Cromwell's position is secured, but at what cost?

"Light" (S2E6) — The end. The scaffold. A life of calculation concludes.
