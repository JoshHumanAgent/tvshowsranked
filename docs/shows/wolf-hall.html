What It Feels Like to Watch

Wolf Hall premiered on BBC Two in January 2015, adapted from Hilary Mantel's Booker Prize-winning novels by Peter Straughan. The series depicts the rise of Thomas Cromwell (Mark Rylance) in the court of Henry VIII (Damian Lewis), from blacksmith's son to the king's closest advisor during the English Reformation. Across two seasons (2015's Wolf Hall and 2024's Wolf Hall: The Mirror and the Light, totaling 12 episodes), the series traces Cromwell's role in Henry's divorce from Catherine of Aragon, marriage to Anne Boleyn, and eventual execution for treason.

The series is distinguished by its interiority—we see events through Cromwell's eyes, understand his calculations, witness his grief and ambition. Mantel's novels revolutionized historical fiction by centering Cromwell, traditionally a villain in the Thomas More narrative, as protagonist. The adaptation maintains this revisionism, depicting Cromwell as brilliant, ruthless, and human—capable of cruelty and tenderness, using his power to help enemies of the old order (the poor, the reformers) while serving a dangerous king.

Mark Rylance's performance is the series' gravitational center—his Cromwell says little, observes constantly, his face revealing calculations and emotions invisible to courtiers focused on ceremony. The "Book of Henry"—Cromwell remembering the king's moods, recording slights, tracking favors—becomes visible in Rylance's silences, his stillness, his sudden movements. It's a masterclass in screen acting that earned Rylance a BAFTA and made him a star.

The supporting cast is exceptional: Damian Lewis's Henry—physically impressive, paranoid, self-pitying, terrifying; Claire Foy's Anne Boleyn—ambitious, desperate, doomed; Anton Lesser's Thomas More—principled and cruel; Jonathan Pryce's Cardinal Wolsey—fallen from grace, Cromwell's mentor. The court is rendered as dangerous machine, with Cromwell navigating its gears until the machine turns on him.

Critical reception was overwhelming—100% Rotten Tomatoes for the first season, with praise for Rylance, the production design, and Mantel's adaptation. The second season (2024), delayed by COVID and Mantel's death in 2022, completed Cromwell's story with equal acclaim. The series is quiet, slow, intensely focused—television as historical art rather than costume drama.

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.70

Thomas Cromwell: Mark Rylance delivers one of television's great performances—restrained, intelligent, dangerous, human. His Cromwell is always watching, always calculating, his stillness more eloquent than dialogue. The character's journey from Wolsey's man to Anne's destroyer to victim is tracked through Rylance's physical performance.

Henry VIII: Damian Lewis brings unexpected depth to a familiar figure—his Henry is physically powerful but psychologically fragile, paranoid, self-pitying, terrifying in his rages and his silences.

Anne Boleyn: Claire Foy plays ambition's cost—Anne's desperation for the throne, her miscarriages, her final failure to produce a son, her execution. Foy makes Anne both victim and architect of her fate.

Why 8.5: Exceptional central performances, particularly Rylance's masterpiece. The limitation is intimate focus—fewer characters deeply developed compared to ensemble shows.
