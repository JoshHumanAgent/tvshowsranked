Preacher

2016 • AMC • 43 Episodes

Score: 7.05
Fantasy, Dark Comedy, Horror

What It Feels Like

Jesse Custer, small-town Texas preacher, merges with a supernatural entity that gives him the power of command—Genesis, offspring of angel and demon. Along with his ex-girlfriend Tulip andIrish vampire Cassidy, he searches for God who has abandoned Heaven. Garth Ennis's blasphemous comic becomes television that makes no apologies. This is religious satire, ultraviolent western, and heartfelt romance simultaneously. The show believes in its absurdity—the Saint of Killers, Arseface, the Grail conspiracy—all of it rendered with conviction and gallons of blood.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (8/10)

Dominic Cooper plays Jesse as earnest despite everything. Ruth Negga's Tulip is chaotic brilliance—every scene alive with her energy. Joseph Gilgun's Cassidy steals every moment as the most lovable degenerate vampire in fiction. The three-way dynamic (preacher, ex-girlfriend, vampire) creates genuine emotional stakes amid the blasphemy.

World Building (8/10)

Seth Rogen and Evan Goldberg adapted Ennis's comic faithfully: Heaven as corporate bureaucracy, Hell as literal, the Grail as secret society protecting divine bloodline. The logic is absurd but internally consistent. Texas small town, New Orleans, and Hell itself all feel inhabited. The rules (angel-demon hybrids, vampire weaknesses, Genesis's power) are established and explored.

Cinematography (7/10)

Visuals match comic aesthetic—garish, bloody, stylized without being artificial. The action sequences embrace practical effects where possible. The Saint of Killers sequences are genuinely terrifying. Hell has distinct production design. TV budget shows but never ruins.

Visual Spectacle (7/10)

Blood. So much blood. Exploding heads, dismemberments, vampire feedings—the show doesn't flinch. But spectacle serves story: when Jesse uses Genesis, when Cassidy fights, when the Saint arrives, the violence is character expression. The effects budget stretches further than expected.

Conceptual Density (7.5/10)

Free will versus divine plan. The problem of evil when God has literally abandoned creation. Whether faith can survive direct contact with its object. The show treats religion seriously enough to mock it thoroughly. Jesse's crisis isn't comedic; it's existential. The blasphemy has purpose.

Narrative Drive (7/10)

Season 1: Find God. Season 2: Run from the Saint. Season 3: Save a soul from Hell. Season 4: Final confrontation. Each season has clear goals with digressions that expand mythology. Pacing varies—the comic's structure took adaptation time.

Narrative Path & Resolution (7/10)

Four seasons complete the comic's main arc. Jesse finds God. Everything resolves—messily, bloodily, with heart. The final season compressed comic content but landed the landing. The show ended deliberately, not cancelled. Satisfying for fans; standalone coherent for newcomers.
