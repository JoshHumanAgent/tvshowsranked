What It Feels Like to Watch

Deadwood is Shakespeare in the mud. David Milch's revisionist Western is set in a South Dakota mining camp in 1876, legally outside the United States and operating by its own rules. There's no law except what people enforce themselves. Gold has been discovered, and everyone—miners, prostitutes, businessmen, criminals—is trying to get rich without getting killed. It's a laboratory of human behavior: civilization emerging from chaos, order imposed on wilderness.

The language is legendary. Milch created a poetic vernacular that blends Elizabethan rhythms with frontier profanity—characters speak in elaborate, compound sentences punctuated with "cocksucker" and "motherfucker." It shouldn't work. It does. The dialogue becomes a kind of music, character revealed through syntax. Al Swearengen (Ian McShane), the camp's criminal kingpin, is a tyrant, a murderer, a pimp—and also the closest thing Deadwood has to a protector. His monologues, delivered to the severed head of a Sioux chief he keeps in a box, are among television's most extraordinary writing.

The ensemble is massive—over twenty regular characters—and every one feels essential. Timothy Olyphant's Seth Bullock is the lawman who resists his own violence. Molly Parker's Alma Garret is the widow who finds strength in addiction and enterprise. Robin Weigert's Calamity Jane is a drunk who can't stop caring. Brad Dourif's Doc Cochran is a healer haunted by the bodies he's seen. Everyone carries damage. Everyone is trying to survive.

Three seasons, thirty-six episodes, canceled too soon. The 2019 movie wrapped the story, thirteen years later, but nothing could recapture the original's density and rhythm. Deadwood is about the founding of America—the greed, the violence, the desperate attempts to create community in a world without rules. It's ugly, beautiful, and unlike anything else in television history.
