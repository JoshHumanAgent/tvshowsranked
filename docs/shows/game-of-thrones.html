What It Feels Like to Watch

Game of Thrones is an epic fantasy about families fighting for control of the Iron Throne. Based on George R.R. Martin's novels, it's not high fantasy—it's political drama with swords. The Starks of Winterfell, the Lannisters of Casterly Rock, the Targaryens across the sea, and dozens of other houses scheme, ally, betray, and die. "When you play the game of thrones, you win or you die," Cersei Lannister explains. She's right.

The show's genius is its scope: seven kingdoms, three continents, dozens of storylines. Ned Stark (Sean Bean) is the honorable lord who dies because he's too honorable. His death establishes the show's thesis: virtue is punished, ruthlessness rewarded. Daenerys Targaryen (Emilia Clarke) hatches dragons, frees slaves, and slowly becomes the tyrant she swore to destroy. Jon Snow (Kit Harington) is the secret heir to the throne who never gets it. Tyrion Lannister (Peter Dinklage) is the brilliant dwarf who drinks and knows things—and serves whoever's winning.

For six seasons, GoT was the most ambitious show on television. "The Rains of Castamere" (the Red Wedding) is the most shocking hour in TV history. "Battle of the Bastards" is the greatest battle ever filmed. "The Winds of Winter" isperfect storytelling. Then season seven compressed. Season eight collapsed. Daenerys's turn to villainy was rushed. The Night King's defeat was anticlimactic. Bran the Broken became king. The ending broke the internet—and not in a good way.

Eight seasons, seventy-three episodes, complete. Game of Thrones was a cultural phenomenon, then it became a cautionary tale. The journey was extraordinary. The destination was a betrayal. "The world is built on murder," a character says. GoT proved it. The show's legacy: television can be as spectacular as film—if it sticks the landing.
