What It Feels Like to Watch

Game of Thrones was the last true monoculture television event—everyone watched it, everyone talked about it, and the internet broke whenever something shocking happened. Adapted from George R.R. Martin's unfinished fantasy series, the show imagined a world where anyone could die at any moment, where heroes were just people who hadn't been betrayed yet, where power was a game played by families who'd been killing each other for generations.

The early seasons are masterpieces of adaptation. Ned Stark (Sean Bean) is introduced as a hero and executed in season one—the show's thesis statement. Daenerys Targaryen (Emilia Clarke) builds an army across the sea, Tyrion Lannister (Peter Dinklage) survives on wit, and the Stark children scatter across Westeros. The world-building is astonishing: Seven Kingdoms, three continents, dragons, ice zombies, magic that feels rare and dangerous. Every house has a history, every character has layers.

The show's peak is seasons three through six: the Red Wedding, the Battle of the Bastards, Cersei's walk of shame, Jon Snow's death and resurrection. The production values are cinema-quality, the battles are epics, the political intrigue is Shakespearean. Then the show outpaced Martin's books, and the quality collapsed. Seasons seven and eight feel like different shows—rushed, character-assassinating, logic-defying. Daenerys becomes a genocidal tyrant in two episodes. Jaime's redemption arc is abandoned. The Night King dies in one battle.

Eight seasons, seventy-three episodes, a finale that alienated millions. Game of Thrones is a tragedy of ambition: It built the greatest television world ever created, then burned it down without a satisfying ending. "You win or you die," the show said. Game of Thrones did both.
