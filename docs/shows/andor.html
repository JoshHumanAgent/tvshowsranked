What It Feels Like to Watch

Andor premiered on Disney+ on September 21, 2022, created by Tony Gilroy (Michael Clayton, Rogue One). Set five years before Rogue One: A Star Wars Story, the series follows Cassian Andor (Diego Luna) from reluctant outlaw to committed revolutionary. Unlike previous Star Wars series, Andor is not for children—it's a slow-burn spy thriller about the mechanics of rebellion, the cost of resistance, and the compromises required to fight an empire.

The series begins with Andor as a cynical thief on the planet Ferrix, covering up an accidental death. He's not heroic; he's not interested in rebellion. But through circumstance—his mother's death, encounters with rebel recruiter Luthen Rael (Stellan Skarsgård), witnessing imperial brutality—he's drawn into the growing Alliance. The narrative moves between Ferrix (his home), Coruscant (the galactic capital where Luthen operates), and various rebel operations (Aldhani heist, prison break on Narkina 5).

What distinguishes Andor is its patience. Episodes don't rush toward action; they build atmosphere, character, and moral complexity. The second episode depicts Andor walking through a town for fifteen minutes, establishing place and pace. The Aldhani theft sequence (episodes 5-6) spends an hour on preparation before the heist itself. The Narkina 5 prison arc (episodes 8-10) depicts the horror of manufactured incarceration with a restraint that makes it more terrifying than any violence could. Gilroy trusts viewers to pay attention, to sit with discomfort, to connect dots across episodes rather than having them explained.

The Empire in Andor is not the moustache-twirling evil of the films but bureaucratic oppression—the petty cruelty of officials, the systemic violence of capitalism, the banality of galactic tyranny. We see the Imperial Security Bureau (ISB) in meetings, debating how to suppress dissent. We see corporate exploitation on Ferrix, the prison industrial complex of Narkina 5, the surveillance state of Coruscant. The series understands that evil is often procedural, that fascism arrives in paperwork and budget meetings.

The ensemble cast is exceptional: Stellan Skarsgård as Luthen Rael, the conflicted spymaster sacrificing everything for the cause; Kyle Soller as Syril Karn, the ISB officer whose ambition masks insecurity; Adria Arjona as Bix Caleen, Andor's friend caught in the crossfire; Fiona Shaw as Maarva Andor, Cassian's adoptive mother whose final speech becomes a call to arms; Denise Gough as Dedra Meero, the ISB officer whose competence makes her terrifying. Each character is complex, contradictory, human.

Season 1's 12 episodes build to the Ferrix uprising—a civilian rebellion sparked by Maarva's funeral, suppressed by imperial troops, witnessed by Andor as he's rescued by Luthen. Season 2 (2025) will continue Andor's journey to Rogue One. Critical reception was exceptional: 96% Rotten Tomatoes, with critics calling it "mature television," "the Star Wars series for people who hate Star Wars," and "proof that franchise TV can be art."

7-Dimension Algorithmic Analysis

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.76

Cassian Andor: Diego Luna returns from Rogue One to play the younger Andor, and his performance is transformative—we see a man who becomes the hero we met in the film through pain and choice. Luna plays Andor's cynicism, his grief over his adoptive mother's death, his gradual political awakening with subtle physicality. The character's arc is from "I don't care about your rebellion" to "I will die for this cause," and Luna makes each step believable.

Luthen Rael: Stellan Skarsgård creates one of Star Wars' most compelling characters—a spymaster who has sacrificed his soul for the rebellion. His speech to Andor about "wearing the armor of the enemy" and his confession to the captured ISB officer about "the day we stop caring about individual lives" are among television's finest moments of moral complexity. Skarsgård's gravitas masks Luthen's terror; his control masks his desperation.

The Ensemble: Kyle Soller's Syril Karn is the series' most unexpected achievement—a man who joins the ISB for self-worth, whose incompetence is heartbreaking, whose eventual ruthlessness is earned. Adria Arjona's Bix suffers terribly and refuses to break. Fiona Shaw's Maarva delivers the season's emotional climax in her pre-recorded funeral speech. Denise Gough's Dedra Meero makes fascism human, and therefore more horrifying.

Comparative Assessment: Breaking Bad (9) achieved greater protagonist depth. Andor matches it in ensemble complexity, particularly the Luthen-Andor relationship and the ISB characters. The Wire (9) achieved similar ensemble density through 60 episodes; Andor approaches it in 12.

Why 8.8: Exceptional ensemble acting—Luna, Skarsgård, Shaw, Gough, and Soller all delivering career-highlight work. The limitation is being only one season complete (of planned two); character arcs are still developing.

World Building
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

The Empire as Bureaucracy: Andor's central achievement is depicting the Empire as institutional evil—the ISB meeting rooms, the corporate offices, the prison bureaucracy. We see the Empire not as villains but as a system that rewards cruelty, that processes people into numbers, that maintains power through paperwork and procedure.

Ferrix: Andor's home planet is rendered with industrial specificity—metal workers, salvage dealers, the foundry where he grew up, the community that protects him. The planet's culture (the funerals, the bell-ringing, the collective identity) makes the final uprising earned rather than manufactured.

Coruscant: The galactic capital is shown as a surveillance state—every citizen tracked, every conversation potentially monitored, luxury masking oppression. Luthen's operation, hidden beneath an antiques shop, suggests how resistance survives—through the cracks of the system.

Narkina 5: The prison planet is the series' most horrifying creation—an industrial facility where prisoners manufacture components for the Death Star (though they don't know it), competing against other shifts for basic comforts. The world-building here is economic: the prison runs on terror and competition, extracting maximum labor through psychological manipulation.

Comparative Assessment: The Wire (9.5) achieved more comprehensive institutional world-building. Andor matches it in depicting the mechanics of oppression and resistance. Few science fiction series have achieved this level of political world-building.

Why 9: Exceptional world-building that expands Star Wars into mature political territory. The bureaucratic Empire, the oppressed communities, the mechanics of rebellion—all rendered with specificity. The limitation is occasional fan-service (references to other Star Wars material) that slightly undermines the standalone vision.

9
Cinematography
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

The Look of Oppression: Andor's cinematography (by various directors of photography across the season) eschews Star Wars gloss for grounded realism. Coruscant is shot in grays and blues—sterile, cold, bureaucratic. Ferrix is rust and metal—working-class, lived-in, real. Narkina 5 is white and fluorescent—clinical, terrifying.

The Aldhani Sequence: The heist episodes achieve cinematic grandeur worthy of a film—the Scottish highlands standing in for an alien world, the preparation and execution shot with patience and scale. The visual storytelling here matches the series' thematic weight.

Ferrix: The industrial sets, the foundry, the streets—every location feels physically real, constructed rather than green-screened. This physicality grounds the series' politics; these are real places where real people live and die.

Comparative Assessment: Breaking Bad (10) achieved greater visual distinction. Andor matches it in quality, bringing cinematic ambition to television. The Mandalorian (7) had glossy but less substantive visuals; Andor prioritizes meaning over beauty.

Why 9: Exceptional cinematography that serves world-building and theme. The color palettes distinguish locations; the camera work is patient and expressive. The limitation is occasional moments where the Disney+ budget shows; some locations feel more constructed than lived-in.

7.5
Visual Spectacle
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.75

The Aldhani Heist: The series' primary set piece—the theft from an Imperial payroll—provides spectacle appropriate to the series' tone. It's grounded, tactical, based on preparation rather than superhuman ability. The visual scale is impressive without being fantastical.

The Ferrix Uprising: The season finale's civil unrest, with citizens battling imperial troopers, achieves visual power through scale—hundreds of extras, real locations, practical effects. The violence is brutal, not spectacular.

Comparative Assessment: Game of Thrones (9.5) or The Expanse (9) provide genuine spectacle—battles, dragons, spaceship combat. Andor is deliberately grounded; its power comes from performance and writing, not visual scale. The Mandalorian has more Star Wars spectacle but less substance.

Why 7.5: Moderate spectacle appropriate to the series' grounded tone. The Aldhani heist and Ferrix uprising provide memorable moments. The limitation is that Andor deliberately avoids the spectacular—it's about people, not pyrotechnics.

9.2
Conceptual Density
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.38

The Nature of Rebellion: Andor explores what resistance requires—Luthen's speech about becoming "the monster they'll fear" interrogates whether revolution can be moral. The series suggests that fighting evil requires becoming somewhat evil yourself; that survival requires compromise; that the Alliance is built on terrible choices.

Fascism and Bureaucracy: The ISB scenes depict how authoritarianism functions—not through charismatic villains but through bureaucrats competing for resources, justifying cruelty as security. Meero's speeches about "peace through order" could be from any surveillance state.

The Personal as Political: Andor's revolution begins with personal grievance—his mother's death, his treatment by imperial police—before expanding to political consciousness. The series respects how people actually change: slowly, through experience, not through speeches.

Prisons and Labor: The Narkina 5 arc explicitly connects the Empire's prison system to slave labor, manufacturing the weapons of its own oppression. This is political cinema—critique of the prison industrial complex disguised as space opera.

Comparative Assessment: The Wire (10) achieved comparable institutional critique. Andor brings similar depth to science fiction. Chernobyl (8) depicted institutional evil through bureaucracy; Andor matches it while adding revolutionary politics.

Why 9.2: Exceptional thematic exploration of fascism, resistance, and moral compromise. The series brings genuine political sophistication to franchise entertainment. The limitation is occasional moments where the Star Wars universe constrains what can be said (the Empire is too obviously Nazi allegory, not allowing more subtle parallels).

9
Narrative Drive
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

The Three Arcs: Season 1 is structured in three movements: Ferrix (Andor's home and introduction), Aldhani (the heist that escalates everything), and Narkina 5/Imperial intrigue (the consequences and escalation). Each builds on the previous, with the political and personal converging in the finale.

Three Episode Rule: The series demands patience—the full opening arc (three episodes) is required for the series to click. Gilroy has said the season was designed for streaming (binge-watching) while being released weekly. The structure works for both: individual episodes have integrity while building toward cumulative effect.

The ISB Plot: The Coruscant scenes, depicting the Empire's response to Andor's activities, provide tension and irony. We know more than the ISB does; we watch them slowly realize what we know. This creates Hitchcockian suspense.

Comparative Assessment: Breaking Bad (10) achieved tighter narrative construction. Andor's patience is deliberate but may test some viewers. The payoff, however, is substantial—the finale earns its emotional power through twelve episodes of buildup.

Why 9: Strong narrative construction with effective three-act structure. The slow burn pays off in emotional weight. The limitation is the three-episode barrier to entry and occasional pacing that may feel slow to viewers expecting action.

8.5
Narrative Resolution
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.85

The Rising: The finale, "Rix Road," depicts the Ferrix uprising and Andor's final choices. He could flee; instead, he stays to witness his mother's funeral, participates in the rebellion, and is rescued by Luthen. The episode suggests he's now committed to the cause.

Luthen and Dedra: The final sequences—Luthen realizing Andor is his man, Dedra's promotion and increased power, the ISB's awareness of the growing threat—set up Season 2 while providing satisfaction for Season 1. The series is part of a larger story (leading to Rogue One) but provides standalone completion.

The Prison Break: The resolution of the Narkina 5 arc—Kino's speech, the escape, Kino's fate—is devastating and earned. The series isn't afraid to leave plot threads hanging (Kino's ultimate fate, Melshi's story) but resolves its immediate tensions.

Comparative Assessment: As an ongoing series, Andor provides interim resolution. Breaking Bad and Better Call Saul (10s) achieved definitive conclusions. Andor's open-ended structure is appropriate to its nature but cannot match perfect finales.

Why 8.5: Solid resolution to Season 1's arcs while setting up Season 2. The Ferrix uprising provides emotional climax. The limitation is being incomplete (part of multi-season structure) and a few threads left unresolved.
