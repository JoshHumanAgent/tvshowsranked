What It Feels Like to Watch

Behind Her Eyes is psychological thriller with a twist—one that became the show's entire reputation. Louise (Simona Brown), a single mother, begins an affair with her boss David (Tom Bateman), a psychiatrist. Meanwhile, she befriends his wife Adele (Eve Hewson), a beautiful, fragile woman with secrets. The triangle forms: Louise sleeps with David, visits Adele, lies to both. But Adele has her own agenda—and her own past, involving a friend named Rob (Robert Aramayo) and something called astral projection.

The show builds as standard noir-adjacent thriller: who is manipulating whom? Adele seems victim; David seems controlling; Louise seems interloper. Flashbacks reveal Adele's past in a psychiatric facility, her friendship with Rob, his death by overdose. Or was it murder? The present escalates: Louise discovers David's first wife died mysteriously; Adele teaches her astral projection; secrets pile up. The premise feels familiar—married man, lonely woman, dangerous wife—until it doesn't.

Then comes the twist, revealed over two final episodes: astral projection is real. Adele and Rob could leave their bodies; Adele switched bodies with Rob before he died. The Adele we've been watching is actually Rob, trapped in a woman's body, now planning to switch with Louise. The twist is audacious—it transforms the genre from psychological thriller to supernatural horror. Critics divided: some called it brilliant misdirection; others found it nonsensical, undermining everything before.

One season, six episodes, complete miniseries. Behind Her Eyes is a conversation piece—the twist that everyone discussed, debated, argued about. The production is slick, the performances committed, the tension genuine. But the supernatural element polarizes. "You have to earn your twist," critics said. Does astral projection earn it? The show believes so. Whether audiences agree depends on their tolerance for genre shifts. "Nothing is what it seems," the tagline says. True enough. What it is, exactly, remains debated.
