What It Feels Like to Watch

Daredevil is the Marvel Netflix show that proved street-level superheroes could work on television—and be better than the movies. Matt Murdock (Charlie Cox) is a blind lawyer in Hell's Kitchen who was blinded by radioactive waste as a child. The accident heightened his other senses to superhuman levels. By day, he's a defense attorney with his best friend Foggy Nelson (Elden Henson). By night, he's a vigilante in a black mask, hunting criminals the law can't touch.

The first season's hallway fight is legendary—a single-take brawl where Matt tears through a gang of human traffickers to save a boy. He's exhausted, injured, barely standing. It's brutal and beautiful, establishing the show's tone: grounded, violent, morally complicated. This isn't the MCU's quippy brightness. This is noir.

Vincent D'Onofrio's Wilson Fisk is one of television's great villains. He's not just a crime boss; he's a man who believes he's saving Hell's Kitchen by destroying it. His love for Vanessa humanizes him. His childhood trauma explains—doesn't excuse—him. When he and Matt finally face each other, it's two men who think they're the hero.

The show explores Catholic guilt, the corruption of institutions, and what justice means when the system fails. Matt's faith is central—he's a devout Catholic who beats people up at night and confesses in the morning. He's trying to be a good man in a city that rewards the corrupt.

Three seasons, each with a distinct arc. Season one establishes the world. Season two introduces the Punisher and Elektra. Season three brings Fisk back and gives Matt his most personal battle yet. The show was cancelled in the Marvel-Netflix divorce, revived by Disney+ years later, but those three seasons stand complete.
