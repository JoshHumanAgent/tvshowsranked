What It Feels Like to Watch

True Detective is an anthology—each season, different story, different characters, different setting. But season one is legendary: Matthew McConaughey and Woody Harrelson as Detectives Rust Cohle and Marty Hart, investigating a ritual murder in Louisiana across seventeen years. The show is Southern Gothic, noir, and metaphysical philosophy. Rust is a nihilist: "Time is a flat circle," he says. "Everything we've ever done or will do, we're gonna do over and over again." He's also a brilliant detective and a wreck of a human being.

The structure is daring: multiple timelines, 1995 and 2012, as Rust and Marty are interviewed separately about a case they thought was solved. The murder investigation leads to a cult, to powerful men, to crimes that have been covered up. Rust's philosophy—pessimistic, haunted, possibly correct—clashes with Marty's conventional morality. Marty cheats on his wife, but he believes in normal life. Rust has no such faith. "I think consciousness is a tragic misstep in evolution," he says. He'd rather not exist.

Season one is a masterpiece: the six-minute tracking shot in "Who Goes There" is famous; the performances are career-defining. Season two collapsed under its own weight—a convoluted noir in LA that couldn't follow season one's perfection. Season three recovered partially: Mahershala Ali as a detective across three timelines, searching for a missing child and, later, the truth. Season four ("Night Country") moved to Alaska, withJodie Foster investigating scientists who vanished. The seasons are uneven. But season one is among the best television ever made.

Four seasons, thirty-nine episodes, ongoing. True Detective proves that anthology works—and that lightning in a bottle is rare. Rust Cohle's philosophy is bleak: "If the only thing keeping a person decent is the reward of heaven, then that person is a piece of shit." But in the finale, he sees a vision—a cosmic joke, maybe, or real grace. "Once there was only dark," he says. "If you ask me, the light's winning." After all the darkness, that's almost hope.

Characters & Acting (10): McConaughey and Harrelson are legendary. Rust Cohle is one of TV's great characters. Mahershala Ali in S3 is also excellent.

Cinematography (10): The six-minute tracking shot. The Louisiana swamps. The Alaskan darkness. Visual poetry.

Conceptual Density (9): Nihilism as detective philosophy. "Time is a flat circle." Metaphysics as crime drama.
