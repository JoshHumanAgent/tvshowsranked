What It Feels Like to Watch

True Detective is an anthology—each season a new story, new characters, new setting. But season one is legendary. 2014: Detectives Rust Cohle (Matthew McConaughey) and Marty Hart (Woody Harrelson) investigate ritualistic murders in Louisiana. The case spans seventeen years: 1995, 2002, 2012. We see the investigation, the aftermath, the men broken by what they found. "Time is a flat circle," Cohle says. "Everything we've ever done or will do, we're gonna do over and over again."

Rust Cohle is one of television's great characters: a pessimist, a nihilist, a man who drinks himself to sleep because being awake is unbearable. He believes consciousness is a mistake—"nature's mistake in self-awareness." He sees clearly, and what he sees is horror. Marty Hart is his opposite:a family man who cheats, a Christian who sins, a normal guy who can't handle the darkness. Together they find the Yellow King, the cult of child murderers, the conspiracy stretching to the highest levels. "You're a bad man," Cohle tells a suspect. "I'm worse."

Season one's direction by Cary Joji Fukunaga is astounding: the six-minute tracking shot through a projects raid; the final confrontation in Carcosa; the nightmare imagery of twig sculptures and antler crowns. The show's thesis: evil exists, systems enable it, and confronting it destroys you. Cohle and Hart solve the case. They're also broken by it. The ending is surprisingly hopeful—Cohle, a nihilist, sees stars. "Once there was only dark," he says. "If you ask me, the light's winning."

Four seasons, thirty episodes, ongoing. Season one is essential. Seasons two and three are mixed; season four ("Night Country") returned to form. But nothing matches the original: McConaughey and Harrelson at their peak, Southern Gothic horror, philosophical depth. "This is a world where nothing is solved," Cohle says. He's right. But sometimes you find the killer.
