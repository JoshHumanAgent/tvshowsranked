What It Feels Like to Watch

Search Party is a comedy that becomes a thriller that becomes a courtroom drama that becomes a cult story. It reinvents itself every season while following the same characters deeper into darkness. Alia Shawkat plays Dory Sief, a listless Brooklynite who becomes obsessed with finding a missing college acquaintance. What starts as a search for meaning becomes a search for a body—or several.

The show's brilliance is in its genre fluidity. Season one is a dark comedy about narcissists doing something decent for the wrong reasons. Dory and her friends—self-absorbed Elliott (John Early), passive Drew (John Reynolds), and oblivious Portia (Meredith Hagner)—decide to investigate the disappearance of Chantal, a woman none of them really knew. Their search is an adventure, a way to escape their empty lives. They feel important for the first time.

Season two is something else entirely. It's a thriller about a cover-up. Season three is a courtroom drama. Season four is a cult story. The show keeps escalating the stakes while maintaining its dark humor and its unsparing critique of millennial narcissism. These are terrible people who keep doing terrible things while convinced of their own goodness.

Alia Shawkat is extraordinary. Her Dory starts as a sympathetic protagonist—a lost young woman searching for purpose—and becomes something frightening: a person who will do anything to maintain her self-image. The transformation is gradual, cumulative, and horrifying. By season four, you're watching a character study of guilt and denial.

The show was ahead of its time in skewering the particular narcissism of privileged young people who mistake aesthetic choices for politics, who treat self-improvement as self-obsession, who mean well and do harm. It's funny, then disturbing, then disturbingly funny.
