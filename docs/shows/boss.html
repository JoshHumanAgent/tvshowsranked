Boss

2011 - 2012 • Starz • 18 Episodes

Score: 7.15
Political Drama

What It Feels Like

Tom Kane is the mayor of Chicago. He's also dying of a degenerative neurological disease that he's hiding from everyone. Kelsey Grammer plays against type as the ruthless politician who controls everything while his body fails. Farhad Safinia created political Shakespeare: power, betrayal, legacy.

The 7-Dimension Analysis

Characters & Acting (9/10)

Kelsey Grammer as Tom Kane: terrifying, vulnerable, Shakespearean. This is Grammer like you've never seen him. The supporting cast (Connie Nielsen, Hannah Ware, Martin Donovan) matches his intensity. The performances are prestige television at its finest.

World Building (8/10)

Chicago politics: the machine, the alliances, the betrayals. The city is rendered as Kane's kingdom—everyone answers to the mayor. The political machinery is complete and corrupt.

Cinematography (7/10)

Starz production: Chicago photographed with political drama gravity, Kane's decline visualized with care. The style serves the performance—Kane is the center of every frame.

Visual Spectacle (3/10)

The spectacle is political: backroom deals, public betrayals, private moments of decline. The "spectacle" is Kane's body failing while his mind remains. Character drama.

Conceptual Density (8/10)

Power and its costs, the body vs. the legacy, whether we can outrun our sins, political corruption as way of life. The show explores Machiavelli through a dying king.

Narrative Drive (8/10)

Kane's secret: the disease, the political enemies, the family betrayal. Each episode escalates the danger. The structure is Shakespearean tragedy—the fall is inevitable, the question is how.

Narrative Path & Resolution (5/10)

Two seasons, cancelled. The story is incomplete—Kane's fall was planned but not finished. The cancellation denied the full tragedy. What exists is excellent; what's missing hurts.
