What It Feels Like to Watch

Freaks and Geeks is the best teenage drama ever made, and it lasted one season. NBC didn't know what to do with it—it was too real, too painful, too honest about how much high school hurts. It's 1980 in suburban Detroit, and the Weir siblings are navigating the social hierarchies of William McKinley High School on opposite ends of the spectrum.

Lindsay Weir (Linda Cardellini) is a former mathlete who's started hanging out with the "freaks"—the burnouts, the stoners, the kids who don't care about grades. Sam Weir (John Francis Daley) is a freshman still playing with GI Joes, navigating junior high with his fellow geeks Neil (Samm Levine) and Bill (Martin Starr). The show moves between their worlds—Lindsay's existential crisis and Sam's daily humiliations.

The freaks include Daniel (James Franco), the charismatic stoner with secret insecurity; Ken (Seth Rogen), the sarcastic cynic; Nick (Jason Segel), the drummer who falls hard for Lindsay; and Kim (Busy Philipps), the tough girl who Lindsay wants to be friends with despite everything. The performances are raw and natural—these feel like real teenagers, not TV teenagers.

The geeks face different challenges—bullying from the popular kids, parents who don't understand, girls who don't notice them. The show takes them seriously. Neil's parents' divorce devastates him. Bill's allergy to peanuts becomes a life-or-death episode. Sam's attempts to be cool fail in ways that hurt.

Judd Apatow produced, and the cast reads like a who's who of future comedy stars—Franco, Rogen, Segel, Starr, all in their teens or early twenties. The show is their origin, and it's also the most emotionally honest high school show ever made. It doesn't flinch from the awkwardness, the cruelty, the uncertainty of being young.

World Building (9): 1980 Detroit suburbia feels real. The details—the clothes, the music, the awkward family dinners—are exactly right.
