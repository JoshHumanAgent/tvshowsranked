What It Feels Like to Watch

Freaks and Geeks is the show that got away. Eighteen episodes aired (three burned off months later), and then it was gone—too smart, too different, too committed to the awkward reality of being a teenager. Set in a Michigan high school in 1980-81, it follows two siblings: Lindsay Weir (Linda Cardellini), a former mathlete trying to reinvent herself as a burnout; and her younger brother Sam (John Frances Daley), a geek navigating the horrors of adolescence with his equally awkward friends.

The production design is meticulous—this is 1980, complete with bad hair, worse clothes, and a cultural landscape that still believes disco might come back. But the show's real achievement is emotional accuracy. Writer/producer Judd Apatow and creator Paul Feig built the series from their own experiences, and every episode contains at least one moment of recognition: the humiliation of wearing the wrong thing, the desperate desire to fit in, the discovery that the cool kids are just as lost as you are.

The cast is now legendary. James Franco, Seth Rogen, Jason Segel, Busy Philipps, Martin Starr—all young, all unformed, all delivering performances that would launch careers. But it's Cardellini who holds the series together, a former good girl drawn to the freaks not because they're cool, but because they're honest about their alienation. Her friendship with Kim (Philipps), the terrifying burnout girl, becomes the show's most surprising relationship—and the most real.

The finale, unaired during the original run, is perfect. Lindsay ditches an academic summer program to follow the Grateful Dead with Kim. It's a terrible decision, made for the right reasons. That's the whole show: young people making terrible decisions for the right reasons, learning to live with the consequences. Eighteen episodes. A career launcher. A cult classic. Cancelled before its time, perfect in what it left behind.
