What It Feels Like to Watch

The Haunting of Hill House is a ghost story where the ghosts aren't the point. Mike Flanagan takes Shirley Jackson's novel and transforms it into a meditation on family, trauma, and the houses we can never leave. The Crain family moves into Hill House in 1992 to renovate and flip it. Five children, two parents, a mansion that wants them to stay forever. We know from the start that the mother doesn't survive. What we don't know is everyone who does, and what surviving costs them.

The series moves between two timelines: the children's summer in Hill House, and their adult lives twenty-six years later, scattered and broken. Steven became a writer who exploits his family's trauma. Shirley runs a funeral home and can't forgive her mother's death. Theo uses her gift for sensing as a child psychologist but numbs herself with gloves and detachment. Luke spirals into addiction. Nell carries a sleep disorder that's always been with her. And Hugh, the father, holds a secret about what really happened that final night.

Flanagan's horror is patient. Ghosts appear in the background, often unnoticed until you look again. The famous "bent-neck lady" sequence is as good as television horror gets—a revelation that recontextualizes everything you thought you knew. The sixth episode, "Two Storms," is a technical marvel: long takes that move through time and space, actors playing the same characters across decades in single shots.

But what elevates Hill House beyond horror is its emotional core. These aren't just scare delivery systems—they're people carrying wounds that won't heal. The house feeds on grief. The ghosts are manifestations of what they can't let go. The final episodes offer a choice: stay with the ghosts forever, or leave them behind and choose the living. It's a choice about trauma itself. Do we remain haunted, or do we forgive ourselves for surviving?

Cinematography (10): Episode six, "Two Storms," is one of television's technical achievements. Long takes spanning decades, actors switching between young and old versions, all in camera. The entire series is visual storytelling at its finest.

Characters & Acting (9): The ensemble is uniformly excellent. Elizabeth Reaser, Oliver Jackson-Cohen, Kate Siegel, Victoria Pedretti—the adult children are each given full arcs. Henry Thomas and Timothy Hutton play the young and old Hugh with matching haunted intensity.

Resolution (9): The finale provides genuine closure. Choices are made. Ghosts are confronted. The Red Room's true nature is revealed. It's a satisfying ending that doesn't undo what came before.

Key Episodes

"The Bent-Neck Lady" (S1E5) — Nell's story. A revelation that devastates. One of horror television's crowning achievements.

"Two Storms" (S1E6) — Technical virtuosity. Long takes across decades. The past and present collapse into one stormy night.

"Silence Lay Steadily" (S1E10) — The finale. The Red Room opens. Choices are made. A family confronts what haunts them.
