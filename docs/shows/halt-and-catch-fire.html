What It Feels Like to Watch

Halt and Catch Fire is television's most underrated drama, a portrait of the personal computer revolution and the early internet told through three deeply flawed visionaries in Dallas's Silicon Prairie. Over four seasons, it charts the evolution from hardware to software to the web to search, always returning to the people who build things and why they build them—and whether building is enough.

Joe MacMillan (Lee Pace) is a former IBM executive obsessed with the future. Gordon Clark (Scoot McNairy) is an engineer whose genius has always come at the cost of his health and family. Cameron Howe (Mackenzie Davis) is a programmer whose code is art and whose anger is fuel. Together they build a portable computer, then a online gaming service, then a web browser, then a search engine. They fight, they reconcile, they fail, they try again.

The show's emotional center is Donna Clark (Kerry Bishé), Gordon's wife, a computer engineer who's always been smarter than everyone around her but keeps being overlooked. Her partnership with Cameron drives season two, their web browser company Mutiny becomes the show's heart, and their eventual falling out and later reconciliation gives the series its most satisfying character arc.

Four seasons, forty episodes, perfect ending. Cameron leaves the company she built. Joe realizes that the future doesn't need him. Gordon dies, too young, of health problems that were always there. The final scene: Donna gathers the original team one more time, and the show cuts to a title card describing what each person went on to do. "They built something," it seems to say. "It mattered." A beautiful meditation on creativity, ambition, and the people who can't stop chasing the next big thing.
