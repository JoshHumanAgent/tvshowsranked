What It Feels Like to Watch

Halt and Catch Fire is a show about people who build things. Set in the 1980s and 90s, it follows a trio of dreamers and engineers trying to revolutionize the computer industry—first PCs, then online services, finally web browsers. Joe MacMillan (Lee Pace) is a visionary and a con man; Gordon Clark (Scoot McNairy) is an engineer whose brilliance wars with his insecurity; Cameron Howe (Mackenzie Davis) is a coder who sees the future and can't imagine doing anything else.

The show begins in Texas, where Joe recruits Gordon and Cameron to reverse-engineer the IBM PC and build a clone that's smaller, faster, cheaper. It's 1983, and the personal computer revolution is still young. Over four seasons, the show moves through the rise of online services, gaming, search, and the early web. Each era brings new challenges, new failures, new betrayals. But the core remains: three people who need each other, even when they can't stand each other.

What makes Halt and Catch Fire special is its emotional intelligence. The technology matters—BBS systems, antivirus software, web browsers—but it's always in service of character. Gordon's health problems, his marriage, his need to create something that matters. Joe's childhood trauma, his desperate need to be seen, his genuine love for the people he exploits. Cameron's fear of success, her resistance to corporate structures, her gradual acceptance that she needs a team. The show understands that building things is easy; building relationships is hard.

The finale is perfect—not the triumph they expected, but the connection they needed. "I have an idea," Cameron says at the end, and you believe the cycle will start again. They failed, they succeeded, they failed again. But they did it together. That's the story.
