What It Feels Like to Watch

Killing Eve begins with a premise so simple it's almost reductive: a cat-and-mouse game between a spy and an assassin. But what elevates it beyond the spy thriller template is the charge between its leads. Sandra Oh's Eve Polastri is obsessed with female killers; Jodie Comer's Villanelle is a psychopath who develops an obsession right back. Their mutual fixation becomes the show's engine, a dance of attraction and violence that makes every confrontation crackle with erotic and lethal tension.

Created by Phoebe Waller-Bridge and adapted from the "Villanelle" novel series by Luke Jennings, Killing Eve premiered on BBC America on 8 April 2018, with the full series running through seventeen episodes concluding on 17 April 2022. The first series, written by Waller-Bridge at the peak of her powers following "Fleabag", delivers something genuinely fresh: a spy show that's funny, stylish, and female-centered without being reductive about any of those things. The humor isn't forced; it emerges from character. Villanelle's gleeful violence is played as comedy, but the comedy is rooted in genuine pathology. The European locations—Tuscany, Paris, Berlin, Bucharest, London, Barcelona—and fashion-forward aesthetic give the show visual distinction—this is spy fiction as lifestyle porn.

The cast extends beyond the central duo: Fiona Shaw provides dry wit as MI6 head Carolyn Martens; Kim Bodnia brings unexpected warmth as Villanelle's handler Konstantin Vasiliev; Harriet Walter appears as Villanelle's trainer Dasha in season three; and Camille Cottin commands the screen as Twelve operative Hélène. Each season brought a new female showrunner—Waller-Bridge, then Emerald Fennell, Suzanne Heathcote, and Laura Neal—a creative strategy that kept the series from calcifying but also meant it never quite consolidated its identity.

But the experience of watching Killing Eve across all four seasons reveals a show that couldn't sustain its initial brilliance. The writer changes after Season 1 are visible in the product—each subsequent season tries different approaches, none matching the first's deft balance of tone. The finale, widely criticized for perpetuating the "Bury Your Gays" trope, demonstrated the problem: a show that had built its identity on the Eve-Villanelle relationship couldn't find a satisfying way to resolve it. Villanelle's death on the bridge, moments after happiness seemed possible, earned the series a place on "worst TV finales" lists. The later seasons aren't bad television, but they're ordinary in a way the first season never was.

Still, the show earns its place in television history. Sandra Oh finally got the leading role her talent deserved, winning a Golden Globe and becoming the first Asian woman nominated for a Primetime Emmy for Lead Actress in a Drama Series. Jodie Comer announced herself as a major presence, winning the Emmy and a BAFTA. The costume design by Phoebe de Gaye, Charlotte Mitchell, and Sam Perry created a visual language that influenced a generation of spy-adjacent content—Villanelle's pink tulle Molly Goddard dress from "I'll Deal with Him Later" became a cultural touchstone that inspired red carpet fashion. David Holmes and Keefus Ciancia's score for the band Unloved provided the sonic signature. When it was good, Killing Eve was as entertaining as television gets. The tragedy is that "when it was good" was mostly just Season 1.

The series received numerous accolades, including the BAFTA for Best Drama Series. Both Oh and Comer won Golden Globes and Primetime Emmys for their performances. Comer and Fiona Shaw also received BAFTA TV Awards. Rotten Tomatoes scores tell the story: Season 1 (96%), Season 2 (92%), Season 3 (80%), Season 4 (53%)—the decline from revolutionary to controversial is charted in the critical response.

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.40

The Electric Duo: Jodie Comer's Villanelle is a creation of remarkable range—gleeful, terrifying, childish, seductive, often in the same scene. She plays a psychopath with genuine unpredictability; you believe she could do anything. The depth of her performance earned her an Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series, making her one of the youngest winners in that category. Sandra Oh's Eve provides the necessary anchor, her intelligence and obsession balanced by physical and emotional vulnerability. Oh's Golden Globe win and historic Emmy nomination for Lead Actress represented overdue recognition for one of television's most versatile performers.

The Supporting Cast: Beyond the central pair, Killing Eve developed supporting characters of intermittent interest. Fiona Shaw's Carolyn Martens provides delicious dry wit and moral ambiguity; Kim Bodnia's Konstantin offers warmth in an unlikely package. Harriet Walter brought physical presence as Dasha, Villanelle's former trainer. But the show's structure keeps these characters in functional roles—enablers of the central dynamic rather than fully realized presences. The inability to develop a compelling ensemble beyond the leads limited the show's emotional range.

The Challenge of Sustaining Chemistry: Across four seasons, the show struggled to evolve the Eve-Villanelle relationship without either consummation or resolution that would break the premise. The writers' room changes meant different visions of what the relationship meant—romantic, professional, intellectual, erotic—and the tonal whiplash damaged both characters. By Season 4, the mutual obsession had become repetitive rather than compelling.

Comparative Assessment: In the spy genre, The Americans (9.5) built an ensemble of equals where Philip and Elizabeth's relationship was deepened by a rich world around them. Slow Horses (8) demonstrates how to create memorable supporting characters in the spy world. Even Killing Eve's closest comparison, Hannibal (8.5), achieved greater psychological complexity in its central obsession. Killing Eve's strength is concentrated in two performances rather than distributed across a rich world.

Why 7: Two exceptional central performances that generate unique chemistry, elevated by award recognition and cultural impact. The score reflects quality at the top with acknowledged thinness below and the failure to evolve the central dynamic across four seasons.

World Building
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 0.98

The Spy Ecosystem: Killing Eve builds a convincing world of intelligence operations and contract killing. MI5, MI6, and the shadowy "Twelve" organization create institutional texture. The bureaucratic infighting at Eve's workplace contrasts effectively with Villanelle's freelance luxury. The show understands that modern espionage involves as much office politics as tradecraft, though it never explores the Twelve with the depth that would make them truly menacing.

The Villanelle Lifestyle: The show's distinctive aesthetic—European locations, high fashion, luxurious apartments—creates wish-fulfillment dimension. Villanelle's kills happen in beautiful places with beautiful clothes; the show makes assassination look aspirational. The production filmed across Tuscany, Paris, Berlin, Bucharest, London, Barcelona, and Margate, using real locations to create scope. This isn't documentary realism; it's stylish fantasy that serves the show's tone perfectly.

The Organization Problem: The Twelve, the shadowy organization employing Villanelle, remained frustratingly vague across four seasons. The show never established clear rules for how the organization functioned, who led it, or what its ultimate goals were. This vagueness allowed narrative flexibility but prevented the world from feeling fully realized. The final season's attempt to resolve the Twelve plotline felt rushed and unsatisfying precisely because the foundation hadn't been laid.

Comparative Assessment: The Americans (7.5) built deeper institutional detail with its KGB and FBI operations. Slow Horses (7) captures bureaucratic dysfunction more sharply. The Bureau (8) demonstrates how to build a comprehensive intelligence service with authentic procedures. Killing Eve's world serves its story without achieving landmark status in the genre.

Why 6.5: Effective spy-world construction with distinctive aesthetic personality. The world serves its story without achieving the depth that would earn higher marks. The vagueness around the Twelve organization costs the show points in this dimension.

Cinematography
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.05

Visual Identity: Killing Eve has one of the most distinctive visual styles in the spy genre—bright, colorful, fashion-forward where the genre traditionally favors muted tones and shadows. The European locations function as visual assets; Paris, Berlin, Rome, Barcelona, and various UK locations become characters. The cinematography by Gilles Nuttgens and others makes the show look expensive and knows it, with production values that elevated the series above standard television.

Set Piece Direction: The kill sequences and confrontations are staged with genuine flair. Villanelle's methods—increasingly elaborate and theatrical—give the show license to create memorable images. The directing in Season 1, from photographers like Harry Bradbeer and Jon East, demonstrates how to make action visually interesting without massive set pieces. The show's comic violence requires precise tone management, which the early directors provided expertly.

The Single-Camera Aesthetic: The show's single-camera setup allows for tight framing on character reactions and dynamic movement during action sequences. The visual language evolved across seasons, becoming more stylized and less grounded as the series progressed—a change that reflected the writing but sometimes worked against the show's initial realism.

Comparative Assessment: True Detective S1 (10) set the ceiling for TV cinematography. In the spy genre, The Night Manager (8) achieves similar visual sophistication. Killing Eve's visual distinction comes from stylistic choice rather than technical innovation, and the consistency declined across seasons as showrunners changed.

Why 7: Strong visual identity, effective use of locations, stylish execution. Not innovative cinematographically but distinctive within its genre. The score reflects the first season's excellence averaged against later decline.

Visual Spectacle
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.70

Assassin as Fashion: Killing Eve's approach to spectacle is unusual—its violence is intimate rather than massive. Villanelle's kills are set pieces, but they're personal: poisoned perfume, hairpins, elaborate traps, improvised weapons. The spectacle comes from creativity within constraints, not from scale. The show's violence is more about the how than the what—watching Villanelle prepare and execute is the entertainment.

The Luxury Aesthetic: Where other shows deliver spectacle through explosions and battles, Killing Eve delivers it through clothes, apartments, and travel. The designer wardrobe—featuring pieces from Molly Goddard, Dries Van Noten, and other luxury brands—became a cultural phenomenon. This is spectacle as lifestyle, an approach that distinguishes the show but limits its visual ambition. The show never attempts large-scale action filmmaking, content with boutique thrills.

The European Travelogue: The production's location filming provides natural spectacle. Watching Villanelle move through Paris, Barcelona's Arc de Triomf, or the Romanian countryside creates a sense of international scope without requiring expensive constructed set pieces. The location scouting is excellent, finding visually interesting spaces that serve the story.

Comparative Assessment: The Bourne franchise and 24 demonstrated spy-spectacle at scale. Hanna (7.5) delivered action with more intensity. Killing Eve's spectacle is boutique rather than blockbuster, which serves the show's tone but limits its score here.

Why 7: Distinctive but small-scale. The show delivers what its premise requires without attempting the spectacle that elevates higher-scoring shows. The fashion and lifestyle elements add value but don't compensate for modest action ambition.

Conceptual Density
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.05

The Obsession Dynamic: The show's central conceptual interest is the Eve-Villanelle relationship and what it means. Are they drawn to each other as adversaries? As romantic interests? As reflections of each other? The ambiguity is genuine, and the show explores it with some depth across four seasons. The concept of two professional women whose mutual obsession transcends their professional opposition—where the hunter becomes fascinated by the hunted and vice versa—gives the show intellectual substance.

The Psychopathy Question: Villanelle's condition opens questions about empathy, identity, and human connection. Can a psychopath love? What does attraction mean when one party has no conscience? These are real questions, even if the show sometimes sacrifices exploration for entertainment. The series' treatment of Villanelle's psychology is more nuanced than typical thriller portrayals of mental illness.

Gender and Power: The show's exploration of female power—both institutional (Eve's place in MI6) and transgressive (Villanelle's rejection of all rules)—gives it contemporary resonance. The series asks what female agency looks like when unconstrained by social norms. Critics like Emily Nussbaum noted the provocative idea that "femininity is itself a sort of sociopathy, whose performance, if you truly nail it, might be the source of ultimate power."

The Decline of Exploration: Subsequent seasons retread the same conceptual ground without deepening it. The potential for exploring the Twelve as a metaphor for patriarchal control, the ethics of Eve's obsession, or the queerness of the central relationship was never fully realized. By Season 4, the concepts felt exhausted rather than enriched.

Comparative Assessment: Hannibal (8.5) explored similar questions with more depth and philosophical rigor. You (8) offers darker psychological excavation. Killing Eve has themes but sometimes uses them as background for stylish entertainment rather than as subjects demanding engagement. The conceptual material is present but inconsistently developed.

Why 7: Genuine thematic content about obsession, psychopathy, and female agency that distinguishes it from pure entertainment. The exploration could be deeper and more consistent across seasons, but the conceptual material is present and occasionally rich.

Narrative Drive
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.20

Season 1 Momentum: The first season is propulsive television. Each episode advances the Eve-Villanelle relationship while delivering clever set pieces. The cat-and-mouse structure creates inherent tension that writer Phoebe Waller-Bridge exploits expertly. From Eve's recruitment by Carolyn to the Rome finale, the pacing is perfect. This is binge-watch television at its most effective, designed to leave viewers desperate for the next episode.

The Decline: Subsequent seasons couldn't maintain the momentum. The structural device—Eve chases Villanelle, they circle each other, circumstances separate them—repeats without fresh variation. By Season 4, the show has lost the propulsive energy that made Season 1 addictive. The chase became routine, and the writers struggled to manufacture reasons for the women to continue their dance. The introduction of new characters (Pam, Yusuf) felt like attempts to delay the inevitable confrontation rather than genuine story developments.

The Season 2 Peak: Emerald Fennell's season deserves credit for finding a new angle—the introduction of Aaron Peel as a target who fascinates Villanelle differently than Eve does, the Rome climax that nearly brings resolution. The second season maintains momentum by raising stakes without fundamentally changing the dynamic.

The Pace of Discovery: The show's structure relies on gradual revelation—Eve learning about Villanelle, Villanelle learning about herself, the audience learning about both. When the revelations stop coming (essentially after Season 2), the pace slows. The show never found a new engine to replace the thrill of discovery.

Comparative Assessment: 24 (9.5) maintained momentum across seasons through structural innovation. Breaking Bad (10) escalated continuously. Killing Eve demonstrates momentum's difficulty when a premise is exhausted. The first two seasons alone would score higher; averaging across the full run brings the score down.

Why 8: Strong start that gave way to diminishing returns. The first season alone would score 9.5; the complete run averages to 8. The show proved unable to evolve its premise without breaking what made it work.

5
Narrative Resolution
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.50

The Finale Problem: Killing Eve's series finale, "The Last Time I Saw You" (Episode 4.08), was widely criticized as a failure—not just in execution but in conception. After four seasons building the Eve-Villanelle relationship, the resolution felt unearned and unsatisfying. Villanelle's death, shot on the bridge immediately after achieving a moment of happiness with Eve, generated accusations of perpetuating the "Bury Your Gays" trope—killing a queer main character moments after happiness, serving shock value over narrative logic.

The Challenge of Resolution: The show had written itself into a corner. Kill Villanelle and you betray the premise; let them live happily and you betray the tension. Better shows find third options—The Americans (10) pulled off a perfect ending that honored its premise while satisfying emotionally. Killing Eve couldn't find that path. The original plan had Eve dying, with Sandra Oh suggesting her character's death "would be the strongest and the most interesting" conclusion. The reversal—killing Villanelle instead—feels like compromise rather than vision.

The Showrunner Defense: Showrunner Laura Neal and creator Sally Woodward Gentle defended the ending, suggesting Villanelle's death represented a "rebirth" for Eve allowing her to return to a "normal life." The actors' defense—that Villanelle's death was "inevitable" and that happy endings weren't possible for these characters—rings hollow given the show's queerness and the history of lesbian characters dying on television. The fan backlash was immediate and sustained, with the finale added to "worst TV finales" lists alongside Dexter and Game of Thrones.

The Unfinished Business: The show failed to resolve the Twelve plotline in a satisfying way, with the organization's leadership dispatched off-screen and its ultimate purpose never explained. Character arcs for Carolyn, Konstantin, and supporting players felt rushed or abandoned. The finale tried to be poetic; it ended up being frustrating.

Comparative Assessment: The Sopranos (9.5) faced similar expectation challenges but delivered an ending of artistic integrity. Hannibal (8) ended prematurely but with perfect final moments. Dexter (3) demonstrates how to destroy a legacy with botched resolution. Killing Eve lands closer to Dexter territory—its ending actively damaged the show's standing and alienated its fanbase.

Why 5: A finale that failed to resolve the show's central relationship in a satisfying way and sparked genuine controversy. Resolution scores reflect how well a show sticks the landing; Killing Eve stumbled badly and landed off the podium.
