Gomorrah

2014–2021 • 5 Seasons • 60 Episodes • Crime Drama
Created by Roberto Saviano, Stefano Bises • Based on Saviano's bestselling exposé
Starring: Marco D'Amore, Salvatore Esposito, Fortunato Cerlino, Ciro Petrone
7.36 / 10

What It Feels Like to Watch

Watching Gomorrah is like descending into a concrete labyrinth where the sun barely penetrates and every shadow conceals a threat. This isn't the romanticized Italian crime of The Godfather—this is Naples' brutal underworld, captured with documentary-level realism that makes you feel the weight of humidity, desperation, and violence in every frame. Roberto Saviano, who wrote the original exposé that exposed the Camorra to the world, brings an authenticity so raw it feels dangerous to watch.

The series immerses you in the claustrophobic world of the Savastano clan and their rivals, where loyalty is currency and betrayal is inevitable. You breathe the same air as Ciro Di Marzio (Marco D'Amore), the "Immortal"—a man whose soul erodes season by season as he climbs the criminal hierarchy. This isn't entertainment; it's an experience that leaves you emotionally exhausted, morally conflicted, and utterly captivated by the tragedy of characters who never had a chance at redemption.

The show's power lies in its rejection of crime drama conventions. There are no heroes here, only survivors. The cinematography captures Naples' periphery—grey tower blocks, decaying infrastructure, and narrow alleyways—creating a prison of concrete that mirrors the characters' trapped existence. When gunfire erupts, it happens with sudden, unceremonious violence. Death comes without warning, and power shifts through executions carried out in broad daylight with chilling casualness. This is Italian crime stripped of all glamour, exposing instead the rotting underbelly of a system that consumes everyone it touches.

Why It Ranks

Characters

7.7

Gomorrah's characters emerge from Naples' concrete sprawl with such authenticity that they feel like documentary subjects rather than dramatic creations. Ciro Di Marzio stands as one of television's most compelling anti-heroes—a man simultaneously brutal and vulnerable, whose moral compass spins freely as survival demands. Marco D'Amore's performance captures the weariness behind Ciro's eyes, the weight of every life taken accumulating like stones in his pockets.

Gennaro "Genny" Savastano's arc represents the series' greatest character achievement—transforming from a privileged, hesitant heir into a calculating, ruthless leader. Salvatore Esposito embodies this evolution with terrifying precision, showing how power corrupts absolutely when coupled with necessity. Supporting players like Conte, Malammore, and the various clan members feel drawn from real life, each carrying distinct motivations and internal conflicts.

The show's treatment of female characters—Patrizia, Imma, Donna Noemi—grants them agency within the patriarchal criminal world. While sometimes underutilized, these women navigate power dynamics with pragmatic intelligence. The series falters occasionally in character consistency during later seasons, as alliances shift rapidly to maintain plot momentum. Some secondary characters receive abbreviated development before violent exits, leaving untapped potential. Nevertheless, the core figures—Ciro and Genny—rank among crime drama's most thoroughly explored tragic figures, their intertwined destinies providing emotional anchors across all five seasons.

World Building

7.5

The world of Gomorrah exists as a fully realized ecosystem of criminal enterprise, complete with its own economics, territories, and power structures. The series meticulously constructs the Camorra's operational reality—drug distribution networks, money laundering operations, political corruption, and international connections spanning from Naples to Honduras to Bulgaria. This isn't generic criminal underworld; it's specifically Neapolitan, rooted in the unique geography and socio-economic conditions of Campania.

Production designer Paki Meduri transforms actual locations into the show's vision of organized crime's infrastructure. The Savastano family compound, with its fortress-like architecture, contrasts sharply with the makeshift headquarters of rival clans, visualizing power dynamics through space itself. The series understands that in this world, territory equals power, and every square meter of Naples' periphery carries strategic significance.

Where the world building stumbles is in its occasional reliance on familiar crime drama tropes—hidden drug labs, corrupt politicians with cartoonish greed, and international cartel meetings that feel borrowed from other series. The expansion into post-Soviet territories and South American drug connections, while based on reality, sometimes sacrifices the intimate scale that made the show distinctive. The micro-level world building—neighborhood dynamics, family hierarchies, street-level economics—remains unparalleled, but the macro-level criminal empire occasionally feels like standard crime fiction rather than Saviano's documented reality.

Cinematography

7.5

Cinematographer Marco Pierotti establishes Gomorrah's signature visual language from the opening frames—a desaturated, almost monochromatic palette that bleeds Naples of color and hope. The camera work favors intimate close-ups that capture micro-expressions of fear, calculation, and brief humanity before shutting down again. Handheld sequences during confrontations create documentary-style urgency, while carefully composed static shots allow tension to build through silence.

The series' visual geography serves narrative purpose masterfully. The camera lingers on architectural decay—crumbling concrete, graffiti-covered walls, broken infrastructure—as metaphor for institutional and moral collapse. Night sequences employ available light sources (street lamps, car headlights, burning fires) to create pools of illumination in overwhelming darkness, characters moving through chiaroscuro landscapes where danger lurks at the edge of visibility.

Later seasons expand the visual vocabulary as the narrative moves beyond Naples, but this geographic expansion sometimes dilutes the aesthetic cohesion that defined earlier episodes. The show's violence, when it comes, is captured with unflinching camera placement—no shaky-cam obfuscation or quick-cut concealment. Instead, the cinematography forces viewers to witness death with the same clinical detachment the characters display. This commitment to visual honesty, even when depicting ugliness, represents the show's cinematographic strength and its occasional difficulty in sustaining visual interest across repetitive locations.

Visual Spectacle

7.0

Gomorrah deliberately rejects the visual spectacle conventions of contemporary crime dramas. There are no stylized shootouts set to carefully curated soundtracks, no slow-motion betrayals with operatic scores. The show's spectacle emerges instead from the raw authenticity of its violence and the oppressive beauty of its urban wasteland backdrops. When action sequences occur, they unfold with messy, chaotic realism—characters fumble with weapons, bullets miss their marks, and death arrives without cinematic ceremony.

The series finds visual impact in contrasts: the brutal execution conducted against the backdrop of a mundane apartment block; lavish criminal gatherings in decaying industrial spaces; expensive suits and watches on men who live in concrete fortresses. This anti-spectacle approach serves thematic purpose—there's no glory in this violence, only survival or death. The show's most visually striking moments often involve stillness rather than motion: a character standing alone on a rooftop overlooking Naples, the camera pulling back to reveal their insignificance against the urban sprawl.

Where the show falters in visual spectacle is its reluctance to fully embrace cinematic grandeur even when the narrative justifies it. Major power shifts and dramatic turning points receive the same visual treatment as routine business, potentially underplaying climactic moments. The limited budget constraints show in occasional action sequences that feel underchoreographed. However, this restraint also distinguishes Gomorrah from more glossy crime series—its power comes not from visual bombast but from the uncomfortable intimacy of its observation. The spectacle, such as it is, emerges from reality rather than invention.

Conceptual Density

7.4

Gomorrah operates as a sustained examination of power's corrupting influence, exploring how criminal infrastructure becomes inseparable from legitimate social and economic systems. Saviano's source material provides conceptual foundations that the series builds upon—questions about complicity, the normalization of violence, and the impossibility of ethical survival within fundamentally corrupt structures. The show interrogates whether redemption exists for those born into criminal ecosystems, or whether escape requires complicity in greater evils.

The series develops rich thematic content around masculinity and performance—how young men adopt postures of ruthlessness to survive, gradually becoming the roles they initially performed. Legacy and inheritance recur as concepts: fathers pass violence to sons, mothers enable destruction to protect bloodlines, and loyalty becomes a trap disguised as virtue. Economic realities underpin every character decision, with the show consistently foregrounding how poverty and limited opportunity funnel young men toward criminal enterprise.

Some conceptual threads remain underexplored—the show gestures toward political corruption and institutional complicity without fully integrating these elements into its narrative architecture. Religious imagery appears frequently (crosses, shrines, Catholic ritual) but rarely receives meaningful examination beyond atmospheric deployment. The series' conceptual sophistication peaks in its middle seasons when balancing character psychology with systemic critique. Later seasons prioritize plot mechanics over thematic exploration, occasionally reducing complex concepts to background texture rather than active narrative forces. Nevertheless, the cumulative interrogation of criminal power's banality and horror distinguishes Gomorrah from less thoughtful entries in the genre.

Narrative Drive

7.0

Gomorrah's narrative engine runs on shifting alliances, sudden violence, and the perpetual motion required to maintain criminal power. The show employs a propulsive structure where episodes build toward explosive confrontations, consequences ripple through subsequent chapters, and no character remains safe from narrative elimination. This approach creates compulsive viewing—each episode ends with sufficient unresolved tension to demand immediate continuation.

The first three seasons maintain exceptional narrative momentum, balancing Ciro and Genny's parallel arcs with clan politics and territorial warfare. Season transitions bring major structural changes—power vacuums, new antagonists, geographic relocation—that refresh the narrative without abandoning established stakes. The writers understand that in crime narratives, death must carry permanent consequence, and they execute major character fatalities with narrative purpose rather than shock value alone.

The fourth and fifth seasons experience narrative fatigue as the show struggles to escalate beyond previous climaxes. Alliance reversals become more frequent and sometimes strain credibility; new characters enter and exit without sufficient development to justify their narrative weight. The series' commitment to bleakness occasionally hardens into predictability—viewers learn that no positive development will survive the episode, potentially reducing emotional investment. The final season attempts closure but lacks the structural inevitability that distinguished earlier narrative achievements. Despite these late-series struggles, Gomorrah's overall narrative architecture—spanning sixty episodes of sustained intensity—represents a considerable achievement in serialized storytelling.

Resolution

7.4

Gomorrah approaches resolution with the same unflinching commitment to bleak realism that defines its entire run. The series refuses the temptation of redemptive closure, instead delivering endings that feel inevitable given everything that preceded them. Character arcs resolve not through dramatic transformation but through the exhaustion of possibility—there are no winners in this world, only survivors temporarily postponing defeat. The show's resolution honors its premise: crime as a system that perpetuates itself through consuming human lives.

The final season brings Ciro's journey to its terminus with appropriate tragedy. His "immortality" nickname takes on ironic weight as he survives situations that kill others while suffering accumulates beyond recovery. The series' conclusion for his character—ambiguous, violent, and emotionally devastating— satisfies because it refuses consolation. Genny's resolution similarly rejects easy moral categorization; power achieved carries costs that cannot be discounted by narrative fiat.

Where resolution falters is in the abbreviated treatment of supporting character arcs. After investing multiple seasons in complex figures, the final season accelerates through their conclusions, sometimes relegating significant developments to off-screen implication. The show's expansion into international criminal networks in later seasons creates loose ends that the finale cannot fully address. However, these compromises feel somewhat appropriate to the narrative's thematic concerns— criminal enterprise continues beyond individual narratives, cycles of violence outlast their participants, and no ending truly ends anything. Gomorrah's resolution succeeds by embracing irresolution, leaving viewers with the unsettling recognition that the world depicted continues spinning beyond the credits, consuming new generations in familiar patterns.

Gomorrah stands as one of crime television's most uncompromising achievements—a five-season descent into Naples' criminal underworld that refuses the comforts of romanticized gangster fiction. Roberto Saviano's authentic foundation, combined with Marco D'Amore and Salvatore Esposito's performances, creates a viewing experience of unusual emotional and moral weight. The show's 7.36 score reflects genuine excellence across all measured dimensions, with particular strength in character development and atmospheric realism.

The series' greatness lies in its rejection of crime drama conventions—no heroes, no redemption arcs, no visual glamour to distance viewers from violence's human cost. What it sacrifices in accessibility, it gains in artistic integrity and lasting impact. Gomorrah demands patience with its bleakness and tolerance for narrative complexity, but rewards committed viewing with insights into power, poverty, and survival that transcend its specific Neapolitan setting. For audiences willing to endure its brutality, this Italian series delivers one of television's most sophisticated examinations of organized crime's human toll.
