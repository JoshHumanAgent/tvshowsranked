What It Feels Like to Watch

The Crown arrived on Netflix on November 4, 2016, as television's most expensive and ambitious royal drama—a multi-decade chronicle of Queen Elizabeth II's reign that would span six seasons and 60 episodes through December 14, 2023. Created by Peter Morgan, who developed the series from his 2006 film The Queen and his 2013 stage play The Audience, the series combined intimate family drama with state history, exploring how duty shaped and constrained the people who bore it. With a reported production budget exceeding $500 million across its run, The Crown established Netflix's prestige credentials and demonstrated what streaming-original content could achieve in scope and polish.

The experience of watching The Crown is one of accumulated intimacy with public figures whose lives have been documented for decades. The series dramatizes historical events—the Suez Crisis, the Kennedy visit, the Aberfan disaster, the miners' strike, Charles and Diana's marriage, the Windsor fire, the death of Diana—but filters them through the private experience of a family whose every decision has public consequence. Elizabeth II is both head of state and mother, grandmother and wife; the series makes her human while respecting the institution she embodies. The central question recurs across decades: what does it cost to be symbolic rather than human?

The series' most distinctive creative choice was recasting its actors every two seasons, an approach that acknowledged both the passage of time and the impossibility of any single performer embodying decades of aging. Claire Foy established the template in Seasons 1-2 (young Elizabeth, early reign, accession and consolidation), winning both Emmy and Golden Globe Awards. Olivia Colman deepened it in Seasons 3-4 (middle Elizabeth, family crises, the Diana years), also winning major awards. Imelda Staunton completed it in Seasons 5-6 (late Elizabeth, the aftermath of Diana's death, the Golden Jubilee), maintaining continuity while finding her own register. Each actor discovered different notes while respecting what came before—Foy's restraint, Colman's warmth, Staunton's steel.

The supporting cast provided the emotional contrast that Elizabeth's studied neutrality required. Vanessa Kirby's Princess Margaret in Seasons 1-2 was the series' most vibrant presence—Elizabeth's foil, the sister who wanted everything duty denied, the tragic princess whose glamour couldn't compensate for purposelessness. Kirby's performance, which earned her a BAFTA, established Margaret as perhaps the show's most compelling character. Helena Bonham Carter and later Lesley Manville inherited the role with different emphases—Bonham Carter emphasizing Margaret's vulnerability and decline, Manville her mortality and final heartbreak. Matt Smith's Prince Philip captured consort frustration, the man who had to walk two steps behind his wife always, whose restlessness found expression in causes (the carriage-driving, the conservation work) that remained perpetually secondary. Tobias Menzies and Jonathan Pryce brought different textures to the aging Philip—Menzies emphasizing the crusty traditionalism, Pryce the spiritual seeking that marked Philip's final decades.

What distinguishes The Crown from other historical drama is its interest in duty as both virtue and prison. Elizabeth's commitment to her role costs her relationships, spontaneity, and sometimes happiness—the series doesn't judge, but it shows the price repeatedly. When she chooses the monarchy over Margaret's happiness (the Peter Townsend affair in Season 1), we understand the decision even as we mourn its cost. When she prioritizes protocol over emotion after Diana's death (Season 6), we see the Institution demanding its tribute. When Charles begs for happiness and Elizabeth offers duty instead, we witness the generational tragedy that the system produces. The Crown is a study in what the British constitution demands of its sovereign—not power, but sacrifice performed as service.

The Diana years—Seasons 4, 5, and 6—represent both the series' highest cultural visibility and its most contested territory. Emma Corrin's Season 4 Diana captured the young princess's vulnerability and media innocence; Elizabeth Debicki's Seasons 5-6 Diana showed the experienced media operator whose Everyman interviews and paparazzi relationships had become strategy. The depiction of Charles and Diana's marriage collapse, the "War of the Waleses," Diana's death in Paris, and its aftermath generated both acclaim and criticism. The Duke of Edinburgh played by Pryce was shown driving through Paris in the early hours after Diana's crash; Elizabeth, isolated at Balmoral, prioritized her grandsons' privacy over public expectation; Tony Blair's government pressed the palace toward the public mourning that eventually occurred. These were the moments when The Crown's "inspired by true events" disclaimer carried the most weight—and when criticism from royal insiders was loudest.

The series has been praised by critics for its acting, directing, writing, cinematography, and production value, winning the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Drama Series for its fourth season and two Golden Globe Awards for Best Television Series – Drama. But its historical inaccuracies have been criticized, particularly in the latter half of the series's run. Former Prime Minister John Major called Season 5's depiction of his government "a barrel-load of malicious nonsense." Culture Secretary Oliver Dowden suggested Netflix add a "fiction" disclaimer. The accuracy debates intensified around Season 6's treatment of Diana's death—though the series depicted only the aftermath, not the crash itself. Creator Morgan defended his approach consistently: "The Crown is a love letter to her and I've nothing to add for now." The series occupies difficult territory between history and drama, between documentary fidelity and fictional technique.

Characters & Acting
Weight: 20% | Weighted Contribution: 1.50

The Three Elizabeths: Claire Foy established the character's core in Seasons 1-2—duty above self, restraint as philosophy, the gradual hardening of someone shaped by responsibility into the role that consumed them. Her Elizabeth is learning to be queen, making mistakes, finding her footing. Foy's performance won Emmy, Golden Globe, and SAG Awards, establishing a template that subsequent actors honored. Olivia Colman brought middle-age complexity in Seasons 3-4—the queen as wife, mother, and institution simultaneously, her warmth covering steel. Colman found the private Elizabeth behind the public façade, particularly in scenes with her children and in the private moments after crises. Imelda Staunton's late Elizabeth in Seasons 5-6 showed the cost of decades of duty—a woman who has become her role so completely that separation seems impossible. Staunton played Elizabeth facing the collapse of her family's reputation, the public turning against the institution after Diana's death, and the mortality of her sister and mother. All three were excellent while maintaining continuity—a remarkable achievement in television casting.

The Supporting Royal Family: Vanessa Kirby's Margaret in Seasons 1-2 was the series' most vibrant presence—Elizabeth's foil, the sister who wanted everything duty denied, the tragic princess whose glamour compensated for purposelessness until it couldn't. Kirby's BAFTA-winning performance gave The Crown its emotional volatility; whenever Margaret appeared, the energy shifted. Matt Smith's Philip captured consort frustration with nuance—the man who had to walk two steps behind, whose restlessness found expression in causes that remained perpetually secondary. Josh O'Connor's Charles in Seasons 3-4 showed the heir's particular misery: raised to be king, never allowed to be himself, in love with a woman he couldn't marry while married to a woman he didn't love. Dominic West's older Charles in Seasons 5-6 deepened the portrayal—bitter, vindicated by events, still seeking the happiness the institution denied. Erin Doherty's Princess Anne provided sardonic relief, the princess who accepted duty without complaint because personality made resistance impossible.

The Diana Years: Emma Corrin's Season 4 Diana captured the young princess's vulnerability and naivety—the nursery teacher who walked into a media crucible and was destroyed by it. Corrin found Diana's gift for connection alongside her strategic innocence. Elizabeth Debicki's Seasons 5-6 Diana showed the mature media operator—the Panorama interview, the revenge dress, the calculated leaks, the landmine campaign. Debicki's performance was particularly fine in depicting Diana's isolation; she was simultaneously the most famous woman in the world and entirely alone. The series gave Diana substantial screen time, making her arguably the central character of Seasons 4-5, which generated both acclaim and criticism for centering the Diana narrative over Elizabeth's.

The Prime Ministers: John Lithgow's Winston Churchill set the template—historical figures rendered with sufficient accuracy to satisfy and sufficient dramatic imagination to engage. Lithgow won an Emmy for the performance, capturing Churchill's wartime greatness alongside his peacetime decline. The series cycled through prime ministers at a pace that matched history: Antony Eden (Jeremy Northam), Harold Macmillan (Anton Lesser), Alec Douglas-Home (Greg Wise), Harold Wilson (Jason Watkins), Edward Heath (Michael Maloney), Jim Callaghan (Patrick Ryecart), Margaret Thatcher (Gillian Anderson), John Major (Jonny Lee Miller), and Tony Blair (Bertie Carvel). Anderson's Thatcher was the most prominent—her performance won awards and generated controversy for its unsparing depiction of the Prime Minister whose policies the series opposed. Miller's Major and Carvel's Blair played key roles in the Diana aftermath season, with Miller particularly aggrieved by his character's depiction.

The Recasting Achievement: The decision to replace actors every two seasons was risky—audiences might reject new performers in familiar roles, continuity might suffer. Instead, the recasting became a feature. Each new cast brought fresh energy while the writing maintained consistency. The transition from Foy to Colman (announced before Season 2 aired) generated anticipation rather than resistance. Colman to Staunton felt natural given the characters' ages. The supporting cast transitions—Kirby to Bonham Carter to Manville, Smith to Menzies to Pryce—followed similar patterns. This approach solved a practical problem (actors aging out of roles) while creating a distinctive artistic signature.

Comparative Assessment: Succession (9.5) achieved comparable family-drama depth in a fictional context with more satirical bite. Downton Abbey (7.5) built ensemble across generations but with less historical constraint. The Crown matches any period drama for performance quality while bearing the additional burden of depicting living people—a challenge its cast navigated with remarkable skill.

Why 7.5: Exceptional performances across three casts, with Claire Foy, Olivia Colman, Vanessa Kirby, and Elizabeth Debicki delivering award-worthy work. The recasting solved production challenges while creating artistic distinction. The score reflects quality maintained across decades of narrative.

World Building
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.20

The Royal World: The series built the royal environment comprehensively—Buckingham Palace, Windsor Castle, Balmoral, Kensington Palace, Sandringham, the royal yacht Britannia, the private apartments, the state rooms, the rituals of court. Production designer Martin Childs and subsequent designers created spaces of documented fidelity; viewers understood where they were and what those spaces meant socially and historically. The private spaces—the queen's study, the sitting rooms where family arguments occurred—contrasted with the public venues where monarchy was performed. The series understood that royalty is spatial as well as symbolic: you are queen in certain rooms, mother in others.

The Historical Context: Each season situated the royal family within its historical moment—post-war austerity, Suez, the Profumo affair, the Swinging Sixties, the economic crises and strikes of the 1970s, the Thatcher revolution, the Diana media explosion, the Blair ascendancy. The family experienced history as both participants and observers; Elizabeth's job was to remain constant while the nation changed around her. The series captured the transition from empire to Commonwealth, from deference to scrutiny, from newspaper coverage to television to paparazzi intrusion. The world building was historical, not invented—the period research informed every frame.

The Institutional Depth: The Crown built the machinery of monarchy—the private secretaries, the press secretaries, the equerries, the ladies-in-waiting who enabled Elizabeth to function. Martin Charteris (Harry Hadden-Paton, then Charles Edwards) explained constitutional options; Michael Adeane (Danny Sapani) managed the transition; Robert Fellowes (Andrew Havill) navigated the Diana crisis while married to Diana's sister. The series showed bureaucracy as character, institutions with their own interests and inertia. The court was not merely setting but actor in the drama.

The Commonwealth Dimension: The series touched on Elizabeth's role as head of the Commonwealth, visiting realms that would become republics, navigating post-colonial relationships, representing British interests abroad. This aspect received less attention than domestic drama but provided international scope. The African tours, Australian visits, and Commonwealth meetings showed monarchy's global dimension.

Comparative Assessment: Downton Abbey (8.5) built period-aristocratic world-building within a single house across changing eras. The Crown achieves comparable detail with larger scope—palaces, Parliament, the Commonwealth, sixty years of changing technology and society. The accuracy of depiction is remarkable given the production constraint of filming in studios (Elstree, Pinewood) rather than actual royal residences.

Why 8: Exceptional world-building across six decades of changing Britain. The series captured both physical environments (palaces, country houses, foreign locations) and institutional systems (the court, Parliament, the Commonwealth). Historical research and production design combined to create a world of documented fidelity.

Cinematography
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.35

Period Luxury: The Crown is among television's most visually beautiful productions—costume design by Michele Clapton, Jane Petrie, and Amy Roberts that recreated six decades of royal and broader fashion; production design that replicated palaces at scale; cinematography by directors including Stephen Daldry, Philip Martin, Benjamin Caron, and Jessica Hobbs that made every frame worthy of display. The reported $500+ million budget showed; it showed on screen in the scale of sets, the quality of costumes, the attention to period detail that made each decade distinct. When Elizabeth aged from youth to elderly, every element aged with her—clothes, makeup, posture, setting. The series used color temperature to mark time: cooler tones for the 1940s and 1950s, warmer for the 1960s, harsh fluorescents for the 1970s, saturated 1980s excess, muted 1990s restraint. The visual language evolved while maintaining consistency.

The Royal Aesthetic: The series captured the visual language of royalty—formal portraits, state ceremonies, the particular way queens and princesses are photographed. Director Stephen Daldry established the template: slow zooms, symmetrical composition, frames that echoed painted portraits. Characters were composed like portrait subjects, acknowledging that real royals exist in the camera's eye continuously. The aesthetic was both realistic and idealized; the series showed how royalty is meant to look while occasionally showing what it costs to maintain that image.

The Coronation and State Ceremonies: The series staged royal events with full production resources—Elizabeth's coronation in Season 1 was rendered with near-documentary fidelity, the Westminster Abbey setting, the anointing hidden from cameras (as it was in reality), the weight of crown and responsibility. State weddings, funerals (the King's funeral, Diana's funeral), the Trooping the Colour, diplomatic receptions—all rendered with scale and period accuracy. The spectacle served story rather than overwhelming it; we saw the events while understanding what they meant to their participants.

The Location Work: Filming took place at Elstree Studios in Borehamwood, Hertfordshire, for the standing sets, but location shooting across the United Kingdom and internationally provided scope. South Africa doubled for Kenya (where the series' events begin, with Elizabeth learning of her father's death at Treetops). Spain provided some Mediterranean exteriors. The use of actual country houses for exterior shots—Wilton House, Belvoir Castle, Burghley House—gave the production architectural authenticity that soundstages couldn't provide fully.

Comparative Assessment: Game of Thrones (9.5) set the ceiling for television cinematography with fantasy spectacle. Downton Abbey (8) achieved similar period beauty with smaller scale. The Crown matches any prestige drama for visual polish while maintaining period accuracy—a combination that distinguishes it in the historical drama field.

Why 9: Exceptional cinematography and production design sustained across sixty episodes and six decades. The series is visually stunning while serving historical accuracy. The budget enabled production values that established Netflix's prestige credentials internationally.

Visual Spectacle
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.85

Royal Event: The series staged royal spectacles with full production resources—the coronation in Season 1, state weddings (Elizabeth and Philip, Charles and Diana), funerals (George VI, Diana), the Trooping the Colour, diplomatic receptions, the Silver and Golden Jubilees. These events were rendered with historical accuracy and cinematic scale. The coronation sequence cost a reported $2 million and filmed over five days at Ely Cathedral doubling for Westminster Abbey. Diana's funeral in Season 6 used thousands of extras and period-accurate processional routes. The spectacle was never empty; each event served character and story.

Period Scope: Six decades of British life provided changing spectacle—post-war austerity with rationing and bombsites, the emergence of the Swinging Sixties with Mary Quant and miniskirts, the strikes and power cuts of the 1970s, the excess and inequality of the 1980s Thatcher years, the Cool Britannia of the 1990s. The series captured how Britain looked across eras, from the royal perspective that showed privilege but also showed that privilege under pressure. The fashion of each period became a visual marker—Margaret's couture gowns, Diana's evolving style from Sloane Ranger to international icon, the Queen's reliably correct dresses in solid colors with matching accessories.

The International Dimension: The series staged international events that broadened spectacle—the Kennedys' visit in Season 2, the Australian tour, the Commonwealth visits, Kennedy's assassination coverage, various state occasions. These sequences provided production scope beyond Britain while serving the narrative of constitutional monarchy's changing global role.

The Personal Scale: Not all spectacle is large-scale. The series found visual drama in intimate moments—a single figure at a window, the queen alone in a vast room, Margaret drinking in her bathtub while the radio played news of her sister's wedding announcement. These smaller images accumulated into a portrait of privilege as isolation. The spectacle of royalty was also the spectacle of loneliness performed in beautiful spaces.

Comparative Assessment: Downton Abbey (8) offers comparable period spectacle with an upstairs-downstairs scope. The Pacifc (9.5) delivered war spectacle at maximum scale. The Crown's spectacle derives from royal ceremony and period detail rather than action—appropriate for its subject and effectively executed.

Why 8.5: Quality spectacle through royal events, period production, and international scope. The $500+ million budget enabled visual grandeur that established Netflix's ability to compete with premium cable and film. The spectacle serves story rather than substituting for it.

Conceptual Density
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.05

Duty as Subject: The Crown's central concept is duty—what it costs, what it enables, what it prevents. Elizabeth's commitment to her role costs her relationships with her sister, spontaneity with her husband, closeness with her children, and perhaps happiness itself. The series doesn't judge duty but examines it, showing both its nobility and its prison-like quality. When Elizabeth chooses monarchy over Margaret's happiness in the Townsend affair, we understand the constitutional reasoning even as we mourn the human cost. When Elizabeth prioritizes protocol over emotion after Diana's death, the series shows the Institution demanding its tribute. The question recurs: what is the value of a symbolic role, and what does maintaining it cost?

Monarchy and Modernity: The series traces the monarchy's adaptation to changing times—the empire giving way to Commonwealth, deference giving way to media scrutiny, tradition giving way to modernity. The institution survives by changing; Elizabeth survives by not changing. The tension is ongoing across six decades. Margaret's rebellion in Season 1, Charles's resistance in Seasons 3-4, Diana's media manipulation in Seasons 5-6—each represents a challenge to the old order that the institution absorbs or deflects. The series explores whether constitutional monarchy can survive in a media age, a post-imperial age, a post-imperial age.

The Personal and the Political: The Crown insists on the connection between personal choice and historical consequence. Elizabeth's decision to keep the family name Windsor rather than adopting Mountbatten (Philip's preference) sends her husband into a fury that echoes through decades. Charles's inability to choose a wife freely produces the Diana catastrophe. Anne's acceptance of duty produces a stable royal while her siblings divorce. The series shows character shaping history within institutional constraints that limit freedom.

The Accuracy Question: Morgan's approach—drama inspired by history rather than confined by it—opens conceptual questions about television's relationship to the recent past. Former Prime Minister John Major called Season 5's depiction "a barrel-load of malicious nonsense." Culture Secretary Oliver Dowden requested disclaimers. Royal biographers contested specific scenes. The series exists in contested territory between documentary and drama, between public record and private truth. Morgan defended his approach: the series captures emotional truth even when it invents specific scenes. The criticism reflects the challenge of depicting living people still shaping their own legacies.

Comparative Assessment: Succession (9.5) examines similar family-power dynamics with greater satirical bite and fictional freedom. Downton Abbey (7) treats similar themes of duty and change within a more conventional period-drama framework. The Crown achieves depth through historical specificity, though the accuracy debates complicate any assessment.

Why 7: Solid conceptual depth about duty, monarchy, and modernization that sustains across six seasons. The ideas serve the historical material without reaching the philosophical audacity of television's most conceptually ambitious dramas.

Narrative Drive
Weight: 15% | Weighted Contribution: 1.05

The Historical Engine: History provided the narrative engine—Elizabeth's accession, the Suez Crisis, Margaret's marriage disappointment, the Kennedy visit, the Profumo affair, Wilson's premiership, the miners' strike, Thatcher's term, the Charles-Diana marriage and divorce, the Windsor fire, Diana's death, the Golden Jubilee, Charles and Camilla's wedding. The series didn't need to invent drama; it selected from documented events across nearly sixty years. Each season had built-in stakes: the monarchy's survival, the prime minister's fate, the family's reputation.

The Family Drama: The drive came from family tension layered onto historical events—Elizabeth versus Margaret (accession versus exclusion), Elizabeth versus Philip (queen versus consort), Elizabeth versus Charles (duty versus desire), Charles versus Diana (marriage as institution versus marriage as feeling). The personal relationships provided momentum when historical events were slow or when constitutional crises seemed abstract to modern audiences. Margaret's doomed romances, Charles's love for Camilla, Diana's loneliness, Anne's marriages—these generated the emotional investment that kept viewers returning.

The Season Structure: Each season had internal coherence—Season 1 (accession and consolidation), Season 2 (institutionalization and family expansion), Season 3 (decline and scandal), Season 4 (Thatcher and Diana), Season 5 (collapse of the Wales marriage), Season 6 (death and renewal). The ten-episode seasons allowed time for events to breathe while maintaining forward movement. Binge-release encouraged viewing as extended films rather than episodic television.

The Diana Peak: Seasons 4-6 represented the series' highest visibility and strongest narrative momentum. Diana's entry in Season 4, the marriage collapse in Season 5, the death and aftermath in Season 6—this material had built-in drama that the earlier seasons sometimes lacked. The audience brought existing knowledge that created anticipation; everyone knew what was coming, which created a specific kind of dramatic engagement.

The Middle Seasons Challenge: Seasons 3 struggled to find the propulsive energy of 1-2 (establishment) or 4-6 (Diana). The period covered (1964-1977) had historical events but lacked the constitutional crises or family melodrama that generated momentum in other seasons. The Aberfan episode was critically acclaimed but represented isolated tragedy rather than ongoing tension. The series' dependence on historical events meant some periods were inherently less dramatic than others.

Comparative Assessment: Succession (10) achieved maximal momentum through character-driven conflict. The Crown achieves moderate momentum through selection from history. The pace is consistent without ever becoming propulsive—the series has stakes, but the stakes are usually resolved eventually by history itself.

Why 7: Moderate narrative drive through historical events and family tension. The momentum is steady rather than urgent because the destination is known and the drama lies in how events are depicted rather than whether they will occur.

Narrative Resolution
Weight: 10% | Weighted Contribution: 0.70

Ending: The Crown concluded on December 14, 2023, with a two-part sixth season that covered Diana's death and its aftermath before ending with Charles and Camilla's 2005 wedding. The final scenes showed Elizabeth imagining her own funeral, considering her legacy, and accepting that she had done her duty. The series reached an ending that felt appropriate: Charles's remarriage represented renewal after the Diana trauma, the Golden Jubilee showed public affection restored, the deaths of Margaret and the Queen Mother cleared the way for a new generation. But complete resolution was impossible—Elizabeth II was still alive when the series was produced and would die during Season 6's post-production. The real ending hadn't occurred.

The Challenge: Historical drama about recent events has inherent resolution issues—history continues beyond the cameras' stopping point, living people continue to act. The Crown addressed this by selecting chapter endings (the wedding) rather than imposing artificial conclusion. The final episodes acknowledged what was to come—William meeting Kate Middleton—but stopped before depicting it. The ending was appropriate to its subject but inevitably incomplete.

The Character Arcs: Elizabeth's arc was complete in thematic terms: the young woman who accepted duty became the elderly woman who had sacrificed everything for it. Margaret's arc ended with her death, shown in the final season. Philip's arc showed him supporting Elizabeth to the end. Charles's arc showed him vindicated but alone. Diana's arc concluded with her death and its aftermath. The series provided resolution for its central figures while leaving the institution ongoing.

The Finale Episode: "Sleep, Dearie Sleep" (Episode 6.10) brought the series to a close with Elizabeth planning her own funeral—a sequence that acquired additional resonance when the real Elizabeth died during Season 6's production. The episode showed Elizabeth in a kind of reverie, considering her legacy, appearing with previous versions of herself (Claire Foy and Olivia Colman in brief cameos), accepting that she had done what was asked. It was quiet, reflective, appropriate—a finale that matched the series' tone rather than exploding into melodrama.

Comparative Assessment: The Americans (10) and Breaking Bad (10) set the standard for deliberate, earned conclusions. The Crown achieved acceptable resolution within the constraints of ongoing history. The score reflects the impossibility of true resolution when the subject continues beyond the narrative.

Why 7: Season-level resolution with appropriate thematic conclusions, limited by the impossibility of true resolution for an ongoing institution. The series ended well without achieving the definitive closure that characterizes television's greatest finales.
