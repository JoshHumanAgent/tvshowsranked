What It Feels Like to Watch

Watching The Handmaid's Tale is an exercise in sustained dread—a slow-burning horror that creeps under your skin and refuses to leave. From the opening frames of the series, with their blood-red habits and stark, puritanical aesthetics, you enter a world that feels simultaneously distant and terrifyingly plausible. This is Margaret Atwood's Gilead brought to visceral life, a theocratic dystopia where women's bodies are state property and fertility is the currency of oppression.

The series hits differently than most prestige television because it refuses to offer the comforts of escapism. There are no heroes riding in to save the day, no clean victories against an obviously evil regime. Instead, we get June Osborne (Elisabeth Moss), a woman stripped of her name, her family, and her autonomy, forced into sexual servitude as a breeding vessel for the ruling class. Moss delivers a performance of such raw intensity that you can feel her character's rage vibrating through the screen—her close-ups become portals into a soul being systematically dismantled and rebuilt in real-time.

The #MeToo resonance of The Handmaid's Tale cannot be overstated. When the series premiered in 2017, it arrived at a cultural moment when women's bodily autonomy was under renewed assault, when whispers about powerful men's abuses were becoming roars. The show felt less like speculative fiction and more like a warning dressed in crimson fabric. Every ceremony, every punishment, every moment of forced compliance carries the weight of historical reality—Atwood famously refused to include anything in her original novel that hadn't already happened somewhere in human history.

"Under His Eye." — The chilling greeting that serves as constant reminder that God and the state are watching, always.

The visual language of the show reinforces its themes of surveillance and control. The camera lingers on empty spaces, on the geometry of oppression—the sharp corners of the Waterford house, the endless corridors of the Colonies, the public gallows where bodies swing as warnings. Director Reed Morano established a visual template in the early episodes that subsequent seasons have maintained: desaturated colors punctuated by that shocking red, intimate close-ups that feel almost suffocating, and a persistent sense that someone is always watching, always judging.

Perhaps most remarkably, The Handmaid's Tale manages to find moments of beauty and connection within its nightmare landscape. The relationships between women—the underground networks of resistance, the small kindnesses exchanged in impossible circumstances, the fierce maternal bonds—provide the emotional anchors that keep viewers invested through six seasons of increasingly harrowing narrative. June's evolution from victim to survivor to revolutionary is not triumphant in the traditional sense; it's messy, morally compromised, and utterly human.

By the series' conclusion, what remains is not hope exactly, but something more durable: the understanding that tyranny can be resisted, that women's bodies and lives and choices matter, and that even in the darkest timeline, the human spirit finds ways to persist. It's television that demands emotional labor from its audience, but rewards that investment with art of uncommon power and relevance.

Why It Ranks

A seven-dimension analysis of television craft and storytelling

Characters & Performance 7.5

Elisabeth Moss's June Osborne stands as one of the most physically demanding performances in television history—a role that requires her to communicate primarily through facial expressions, body language, and voiceover narration. Her transformation from frightened captive to hardened revolutionary is masterfully charted across six seasons, though the show sometimes struggles to give her meaningful agency within Gilead's oppressive structure. The supporting cast provides excellent work: Yvonne Strahovski brings heartbreaking complexity to Serena Joy, a villain who becomes increasingly sympathetic even as she remains complicit; Ann Dowd's Aunt Lydia is terrifying in her conviction, a true believer who genuinely thinks she's saving souls; Samira Wiley's Moira provides necessary grounding and representation of life beyond Gilead's borders.

However, the character dimension suffers from some structural problems. Commander Fred Waterford (Joseph Fiennes) never fully escapes caricature as a smug abuser, despite the show's attempts at humanization. The ensemble sometimes feels underutilized, with promising characters like Emily (Alexis Bledel) receiving arcs that fade in and out of relevance. Seasonal resets that return June to Gilead after escapes strain credibility and character consistency. The show's commitment to June's perspective, while narratively valuable, sometimes limits the development of the rich ensemble surrounding her. Still, the relationships between women—June and Serena's twisted interdependence, June and Janine's sisterhood forged in trauma, the maternal bonds that transcend biology—these are rendered with emotional authenticity that elevates the entire production.

World Building 8.0

Gilead is constructed with the obsessive detail of a museum exhibit documenting an actual totalitarian regime. The world-building succeeds because it feels organic rather than expositional—we understand Gilead's hierarchies through visual cues, through costumes, through the architecture of oppression. The color-coding system (red for Handmaids, blue for Wives, green for Marthas, brown for Aunts) provides instant visual storytelling that communicates social stratification without dialogue. The show expands Atwood's original vision to include the Colonies, where "Unwomen" labor in toxic waste; Jezebels, where covert sexual exploitation continues; and the diplomatic apparatus that maintains Gilead's international standing.

The expansion of Gilead's geography across seasons is both a strength and weakness. Introducing Chicago as a war zone and showing the internal resistance movement adds texture, but the world sometimes feels smaller than a totalitarian nation should. The glimpses of Canada as refuge and of the geopolitical negotiations that keep Gilead viable are fascinating, suggesting a complex international order that accepts atrocity for stability. The show's most powerful world-building moments come through the small details: the way Handmaids respond to their names, the public executions staged as religious ritual, the household prayers that double as psychological warfare. This is a fully realized nightmare, coherent in its internal logic and terrifying in its implications.

Cinematography 8.0

The visual language of The Handmaid's Tale represents some of the most distinctive cinematography in contemporary television. The early episodes, particularly those directed by Reed Morano, established a template that subsequent cinematographers have maintained and evolved: wide-angle lenses that distort faces into desperate masks, extreme close-ups that feel almost invasive in their intimacy, a color palette drained to near-monochrome except for those shocking reds. The recurring image of handmaids framed in windows, doorways, and geometric spaces reinforces their entrapment without a word of dialogue.

The show employs lighting as storytelling, with shadows that swallow characters whole and harsh fluorescents that illuminate the institutional cruelty of Gilead's structures. Night sequences are particularly striking, with visibility limited to create tension and uncertainty. The contrast between the austere interiors of Waterford's mansion and the blasted landscapes of the Colonies provides visual variety that prevents the grim aesthetic from becoming monotonous. Some of the most memorable sequences—June's first ceremony, the mass executions, the slow-motion climactic moments of violence—are masterclasses in building dread through composition and camera movement. If there's a critique to be made, it's that the visual style occasionally overwhelms the content, with beautifully composed shots of suffering that risk aestheticizing trauma. But overall, this is television that understands the camera as a tool of emotional manipulation and employs it with precision.

Visual Spectacle 7.0

The Handmaid's Tale operates with intentional restraint when it comes to spectacle, understanding that its power comes from intimacy rather than scale. The production design creates Gilead as a place of oppressive normalcy—the grandeur of the Waterford mansion, the institutional sterility of the Red Center, the blasted hellscape of the Colonies are all achieved through careful location work and set construction rather than digital augmentation. The visual impact comes from juxtaposition: the red habits against grey concrete, the brutalist architecture housing medieval ritual, the natural beauty of the Massachusetts landscape corrupted by human evil.

When the show does go big, it tends to be effective: the wall where bodies hang as warnings provides recurring visual punctuation; the train that carries handmaids to their fates is appropriately ominous; the military checkpoints and checkpoints create tension through their mundanity. However, the series' budget limitations sometimes show, particularly in crowd scenes that feel underpopulated for a nation supposedly in the grip of religious revolution. The Canadian sequences, meant to represent freedom, sometimes look surprisingly small-scale. What the show lacks in sweeping vistas it makes up for in architectural menace—the spaces of Gilead feel heavy with history and threat. The visual spectacle works not despite its modesty but because of it, grounding Atwood's dystopia in recognizable spaces that make it all the more disturbing.

Conceptual Density 8.5

Adapted from Margaret Atwood's already intellectually rich novel, the series expands its source material's thematic concerns into a multi-season meditation on power, gender, religion, and resistance. The conceptual density is remarkable—nearly every scene operates on multiple levels, addressing not just plot but the philosophical underpinnings of totalitarian control. The show interrogates complicity: how Serena Joy helped build a system that ultimately imprisons her, how civilians participate in their own oppression, how international communities rationalize inaction. It explores bodily autonomy as political territory, reproductive rights as foundational to all other freedoms, and language as a tool of both domination and liberation.

The series' engagement with its historical moment—premiering as the #MeToo movement gained momentum, continuing through attacks on abortion access—gives it documentary weight despite its fictional premise. The writers' room clearly understands the theological underpinnings of Gilead's ideology, engaging seriously with Christian nationalism and theocracy rather than treating religious extremism as cartoon villainy. The conceptual framework extends to the structure of the narrative itself, with unreliable narration, fragmented chronology, and the tension between individual survival and collective resistance. If there's a criticism, it's that the show sometimes becomes heavy-handed in its political messaging, but given the stakes of the subject matter, that directness feels earned. This is television that expects its audience to think, to make connections between fiction and reality, and to sit with uncomfortable implications.

Narrative Drive 7.0

The Handmaid's Tale operates with a narrative tension between its serialized structure and the essentially static nature of life in Gilead. The early seasons maintain propulsive momentum through June's increasing radicalization and attempts at escape, but the show struggles with the fundamental problem of a protagonist trapped in an inescapable system. Recurring cycles of hope and crushing disappointment—June plans escape, June nearly succeeds, June remains in Gilead—create emotional fatigue that begins to undermine narrative investment by the middle seasons. The series finds new momentum when it shifts focus to resistance networks and begins moving characters outside Gilead's borders, allowing for a broader story about international politics and exile.

The structure of individual episodes often emphasizes atmosphere over plot advancement, with slow-burn sequences that build tension through isolation and dread. This works beautifully in early seasons but becomes frustrating as the narrative stretches across six years. The show's strongest narrative choices often involve ensemble digressions—the backstory episodes that explain how characters became who they are, the glimpse into other households and other forms of resistance. The voiceover narration, lifted from Atwood's novel, sometimes over-explains what the visuals already communicate, though at its best it provides devastating interiority. By the final seasons, the narrative finds surer footing by committing to June's transformation into active resistance, though some fans feel this evolution comes too late. The series tells a complete story, but it's one that probably didn't require six seasons to achieve its full impact.

Resolution & Legacy 6.5

The Handmaid's Tale concludes with the same moral complexity that defined it, refusing easy answers about justice, forgiveness, and the possibility of healing after trauma. June's final arc—moving from victim to avenger to someone who must choose what kind of person she wants to be in freedom—provides satisfying emotional closure even as it leaves political questions deliberately open. The series finale acknowledges that Gilead continues, that revolutions are incomplete, that survivors carry their wounds into any future they manage to build. This refusal of triumphalism is artistically appropriate but may leave viewers craving more definitive catharsis after six seasons of suffering.

The show's legacy is secure as a cultural touchstone that helped elevate streaming television to prestige status. It proved that Hulu could compete in the original content space, that stories about women's experiences could be commercially viable, and that speculative fiction could be politically urgent. Its imagery—the handmaids' habits, the Latin resistance graffiti, the handmaid's tale as oral history—has entered the visual vocabulary of protest and dissent. However, the series will also be remembered for its uneven middle seasons, for stretching its premise perhaps too thin, for the decision to continue beyond Atwood's original novel into territory that sometimes felt less thematically focused than what came before. The announced sequel The Testaments may ultimately reshape how the original series is remembered, but as it stands, The Handmaid's Tale remains essential television that didn't quite stick its landing with the same precision that characterized its opening.

Total Weighted Score
7.55
The Handmaid's Tale stands as one of the most culturally significant television series of the 2010s—a visually stunning, politically urgent adaptation that occasionally struggled with the structural demands of multi-season storytelling. Elisabeth Moss delivers a career-defining performance, and the show's engagement with bodily autonomy, religious extremism, and women's resistance feels increasingly essential. While pacing issues and some repetitive narrative beats prevent it from achieving true perfection, its conceptual density and emotional power make it required viewing for understanding our contemporary political moment. A harrowing, beautiful, imperfect masterpiece.
