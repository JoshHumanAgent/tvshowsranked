What It Feels Like to Watch

There's a particular feeling that comes with watching The X-Files, something that has remained remarkably potent despite the passage of decades. It's that distinctive texture of grainy film stock meeting gloomy Pacific Northwest forests, where the rain seems to fall perpetually through amber-tinted light. From the moment Mark Snow's haunting synthesizer theme begins—those four descending notes that have become synonymous with unexplained phenomena—you're transported into a world where the boundary between the explainable and the inexplicable is perpetually dissolving.

The show wraps around you like damp Seattle fog. It whispers that the government is hiding things, that the truth is out there but deliberately obscured, and that sometimes the monsters are all too human. There's an intimacy to the experience that transcends typical television viewing. You're not just watching FBI agents investigate cases; you're becoming a partner in their quest, a believer in Mulder's office basement where the sun never shines and the cases deemed too strange for the Bureau's main floor accumulate like geological layers of the unexplainable.

Watching The X-Files today carries an additional dimension of nostalgic weight. The flip phones, the oversized suits, the grain of analog video footage—all serve as time capsules of a pre-digital age when information moved slower and conspiracy theories felt more grounded in physical documentation rather than internet ephemera. Yet remarkably, the show's core themes of government overreach, institutional distrust, and the unknown feel as relevant now as they did in the mid-1990s. It's watching history that feels like prophecy, entertainment that carries the weight of cultural anthropology.

Most of all, watching The X-Files feels like entering a dialogue with the unknown itself. It respects your intelligence while pushing your imagination into uncomfortable territories. It asks you to believe while teaching you to question. It's television as intellectual puzzle, emotional journey, and primal thrill ride all simultaneously.

Mulder & Scully Chemistry

David Duchovny and Gillian Anderson created one of television's most compelling partnerships. Their relationship evolved from professional skeptics to deeply bonded partners whose trust became the emotional anchor of the entire series. The will-they-won't-they tension sustained over eleven seasons became the template for modern procedural chemistry.

Alien Mythology

The overarching conspiracy narrative involving government cover-ups, alien colonization, and the Syndicate's shadowy dealings created an intricate mythology that rewarded dedicated viewers. The Cigarette Smoking Man, the black oil, the colonization date—these elements built a dark cosmic tapestry that became the show's signature.

Monster-of-the-Week

Between mythology episodes, the show excelled at standalone horror stories about genetic mutants, supernatural entities, and bizarre phenomena. These episodes showcased the show's versatility, from terrifying creature features like "Home" to poignant character studies like "Clyde Bruckman's Final Repose."

'90s Nostalgia

The show perfectly captured a specific pre-internet zeitgeist of conspiracy culture, UFO fascination, and institutional distrust. The aesthetic—grainy film, atmospheric Pacific Northwest locations, analog technology—creates a distinctive time capsule that paradoxically feels more relevant today.

Why It Ranks

Characters 7.8 / 10

The X-Files derives its enduring strength from the complexity of its central characters and the rich supporting cast that orbits them. Fox Mulder initially appears as the stereotypical tortured believer—brilliant but damaged, driven by the childhood trauma of his sister's abduction. David Duchovny imbues Mulder with layers of wit, vulnerability, and an almost childlike wonder that prevents the character from becoming merely a conspiracy caricature. His journey from obsessive seeker to nuanced agent willing to question his own certainties represents meaningful character growth across multiple seasons.

Dana Scully provides the perfect counterbalance, and in many ways, became the show's most revolutionary creation. Gillian Anderson crafted a character who was rigorously scientific yet never cold, skeptical but open to possibilities. Scully's devout Catholicism added spiritual dimensions that complicated her rationalist approach. She was a fully realized professional woman in a male-dominated field, whose competence and integrity never wavered. The skeptic/believer dynamic could have become repetitive, but both actors evolved their relationship organically, creating a partnership built on mutual respect that transcended romantic tension.

Supporting characters enriched the universe considerably. The Cigarette Smoking Man (William B. Davis) became iconic as the embodiment of shadowy government conspiracy. Walter Skinner (Mitch Pileggi) transformed from bureaucratic antagonist to reluctant ally with moral complexity. The Lone Gunmen provided comic relief while expanding the show's conspiracy mythology. Even guest characters in monster-of-the-week episodes often received full humanization, with villains like Eugene Tooms or sympathetic figures like Clyde Bruckman becoming memorable presences. Character development remained consistent even through cast changes in later seasons, with Robert Patrick's Agent Doggett bringing a military pragmatism that contrasted interestingly with Mulder's intuitive approach.

World Building 7.5 / 10

The X-Files constructed one of television's most intricate alternate realities, layering conspiracy upon conspiracy until the full scope of government-alien collaboration became almost overwhelming in its complexity. The mythology arc, spanning multiple seasons and culminating in feature films, created a dark alternative history where the Cold War served as cover for cooperation between shadowy human syndicates and alien colonization forces. This world-building ambition was unprecedented for 1990s television.

The show's universe expanded beyond individual cases to encompass complex institutional politics. The FBI hierarchy, with its mundane bureaucracy existing alongside extraordinary secret cases, grounded the supernatural elements in recognizable reality. The consortium meetings between shadowy figures in smoke-filled rooms created a governmental underworld operating beneath constitutional oversight. Elements like Majestic 12, the Cancer Man's personal history with the Mulder family, and the gradual revelation of colonization timelines demonstrated meticulous long-term planning.

However, the world-building eventually collapsed under its own weight by later seasons. Too many contradictory revelations, timeline inconsistencies, and the challenges of extending the alien colonization narrative indefinitely led to narrative fatigue. The 2016 revival struggled to reconcile mythology threads that had become hopelessly tangled. Despite these later struggles, the foundational world-building remains influential, establishing templates for X-Files-inspired shows from Fringe to Stranger Things. The balance between recognizable American institutions and unrestrained speculative fiction created a template that defined paranormal procedural storytelling.

Cinematography 7.5 / 10

The X-Files distinguished itself through visual storytelling that elevated television cinematography to cinematic standards in an era when most shows remained static and brightly lit. Director David Nutter, cinematographer John Bartley, and the production team developed a distinctive visual language centered on atmospheric dread and environmental storytelling. The show's signature aesthetic—rain-slicked Vancouver streets, fog-shrouded forests, stark institutional interiors—created an immediately recognizable visual identity.

Lighting design played crucial roles in establishing tone. Shadows weren't merely absences of light but active participants in scene construction. The basement office's perpetual artificial illumination, government buildings' harsh fluorescent sterility, and rural locations' naturalistic gloom each communicated character states and thematic concerns. Color grading evolved across seasons, with early episodes emphasizing browns and yellows (the famous "X-Files amber") that suggested both nostalgia and decay.

Camera movement balanced procedural restraint with horror invention. Static two-shots during interrogation sequences maintained procedural authenticity, while sudden handheld sequences during creature attacks injected visceral immediacy. The show employed unconventional angles—shooting through window blinds, using reflections and refractions, employing extreme close-ups during revelation moments—to disorient viewers and suggest hidden perspectives. The transition from 4:3 to widescreen format in later seasons, while inevitable, initially disrupted the established aesthetic. By the revival, advances in digital cinematography allowed for richer night work but sometimes sacrificed the grain analog aesthetic that had defined the show's visual character.

Visual Spectacle 7.0 / 10

Operating within network television constraints of the 1990s, The X-Files delivered impressive visual spectacle through creative practical effects and selective digital enhancement rather than overwhelming CGI. The show's creature designs—from Eugene Tooms' boneless flexibility to the Flukeman's parasitic grotesqueness—relied on prosthetics and animatronics that aged remarkably well due to the show's preference for shadow and suggestion over full revelation.

Special effects sequences appeared sporadically but impactfully. The black oil entity, with its tendril-like movements and violation of human hosts, created genuinely disturbing visual imagery. Alien spaceship encounters, government installations, and scientific anomalies received proportionate attention—the show never let effects overwhelm narrative. When spectacle arrived, it mattered: the Arctic facility in "Ice," the submarine in "Colony"/"End Game," or the massive set pieces of the feature films demonstrated expanded resources when circumstances justified them.

Action sequences evolved considerably throughout the series. Early episodes emphasized dread and psychological tension over physical confrontation. As the show progressed, particularly into its cinematic phase and later seasons, set pieces became more elaborate—chases, shootouts, and physical combat all receiving increased emphasis. However, this evolution brought mixed results; some action felt forced, inserted to match escalating genre competition rather than emerging organically from character and plot. The revival seasons sometimes struggled with pacing between contemplative investigation and blockbuster spectacle expectations. Overall, the show understood its visual limits and often used them advantageously, letting imagination fill gaps that unlimited CGI might have rendered mundane.

Conceptual Density 7.4 / 10

The X-Files operated at an intellectual level rare for broadcast television, weaving together folklore, parapsychology, conspiracy theory, religious symbolism, and frontier science into dense narrative tapestries. Each episode could reference multiple traditions: genetic mutation episodes drew upon eugenics history and biological ethics; alien episodes invoked Cold War paranoia, ufology literature, and ancient astronaut theories; supernatural episodes engaged Catholic theology, occult traditions, and Jungian psychology.

The mythology arc in particular demanded active viewership. Following the Syndicate's schemes required tracking colonization plans, alien factions, government alliances, vaccine development, and individual character motivations across dozens of episodes and years of real-world continuity. The show rewarded attentive viewing with connections that less engaged viewers might miss entirely. The Cigarette Smoking Man's personal connections to both Mulder's family and Scully's abduction weren't immediately obvious but retroactively enriched earlier episodes.

However, conceptual density sometimes became overwhelming. By the later mythology episodes, the accumulation of revelations, reversals, and timeline complications threatened to alienate casual viewers. The show occasionally sacrificed clarity for mystery, leaving significant questions unanswered or providing answers that raised new questions rather than resolving existing ones. The revival seasons attempted to simplify mythology while introducing new science fiction concepts (genetic manipulation, Spartan virus, etc.) but struggled to balance accessibility for new viewers with satisfaction for dedicated fans. Despite these challenges, the show's willingness to engage with complex ideas—its fundamental respect for viewer intelligence—remains its most admirable conceptual achievement.

Narrative Drive 7.4 / 10

The X-Files pioneered the hybrid episodic/serialized format that has become standard for modern genre television. Its dual structure—alternating between standalone "monster-of-the-week" episodes and mythology episodes advancing ongoing conspiracy plots—created multiple vectors of narrative momentum. Standalone episodes provided immediate satisfaction within 44 minutes, while mythology episodes built accumulated stakes across seasons.

The monster-of-the-week episodes often achieved remarkable narrative efficiency. Writers like Darin Morgan, Glen Morgan, and James Wong crafted self-contained stories that could evoke dread, explore moral complexity, or deliver emotional catharsis within tight time constraints. Episodes like "Clyde Bruckman's Final Repose" (about a reluctant psychic), "Jose Chung's From Outer Space" (metafictional examination of belief), or "Home" (horror about isolated family mutation) demonstrated how standalone format could achieve thematic depth rivaling serialized storytelling.

The mythology narrative drove significant viewer investment through the show's peak years, with major revelations—Scully's cancer, sister abduction confirmation, colonization timeline—arriving at satisfying intervals. Cliffhanger season endings became expectations, with "Anasazi"/"The Blessing Way"/"Paper Clip" and "Gethsemane"/"Redux" particularly effective at launching sustained narrative momentum across season breaks. However, sustaining meaningful escalation across eleven seasons plus feature films proved impossible. Later mythology episodes suffered from diminishing returns, with reveals feeling repetitive or contradictory. The 2016 revival attempted to restart narrative momentum but struggled to justify new developments after conclusive endings. Nevertheless, the core narrative architecture established templates that influenced Lost, Fringe, and virtually every genre show since.

Resolution 7.4 / 10

Evaluating The X-Files' resolution requires distinguishing between different types of endings the show attempted across its various phases. The original series finale, "The Truth" (2002), attempted to resolve key mythology elements while acknowledging that many questions had to remain open. It provided definitive conclusions regarding Mulder's trial, the conspiracy's exposure, and the series' thematic preoccupation with truth versus institutional power, though alien colonization remained pending rather than fulfilled.

The 2008 feature film The X-Files: I Want to Believe deliberately stepped away from mythology to focus on a standalone thriller about medical ethics and faith. While commercially disappointing, it offered a form of resolution—Mulder and Scully in explicit romantic relationship, both having left the FBI, confronting questions of belief and redemption. This conclusion emphasized character over plot, suggesting that Mulder and Scully's partnership itself was "the truth" worth believing in.

The 2016 revival season complicated resolution considerably, reopening mythology threads that had been closed and introducing new conspiracies. While the revival provided additional closure regarding William's identity and the Syndicate's ultimate fate, it also introduced new questions about the Spartan virus and humanity's engineered future. The cliffhanger ending of "My Struggle IV" left several characters in ambiguous states, though with Mulder and Scully united and expecting another child. Resolution-wise, The X-Files suffers from having multiple endings across different media and eras, each partially satisfying certain expectations while disappointing others. Individual episodes, particularly character-focused outings, often achieved beautiful resolution within their 44-minute formats, while the overarching mythology resisted definitive closure—a fitting metaphor for the show's themes, perhaps, but occasionally frustrating for invested viewers seeking answers.

Final Score
7.4

The X-Files stands as one of television's most influential genre achievements, fundamentally shaping how supernatural procedural television operates. Its blend of paranoid conspiracy mythology with standalone horror storytelling created a template that dominated 1990s genre entertainment and continues influencing contemporary shows. David Duchovny and Gillian Anderson's partnership provided an emotional anchor that transcended the show's increasingly convoluted mythology, while Chris Carter and the writing staff demonstrated unprecedented ambition for network television world-building.

A score of 7.4 reflects genuine greatness compromised by later-season decline and mythology bloat, yet the foundational excellence remains undimmed. From its atmospheric visual style to its intellectual fearlessness, The X-Files proved that genre television could achieve genuine art. The truth may remain out there, but the show's impact is definitively here—with us still.
