The Show That Wasn't Afraid to Be Weird

The OA is one of television's great enigmas—a series that dared to be incomprehensible and trusted its audience to follow. Brit Marling and Zal Batmanglij created something that shouldn't exist: a mainstream Netflix show about interdimensional travel, interpretive dance as liberation, and the power of storytelling to save lives.

Prairie's Story

The structure is nesting dolls. Prairie Johnson returns after seven years missing—blind when she vanished, sighted when she returns, refusing to say where she was. She gathers five strangers in an abandoned house and tells them a story that might be true, might be fantasy, or might be something in between.

The Movements

The "movements"—choreographed gestures Prairie teaches her followers—are either dimension-crossing magic or the delusions of a traumatized woman. The show never tells us which. The season one finale, in which these movements are performed in a school shooting situation, is one of television's most audacious sequences. Whether you buy it depends entirely on how much weirdness you'll accept.

Season Two's Expansion

The second season leaps into a parallel dimension, introducing a detective story, a giant invisible octopus, and a meta-narrative that suggests our world might be fiction too. It's bolder and stranger than season one—which is why Netflix cancelled it before resolution.

The Unfinished Ending

The score's resolution reflects the cancellation. The show was planned for five seasons; it got two. What exists is incomplete—but the journey is unlike anything else on television. For viewers who accept ambiguity, The OA offers experiences no other show provides.

Verdict: Ambitious, baffling, beautiful. A show that trusted its audience more than its platform.
