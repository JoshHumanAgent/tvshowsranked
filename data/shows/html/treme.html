Post-Katrina New Orleans

Tremé is David Simon's love letter to New Orleans—a show about a city rebuilding itself through music, food, and stubborn refusal to disappear. It's not The Wire; it's something gentler and more humane. The crime exists, but so do the second lines, the brass bands, and the gumbo.

Music as Narrative

The show centers on musicians—real New Orleans artists playing themselves alongside actors. Wendell Pierce as Antoine Batiste, a trombonist always one gig away from stability. Clarke Peters as Albert "Big Chief" Lambreaux, Mardi Gras Indian chief rebuilding his tribe. The music isn't soundtrack; it's plot, character, and meaning.

The World Building Score

Nine out of ten. No show has ever captured a city this completely—the food, the music, the politics, the corruption, the resilience. Simon and co-creator Eric Overmyer treat New Orleans as a character deserving respect, not a backdrop for outsider stories.

Slow Television

The narrative drive score reflects Tremé's intentional pacing. This isn't binge television; it's Sunday night television, best absorbed slowly. Stories unfold over seasons, not episodes. If you need constant momentum, this isn't your show. If you want immersion, it's essential.

The Complete Story

Four seasons, thirty-six episodes, a full arc. HBO let Simon finish on his terms, and the final season provides real closure. The resolution score reflects a show that knew when to end.

Verdict: Television as cultural preservation. David Simon's most human work.
