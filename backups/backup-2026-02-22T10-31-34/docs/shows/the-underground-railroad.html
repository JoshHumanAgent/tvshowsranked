What It Feels Like to Watch

The Underground Railroad premiered on Amazon Prime Video on May 14, 2021, created and directed by Barry Jenkins from Colson Whitehead's Pulitzer Prize-winning 2016 novel. The ten-episode limited series reimagines the historical Underground Railroad as an actual subterranean train system transporting escaped slaves through antebellum America. The series follows Cora (Thuso Mbedu), a young enslaved woman who escapes a Georgia plantation and travels through different states, each representing a different aspect of American racism and oppression.

The series is a profound reimagining of American history through the lens of magical realism. By literalizing the Underground Railroad as an actual train system—complete with conductors, stations, and tracks—Jenkins creates a visual metaphor for the secret networks that helped enslaved people escape while also suggesting the impossible dream of freedom in a nation built on slavery. The train becomes both real and symbolic, historical and fantastical.

Each state Cora travels through represents a different historical and philosophical approach to race in America: South Carolina presents itself as progressive with eugenics programs and forced sterilization disguised as benevolence; North Carolina has banned Black people entirely, with "freedom trails" of lynching victims; Tennessee is a wasteland of disease and destruction; Indiana offers a brief glimpse of Black prosperity and community before violence destroys it. This structure allows the series to examine the many faces of American racism.

Thuso Mbedu's performance as Cora is extraordinary—she carries the series through unimaginable suffering while maintaining dignity and humanity. Cora's journey from terrified escapee to determined survivor to someone who chooses to stop running and fight back tracks Mbedu's remarkable range. Her physical performance—running through forests, hiding in attics, the exhaustion and terror etched on her face—makes Cora's ordeal viscerally real.

The supporting cast creates a world of complex characters: Joel Edgerton as Ridgeway, the slave catcher whose pursuit of Cora becomes obsession, his character given philosophical depth through his conversations with Cora about the "American spirit" and Manifest Destiny; Aaron Pierre as Caesar, the escaped slave who first brings Cora to the railroad and whose fate haunts her; William Jackson Harper as Royal, the Indiana farmer who offers Cora love and community; Sheila Atim as Grace, another escapee who shares Cora's journey; Lily Rabe as Ethel, the North Carolina woman who betrays Cora; and Chase W. Dillon as Homer, Ridgeway's young Black assistant whose loyalty to his captor represents the psychological damage of slavery.

Barry Jenkins's direction is visually and emotionally stunning. Known for Moonlight (2016) and If Beale Street Could Talk (2018), Jenkins brings his signature style—intimate close-ups, golden-hour lighting, moments of quiet beauty amid horror—to television. The series is filled with Jenkins's trademark shots: faces in profile, hands touching, the play of light on skin. Even the most horrific scenes are shot with aesthetic care that never feels exploitative.

The cinematography by James Laxton (Jenkins's frequent collaborator) creates a visual language that shifts between the warm tones of memory and the cold reality of the present. The Underground Railroad itself is shot with almost magical lighting—golden and hopeful, suggesting the promise of freedom even as the reality proves more complex. The various states have distinct visual palettes: South Carolina's false brightness, North Carolina's stark whiteness, Tennessee's desaturated wasteland, Indiana's warm community.

The series' sound design and score by Nicholas Britell (another Jenkins collaborator) creates atmosphere through ambient sound, period-appropriate music, and unsettling tones. The sound of the train, the creaking of the house, the ambient noise of each state—the audio landscape is as carefully constructed as the visual.

The series doesn't shy away from the brutality of slavery and its aftermath. The violence—whippings, lynchings, the casual cruelty of slave owners and slave catchers—is depicted with unflinching honesty. But Jenkins never lets the violence become spectacle; it's always tied to character and theme, always serving the larger story about the damage slavery inflicts on both the enslaved and the nation.

The series' engagement with history is complex. By literalizing the Underground Railroad, Jenkins creates a visual metaphor that makes the historical network tangible while also suggesting its limitations—the train doesn't lead to freedom but to different forms of oppression. The series suggests that there was no true escape from slavery in America, that the entire nation was complicit in the system. This is history as tragedy, not triumph.

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive—the series holds 94% on Rotten Tomatoes, with critics praising Jenkins's direction, Mbedu's performance, and the series' emotional depth. It won the Golden Globe for Best Limited Series and received numerous Emmy nominations. Critics noted that the series transcended the horror genre to become a profound meditation on American history and trauma.

Barry Jenkins's Cinematic Vision

Jenkins brought his signature style from Moonlight and If Beale Street Could Talk to television—intimate close-ups, golden-hour lighting, moments of quiet beauty amid horror. His approach treats the material with the reverence of cinema, using the expanded runtime of television to develop characters and themes with novelistic depth. The result is a ten-hour film that ranks among the medium's greatest achievements.

Jenkins's visual language emphasizes the humanity of the enslaved—close-ups that capture the full range of human emotion, lighting that renders Black skin with beauty and dignity, compositions that suggest both the oppression of slavery and the resilience of the human spirit. This approach counters the historical tendency to depict enslaved people as either victims or heroes, instead showing them as fully human individuals.

Episode Guide and Key Sequences

Episode 1 "Georgia": The series opens with Cora's life on the Randall plantation and her decision to escape after Caesar approaches her about the Underground Railroad. The episode establishes the brutality of slavery and Cora's determination. The escape sequence, culminating in Cora and Caesar finding the actual railroad, provides the series' central visual metaphor.

Episode 2 "South Carolina": Cora and Caesar arrive in what appears to be a progressive state—Black people hold jobs, receive education, live in dignity. But the episode reveals the horror beneath: eugenics programs, forced sterilization, the "scientific" racism that views Black bodies as subjects for experimentation. The episode critiques progressive rhetoric that masks continued oppression.

Episode 3 "North Carolina": Cora hides in an attic (the series' reference to Harriet Jacobs's Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl) in a state that has banned Black people entirely. The "freedom trail" of lynching victims, the white population's complicity, the terror of existing in a place where your very presence is illegal—this episode depicts the most extreme form of American racism.

Episode 4 "The Great Spirit of the Dead": Cora's journey through Tennessee, a wasteland of disease and destruction. The episode's fever-dream quality, the encounter with the traveling show, the continued pursuit by Ridgeway—all create a sense of American landscape as nightmare.

Episode 5 "Indiana": Cora finds temporary refuge in a community of free Black people—a farm, a school, a vision of what America could be. Her relationship with Royal offers hope. But the episode's climax—the white mob's attack, the destruction of the community—demonstrates that there is no safe place in America for Black freedom.

Episodes 6-10: The final episodes bring Cora's journey to its conclusion—Ridgeway's final pursuit, the revelation of Cora's mother's fate, Cora's choice to stop running and confront her past, and the ambiguous ending that suggests both survival and continued struggle. The series refuses easy resolution, acknowledging that the wounds of slavery cannot be healed by one woman's journey.

The series stands as one of the most ambitious and accomplished works in television history—a ten-hour film that uses the horror genre to examine the deepest wounds of American history. Jenkins proves that television can achieve the artistic heights of cinema while maintaining the emotional intimacy of the small screen. The Underground Railroad is essential viewing—not just for horror fans, but for anyone interested in what television can achieve when it dares to be ambitious.
